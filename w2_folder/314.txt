<p><b>Malachite green</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a> that is used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyestuff" title="Dyestuff">dyestuff</a> and controversially as an antimicrobial in <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>. Malachite green is traditionally used as a dye for materials such as <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leather" title="Leather">leather</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>. Despite its name the dye is not prepared from the <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> <a href="/wiki/Malachite" title="Malachite">malachite</a>, and the name just comes from the similarity of color.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structures_and_properties">Structures and properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malachite_green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structures and properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Malachite green is classified in the dyestuff industry as a <a href="/wiki/Triarylmethane_dye" title="Triarylmethane dye">triarylmethane dye</a> and also using in pigment industry. Formally, malachite green refers to the chloride salt [C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>C(C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>N(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>]Cl, although the term malachite green is used loosely and often just refers to the colored <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cation" title="Cation">cation</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Oxalate" title="Oxalate">oxalate</a> salt is also marketed. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anion" title="Anion">anions</a> have no effect on the color. The intense green color of the cation results from a strong absorption band at 621 nm (<a href="/wiki/Mass_attenuation_coefficient" title="Mass attenuation coefficient">extinction coefficient</a> of <span class="nowrap">10<sup>5</sup> M<sup>−1</sup> cm<sup>−1</sup></span>).
</p>

<p>Malachite green is prepared by the condensation of <a href="/wiki/Benzaldehyde" title="Benzaldehyde">benzaldehyde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dimethylaniline" title="Dimethylaniline">dimethylaniline</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Leuco_dye" title="Leuco dye">leuco</a> malachite green (LMG):
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>CHO + 2 C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>N(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> →  C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>CH(C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>N(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Second, this colorless leuco compound, a relative of <a href="/wiki/Triphenylmethane" title="Triphenylmethane">triphenylmethane</a>, is oxidized to the cation that is MG:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>CH(C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>N(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + HCl + ​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> O<sub>2</sub> → [C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>C(C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>N(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>]Cl + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>A typical oxidizing agent is <a href="/wiki/Manganese_dioxide" title="Manganese dioxide">manganese dioxide</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:640px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MalachiteGr%26Leuco.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="514" data-file-width="2895" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/MalachiteGr%26Leuco.png/638px-MalachiteGr%26Leuco.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/MalachiteGr%26Leuco.png/957px-MalachiteGr%26Leuco.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/MalachiteGr%26Leuco.png/1276px-MalachiteGr%26Leuco.png 2x" width="638"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MalachiteGr%26Leuco.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>On the left is leuco-malachite Green (LMG) and on the right are the two equivalent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resonance_structure" title="Resonance structure">resonance structures</a> of the MG cation. The alcohol derivative of MG is derived from LMG by replacement of the unique C–H by C–OH.</div></div></div>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<p>Hydrolysis of MG gives an <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>[C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>C(C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>N(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>]Cl + H<sub>2</sub>O → C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>C(OH)(C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>N(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + HCl</dd></dl>
<p>This alcohol is important because it, not MG, traverses cell membranes. Once inside the cell, it is metabolized into LMG. Only the cation MG is deeply colored, whereas the leuco and alcohol derivatives are not. This difference arises because only the cationic form has extended pi-delocalization, which allows the molecule to absorb visible light.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malachite_green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Leuco_dye" title="Leuco dye">leuco</a> form of malachite green was first prepared by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermann_Emil_Fischer" title="Hermann Emil Fischer">Hermann Fischer</a> in 1877 by condensing <a href="/wiki/Benzaldehyde" title="Benzaldehyde">benzaldehyde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dimethylaniline" title="Dimethylaniline">dimethylaniline</a> in the molecular ratio 1:2 in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="floatleft"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Malachite_green_preparation1.tif" title="Synthesis of malachite green"><img alt="Synthesis of malachite green" data-file-height="775" data-file-width="1847" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Malachite_green_preparation1.tif/lossy-page1-500px-Malachite_green_preparation1.tif.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Malachite_green_preparation1.tif/lossy-page1-750px-Malachite_green_preparation1.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Malachite_green_preparation1.tif/lossy-page1-1000px-Malachite_green_preparation1.tif.jpg 2x" width="500"/></a></div>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malachite_green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Malachite green is traditionally used as a dye. Millions of kilograms of MG and related triarylmethane dyes are produced annually for this purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>MG is active against the <a href="/wiki/Oomycete" title="Oomycete">oomycete</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Saprolegnia" title="Saprolegnia">Saprolegnia</a></i>, which infects fish eggs in commercial <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>, MG has been used to treat <i><a href="/wiki/Saprolegnia" title="Saprolegnia">Saprolegnia</a></i> and is used as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibacterial" title="Antibacterial">antibacterial</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It is a very popular treatment against <i><a href="/wiki/Ichthyophthirius_multifiliis" title="Ichthyophthirius multifiliis">Ichthyophthirius multifiliis</a></i> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freshwater" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> <a href="/wiki/Aquarium" title="Aquarium">aquaria</a>. The principal <a href="/wiki/Metabolite" title="Metabolite">metabolite</a>, LMG, is found in fish treated with malachite green, and this finding is the basis of controversy and government regulation. See also <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobials_in_aquaculture" title="Antimicrobials in aquaculture">Antimicrobials in aquaculture</a>.
</p><p>MG has frequently been used to catch thieves and pilferers. The bait, usually money, is sprinkled with the anhydrous powder. Anyone handling the contaminated money will find that on upon washing the hands, a green stain on the skin that lasts for several days will result.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niche_uses">Niche uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malachite_green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Niche uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bacillus_subtilis_Spore.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="500" height="195" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Bacillus_subtilis_Spore.jpg/260px-Bacillus_subtilis_Spore.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Bacillus_subtilis_Spore.jpg/390px-Bacillus_subtilis_Spore.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7a/Bacillus_subtilis_Spore.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bacillus_subtilis_Spore.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A preparation of <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_subtilis" title="Bacillus subtilis">Bacillus subtilis</a></i> showing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endospores" title="Endospores">endospores</a> stained with malachite green (vegetative cells are stained red)</div></div></div><p> Numerous niche applications exploit the intense color of MG. It is used as a <a href="/wiki/Staining" title="Staining">biological stain</a> for <a href="/wiki/Microscopy" title="Microscopy">microscopic</a> analysis of <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cell biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Histology" title="Histology">tissue samples</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Gimenez_stain" title="Gimenez stain">Gimenez staining method</a>, basic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuchsin" title="Fuchsin">fuchsin</a> stains bacteria red or magenta, and malachite green is used as a blue-green <a href="/wiki/Counterstain" title="Counterstain">counterstain</a>. Malachite green is also used in <a href="/wiki/Endospore_staining" title="Endospore staining">endospore staining</a>, since it can directly stain <a href="/wiki/Endospore" title="Endospore">endospores</a> within bacterial cells; here a <a href="/wiki/Safranin" title="Safranin">safranin</a> counterstain is often used. Malachite green can also be used as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturable_absorber" title="Saturable absorber">saturable absorber</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dye_laser" title="Dye laser">dye lasers</a>, or as a <a href="/wiki/PH_indicator" title="PH indicator">pH indicator</a> between pH 0.2–1.8. However, this use is relatively rare. Leuco-malachite green (LMG) is used as a detection method for latent blood in <a href="/wiki/Forensic_science" title="Forensic science">forensic science</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">Hemoglobin</a> catalyzes the reaction between LMG and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>, converting the colorless LMG into malachite green. Therefore, the appearance of a green color indicates the presence of <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malachite_green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1992, Canadian authorities determined that eating fish contaminated with malachite green posed a significant health risk.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Malachite green was classified a Class II Health Hazard. Due to its low manufacturing cost, malachite green is still used in certain countries with less restrictive laws for non <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a> purposes. In 2005, analysts in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> found traces of malachite green in <a href="/wiki/Eel" title="Eel">eels</a> and fish imported from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. In 2006, the United States <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) detected malachite green in seafood imported from China, <a href="/wiki/Applied_Food_Technologies#Antimicrobials_in_aquaculture" title="Applied Food Technologies">among others</a>, where the substance is also banned for use in aquaculture. In June 2007, the FDA blocked the importation of several varieties of seafood due to continued malachite green contamination.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The substance has been banned in the United States since 1983 in food-related applications. It is banned in the UK, also.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
Animals <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolize</a> malachite green to its <a href="/wiki/Leuco_dye" title="Leuco dye">leuco</a> form. Being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipophillic" title="Lipophillic">lipophillic</a> (the leuco form has a <a href="/wiki/Partition_coefficient" title="Partition coefficient">log <i>P</i></a> of 5.70), the <a href="/wiki/Metabolite" title="Metabolite">metabolite</a> is retained in <a href="/wiki/Catfish" title="Catfish">catfish</a> muscle longer (<a href="/wiki/Biological_half-life" title="Biological half-life">HL</a> = 10 days) than is the parent molecule (HL = 2.8 days).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Malachite_green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Median_lethal_dose" title="Median lethal dose">LD<sub style="font-size:-1">50</sub></a> (oral, mouse) is 80 mg/kg.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Rats fed malachite green experience "a dose-related increase in liver <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_adducts" title="DNA adducts">DNA adducts</a>" along with lung <a href="/wiki/Adenoma" title="Adenoma">adenomas</a>. Leucomalachite green causes an "increase in the number and severity of changes". As leucomalachite green is the primary metabolite of malachite green and is retained in fish muscle much longer, most human dietary intake of malachite green from eating fish would be in the leuco form. During the experiment, rats were fed up to 543 ppm of leucomalachite green, an extreme amount compared to the average 5 ppb discovered in fish. After a period of two years, an increase in lung adenomas in male rats was discovered but no incidences of liver tumors. Therefore, it could be concluded that malachite green caused carcinogenic symptoms, but a direct link between malachite green and <a href="/wiki/Liver_tumor" title="Liver tumor">liver tumor</a> was not established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
