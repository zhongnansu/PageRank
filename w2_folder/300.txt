<p><b>Europe Ecology – The Greens</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Europe Écologie Les Verts</i> <small></small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[øʁɔp‿ekɔlɔʒi levɛʁ]</a></span>, <b>EELV</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green</a> and <a href="/wiki/Centre-left_politics" title="Centre-left politics">centre-left</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_France" title="List of political parties in France">political party</a> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nordsieck_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nordsieck-2">[2]</a></sup> The party was formed on 13 November 2010 from the merger of <a href="/wiki/The_Greens_(France)" title="The Greens (France)">The Greens</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europe_%C3%89cologie" title="Europe Écologie">Europe Ecology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lansford2014_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lansford2014-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europe_Ecology_%E2%80%93_The_Greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/French_municipal_elections,_2008" title="French municipal elections, 2008">2008 municipal elections</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Greens_(France)" title="The Greens (France)">The Greens</a> sought to increase their political influence. Echoing these calls, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Cohn-Bendit" title="Daniel Cohn-Bendit">Daniel Cohn-Bendit</a> proposed the creation of open electoral lists for the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2009_(France)" title="European Parliament election, 2009 (France)">2009 European elections</a> and the Greens' leadership allowed for the exploration of this possibility. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europe_%C3%89cologie" title="Europe Écologie">Europe Ecology</a> (EE), launched in the autumn of 2008, allowed The Greens to create a wider <a href="/wiki/Electoral_alliance" title="Electoral alliance">electoral alliance</a> with <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalists</a> and social activists who had not been party members in the past. The new structure included, alongside longtime Green politicians, new activists or environmentalists such as <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Besset" title="Jean-Paul Besset">Jean-Paul Besset</a> (close to <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Hulot" title="Nicolas Hulot">Nicolas Hulot</a>), <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Bov%C3%A9" title="José Bové">José Bové</a> (<a href="/wiki/Alter-globalization" title="Alter-globalization">alter-globalisation</a> activist from the <i>Confédération paysanne</i>), <a href="/wiki/Yannick_Jadot" title="Yannick Jadot">Yannick Jadot</a> (former head of <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> France), <a href="/wiki/Eva_Joly" title="Eva Joly">Eva Joly</a> (magistrate) and <a href="/wiki/Mich%C3%A8le_Rivasi" title="Michèle Rivasi">Michèle Rivasi</a> (founder of <a href="/wiki/Commission_for_Independent_Research_and_Information_on_Radioactivity" title="Commission for Independent Research and Information on Radioactivity">CRIIAD</a>).
</p><p>EE was successful in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2009_(France)" title="European Parliament election, 2009 (France)">2009 European elections</a> on 7 June 2009, reaching third place in France with 16.3% of the vote, only a few thousand votes behind the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(France)" title="Socialist Party (France)">Socialist Party</a> (PS), winning 14 of France's 72 seats in the European Parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lansford2014_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lansford2014-4">[4]</a></sup> The experience led to further attempts to expand the French green movement, ahead of the <a href="/wiki/French_regional_elections,_2010" title="French regional elections, 2010">2010 regional elections</a>. Europe Ecology ran independent lists in the first round in every region, once again with the participation of new activists including <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Meirieu" title="Philippe Meirieu">Philippe Meirieu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Vichnievsky" title="Laurence Vichnievsky">Laurence Vichnievsky</a> or <a href="/wiki/Augustin_Legrand" title="Augustin Legrand">Augustin Legrand</a>. While they fell short of their 2009 success, EE nevertheless managed to win 12.5% of the vote nationally (third place).
</p><p>The Greens and those new activists who joined the movement by way of EE – but who did not wish to join the party – began talks to allow for the creation of a new, enlarged <a href="/wiki/Political_movement" title="Political movement">political movement</a>. In October and November 2010, EE and later The Greens ratified new statutes and a new manifesto. Notably, these new statutes allowed for "cooperators" - individuals who did not join the party as full paying members but who are nonetheless allowed to run as candidates, vote in presidential primaries and partake in debates over the platform.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrechonPierre_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BrechonPierre-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The official launch of the new party, presented as a new political force, was held in <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a> on 9 November 2010. The new party adopted the name Europe Ecology – The Greens (<i>Europe Écologie Les Verts</i>, EELV). However, the launch of the party was marked by tensions between longtime politicians from the former Green party and new activists from various non-political social movements. Jean-Paul Besset, for example, resigned all his leadership responsibilities in EELV within weeks and denounced a "poisonous <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> atmosphere".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A month later, <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Meirieu" title="Philippe Meirieu">Philippe Meirieu</a> was named as the first president of the party's new federal council, created by the EELV statutes.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/French_cantonal_elections,_2011" title="French cantonal elections, 2011">2011 cantonal elections</a>, EELV won 8.2% of the vote nationally – becoming the third largest force on the left behind the PS and <a href="/wiki/Left_Front_(France)" title="Left Front (France)">Left Front</a> (FG). Although the traditional runoff deals were sealed with both of these parties, some EELV candidates qualified for the runoff against other left-wing candidates did not withdraw, creating tensions with EELV's traditional left-wing allies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Ultimately, EELV won 27 seats, 16 more seats than what the Greens had won in the same series of cantons in 2004.
</p><p>A presidential primary to nominate a candidate for the <a href="/wiki/French_presidential_election,_2012" title="French presidential election, 2012">2012 presidential election</a>, open to members and cooperators, was held in June and July 2011. Four candidates sought the EELV nomination, most notably <a href="/wiki/Eva_Joly" title="Eva Joly">Eva Joly</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">MEP</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Hulot" title="Nicolas Hulot">Nicolas Hulot</a>, a well known TV personality and environmentalist. Joly emerged victorious in the runoff on 12 July with 58.16%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/French_Senate_election,_2011" title="French Senate election, 2011">2011 senatorial elections</a>, an agreement with the PS allowed for the first centre-left senatorial majority under the <a href="/wiki/French_Fifth_Republic" title="French Fifth Republic">Fifth Republic</a> and the creation of the first entirely green parliamentary group.
</p><p>On 15 November 2011, EELV and the PS signed a coalition agreement prior to the 2012 presidential election. The agreement included a commitment to reduce the share of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_energy_in_France" title="Nuclear energy in France">nuclear energy in France</a> from 75% to 50% by 2025, the progressive shutdown of 24 nuclear reactors, the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tax" title="Carbon tax">carbon tax</a> and raising taxes on very high incomes. The agreement also included an ad hoc electoral deal for the <a href="/wiki/French_legislative_election,_2012" title="French legislative election, 2012">2012 legislative elections</a> in which the PS conceded over 60 constituencies to EELV, which would allow EELV to form a parliamentary group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> On 8 May 2012, following the left's victory with <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Hollande" title="François Hollande">François Hollande</a>, EELV's federal council voted in favour of cabinet participation in the new left-wing government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/French_presidential_election,_2012" title="French presidential election, 2012">2012 presidential election</a>, EELV candidate Eva Joly won 2.3% of the vote and was eliminated in the first round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lansford2014_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lansford2014-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson2014_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson2014-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/French_legislative_election,_2012" title="French legislative election, 2012">2012 legislative elections</a>, EELV candidates won 5.46% nationally and elected a record 17 deputies (in addition to one member of the regionalist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breton_Democratic_Union" title="Breton Democratic Union">Breton Democratic Union</a>, backed by EELV). However, every EELV deputy who was victorious had benefited from the endorsement of the PS, although many faced local PS dissidents.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ayrault_Government" title="Ayrault Government">government of Jean-Marc Ayrault</a> formed on 16 May 2012, EELV has two cabinet ministers: former party leader <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9cile_Duflot" title="Cécile Duflot">Cécile Duflot</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_Housing_(France)" title="Minister of Housing (France)">minister of housing and territorial equality</a>, and former MEP <a href="/wiki/Pascal_Canfin" title="Pascal Canfin">Pascal Canfin</a> as junior minister for international development.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2014_(France)" title="European Parliament election, 2014 (France)">2014 European elections</a> on 25 May 2014, EELV received 8.95% of the vote, sixth place nationally, returning 6 MEPs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideology">Ideology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europe_Ecology_%E2%80%93_The_Greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ideology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a green party, EELV prioritises and emphasises environmental issues. It calls for a 40% reduction in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CO2_emissions" title="CO2 emissions">CO<sub>2</sub> emissions</a>, phasing out <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear energy</a> in favour of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>, the creation of 600,000 'green jobs', eco-friendly <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planning</a> (the creation of green housing and the promotion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transportation" title="Public transportation">public transportation</a>), the development of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a> and a moratorium on the use of <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organism" title="Genetically modified organism">genetically modified organisms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-projet_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-projet-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Independent_sources" title="Wikipedia:Independent sources"><span title="This claim needs a reference to a independent, third-party source. (August 2015)">third-party source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> EELV, like the Greens before it, has generally opposed large-scale development projects, most recently the <i><a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9roport_du_Grand_Ouest" title="Aéroport du Grand Ouest">Aéroport du Grand Ouest</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Notre-Dame-des-Landes" title="Notre-Dame-des-Landes">Notre-Dame-des-Landes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Loire-Atlantique" title="Loire-Atlantique">Loire-Atlantique</a>), although the PS and the incumbent government officially support the project.
</p><p>On economic issues, EELV leans strongly to the left. Besides the creation of 'green jobs' in fields such as thermal isolation and renewable energies, it also supports a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tax" title="Carbon tax">carbon tax</a> and raising the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_tax" title="Progressive tax">progressive income tax</a> levels for high earners (60% for incomes between €100,000 and €500,000, 70% for incomes over €500,000). EELV is close to some <a href="/wiki/Anti-capitalism" title="Anti-capitalism">anticapitalist</a> and many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alter-globalisation" title="Alter-globalisation">alter-globalisation</a> activists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrechonPierre_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-BrechonPierre-5">[5]</a></sup> In its alternative budget in 2011, EELV proposed to reduce the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_debt" title="Public debt">public debt</a> by closing fiscal loopholes.
</p><p>The party has traditionally supported <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_federalism" title="European federalism">European federalism</a>, although many of its European policies are in conflict with the current direction and leadership of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. EELV, like the Greens before it, has been one of the strongest proponents of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decentralisation" title="Decentralisation">decentralisation</a>, officially supporting "differentiated federalism" which would devolve significant powers to the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_France" title="Regions of France">regions of France</a>. The regionalist federation <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_R%C3%A9gions_et_Peuples_Solidaires" title="Fédération Régions et Peuples Solidaires">Régions et Peuples Solidaires</a></i> has long been closely allied to the green movement in France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrechonPierre_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-BrechonPierre-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Alfonsi" title="François Alfonsi">François Alfonsi</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Party_of_the_Corsican_Nation" title="Party of the Corsican Nation">Party of the Corsican Nation</a> (PNC) was elected to the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> on an EE list in 2009.
</p><p>On moral issues, the green movement has tended to take <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_liberal" title="Social liberal">socially liberal</a> positions: it has supported <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Same-sex_adoption" title="Same-sex adoption">same-sex adoption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Euthanasia" title="Euthanasia">euthanasia</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drug_liberalisation" title="Drug liberalisation">legalisation of soft drugs</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It also supports political reform, including <a href="/wiki/Right_of_foreigners_to_vote" title="Right of foreigners to vote">voting rights for foreigners</a> in both local and national elections, abolishing the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cumul_des_mandats" title="Cumul des mandats">cumul des mandats</a></i>, term limits and a 'Sixth Republic' with a more powers for the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_system" title="Parliamentary system">parliament</a> and <a href="/wiki/Direct_democracy" title="Direct democracy">direct democracy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-projet_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-projet-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Independent_sources" title="Wikipedia:Independent sources"><span title="This claim needs a reference to a independent, third-party source. (August 2015)">third-party source needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The greens have long promoted <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> in politics, its leadership and electoral candidates tend to respect gender parity and the EELV group in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_National_Assembly" title="French National Assembly">French National Assembly</a> has two co-presidents, one male and one female.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_elections">Other elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europe_Ecology_%E2%80%93_The_Greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/French_regional_elections,_2010" title="French regional elections, 2010">2010 regional elections</a>: EE lists won 12.2% nationally in the first round. It won its best result (17.82%) in <a href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne-Alpes" title="Rhône-Alpes">Rhône-Alpes</a>. All its lists withdrew and merged with PS lists, except in <a href="/wiki/Brittany_(administrative_region)" title="Brittany (administrative region)">Brittany</a>, where it maintained its own separate list and won 17.4% of the votes in the runoff.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/French_cantonal_elections,_2011" title="French cantonal elections, 2011">2011 cantonal elections</a>: EELV won 8.22% nationally and 27 seats.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership">Leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Europe_Ecology_%E2%80%93_The_Greens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The party executive is formed by the Executive Bureau. The national secretary is the leader of the executive bureau and is the party's most senior leader. The federal council is composed of 150 members (75 men and 75 women) and serves as the party's parliament, meeting on a monthly basis.
</p>
<ul><li><b>National secretaries:</b> <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9cile_Duflot" title="Cécile Duflot">Cécile Duflot</a> (2010-2012), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pascal_Durand&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pascal Durand (page does not exist)">Pascal Durand</a> (2012-2013), <a href="/wiki/Emmanuelle_Cosse" title="Emmanuelle Cosse">Emmanuelle Cosse</a> (2013-2016), David Cormand (2016-present)</li>
