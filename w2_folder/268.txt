<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Evergreen (journalism)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>Within the context of journalism and broadcasting, <b>evergreen</b> content is content that is not time-sensitive. Evergreen content does not rely on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_events" title="Current events">current events</a>; thus, an evergreen story can be prepared, then mothballed until it is needed to fill time on a slower news day or on a holiday when fewer journalists are on duty. The term is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> trees.
</p><p>An evergreen <a href="/wiki/News_magazine" title="News magazine">news magazine</a> has more flexibility in production, not having to be produced on a set time frame; instead of producing a new newscast every day or week, a show consisting of evergreen content can produce several episodes at once and release them in sequence. In contrast, such content is not as responsive to breaking developments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Feature_story" title="Feature story">Feature stories</a> and <a href="/wiki/Human_interest_story" title="Human interest story">human interest stories</a> are usually evergreen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pro_feature_writing_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pro_feature_writing-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Evergreen television shows are ideal for <a href="/wiki/Rerun" title="Rerun">reruns</a>. <i><a href="/wiki/Seinfeld" title="Seinfeld">Seinfeld</a></i>, for example, has been one of the most successful sitcoms in off-network syndication for over two decades,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-craw20170208_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-craw20170208-3">[3]</a></sup> as its <a href="/wiki/Observational_comedy" title="Observational comedy">observational comedy</a> did not rely on pop culture references that could become dated; in contrast, <i><a href="/wiki/Murphy_Brown" title="Murphy Brown">Murphy Brown</a></i>, a show of similar longevity and popularity from the same era, was a syndication failure in part because of its frequent reliance upon current events of the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A show's evergreen status can also be grounds for <a href="/wiki/Cancellation_(television)" title="Cancellation (television)">cancellation</a> once a show has built up enough of a backlog of episodes that can be rerun without the viewer realizing the show has ended production; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jerry_Springer_Show" title="The Jerry Springer Show">The Jerry Springer Show</a></i> is one such example.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
