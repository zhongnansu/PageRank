<p>The <b>Thomas A. Greene Memorial Museum</b>, also known as <b>Greene Geological Museum</b> or <b>Greene Museum</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossil</a> museum in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milwaukee,_Wisconsin" title="Milwaukee, Wisconsin">Milwaukee, Wisconsin</a> administered by the Department of Geosciences at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Milwaukee" title="University of Wisconsin–Milwaukee">University of Wisconsin–Milwaukee</a>.  The current curator is Dr. Stephen Q. Dornbos, a paleontologist and Associate Professor of Geosciences at UWM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_building">Original building</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_A._Greene_Memorial_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Original building">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original fireproof museum building, designed by noted Milwaukee architect, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Eschweiler" title="Alexander Eschweiler">Alexander Eschweiler</a>, held the collection of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_A._Greene" title="Thomas A. Greene">Thomas A. Greene</a>, an amateur <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologist</a>. In 1913, Greene's heirs, Mrs. H.A.J. Upham and Mr. Howard Greene,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www4.uwm.edu_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-www4.uwm.edu-4">[4]</a></sup> had the facility built to house his collection,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhlsum_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nhlsum-2">[2]</a></sup> and it was declared a <a href="/wiki/National_Historic_Landmark" title="National Historic Landmark">National Historic Landmark</a> in 1993, in recognition of the collection's significance as a largely intact amateur geological collection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhlsum_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-nhlsum-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nrhpinv2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nrhpinv2-5">[5]</a></sup> The collection has been removed from the building, and is now displayed and housed in Lapham Hall on the UWM campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhlsum_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-nhlsum-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The building has been renovated and is currently used as academic space for UWM's Sam and Helen Stahl Center for Jewish Studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collection">Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_A._Greene_Memorial_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greene amassed most of the fossils in his collection, totaling about 75,000 specimens, during the 1880s and 1890s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www4.uwm.edu_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-www4.uwm.edu-4">[4]</a></sup>  The majority of these specimens come from the Silurian Niagara Formation and the Devonian Hamilton Formation in the vicinity of southeastern Wisconsin.  Preserved mostly as internal molds in dolomite, these fossils include a wide range of Silurian marine invertebrate groups including trilobites, crinoids, tabulate and rugose corals, brachiopods, cephalopods, and gastropods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www4.uwm.edu_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-www4.uwm.edu-4">[4]</a></sup>  Devonian fossils consist mainly of fish and plant remains.  All of these fossils were collected from quarry outcrops that no longer exist, making the collection irreplaceable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www4.uwm.edu_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-www4.uwm.edu-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The mineral collection contains a wide range of minerals and ores from localities throughout North America, as well as some from overseas.  It includes exquisite examples of amethyst, apatite, stibnite, copper, tungsten, molybdenum, vanadium, cobalt, nickel, mercury compounds, aluminum compounds, malachite, lapis-lazuli, tourmaline, garnet, labradorite, fluorite, beryl, and turquoise, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www4.uwm.edu_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-www4.uwm.edu-4">[4]</a></sup> Perhaps the highlight of the mineral collection is a piece of iron meteorite that landed in nearby Washington County, Wisconsin.  It was given to Greene by the scientist <a href="/wiki/Increase_A._Lapham" title="Increase A. Lapham">Increase A. Lapham</a>, the namesake of Lapham Hall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www4.uwm.edu_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-www4.uwm.edu-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Greene collaborated with another amateur, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Fisk_Holbrook_Day" title="Fisk Holbrook Day">Fisk Holbrook Day</a>, whose home in <a href="/wiki/Wauwatosa,_Wisconsin" title="Wauwatosa, Wisconsin">Wauwatosa, Wisconsin</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dr._Fisk_Holbrook_Day_House" title="Dr. Fisk Holbrook Day House">Dr. Fisk Holbrook Day House</a>, has also been designated a National Historic Landmark.
</p>
