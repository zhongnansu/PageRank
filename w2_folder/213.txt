<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Green manure</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, <b>green manure</b> is created by leaving uprooted or sown crop parts to wither on a field so that they serve as a <a href="/wiki/Mulch" title="Mulch">mulch</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soil_amendment" title="Soil amendment">soil amendment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The plants used for green manure are often <a href="/wiki/Cover_crop" title="Cover crop">cover crops</a> grown primarily for this purpose. Typically, they are ploughed under and incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> while green or shortly after flowering. Green manure is commonly associated with <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a> and can play an important role in sustainable annual cropping systems.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_manure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Green manures usually perform multiple functions that include soil improvement and soil protection:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leguminous" title="Leguminous">Leguminous</a> green manures such as <a href="/wiki/Clover" title="Clover">clover</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vetch" title="Vetch">vetch</a> contain <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation" title="Nitrogen fixation">nitrogen-fixing</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbiotic" title="Symbiotic">symbiotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Root_nodules" title="Root nodules">root nodules</a> that fix atmospheric nitrogen in a form that plants can use. This performs the vital function of fertilization.  If desired, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> manures may also be added.</li></ul>
<dl><dd>Depending on the species of cover crop grown, the amount of nitrogen released into the soil lies between 40 and 200 pounds per acre. With green manure use, the amount of nitrogen that is available to the succeeding crop is usually in the range of 40-60% of the total amount of nitrogen that is contained within the green manure crop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sullivan_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sullivan-2">[2]</a></sup></dd></dl>

<ul><li>Green manure acts mainly as <a href="/wiki/Soil_acidification" title="Soil acidification">soil-acidifying</a> matter to decrease the alkalinity/pH of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alkali_soils" title="Alkali soils">alkali soils</a> by generating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humic_acid" title="Humic acid">humic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a>.</li>
<li>Incorporation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cover_crops" title="Cover crops">cover crops</a> into the soil allows the nutrients held within the green manure to be released and made available to the succeeding crops. This results immediately from an increase in abundance of soil microorganisms from the degradation of plant material that aid in the decomposition of this fresh material.  This additional decomposition also allows for the re-incorporation of nutrients that are found in the soil in a particular form such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> (N), <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> (K), <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a> (P), <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> (Ca), <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> (Mg), and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> (S).</li>
<li>Microbial activity from incorporation of cover crops into the soil leads to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Mycelium" title="Mycelium">mycelium</a> and viscous materials which benefit the health of the soil by increasing its <a href="/wiki/Soil_structure" title="Soil structure">soil structure</a> (i.e. by aggregation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sullivan_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sullivan-2">[2]</a></sup></li></ul>
<dl><dd>The increased percentage of organic matter (<a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>) improves <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> infiltration and retention, aeration, and other soil characteristics. The soil is more easily turned or tilled than non-aggregated soil. Further aeration of the soil results from the ability of the root systems of many green manure crops to efficiently penetrate compact soils. The amount of <a href="/wiki/Humus" title="Humus">humus</a> found in the soil also increases with higher rates of decomposition, which is beneficial for the growth of the crop succeeding the green manure crop. Non-leguminous crops are primarily used to increase biomass.</dd></dl>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">root</a> systems of some varieties of green manure grow deep in the soil and bring up nutrient resources unavailable to shallower-rooted crops.</li>
<li>Common <a href="/wiki/Cover_crop#Weed_management" title="Cover crop">cover crop functions of weed suppression</a>. Non-leguminous crops are primarily used (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Buckwheat" title="Buckwheat">buckwheat</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lawrence_1980_145_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_1980_145-4">[4]</a></sup> The deep rooting properties of many green manure crops make them efficient at suppressing <a href="/wiki/Weed" title="Weed">weeds</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>Some green manure crops, when allowed to <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flower</a>, provide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forage_(honeybee)" title="Forage (honeybee)">forage</a> for pollinating <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>. Green manure crops also often provide habitat for predatory beneficial insects, which allow for a reduction in the application of insecticides where cover crops are planted.</li>
<li>Some green manure crops (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Winter_wheat" title="Winter wheat">winter wheat</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_rye" title="Winter rye">winter rye</a>) can also be used for grazing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lawrence_1980_145_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_1980_145-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Erosion_control" title="Erosion control">Erosion control</a> is often also taken into account when selecting which green manure cover crop to plant.</li>
<li>Some <a href="/wiki/Cover_crop#Pest_management" title="Cover crop">green crops reduce plant insect pests</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cover_crop#Disease_management" title="Cover crop">diseases</a>. <a href="/wiki/Verticillium_wilt" title="Verticillium wilt">Verticillium wilt</a> is especially reduced in potato plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Incorporation of green manures into a farming system can drastically reduce the need for additional products such as supplemental fertilizers and pesticides.
</p><p>Limitations to consider in the use of green manure are time, energy, and resources (monetary and natural) required to successfully grow and utilize these cover crops. Consequently, it is important to choose green manure crops based on the growing region and annual precipitation amounts to ensure efficient growth and use of the cover crop(s).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrient_creation">Nutrient creation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_manure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nutrient creation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Green manure is broken down into plant nutrient components by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterotrophic" title="Heterotrophic">heterotrophic</a> bacteria that consumes organic matter. Warmth and moisture contribute to this process, similar to creating <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compost</a> fertilizer. The plant matter releases large amounts of carbon dioxide and weak acids that react with insoluble soil minerals to release beneficial nutrients. Soils that are high in <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> minerals, for example, can be given green manure to generate a higher <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> content in the soil, which in turn acts as a fertilizer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lawrence_1980_145_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_1980_145-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The ratio of carbon to <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> in a plant is a crucial factor to consider, since it will impact the nutrient content of the soil and may starve a crop of nitrogen, if the incorrect plants are used to make green manure. The ratio of carbon to nitrogen will differ from species to species, and depending upon the age of the plant. The ratio is referred to as C:N. The value of N is always one, whereas the value of carbon or carbohydrates is expressed in a value of about 10 up to 90; the ratio must be less than 30:1 to prevent the manure bacteria from depleting existing nitrogen in the soil. <a href="/wiki/Rhizobium" title="Rhizobium">Rhizobium</a> are soil organisms that interact with green manure to retain atmospheric nitrogen in the soil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">Legumes</a>, such as beans, alfalfa, clover and lupines, have root systems rich in rhizobium, often making them the preferred source of green manure material.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crops">Crops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_manure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Crops">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Late-summer and fall green manure crops are <a href="/wiki/Oat" title="Oat">oats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rye" title="Rye">rye</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mofga_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-mofga-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other green manure crops:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">Alfalfa</a>, which sends roots deep to bring nutrients to the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legumes_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-legumes-9">[note 1]</a></sup></li>

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lupin" title="Lupin">Lupin</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Groundnut" title="Groundnut">Groundnut</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Millet" title="Millet">Millet</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-non_legume_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-non_legume-10">[note 2]</a></sup></li>

<li><i><a href="/wiki/Phacelia_tanacetifolia" title="Phacelia tanacetifolia">Phacelia tanacetifolia</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Radish" title="Radish">Radish</a> such as tillage radish or daikon radish.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sesbania" title="Sesbania">Sesbania</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legume_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-legume-11">[note 3]</a></sup></li>

<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sudangrass" title="Sudangrass">Sudangrass</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-non_legume_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-non_legume-10">[note 2]</a></sup></li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_manure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The value of green manure was recognized by farmers in India for thousands of years, as mentioned in treatises like Vrikshayurveda. In Ancient Greece too, farmers ploughed broad bean plants into the soil. Chinese agricultural texts dating back hundreds of years refer to the importance of grasses and weeds in providing nutrients for farm soil. It was also known to early North American colonists arriving from Europe. Common colonial green manure crops were rye, buckwheat and oats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lawrence_1980_145_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_1980_145-4">[4]</a></sup> Traditionally, the incorporation of green manure into the soil is known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fallow" title="Fallow">fallow</a> cycle of <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a>, which was used to allow the soil to regain its fertility after the harvest.
</p>
