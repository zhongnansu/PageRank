<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Runaway greenhouse effect</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Ambox current red.svg" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="360" height="34" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" width="42"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article needs to be <b>updated</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span> <small><i>(November 2017)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>A <b>runaway greenhouse effect</b> is a process in which a net <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> between surface temperature and atmospheric opacity increases the strength of the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> on a planet until its oceans boil away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-utparg_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-utparg-2">[2]</a></sup> An example of this is believed to have happened in the early history of <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>. On the Earth, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPCC" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> states that "a 'runaway greenhouse effect'—analogous to [that of] Venus—appears to have virtually no chance of being induced by <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> activities."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other large-scale climate changes are sometimes loosely called a "runaway greenhouse effect" although it is not an appropriate description. For example, it has been hypothesized that large releases of greenhouse gases may have occurred concurrently with the <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian–Triassic extinction event">Permian–Triassic extinction event</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benton_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Benton-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBRM96111_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-HBRM96111-5">[5]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Paleocene%E2%80%93Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum</a>. Other terms, such as "<a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt climate change</a>", or tipping points could be used when describing such scenarios.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_greenhouse_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This term was coined by <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">Caltech</a> scientist <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Ingersoll" title="Andrew Ingersoll">Andrew Ingersoll</a> in a paper that described a model of the atmosphere of Venus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  Water vapor initially in the atmosphere of Venus absorbed outgoing radiation which caused the planet to heat and increase water evaporation, leading to a positive feedback loop.  High abundance of water vapor in the atmosphere allowed <a href="/wiki/Photodissociation" title="Photodissociation">photodissociation</a> to occur, with lighter hydrogen gas escaping to space and oxygen reacting with surface rocks.  This model is supported by the deuterium/hydrogen ratio on Venus which is 150 times greater than the D/H ratio on Earth.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feedbacks">Feedbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_greenhouse_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Feedbacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Positive feedbacks do not have to lead to a runaway effect, as the <a href="/wiki/Gain_(electronics)" title="Gain (electronics)">gain</a> is not always sufficient. A strong negative feedback always exists (radiation from a planet increases in proportion to the fourth power of temperature, in accordance with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stefan-Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan-Boltzmann law">Stefan-Boltzmann law</a>) so the positive feedback effect has to be very strong to cause a runaway effect (see <a href="/wiki/Gain_(electronics)" title="Gain (electronics)">gain</a>). An increase in temperature from greenhouse gases leading to increased water vapor (which is itself a greenhouse gas) causing further warming is a positive feedback, but not a runaway effect, on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Positive feedback effects are common (e.g. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" title="Ice-albedo feedback">ice-albedo feedback</a>) but runaway effects do not necessarily emerge from their presence.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Venus">Venus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_greenhouse_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Venus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Venuspioneeruv.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2158" data-file-width="2008" height="236" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Venuspioneeruv.jpg/220px-Venuspioneeruv.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Venuspioneeruv.jpg/330px-Venuspioneeruv.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Venuspioneeruv.jpg/440px-Venuspioneeruv.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Venuspioneeruv.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Venus' oceans may have boiled away in a runaway greenhouse effect.</div></div></div>
<p>A runaway greenhouse effect involving carbon dioxide and water vapor may have occurred on <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In this scenario, early Venus may have had a global ocean. As the brightness of the early Sun increased, the amount of water vapor in the atmosphere increased, increasing the temperature and consequently increasing the evaporation of the ocean, leading eventually to the situation in which the oceans boiled, and all of the water vapor entered the atmosphere. On Venus today there is little water vapor in the atmosphere. If water vapor did contribute to the warmth of Venus at one time, this water is thought to have escaped to space. Some evidence for this scenario comes from the extremely high deuterium to hydrogen ratio in Venus' atmosphere, roughly 150 times that of Earth, since light hydrogen would escape from the atmosphere more readily than its heavier <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  Venus is sufficiently strongly heated by the Sun that water vapor can rise much higher in the atmosphere and be split into <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> by ultraviolet light. The hydrogen can then escape from the atmosphere and the oxygen recombines. Carbon dioxide, the dominant greenhouse gas in the current Venusian atmosphere, owes its larger concentration to the weakness of carbon recycling as compared to <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, where the carbon dioxide emitted from volcanoes is efficiently <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subducted</a> into the Earth by plate tectonics on geologic time scales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth">Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_greenhouse_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Runaway_climate_change" title="Runaway climate change">Runaway climate change</a></div>
<p>Earth's climate has swung repeatedly between warm periods and <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice ages</a> during its history.  In the current climate the gain of the positive feedback effect from increased atmospheric water vapor, as well as Earth being too far away from the Sun at its current luminosity for such to occur is well below that which is required to boil away the oceans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Climate scientist <a href="/wiki/John_T._Houghton" title="John T. Houghton">John Houghton</a> has written that "[there] is no possibility of [Venus's] runaway greenhouse conditions occurring on the Earth".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, climatologist <a href="/wiki/James_Hansen" title="James Hansen">James Hansen</a> disagrees. In his <i><a href="/wiki/Storms_of_My_Grandchildren" title="Storms of My Grandchildren">Storms of My Grandchildren</a></i> he says that burning coal and mining <a href="/wiki/Shale_oil" title="Shale oil">shale oil</a> will result in runaway greenhouse on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> A re-evaluation in 2013 of the effect of water vapor in the climate models showed that James Hansen's outcome might be possible, but requires ten times the amount of CO<sub>2</sub> we could release from burning all the oil, coal, and natural gas in Earth's crust. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Further, Benton and Twitchett have a different definition of a runaway greenhouse;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benton_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Benton-4">[4]</a></sup> events meeting this definition have been suggested as a cause for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleocene-Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_dying" title="Great dying">great dying</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distant_future">Distant future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_greenhouse_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distant future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future_of_the_Earth" title="Future of the Earth">Future of the Earth</a></div>
<p>Most scientists believe that a runaway greenhouse effect is actually inevitable in the long term as the Sun gradually gets bigger and hotter as it ages. Such will potentially spell the end of all life on Earth. As the Sun becomes 10% brighter in about one billion years' time, the surface temperature of Earth will reach 47 °C (117 °F), causing the temperature of Earth to rise rapidly and its oceans to boil away until it becomes a greenhouse planet similar to Venus today.
</p><p>According to astrobiologists <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ward_(paleontologist)" title="Peter Ward (paleontologist)">Peter Ward</a> and <a href="/wiki/Donald_E._Brownlee" title="Donald E. Brownlee">Donald Brownlee</a> in their book <i>The Life and Death of Planet Earth</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> the current loss rate is approximately one millimeter of ocean per million years, but this rate is gradually accelerating as the sun gets warmer, to perhaps as fast as one millimeter every 1000 years. Ward and Brownlee predict that there will be two variations of this future warming feedback: the "moist greenhouse" where water vapor dominates the troposphere while water vapor starts to accumulate in the stratosphere, and the "runaway greenhouse" where water vapor becomes a dominant component of the atmosphere that the Earth starts to undergo rapid warming that could send its surface temperature to over 900 °C (1,650 °F) as the atmosphere will be totally overwhelmed by water vapor, causing its entire surface to melt and killing all life, perhaps in about three billion years' time. Either way, the loss of oceans will inevitably turn the Earth into a primarily desert world with the only water left being a few evaporating ponds scattered near the poles, and huge salt flats around what was once the ocean floor, much like the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a> in Chile or <a href="/wiki/Badwater_Basin" title="Badwater Basin">Badwater Basin</a> in Death Valley, where the last life may remain for a few billion more years. Because of this, in the former case the loss of oceans will <i>save</i> the last life instead of destroying it completely. However, complex life like plants and animals will be long extinct before this happens, as the loss of oceans will cause plate tectonics to come to halt; water is a lubricant for tectonic activity and the loss of all water will make the crust too hard and dry to be subducted, therefore causing the carbon cycle to cease altogether (there would be no more volcanoes to return CO<sub>2</sub>  back into the atmosphere).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physics_of_the_runaway_greenhouse">Physics of the runaway greenhouse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_greenhouse_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Physics of the runaway greenhouse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Normally, when a planet's radiation balance is perturbed (e.g., by increasing the amount of sunlight it gets or changing the greenhouse concentration, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiative_Forcing" title="Radiative Forcing">Radiative Forcing</a>) it will transition to a new temperature until a stabilizing feedback known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stefan-Boltzmann_Law" title="Stefan-Boltzmann Law">Stefan-Boltzmann response</a> restores an equilibrium between the amount of energy the body absorbs and that which it emits. For example, if the Earth received more sunlight it would result in a temporary disequilibrium (more energy in than out) and result in warming. However, because the Stefan-Boltzmann response mandates that this hotter planet emits more energy, eventually a new radiation balance can be reached and the temperature will be maintained at its new, higher value.
</p><p>However, when the planet has an operating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapor_feedback" title="Water vapor feedback">water vapor feedback</a>, the efficiency of the greenhouse effect increases as the temperature does, and so the outgoing radiation to space increases less rapidly than for a pure Stefan-Boltzmann radiator behaving like a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackbody" title="Blackbody">blackbody</a>. Eventually, the infrared absorption increases so much that the amount of energy escaping to space no longer depends on the temperature at the surface, and asymptotes to a fixed value, the <a href="/wiki/Kombayashi%E2%80%93Ingersoll_limit" title="Kombayashi–Ingersoll limit">Kombayashi–Ingersoll limit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>  If the amount of energy that the planet receives from the star (or from internal heat sources) exceeds this value, radiative equilibrium can never be achieved.  The result is a runaway that continues until the water vapor feedback ceases, which may be when the entire ocean is evaporated and dispersed to space.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connection_to_habitability">Connection to habitability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Runaway_greenhouse_effect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Connection to habitability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitable_zone" title="Habitable zone">habitable zone</a> has been used by planetary scientists and astrobiologists to define an orbital region around a star in which a planet (or moon) can sustain liquid water. Under this definition, the inner edge of the habitable zone (i.e., the closest point to a star that a planet can be until it can no longer sustain liquid water) is determined by the point in which the runaway greenhouse process occurs. For sun-like stars, this inner edge is estimated to reside at roughly 84% the distance from the Earth to the sun<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> although feedback such as cloud-induced albedo increase could modify this estimate somewhat.
</p>
