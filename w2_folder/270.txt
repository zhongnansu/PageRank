<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Evergreen Cooperatives</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Evergreen Cooperatives</b> are a connected group of <a href="/wiki/Worker_cooperative" title="Worker cooperative">worker-owned cooperatives</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, USA. They are committed to local, worker-owned job creation; <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green</a> and <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a> workplaces; and community economic development.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Cooperatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Cooperatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Much of the idea of worker control came about in <a href="/wiki/Youngstown,_Ohio" title="Youngstown, Ohio">Youngstown, Ohio</a>, in  1977, when the <a href="/wiki/Youngstown_Sheet_and_Tube" title="Youngstown Sheet and Tube">Youngstown Sheet and Tube</a> company abruptly closed and laid off 5,000 workers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In an effort to stop the layoffs, the workers and city attempted to buy a steel mill and control it themselves. Although the effort failed, it gave rise to the idea of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Worker_self-management" title="Worker self-management">worker self-management</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Evergreen initiative was created<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2014)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland_Foundation" title="Cleveland Foundation">Cleveland Foundation</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" title="Cleveland, Ohio">City of Cleveland</a> government, The  Democracy Collaborative at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland,_College_Park" title="University of Maryland, College Park">University of Maryland, College Park</a>, and the Ohio Employee Ownership Center at Kent State University, in collaboration with some of Cleveland's most important "anchor institutions", such as <a href="/wiki/Case_Western_Reserve_University" title="Case Western Reserve University">Case Western Reserve University</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Clinic" title="Cleveland Clinic">Cleveland Clinic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_Hospitals" title="University Hospitals">University Hospitals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buffa_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buffa-3">[3]</a></sup> In November 2010, Evergreen Cooperatives consisted of the Evergreen Cooperative Laundry, Ohio Cooperative Solar, Green City Growers Cooperative and the Neighborhood Voice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-homepage_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-homepage-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Worker-owned_co-operatives">Worker-owned co-operatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Cooperatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Worker-owned co-operatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Evergreen is one of a number of systems of <a href="/wiki/Worker_cooperative" title="Worker cooperative">worker-owned co-operatives</a> pioneering an alternative model of business in the United States, based on the highly successful <a href="/wiki/Mondragon_Corporation" title="Mondragon Corporation">Mondragon Corporation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">Basque Region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buffa_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buffa-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwartz_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwartz-5">[5]</a></sup> These systems emphasize the network aspect of the Mondragon system — a connected group of semi-autonomous businesses, each owned and controlled by its workers but part of a mutually supportive, worker-owned and worker-controlled association — as opposed to the smaller, more fragmented worker co-ops that have existed in the U.S. for many years.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evergreen_Cooperative_Laundry">Evergreen Cooperative Laundry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Cooperatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Evergreen Cooperative Laundry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Evergreen Cooperative Laundry (ECL) is an industrial laundry serving local hospitals, hotels and other institutions. The ECL was funded<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2013)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> with $5.8 million: $1.5 million from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Housing_and_Urban_Development" title="Department of Housing and Urban Development">Department of Housing and Urban Development</a> and the City of Cleveland, $1.8 million in New Markets tax credits, $750,000 from the Cleveland Foundation, and $1.5 million from two banks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GEO_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-GEO-6">[6]</a></sup> It operates at the capacity of 10 million pounds of sheets and towels per year, which represents 4% of the local market. According to some sources, the laundry has the potential to expend to 20 million pounds per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Its customers include two large nursing homes in the local area — Judson Retirement and McGregor Homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buffa_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Buffa-3">[3]</a></sup> The laundry’s <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a> certified building uses the latest energy efficient equipment:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breckenridge_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Breckenridge-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>It saves 35% of energy by warming up the clean water with heat from the used water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grahl_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grahl-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li>It eliminates hazardous waste by using EPA-approved chemicals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grahl_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Grahl-9">[9]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The laundry hired 50 employees with the prospect for 35 more workers by the end of the year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trattner_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trattner-12">[12]</a></sup> Employees received on-the-job technical training<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buffa_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Buffa-3">[3]</a></sup> and worked with Towards Employment, a workforce readiness organization which focuses on assisting groups which have typically had difficulty transitioning to gainful employment (e.g., people coming off welfare or out of jail).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breckenridge_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Breckenridge-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Employees are paid $8 an hour for the first six months, while they are on a trial period. After that, they are considered for the membership in the co-operative by the peers’ voting. If they are admitted, the salary grows to $10.50 an hour, with 50 cents collected towards the ownership share. After seven years working in the laundry, the individual’s share will be equal to $65,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GEO_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-GEO-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breckenridge_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Breckenridge-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ohio_Cooperative_Solar">Ohio Cooperative Solar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Cooperatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ohio Cooperative Solar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ohio Cooperative Solar (OCS), a partner member of the Evergreen Cooperatives, employs area residents to help local institutions become green using solar power and weatherizing techniques to improve their <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a>. OCS owns and installs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> solar panels on Cleveland-area institutional, governmental and commercial buildings and performs weatherizing projects for the area's low-income housing in the solar off-season. OCS is entirely worker-owned by citizens who "face barriers to employment".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-What_is_OCS_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-What_is_OCS-13">[13]</a></sup> OCS was launched in October 2009 and was profitable within its first five months in business. By April 2010, OCS had fourteen employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buffa_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Buffa-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>OCS’ customer list includes large Cleveland institutions such as Cleveland Clinic, University Hospitals, Case Western Reserve University, City of Cleveland and the Cleveland Housing Network. In some instances, the client purchases the solar panels from OCS and hires the company to install them. Under this scenario, the client is then responsible for the maintenance of the system and arranging credits with the local utility, insurance and taxes. Alternatively, OCS will own the solar system, be responsible for all the arrangements, and sell the electricity at a negotiated rate to the client. This is the arrangement OCS has with the majority of its clients; it is expected that the project will create approximately 20 new full-time machinery operator and installer jobs to economically disadvantaged neighborhoods in the near future.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shanahan_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shanahan-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Legislation (Senate Bill 221) passed into <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> state law mandates that utilities provide at least 25% of their electricity from alternative energy sources, including at least one-half percent from solar energy, by the year 2025.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>  According to the OCS chief executive officer, Steve Kiel, this means that Ohio must produce 60 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megawatt" title="Megawatt">megawatts</a> of solar generating capacity in the year 2012. The state's current annual production is two megawatts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Video_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Video-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>To help Ohio meet this legislative mandate, OCS’ technical director Erika Weliczko announced that the company will be breaking new ground by "targeting several megawatts over the next couple of years…(T)hat’s on the order of nothing that’s been done in Ohio to date."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Video_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Video-16">[16]</a></sup> In the next three years,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> OCS plans to have 50 to 100 employee-owners at work installing and maintain the solar panels necessary to meet the new state mandate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shanahan_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shanahan-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alperovitz_2010_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alperovitz_2010-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>When not working on solar panels, OCS employees work in the year-round <a href="/wiki/Weatherization" title="Weatherization">weatherization</a> program focused on households throughout Cleveland. According to Casey Gillfeather, OCS director of operations, the weatherization process includes insulating exterior walls, wrapping the hot water tank, installing an energy-efficient dryer vent, weatherize the basement, and insulating the attic in order to reduce <a href="/wiki/Domestic_energy_consumption" title="Domestic energy consumption">energy consumption</a> of the house by one-third.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OCS_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-OCS-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_City_Growers_Cooperative">Green City Growers Cooperative</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Cooperatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Green City Growers Cooperative">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Green_City_Growers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="4928" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Green_City_Growers.jpg/220px-Green_City_Growers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Green_City_Growers.jpg/330px-Green_City_Growers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Green_City_Growers.jpg/440px-Green_City_Growers.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Green_City_Growers.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Green City Growers greenhouse under construction in September 2012</div></div></div>
<p>Green City Growers Cooperative (GCGC) was conceived in 2008 as an entirely worker-owned, year-round, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroponic" title="Hydroponic">hydroponic</a> food production <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> that could supply Cleveland-area retailers and wholesalers with fresh produce. The project is in the development stage, with financing and design details currently being determined. The dream looms large in the mind of Alayne Reitman, who came up with the original idea for the GCGC and is now the chief executive officer of the project: "We’re talking about a 5.5 <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">acre</a> greenhouse that will produce about 5-6 million heads of <a href="/wiki/Lettuce" title="Lettuce">lettuce</a> annually and another 300,000 pounds of herbs annually."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_Video_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-EC_Video-16">[16]</a></sup> Even in the poorest neighborhoods of Cleveland, people spend about $1,000 each on food per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philpott_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Philpott-19">[19]</a></sup>  The hope of the Growers Cooperative is to capture some of that expenditure by providing healthy, local options.
</p><p>To date,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the team implementing the project has begun an initial inquiry into what crops potential customers would like produced, developed a business plan that proposes the hiring of more than 40 employee owners, identified "green" energy sources and applied for and received an <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Housing_and_Urban_Development" title="United States Department of Housing and Urban Development">HUD</a> grant and loan package that will allow the remediation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brownfield" title="Brownfield">brownfield</a> site and development of the future facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The team has received $10 million in federal loans and grants to date.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alperovitz_2010_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alperovitz_2010-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the near future,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the Growers Cooperative will finalize its designs and consolidate ten acres of land to house their new facilities, which will include the 230,000-square-foot (21,000 m<sup>2</sup>) greenhouse, a packing building, offices and advanced energy facilities. It is projected that the GCGC greenhouse will "almost certainly become the largest urban food-producing greenhouse in the country".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alperovitz_2010_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-Alperovitz_2010-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neighborhood_Voice"><i>Neighborhood Voice</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Cooperatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Neighborhood Voice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Greater University Circle Neighborhood Voice</i> is a free, student-owned and student-run newspaper and online news source covering worker co-op activity in Cleveland and other issues of concern to residents of the Buckeye-Shaker, Central, East Cleveland, Fairfax, Glenville, Hough, Little Ital, and University Circle neighborhoods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
