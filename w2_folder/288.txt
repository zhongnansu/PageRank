<p>The <b>Green Party of Canada</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Parti vert du Canada</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_federal_political_parties_in_Canada" title="List of federal political parties in Canada">federal political party</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> that was founded in 1983.
</p><p>Its broad and multi-issue political platform reflects its core values of ecological wisdom, social justice, grassroots democracy and non-violence. It has been led by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_May" title="Elizabeth May">Elizabeth May</a> since 26 August 2006.
</p><p>The party broke 1% of the popular vote in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2004" title="Canadian federal election, 2004">2004 federal election</a>, when it received 4.3% and qualified for federal funding. Its support has ranged between 3.1% and 14% in public opinion polls since the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2006" title="Canadian federal election, 2006">2006 federal election</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2008" title="Canadian federal election, 2008">2008 federal election</a>, the Green Party of Canada was invited to the debates for the first time<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-debates_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-debates-7">[7]</a></sup> and achieved a high mark of 6.8% of the popular vote. With just under a million votes, it was the only federally funded party to receive more votes than in 2006, but it still failed to win any seats. In the 2011 federal election the Green Party of Canada decided to focus on increasing seats over increasing votes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and succeeded in sending its first MP to Ottawa, while its share of the popular vote dropped to below 4% for the first time in eleven years.
</p><p>On 30 August 2008, independent MP <a href="/wiki/Blair_Wilson" title="Blair Wilson">Blair Wilson</a> joined the Green Party during Parliament's summer recess, technically becoming its first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">Member of Parliament</a> (MP). He was defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2008" title="Canadian federal election, 2008">2008 federal election</a>, which was called before he had a chance to sit in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Canada" title="House of Commons of Canada">House of Commons</a> as a Green MP.
</p><p>On 2 May 2011, Green Party leader Elizabeth May became the first elected Green Party MP to sit in the House of Commons. She won the riding of <a href="/wiki/Saanich%E2%80%94Gulf_Islands" title="Saanich—Gulf Islands">Saanich—Gulf Islands</a> in coastal <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In winning her seat, May also became one of the few Greens worldwide to be elected in a federal, single-seat election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-global_greens_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-global_greens-6">[6]</a></sup> On 13 December 2013, <a href="/wiki/Thunder_Bay%E2%80%94Superior_North" title="Thunder Bay—Superior North">Thunder Bay—Superior North</a> MP <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Hyer" title="Bruce Hyer">Bruce Hyer</a>, who had left the <a href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party" title="New Democratic Party">New Democratic Party</a> (NDP) in 2012 to sit as an independent after breaking party lines to vote in favour of a repeal of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Firearms_Registry" title="Canadian Firearms Registry">Long Gun Registry</a>, joined the party, resulting in a record two-member caucus in parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBC-Hyer_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBC-Hyer-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the federal election on 19 October 2015, May was re-elected in the riding of Saanich—Gulf Islands and was the only Green Party member to win a seat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emay2015_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emay2015-11">[11]</a></sup> Hyer lost the election to <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal Party</a> candidate <a href="/wiki/Patty_Hajdu" title="Patty Hajdu">Patty Hajdu</a> in his riding of Thunder Bay-Superior North.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hajdu_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hajdu-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The Greens have always had leftist and centrist factions that have been ascendant at different times in the party's history. Many Greens also claim that this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-right_politics" title="Left-right politics">traditional left-right political spectrum</a> analysis does not accurately capture the ecological orientation of the Green Party that is neither pragmatic or principled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  The ecumenical approach (expressing affinities with all Canadian political tendencies and making cases to voters on all parts of the left-right spectrum) has been advocated by those who believe their success can also be measured by the degree to which other parties adopt Green Party policies.  By this measure of success, the adoption of a revenue-neutral <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tax" title="Carbon tax">carbon tax</a> at the British Columbia government level, <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emission reduction programs, and  the promotion of the Green (Tax) Shift by the federal Liberal Party under former leader Stéphane Dion, indicate that Green Party policies are gaining traction in Canada.
</p><p>An emphasis on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_tax_shift" title="Green tax shift">green tax shift</a> in the 2004 platform, which favoured partially reducing income and corporate taxes (while increasing taxes on polluters and energy consumers), created questions as to whether the Green Party was still <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">on the left</a> of the political spectrum, or was taking a more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eco-capitalist" title="Eco-capitalist">eco-capitalist</a> approach by reducing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progressive_taxation" title="Progressive taxation">progressive taxation</a> in favour of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regressive_taxation" title="Regressive taxation">regressive taxation</a>. Green Party policy writers have challenged this interpretation by claiming that any unintended "regressive" tax consequences from the application of a Green Tax Shift would be intentionally offset by changes in individual tax rates and categories as well as an 'eco-tax" refund for those who pay no tax.
</p><p>Under Elizabeth May's leadership, the Green Party has begun to receive more mainstream media attention on other party policy not directly related to the environment — for example, supporting labour rights<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and poppy legalization in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies">Policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2011, the Green Party of Canada's policies include:
</p>
<ul><li>Reduced <a href="/wiki/Payroll_tax" title="Payroll tax">payroll</a> and income taxes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
<li>Increased taxes on polluters</li>
<li>Income splitting for families</li>
<li>A national childcare plan</li>
<li>Support for family farms</li>
<li>Government transparency</li>
<li>Proportional representation</li>
<li>Regulation and taxation of <a href="/wiki/Cannabis" title="Cannabis">cannabis</a></li>
<li>Cutting subsidies for industries that pollute</li>
<li>Subsidy for public transit and environmentally friendly technology</li>
<li>Transfers to municipalities for infrastructure</li>
<li>Access to information</li>
<li>Maintain a competitive corporate tax rate</li>
<li>Mandatory labeling of all genetically engineered (GE) foods and food ingredients</li>
<li>Maintain the ban on GE wheat and oppose GE alfalfa.</li>
<li>Federal animal welfare reform</li>
<li>Establishment of a National Pharmacare programme</li>
<li>Closing foreign tax loopholes</li>
<li>Significant shift to investment in renewable energy</li>
<li>Expansion of the ecoEnergy retrofit program</li>
<li>Reduce employee and employer contributions to EI and CPP by 1/3</li>
<li>Ending the logging tax credit</li>
<li>Scaling military spending back to 2005 levels, and reorient toward peacekeeping</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership">Leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Elizabeth_May.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1807" data-file-width="1200" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Elizabeth_May.jpg/170px-Elizabeth_May.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Elizabeth_May.jpg/255px-Elizabeth_May.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Elizabeth_May.jpg/340px-Elizabeth_May.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Elizabeth_May.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_May" title="Elizabeth May">Elizabeth May</a> on 17 March 2007, after announcing her candidacy in Central Nova</div></div></div>
<p>Long-time environmental activist and lawyer Elizabeth May won the leadership of the federal Green party at a convention in Ottawa on 26 August 2006. May won with 2,145 votes, or 65.3 per cent of the valid ballots cast defeating two other candidates. The second-place finisher <a href="/wiki/David_Chernushenko" title="David Chernushenko">David Chernushenko</a>, an environmental consultant, owner of Green &amp; Gold Inc. and two time candidate, collected 1,096 votes or 33.3 per cent of the total, while Jim Fannon, real estate agent at Re/Max Garden City Realty, four time candidate and founder of Nature's Hemp finished a distant third, collecting just 29 votes or 0.88 per cent of the vote. ("None of the above" finished last with 13 votes or 0.44 per cent of the final vote.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 21 November 2006, May appointed outgoing <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_British_Columbia" title="Green Party of British Columbia">Green Party of British Columbia</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Adriane_Carr" title="Adriane Carr">Adriane Carr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> television host <a href="/wiki/Claude_Genest" title="Claude Genest">Claude Genest</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deputy_Leader" title="Deputy Leader">Deputy Leaders</a> of the Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/David_Chernushenko" title="David Chernushenko">David Chernushenko</a>, who ran against <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_May" title="Elizabeth May">Elizabeth May</a> for the party leadership, was the Senior Deputy to the Leader for the first year after May was elected leader.
</p><p>Previous leader <a href="/wiki/Jim_Harris_(politician)" title="Jim Harris (politician)">Jim Harris</a> was elected to the office with over 80% of the vote and the support of the leaders of all of the provincial level Green parties. He was re-elected on the first ballot by 56% of the membership in a leadership challenge vote in August 2004. Tom Manley placed second with over 30% of the vote. A few months after the 2004 convention, Tom Manley was appointed Deputy Leader. On 23 September 2005, Manley left the party to join the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal Party of Canada</a>.
</p><p>in March 2018, Green party Leader Elizabeth May appointed journalist and broadcaster Jo-Ann Roberts as a deputy leader along with environmentalist Daniel Green of Montreal. Roberts ran as Green party candidate in a Victoria, B.C. riding during the 2015 federal election and finished second.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Green_Party_of_Canada" title="History of the Green Party of Canada">History of the Green Party of Canada</a></div>
<p>About one month before the 1980 federal election, eleven candidates, mostly from ridings in the Atlantic provinces, issued a joint press release declaring that they were running on a common platform. It called for a transition to a non-nuclear, conserver society. Although they ran as independents, they unofficially used the name "Small Party" as part of their declaration of unity - a reference to the "<a href="/wiki/Small_Is_Beautiful" title="Small Is Beautiful">small is beautiful</a>" philosophy of <a href="/wiki/E._F._Schumacher" title="E. F. Schumacher">E. F. Schumacher</a>. This was the most substantial early attempt to answer the call for an ecologically oriented Canadian political party. A key organizer (and one of the candidates) was Elizabeth May, who is now leader of the Greens.
</p><p>The Green Party of Canada was founded at a conference held at Carleton University in Ottawa in 1983. Under its first leader, <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Hancock" title="Trevor Hancock">Trevor Hancock</a>, the party ran 60 candidates in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1984_Canadian_federal_election" title="1984 Canadian federal election">1984 Canadian federal election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Green Party of Canada is independent of other green parties around the world. However, all Green parties share the same philosophy. Its provincial counterparts in <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_British_Columbia" title="Green Party of British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Alberta_(2011%E2%80%93present)" title="Green Party of Alberta (2011–present)">Alberta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Saskatchewan" title="Green Party of Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Manitoba" title="Green Party of Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Ontario" title="Green Party of Ontario">Ontario</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Quebec" title="Green Party of Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_New_Brunswick" title="Green Party of New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Nova_Scotia" title="Green Party of Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Prince_Edward_Island" title="Green Party of Prince Edward Island">Prince Edward Island</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> support green economics, progressive social planning, and responsible and accountable governance.
</p><p>The Quebec wing hosted the 1990 Canadian Greens conference in Montreal. But soon after that, Canada's constitutional problems interfered, and many Quebec candidates abandoned the Greens in favour of a Quebec sovereigntist party, the <a href="/wiki/Bloc_Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois" title="Bloc Québécois">Bloc Québécois</a>. There were only six Green candidates from Quebec in the 1993 election. In the spring of 1996, although the hopes of electing a representative to the BC legislature proved premature, Andy Shadrack in the interior of the province received over 11% of the vote. Overall, the party's proportion of the popular vote surged to a new high. Shadrack was also the most popular Green candidate in the 1997 federal election, scoring over 6% of the popular vote in West Kootenay-Okanagan.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Joan_Russow_years">Joan Russow years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Joan Russow years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>British Columbia's <a href="/wiki/Joan_Russow" title="Joan Russow">Joan Russow</a> became leader of the Green Party of Canada on 13 April 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-24">[24]</a></sup> Russow won 52% of the ballots cast in the 1997 leadership race, surpassing Ontario's <a href="/wiki/Jim_Harris_(politician)" title="Jim Harris (politician)">Jim Harris</a> (39%) and Rachelle Small (8%). Immediately upon attaining the leadership, Russow was plunged into a federal general election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-24">[24]</a></sup> Russow's campaign in 1997 set a number of important precedents. <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_1997" title="Canadian federal election, 1997">1997 federal election</a> was the first campaign in which the Greens conducted a national leader's tour, presented a national platform and a bilingual campaign. Previous campaigns, due in part to the party's few resources and, in part, to the party's constitutional straitjacket, had been characterized by policy and spokespeople operating, at best, province-by-province and, at worst, riding-by-riding. In her own riding of <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(electoral_district)" title="Victoria (electoral district)">Victoria</a>, Russow received just shy of 3000 votes and 6% of the popular vote.
</p><p>Since its inception, the party has been developing as an organization, expanding its membership and improving its showing at the polls. In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2000" title="Canadian federal election, 2000">2000 federal election</a>, the party fielded 111 candidates, up from 78 in 1997.
</p><p>Candidates were not run in <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>, as a result of ongoing divisions over Joan Russow's refusal to endorse the Green candidate in an earlier St. John's West by-election. (The candidate in question supported the seal hunt and mining development, as most locals did.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-25">[25]</a></sup> This caused much uncertainty and friction between Newfoundland's Terra Nova Green Party<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-25">[25]</a></sup> Association and the Green Party leader as the party gradually adapted to the realities of functioning as a true national party rather than a disorganized federation of local activists.
</p><p>The conflicts left Russow isolated and alienated from most members of the party. Volunteer efforts were substantially absorbed in provincial campaigns between 2001 and 2003, and the federal party became dormant between elections, as was typical in the past. <a href="/wiki/Chris_Bradshaw" title="Chris Bradshaw">Chris Bradshaw</a> served the party as interim leader from 2001 to February 2003.  During his term, the party ended its sharing of office and staff with the Ontario party, establishing its own office in the national capital of Ottawa.
</p><p>Russow left the party in 2001 and has now criticized the Green party for not following their policies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Breakthrough_under_Jim_Harris">Breakthrough under Jim Harris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Breakthrough under Jim Harris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jim_Harris_of_the_Green_Party.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="493" data-file-width="347" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Jim_Harris_of_the_Green_Party.jpg/180px-Jim_Harris_of_the_Green_Party.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Jim_Harris_of_the_Green_Party.jpg/270px-Jim_Harris_of_the_Green_Party.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/45/Jim_Harris_of_the_Green_Party.jpg 2x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jim_Harris_of_the_Green_Party.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jim_Harris_(politician)" title="Jim Harris (politician)">Jim Harris</a>, Leader of the party from 2003 to 2006</div></div></div>
<p>In February 2003, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Harris_(politician)" title="Jim Harris (politician)">Jim Harris</a>, in his second bid for the leadership, defeated John Grogan of <a href="/wiki/Valemount" title="Valemount">Valemount</a>, British Columbia, and Jason Crummey. Crummey was originally from Newfoundland and involved with Newfoundland and Labrador Terra Nova Greens.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2004" title="Canadian federal election, 2004">2004 federal election</a> the Green Party of Canada became the fourth federal political party ever to run candidates in all the ridings. When the ballots were counted, the Green Party secured 4.3 percent of the popular vote, thereby surpassing the 2 percent threshold required for party financing under new <a href="/wiki/Elections_Canada" title="Elections Canada">Elections Canada</a> rules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Momentum continued to build around the Green Party of Canada and in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2006" title="Canadian federal election, 2006">2006 federal election</a> the Green Party again ran 308 candidates and increased its share of the popular vote to 4.5 percent, once again securing federal financing as a result.
</p><p>The party's <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2006" title="Canadian federal election, 2006">2006 election campaign</a> was disrupted by allegations made by Matthew Pollesell, the party's former assistant national organizer, that Harris had not filed a proper accounting of money spent during his 2004 leadership campaign, as required by law. Pollesell issued a request that Elections Canada investigate. Pollesell and another former party member, Gretchen Schwarz, were subsequently warned by the party's legal counsel to retract allegations they had made or face a possible legal action. Dana Miller, who served in the party's shadow cabinet with responsibility for human-rights issues, made public her earlier complaints that the party has violated election law and its own constitution and has also asked for an Elections Canada investigation. Miller had been expelled from the party after filing a complaint within the party in April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elizabeth_May_years">Elizabeth May years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Elizabeth May years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emay_photo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="257" data-file-width="211" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Emay_photo.jpg/180px-Emay_photo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e8/Emay_photo.jpg 1.5x" width="180"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emay_photo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_May" title="Elizabeth May">Elizabeth May</a>, July 2014</div></div></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Leadership_convention" title="Leadership convention">leadership vote</a> was held at the party's August 2006 convention. On 24 April 2006, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Harris_(politician)" title="Jim Harris (politician)">Jim Harris</a> announced his intention not to stand for re-election as party leader.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Three candidates officially entered the leadership race: <a href="/wiki/David_Chernushenko" title="David Chernushenko">David Chernushenko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_May" title="Elizabeth May">Elizabeth May</a>, and Jim Fannon.  May won the leadership with 65% of the vote on the first ballot.
</p><p>On 22 October 2006, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_May" title="Elizabeth May">Elizabeth May</a> announced she would run in the federal by-election to be held on 27 November 2006, in <a href="/wiki/London_North_Centre" title="London North Centre">London North Centre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>. She finished second behind the Liberal candidate but garnered 26% of the popular vote.
</p><p>On 30 August 2008, Vancouver area MP <a href="/wiki/Blair_Wilson" title="Blair Wilson">Blair Wilson</a> became the first-ever Green Member of Parliament, after sitting for nearly a year of the <a href="/wiki/39th_Canadian_Parliament" title="39th Canadian Parliament">39th Canadian Parliament</a> as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_(politician)" title="Independent (politician)">Independent</a>. He had been a <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal</a> MP, but stepped down voluntarily from the caucus earlier in the parliament after anonymous allegations of campaign finance irregularities, most of which he was later cleared after a 9-month investigation by <a href="/wiki/Elections_Canada" title="Elections Canada">Elections Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Wilson had joined the Green Party during Parliament's summer recess and never sat in the House of Commons as a Green MP.
</p><p>After initial opposition from three of the four major political parties, May was invited to the leaders' debates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-debates_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-debates-7">[7]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2008" title="Canadian federal election, 2008">2008 federal election</a>, the party increased its share of the popular vote by 2.33% (to 6.80%), being the only federally funded party to increase its total vote tally over 2006, attracting nearly 280,000 new votes. However, the party failed to elect a candidate. Some prominent Green Party members blamed the public discussion of strategic voting  and the media's misrepresentation of May's comments during the election campaign for the failure of some promising candidates to reach Election Canada's 10% reimbursement threshold, as well as reducing the party's federal funding based on popular vote.
</p><p>On 11 August 2010, 74% percent of Green party members voted to hold a <a href="/wiki/Leadership_review" title="Leadership review">leadership review</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2011" title="Canadian federal election, 2011">next election</a>, instead of in August 2010, which was when May's four-year term as leader was set to end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2011" title="Canadian federal election, 2011">2011 federal election</a>, May was the first Green Party candidate to win a seat in the House of Commons. On 13 December 2013, Ontario MP <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Hyer" title="Bruce Hyer">Bruce Hyer</a> announced that he would join the Green Party of Canada, doubling the number of members the party has in the House of Commons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBC-Hyer_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-CBC-Hyer-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Canadian_federal_election,_2015_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2768" data-file-width="3254" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Canadian_federal_election%2C_2015_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg/220px-Canadian_federal_election%2C_2015_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Canadian_federal_election%2C_2015_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg/330px-Canadian_federal_election%2C_2015_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Canadian_federal_election%2C_2015_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg/440px-Canadian_federal_election%2C_2015_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Canadian_federal_election,_2015_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Results of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2015" title="Canadian federal election, 2015">2015 Canadian federal election</a> showing support for Green candidates by riding</div></div></div>
<p>On 19 October 2015, May was re-elected, in the riding of Saanich—Gulf Islands in British Columbia as the single Green Party member to win a seat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NP15-1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-NP15-1-31">[31]</a></sup> Bruce Hyer lost his seat in the riding of Thunder Bay-Superior North.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hajdu_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hajdu-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>

</p><p>There have been three Green Members of Parliament in Canadian history:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bruce_Hyer" title="Bruce Hyer">Bruce Hyer</a> (<a href="/wiki/Thunder_Bay%E2%80%94Superior_North" title="Thunder Bay—Superior North">Thunder Bay—Superior North</a>, 2013-2015) - first elected in <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2008" title="Canadian federal election, 2008">2008</a> as a member of the <a href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party" title="New Democratic Party">New Democratic Party</a>, Hyer left the NDP in 2012 to sit as an Independent after disagreement with his party over firearms legislation. Hyer joined the Green Party and its parliamentary caucus in 2013. He was defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2015" title="Canadian federal election, 2015">2015 election</a> by <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal</a> <a href="/wiki/Patty_Hajdu" title="Patty Hajdu">Patty Hajdu</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_May" title="Elizabeth May">Elizabeth May</a> (<a href="/wiki/Saanich%E2%80%94Gulf_Islands" title="Saanich—Gulf Islands">Saanich—Gulf Islands</a>, 2011–present) - elected as a Green in <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2011" title="Canadian federal election, 2011">2011</a> and re-elected in <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2015" title="Canadian federal election, 2015">2015</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blair_Wilson" title="Blair Wilson">Blair Wilson</a> (<a href="/wiki/West_Vancouver%E2%80%94Sunshine_Coast%E2%80%94Sea_to_Sky_Country" title="West Vancouver—Sunshine Coast—Sea to Sky Country">West Vancouver—Sunshine Coast—Sea to Sky Country</a>, 2008) - elected in <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2006" title="Canadian federal election, 2006">2006</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal</a>. Wilson left the Liberal caucus in 2007 to sit as an Independent following allegations of improper campaign financing. In 2008, he joined the Green Party, becoming its first and only parliamentarian. He was defeated <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2008" title="Canadian federal election, 2008">later in 2008</a> by <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Conservative Party of Canada">Conservative</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Weston_(politician)" title="John Weston (politician)">John Weston</a>. Wilson joined the Green Party during Parliament's summer break, and the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2008" title="Canadian federal election, 2008">2008 election</a> was called before the House reconvened. As such, he never physically sat in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Canada" title="House of Commons of Canada">House of Commons</a> as a Green MP.</li></ul>
<p>One other Member of Parliament has been affiliated with the Green Party, but was never a caucus member:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez-Melo" title="José Núñez-Melo">José Núñez-Melo</a> - elected in <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2011" title="Canadian federal election, 2011">2011</a> as a <a href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party" title="New Democratic Party">New Democrat</a> in the riding of <a href="/wiki/Laval_(electoral_district)" title="Laval (electoral district)">Laval</a>, Núñez-Melo was barred by the NDP from seeking nomination for the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2015" title="Canadian federal election, 2015">2015 election</a> after he publicly criticized the nomination process. After the dropping of the writ, Núñez-Melo announced he would run for re-election in <a href="/wiki/Vimy_(electoral_district)" title="Vimy (electoral district)">Vimy</a> as a Green Party candidate. He was defeated by <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal</a> <a href="/wiki/Eva_Nassif" title="Eva Nassif">Eva Nassif</a>. As Parliament was dissolved for the election at the time of Núñez-Melo's change in affiliation, he was never formally recorded as a Green MP.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electoral_status">Electoral status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Electoral status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Green_politicians_who_have_held_office_in_Canada" title="List of Green politicians who have held office in Canada">List of Green politicians who have held office in Canada</a></div>
<p>The Green Party of Canada has fielded candidates in all of the nation's <a href="/wiki/Electoral_district_(Canada)" title="Electoral district (Canada)">ridings</a> in two federal elections.
</p><p>In the Federal election of 19 October 2015 May was re-elected to her seat in the riding of Saanich–Gulf Islands and is the Green Party's only elected member to the Canadian Parliament, and the only one to sit in Parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emay2015_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Emay2015-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Blair_Wilson" title="Blair Wilson">Blair Wilson</a>, the existing MP for <a href="/wiki/West_Vancouver%E2%80%94Sunshine_Coast%E2%80%94Sea_to_Sky_Country" title="West Vancouver—Sunshine Coast—Sea to Sky Country">West Vancouver—Sunshine Coast—Sea to Sky Country</a>, joined the Green Party on 30 August 2008, and became the first Green MP.  However, Wilson became a Green Member while Parliament was on summer recess, and the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2008" title="Canadian federal election, 2008">2008 federal election</a> was called on 7 September 2008, before the House reconvened, he never sat in the House as a Green MP.
</p><p>Members of the party have achieved municipal offices, though most were elected as individuals and not on Green Party slates or labels in local non-partisan municipal elections. However, some people have been elected with a Green Party affiliation identified directly on the ballot. The first two were elected in the November 1999 municipal elections:
</p>
<ul><li>Art Vanden Berg, elected as a City Councillor in Victoria, British Columbia, and</li>
<li>Roslyn Cassells, elected to the Vancouver Parks Board on the same day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>Sonya Chandler was elected municipally to <a href="/wiki/Victoria_City_Council" title="Victoria City Council">Victoria City Council</a> in Victoria BC in 2005, and re-elected with her co-candidate Philippe Lucas in 2010 -  both under the Green Party banner (noted on the ballot)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Adriane_Carr" title="Adriane Carr">Adriane Carr</a> was elected as a City Councillor in Vancouver, British Columbia in 2011</li>
<li>Former Green Party President <a href="/wiki/John_Streicker" title="John Streicker">John Streicker</a> was also elected to Whitehorse City Council in the Yukon Territory in 2011</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Andrea_Reimer" title="Andrea Reimer">Andrea Reimer</a> was originally elected as a trustee on the <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_School_Board" title="Vancouver School Board">Vancouver School Board</a> in 2002 as a Green, but currently sits on <a href="/wiki/Vancouver_City_Council" title="Vancouver City Council">Vancouver City Council</a> as a member of the municipal <a href="/wiki/Vision_Vancouver" title="Vision Vancouver">Vision Vancouver</a> party.
</p><p>Former Councillor Elio Di Iorio was narrowly defeated in his 2006 reelection bid in <a href="/wiki/Richmond_Hill,_Ontario" title="Richmond Hill, Ontario">Richmond Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> and former Councillor Rob Strang did not run for reelection in <a href="/wiki/Orangeville,_Ontario" title="Orangeville, Ontario">Orangeville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>. The late Richard Thomas served as reeve of <a href="/wiki/Armour,_Ontario" title="Armour, Ontario">Armour Township, Ontario</a> from 2003 until his death in 2006.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exclusion_from_debates">Exclusion from debates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Exclusion from debates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 2004 election, the consortium of Canadian television networks did not invite Jim Harris to the televised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_leaders_debates" title="Canadian leaders debates">leaders debates</a>. The primary reason given for this was the party's lack of representation in the House of Commons. There were unsuccessful legal actions by the party, a petition by its supporters to have it included, and statements by non-supporters such as <a href="/wiki/Ed_Broadbent" title="Ed Broadbent">Ed Broadbent</a> who believed it should be included. The Green Party was also not included in the leaders' debates for the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2006" title="Canadian federal election, 2006">2006 election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The same reason was given.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 8 September 2008, the consortium announced that they would once again exclude the Greens from the French and English debates for the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2008" title="Canadian federal election, 2008">2008 election</a>. The party had secured a seat in the House at this point (Blair Wilson), satisfying the necessary criteria used in all previous debates dating to at least 1993. While Wilson was not <i>elected</i> as a Green MP, nor had he even sat in the House as one, the situation paralleled that of the Bloc Québécois in 1993. All of the Bloc's members had been elected as either Conservatives or Liberals or, in <a href="/wiki/Gilles_Duceppe" title="Gilles Duceppe">Gilles Duceppe</a>'s case, as an independent, before the group formally registered as a political party. The Bloc was nevertheless included in the 1993 debates.
</p><p>However, the consortium said that three parties (later identified as the Conservatives, NDP, and one other party) had threatened to boycott the debate if the Green Party was included, and that it had decided it was better to proceed with the four larger parties "in the interest of Canadians". Liberal leader Stéphane Dion supported May's inclusion in the debates but said he would also pull out if Harper withdrew. Bloc leader Gilles Duceppe said that while his party was against the Greens' inclusion, he would attend the debate whether or not they were included.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The Green Party said it would sue to force the consortium to allow it to participate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> This was not necessary, however, because of the networks' reversal two days later. Many people protested and threatened to boycott Layton and Harper by staging protests, as well as phoning in and e-mailing the networks and the opposing parties, prompting both parties to recant their position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2011" title="Canadian federal election, 2011">2011 Canadian federal election</a>, the consortium of broadcasters playing host to the political debates (consisting of <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Canadian Broadcasting Corporation">CBC</a>, <a href="/wiki/CTV_Television_Network" title="CTV Television Network">CTV</a>, <a href="/wiki/Global_Television_Network" title="Global Television Network">Global</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ici_Radio-Canada_T%C3%A9l%C3%A9" title="Ici Radio-Canada Télé">Radio-Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/TVA_(Canada)" title="TVA (Canada)">TVA</a>) announced it would only invite the leaders of the four recognized parties in the House of Commons, namely the Conservative Party, the Liberal Party, the Bloc Québécois and the NDP. Therefore, the Green Party would be excluded.   "This is an unacceptable, outrageous, high-handed attempt to shut down democracy in this country," May said in an interview aired on CBC News.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2011debate_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-2011debate-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Prior to the October 2015 Federal election, May was invited to participate in two of the debates: one hosted by Maclean's magazine on 6 August 2015 and the first French language debate hosted by Radio-Canada on 24 September 2015. However, May was excluded from the other two debates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tweets1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-tweets1-39">[39]</a></sup> After being advised of the exclusion from The Munk Debate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Munk1_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Munk1-40">[40]</a></sup> on Canada’s Foreign Policy on 28 September 2015, May took her message to social media where she attacked the Harper government using tweets on Twitter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tweets1_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-tweets1-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Internet_innovation">Internet innovation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Internet innovation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the organizing and election planning was centralized, policy development was to be decentralized. In February 2004, the Green Party of Canada Living Platform was initiated by the Party's former Head of Platform and Research, Michael Pilling. Using wiki technology, the goal of the Living Platform was to open the party's <a href="/wiki/Participatory_democracy" title="Participatory democracy">participatory democracy</a> to the public to help validate its policies against broad public input. It also made it easy for candidates to share their answers to public interest group questionnaires, find the best answers to policy questions, and for even rural and remote users, and Canadians abroad, to contribute to Party policy intelligence.
</p><p>To this end, the Green Party used the Living Platform to develop election platforms for 2004 and 2005 were developed, thus making the Green Party of Canada the first political party to use a wiki for such a purpose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membership_exclusions">Membership exclusions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Membership exclusions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1998, the party adopted a rule that forbids membership in any other federal political party. This was intended to prevent the party from being taken over.
</p><p>In the past, some Green Party members have been comfortable openly working with members of other political parties. For instance, GPC members <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bevan-Baker" title="Peter Bevan-Baker">Peter Bevan-Baker</a> and Mike Nickerson worked with Liberal MP <a href="/wiki/Joe_Jordan_(politician)" title="Joe Jordan (politician)">Joe Jordan</a> to develop the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Canada_Well-Being_Measurement_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Canada Well-Being Measurement Act (page does not exist)">Canada Well-Being Measurement Act</a> that called upon the government to implement <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genuine_Progress_Indicator" title="Genuine Progress Indicator">Genuine Progress Indicators</a> (GPI). While the act was introduced into the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Canada" title="House of Commons of Canada">House of Commons</a> as a private members bill, it never became law.  A small number of Greens who advocate the more cooperative approach to legislation object to the new rule not to hold cross-memberships, a tool they occasionally employed.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="May-Dion_electoral_co-operation">May-Dion electoral co-operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: May-Dion electoral co-operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A9phane_Dion" title="Stéphane Dion">Stéphane Dion</a> winning the Liberal leadership on a largely environmentalist platform, and both the Liberals and Greens having a shared interest in both defeating the Conservatives, whose environmental policies have come under criticism from members of both parties, some political observers questioned if an alliance of some sort between the two parties might take place.
</p><p>When Green Party leader Elizabeth May made the announcement that she would run in <a href="/wiki/Central_Nova" title="Central Nova">Central Nova</a>, then held by Conservative cabinet minister <a href="/wiki/Peter_MacKay" title="Peter MacKay">Peter MacKay</a>, local Liberals would "neither confirm nor deny" that they had had discussions with May over ways to unseat MacKay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> On 21 March, Dion said, "Madame May and I have conversations about how we may work together to be sure that this government will stop to do so much harm to our environment". The speculation was confirmed when Dion and May agreed not to run candidates in each other's ridings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>May earlier attempted to broker a deal with the NDP, by contacting <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Lewis" title="Stephen Lewis">Stephen Lewis</a> to set up a meeting with party leader <a href="/wiki/Jack_Layton" title="Jack Layton">Jack Layton</a>, who both rejected the notion outright. When the May-Dion deal was announced, it was criticized by the Conservatives and NDP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ultimately  May failed in her bid to get elected in Central Nova, losing to McKay by 18,240 votes (46.6%) to 12,620 (32.24%) in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2008" title="Canadian federal election, 2008">2008 federal election</a>. The New Democratic Party candidate, Louise Lorifice, placed third with 7,659 votes (19.56%).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_2008-2009_parliamentary_dispute">Role in 2008-2009 parliamentary dispute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Role in 2008-2009 parliamentary dispute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In December 2008, during the <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_Canadian_parliamentary_dispute" title="2008–09 Canadian parliamentary dispute">2008–09 Canadian parliamentary dispute</a>, May announced the Green Party would support, from outside parliament, the proposed coalition between the Liberals and the NDP (with the parliamentary support of the Bloc Québécois), which was then attempting to displace the incumbent Conservative government. Liberal leader Stéphane Dion indicated that the Green Party would be given input, but not a veto, over coalition policy and also left open the possibility of May being appointed to the Senate if Dion were to become prime minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Ultimately, however, the coalition fell apart after Prime Minister Stephen Harper, in order to delay an impending non-confidence vote, advised the <a href="/wiki/Governor_General_of_Canada" title="Governor General of Canada">Governor General</a> to prorogue parliament. Liberal leader Dion resigned and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Ignatieff" title="Michael Ignatieff">Michael Ignatieff</a> and, when parliament resumed in January 2009, the Liberal Party decided to support the Conservative government's new proposed budget. While parliament was prorogued, Harper also announced his intention to fill all current and upcoming Senate vacancies with Conservative appointees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paul_Estrin_controversy">Paul Estrin controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Paul Estrin controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In August 2014, President elect Paul Estrin published a blog post on the Green Party's website criticizing the actions of <a href="/wiki/Hamas" title="Hamas">Hamas</a> during the <a href="/wiki/2014_Israel%E2%80%93Gaza_conflict" title="2014 Israel–Gaza conflict">2014 Israel–Gaza conflict</a>. In his article, "Why Gaza Makes Me Sad", Estrin talked about Hamas' "desire to obliterate" the State of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and how the terrorist group uses children as human shields.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>  Estrin's blog post was subsequently deleted by the party, with many party seniors and decision makers, including Elizabeth May, distancing themselves from Estrin, with a large majority of the party calling on him to resign. On 5 August, Estrin resigned, criticizing the party for betraying their commitment to values of inclusivity and open public discourse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>  Elizabeth May accepted the resignation of Estrin, stating that he was not forced to resign but did so of his own volition. May has said that the problem with his statements were the "confusion" they caused because they differed from party lines, but confirmed that Estrin was indeed a "true Green".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monika_Schaefer_controversy">Monika Schaefer controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Monika Schaefer controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In early July 2018 in Germany, the trial of former Green Party candidate Monika Schaefer of <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> and her German-Canadian brother, Alfred Schaefer, both for <a href="/wiki/Holocaust_denial" title="Holocaust denial">Holocaust denial</a>, started. They were charged with Volksverhetzung, "incitement to hatred". Ms. Schaefer was the Green Party candidate in the Alberta riding of Yellowhead during the federal elections in 2006, 2008 and 2011 but the Green Party announced that her membership in the Party would be terminated<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenparty.ca_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenparty.ca-52">[52]</a></sup> after news reports surfaced of a video Ms. Schaefer had posted on YouTube, denying the genocide of Jews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> (In August 2016, however, Ms. Schaefer told the news media that she had rescinded her own membership a year earlier.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the July 2016 video,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Ms. Schaefer described the Holocaust as "the biggest and most pernicious lie in all of history" and added that those in concentration camps had been kept as healthy and as well-fed as possible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Party Leader Elizabeth May provided this quote to the news media in July 2016: "I am shocked by comments made by Ms. Schaefer and I condemn her terribly misguided and untrue statements. Ms. Schaefer does not represent the values of the Green Party nor of our membership."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenparty.ca_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-greenparty.ca-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>There is currently no provincial Green Party <i>per se</i> in <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>. An association called the Terra Nova Greens (TNG) were created in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>  It became the Green Party of Canada's "Official Unit" for Newfoundland and Labrador.  In 1998, the TNG ran a full slate of candidates for the national executive but none were elected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> In 2000 TNG again ran a slate of candidates who were not elected.  TNG also fielded independent candidates in three different provincial general elections.  They remained the federal party's "Official Unit" until 2007, but most supporters cut ties to the national party in 2006 (or earlier) over its opposition to the traditional Newfoundland <a href="/wiki/Seal_hunting" title="Seal hunting">seal hunting</a>. TNG was never a registered provincial political party. It was ultimately disbanded under the auspices of a Green Party of Canada paid-employee who was hired to organize the province.  As of 2014, there are ongoing efforts to establish a provincial Green Party in Newfoundland and Labrador.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Alberta_Greens" title="Alberta Greens">Alberta Greens</a> were deregistered on 16 July 2009, following a severe internal dispute and the failure of the party to file its financial returns with <a href="/wiki/Elections_Alberta" title="Elections Alberta">Elections Alberta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Alberta" title="Green Party of Alberta">Green Party of Alberta</a> was registered in 2011 as a successor.
</p>
