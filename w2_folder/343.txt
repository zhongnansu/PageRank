<p>The <b>Wales Green Party</b> (WGP; <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy">Plaid Werdd Cymru</i>) is a semi-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_entity" title="Autonomous entity">autonomous</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frankland_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frankland-1">[1]</a></sup> political party within the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party of England and Wales</a>. It covers <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and is the only regional party with semi-autonomous status within the GPEW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frankland_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frankland-1">[1]</a></sup> The WGP contests elections for the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a> (as well as at the <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Local government in the United Kingdom">local</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK_Parliament" title="UK Parliament">UK</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European</a> level) and has its own newsletters, membership list, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annual_General_Meeting" title="Annual General Meeting">AGMs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manifesto" title="Manifesto">manifesto</a>. 
</p><p>The WGP leader is Grenville Ham, and the Deputy Leaders are Benjamin Smith and Mirka Virtanen. Wales is represented internally within the GPEW by Louise Davies and Kathryn Driscoll on the Green Party Regional Council, and the WGP Leader on the GPEW executive. Both sets of positions are directly elected by postal ballot. Wales-wide decisions are taken by the Wales Green Party Council made up of the spokespeople, senior officers, and a representative from each local party.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership">Leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Wales Green Party has always had its own spokesperson (now referred to as leader). Jake Griffiths became leader in 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> succeeding Leila Kiersch.  Pippa Bartolotti was elected to succeed Griffiths in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Grenville Ham became leader in March 2017 following the resignation of Alice Hooker-Stroud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WGPSite1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-WGPSite1-4">[4]</a></sup> He left the Green Party to join <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> in October 2018, after the Wales Green Party had voted not to become independent from England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Anthony Slaughter became deputy leader in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Alice Hooker-Stroud and Hannah Pudner were elected as the leader and deputy leader in late 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  The Wales Green party is represented internally within the GPEW by Louise Davies and Kathryn Driscoll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> on the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales#Regional_Council" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party Regional Council</a> (GPRC).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green Parties in the United Kingdom have their roots in the <a href="/wiki/PEOPLE_Party" title="PEOPLE Party">PEOPLE Party</a> which was founded in 1973. This became the Ecology Party three years later, and then the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(UK)" title="Green Party (UK)">Green Party</a> in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-John_Wiley_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-John_Wiley-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1990, the Scottish and Northern Irish branches left the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(UK)" title="Green Party (UK)">UK Greens</a> to form separate parties. The English and Welsh parties became the Green Party of England and Wales, with the Welsh branch being semi-autonomous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frankland_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Frankland-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1992" title="United Kingdom general election, 1992">1992 general election</a>, local Greens entered an electoral alliance with <a href="/wiki/Plaid_Cymru" title="Plaid Cymru">Plaid Cymru</a> in the constituency of <a href="/wiki/Ceredigion_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Ceredigion (UK Parliament constituency)">Ceredigion and Pembroke North</a>. The alliance was successful with <a href="/wiki/Cynog_Dafis" title="Cynog Dafis">Cynog Dafis</a> being returned in a surprise result as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a>, defeating the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrat</a> incumbent by over 3,000 votes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The agreement broke down by 1995 following disagreement within the Welsh Green Party over endorsing another party's candidate, though Dafis would go on to serve in parliament as a Plaid Cymru member until 2000, and in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Wales" title="National Assembly of Wales">National Assembly of Wales</a> from 1999 until 2003. Dafis later stated that he did not consider himself to be the "first Green MP".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Welsh_local_elections,_2017" title="Welsh local elections, 2017">2017 Welsh local election</a>, the Wales Green Party's had their first county councillor elected to <a href="/wiki/Powys_County_Council" title="Powys County Council">Powys County Council</a>, for the <a href="/wiki/Llangors" title="Llangors">Llangors</a> ward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2018 the party held a vote on whether to split from the GPEW to form a separate organisation. Of those members who voted, 65% voted against the proposal, despite the leader Grenville Ham campaigning for independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wales_Young_Greens">Wales Young Greens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wales Young Greens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wales Young Greens is the youth and student branch of the Wales Green Party. Its current Co-chairs are Andrew Creak and Ramona Sharples.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Welsh_Green_Pride">Welsh Green Pride</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Welsh Green Pride">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Welsh Green Pride is the LGBTIQA+ Liberation group within the Wales Green Party which runs alongside but separate to the GPEW group LGBTIQA+ Greens. Its current Spokesperson is Ash Jones, and it has two deputy spokespeople Mike Whittall and Michael Cope. The group started a UK wide review of the discriminatory blood ban against men who have sex with men.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Welsh_Assembly_elections">Welsh Assembly elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Welsh Assembly elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2016">2016</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wales_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2016">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In September 2015, <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Womack" title="Amelia Womack">Amelia Womack</a>, Deputy Leader of GPEW, announced her intention to stand in the National Assembly elections for Wales Green Party. An ITV article titled "Green deputy leader wants to switch to Welsh politics" wrote of Newport-born Womack's intention to stand in the Welsh elections saying; "She's seeking the nomination for the Cardiff Central constituency and – more significantly – hoping to be top of the Wales Green Party's regional list for <a href="/wiki/South_Wales_Central_(National_Assembly_for_Wales_electoral_region)" title="South Wales Central (National Assembly for Wales electoral region)">South Wales Central</a>." Notably the article went on to say "Opinion polls have occasionally suggested that the Greens could gain a list seat in the Senedd".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>10 February 2016 Welsh Greens abandoned progressive alliance negotiations a few months before the Senedd elections<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Wales Green Party who create their own set of devolved policies around devolved issues in Wales were hopeful of gaining three Assembly seats from the <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> lists in the 2016 elections<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> despite being deemed the 3rd Greenest Party in Wales by Friends of the Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>No Welsh Greens were elected to the Senedd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
