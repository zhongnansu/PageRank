<p>The <b>Green Party in Northern Ireland</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Green_party" title="Green party">green party</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nordsieck_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nordsieck-2">[2]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> which works in co-operation with green parties across Britain and Ireland, Europe and globally. Like many green parties around the world, its origins lie in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear" title="Anti-nuclear">anti-nuclear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Labour_movement" title="Labour movement">labour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peace_movement" title="Peace movement">peace movements</a> of the 1970s and early 1980s.
</p><p>Since 2006, the party has operated as a region of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Ireland)" title="Green Party (Ireland)">Green Party of Ireland</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greensunite_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greensunite-4">[4]</a></sup> and also maintains links with other Green parties, including the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party" title="Scottish Green Party">Scottish Green Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party of England and Wales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcprofile_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbcprofile-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The party has a youth wing operating in Northern Ireland, the <a href="/wiki/Young_Greens_(Ireland)" title="Young Greens (Ireland)">Young Greens</a>.
</p><p>The party also has LGBTQ policies and an activist group operating in Northern Ireland, the Queer Greens.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies">Policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green Party has four key values: <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_sustainability" title="Environmental sustainability">environmental sustainability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grassroots_democracy" title="Grassroots democracy">grassroots democracy</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-violence" title="Non-violence">non-violence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It is considered to be more to the left than most parties in Northern Ireland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Green Party has been involved in several major campaigns since entering the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>, including clean rivers and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear-free_zone" title="Nuclear-free zone">anti-nuclear</a> campaigns, opposition to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fracking" title="Fracking">fracking</a>, and fighting the austerity agenda. It has also campaigned against the development of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incinerator" title="Incinerator">incinerators</a> at Belfast North Foreshore and <a href="/wiki/Lough_Neagh" title="Lough Neagh">Lough Neagh</a>, and against proposals to extend the airport runway at <a href="/wiki/George_Best_Belfast_City_Airport" title="George Best Belfast City Airport">George Best Belfast City Airport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcprofile_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbcprofile-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Green Party campaigns not just for more environmental protection but also for politics for the common good. MLA <a href="/wiki/Steven_Agnew" title="Steven Agnew">Steven Agnew</a> has championed the rights of children in Northern Ireland through his Private Member's Bill which is seeking to establish a statutory duties on government departments to work together to deliver optimum children's services. Agnew has also been a long-standing supporter of <a href="/wiki/Integrated_education" title="Integrated education">integrated education</a> and a society based on equal rights and mutual respect for all traditions. This has included bringing forward the first motion on <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a> to the Northern Ireland Assembly in 2011. The party has also called for funding to be focused on improving public transport infrastructure and supports the creation of an independent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_protection_agency" title="Environmental protection agency">environmental protection agency</a> for Northern Ireland. They also campaign for a shift to <a href="/wiki/Alternative_energy" title="Alternative energy">alternative energy</a> for Northern Ireland and were involved in the setting up of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lobby_group" title="Lobby group">lobby group</a> for the sector. The Green Party in Northern Ireland campaigns for transparency in political funding, responsive local government, effective community planning, dynamic and sustainable local economies, environmental protection, and for animal welfare.
</p><p>On the constitutional status of Northern Ireland, the Green Party's leader <a href="/wiki/Steven_Agnew" title="Steven Agnew">Steven Agnew</a> believes the status quo should remain "until the people of Northern Ireland decide otherwise", but stated that the Green Party does not consider the issue as one that should divide its members or society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The party has also called for greater transparency in politics, arguing that political donations in Northern Ireland should be made public. Northern Ireland is the only part of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> where political donations are secret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_local_elections,_1981" title="Northern Ireland local elections, 1981">Northern Ireland local elections of May 1981</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Emerson" title="Peter Emerson">Peter Emerson</a>, Avril McCandless and Malcolm Samuels stood as the first candidates to use the Ecology label in Northern Ireland and gained 202, 81 and 61 votes respectively; the first in a large urban area, the other two in smaller rural constituencies. Emerson had previously stood in the same area in 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 1983, the Northern Ireland Ecology Party was launched at a press conference held in the <a href="/wiki/Europa_Hotel,_Belfast" title="Europa Hotel, Belfast">Europa Hotel, Belfast</a>, with members of the British and Irish Ecology parties in attendance. At the same time, the three parties put forward one combined policy on Northern Ireland, the first time that UK and Irish political parties had held a common Northern Ireland policy.
</p><p>In 1985, ecology parties throughout the Republic of Ireland and the United Kingdom changed their names to Green Party.
</p><p>The party became a region of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Ireland)" title="Green Party (Ireland)">Green Party of Ireland</a> in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greensunite_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Greensunite-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcprofile_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-bbcprofile-5">[5]</a></sup> These arrangements are said to demonstrate the Northern Ireland party's cross-community nature, as the Green Party claim to be the only party that actually lives the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> through its operational set up through North-South and East-West links.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In 2007, a Green society was established at <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_University_Belfast" title="Queen's University Belfast">Queen's University Belfast</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 2010, the LGBT Greens NI were established: a policy group and lobby group specialising in LGBT community issues within Northern Ireland. The LGBT group dissolved in early 2012, as their main aim — pushing for the inclusion of same-sex marriage within party policy — was achieved at the 2011 AGM after a unanimous vote.
</p><p>In February 2015, the Queer Greens party group was set up to become the LGBTQ issues and activist wing of the party. The group is taking charge of party policy on <a href="/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_Northern_Ireland" title="LGBT rights in Northern Ireland">LGBTQ rights</a>, issues, welfare, campaigning, lobbying and raising awareness.
</p><p>On 14 January 2016, the party announced that it had selected Ellen Murray as its candidate to contest the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election,_2016" title="Northern Ireland Assembly election, 2016">2016 Northern Ireland Assembly</a> elections for West Belfast, making her the first openly Transgender person to stand for election on the island of Ireland.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Election_results">Election results</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Election results">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The party's first electoral success in Northern Ireland was at the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_local_elections,_2005" title="Northern Ireland local elections, 2005">local council elections of 2005</a>. Cllr Raymond Blaney was elected onto <a href="/wiki/Down_District_Council" title="Down District Council">Down District Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brian_Wilson_(Northern_Ireland_politician)" title="Brian Wilson (Northern Ireland politician)">Brian Wilson</a>, formerly of the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Party_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Alliance Party of Northern Ireland">Alliance Party</a>, took a seat on <a href="/wiki/North_Down_Borough_Council" title="North Down Borough Council">North Down Borough Council</a>. The party's third local councillor was Ciaran Mussen, elected to <a href="/wiki/Newry_and_Mourne_District_Council" title="Newry and Mourne District Council">Newry and Mourne District Council</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election,_2007" title="Northern Ireland Assembly election, 2007">2007 Northern Ireland Assembly election</a>, the Green Party won its first seat in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Brian_Wilson_(Northern_Ireland_politician)" title="Brian Wilson (Northern Ireland politician)">Brian Wilson</a> won a seat in the <a href="/wiki/North_Down_(Assembly_constituency)" title="North Down (Assembly constituency)">North Down</a> constituency. Overall the party won 11,985 first preference votes or 1.7% of the total – a rise of 1.4% since the 2003 Assembly election.
</p><p>In 2009, the Green Party stood Steven Agnew in the European election — he secured 15,674 votes, trebling the Green Party's share of the vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbcprofile_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-bbcprofile-5">[5]</a></sup> The Greens fought the election on the <a href="/wiki/Green_New_Deal" title="Green New Deal">Green New Deal</a>, calling for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Job_creation" title="Job creation">job creation</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_energy" title="Green energy">green energy</a> sector.
</p><p>The Greens fielded four candidates in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2010" title="United Kingdom general election, 2010">2010 UK general election</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> none of which managed to secure a seat. However, the number of votes for Green candidates more than trebled.
</p><p>Brian Wilson MLA stood down ahead of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election,_2011" title="Northern Ireland Assembly election, 2011">2011 Assembly election</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> in which the party won a seat on North Down council,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ndcoun_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ndcoun-16">[16]</a></sup> with their candidate <a href="/wiki/Steven_Agnew" title="Steven Agnew">Steven Agnew</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Agnew subsequently stepped down from his position on North Down Borough Council as the party took a strong stand against so called 'double jobbing' or dual mandate; he was replaced by <a href="/wiki/John_Barry_(politician)" title="John Barry (politician)">John Barry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ndcoun_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-ndcoun-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
