<p>The <b>Joseph Green House</b> was a historic two-story home in <a href="/wiki/Orange_Park,_Florida" title="Orange Park, Florida">Orange Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. It was the oldest building of Orange Park's black community, located at 531 McIntosh Avenue. On July 15, 1998, it was added to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>, becoming the Register's only minority-owned property in <a href="/wiki/Clay_County,_Florida" title="Clay County, Florida">Clay County</a> at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-demo_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-demo-3">[3]</a></sup> The following year, it was demolished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-demo_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-demo-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Green was a black carpenter from Mississippi who settled in Orange Park around 1886 and built this home in 1893.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-examples_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-examples-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He built in total 15 homes in the area, of which this was the last to remain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recognized_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-recognized-6">[6]</a></sup> His daughter-in-law sold the home to St. James <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AME_Church" title="AME Church">AME Church</a> for $30,000 in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lawsuit_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-lawsuit-7">[7]</a></sup> The church then used it as a rental property,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-examples_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-examples-4">[4]</a></sup> so it was not open for public tours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coast_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-coast-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>AME Churches in Florida, the parent church of the local congregation, sued a number of local congregants to gain ownership of the property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lawsuit_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-lawsuit-7">[7]</a></sup> The parent church's bishop  said the local church had promised to build a new sanctuary on that spot, so he agreed to drop the lawsuit on the condition that the home be demolished.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-demo_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-demo-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
