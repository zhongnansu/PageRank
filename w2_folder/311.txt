<p>Many languages do not distinguish between what in English are described as "blue" and "green" and instead use a cover term spanning both. To describe this English <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexical_gap" title="Lexical gap">lexical gap</a>, linguists use the <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> word <i>grue</i>, from <i>green</i> and <i>blue</i>, which the philosopher <a href='/wiki/Nelson_Goodman#Induction_and_"grue"' title="Nelson Goodman">Nelson Goodman</a> coined in his 1955 <i><a href="/wiki/Fact,_Fiction,_and_Forecast" title="Fact, Fiction, and Forecast">Fact, Fiction, and Forecast</a></i> to illustrate the "<a href="/wiki/New_riddle_of_induction" title="New riddle of induction">new riddle of induction</a>".
</p><p>The exact definition of "blue" and "green" may be complicated by the speakers not primarily distinguishing the hue, but using terms that describe other color components such as saturation and luminosity, or other properties of the object being described. For example, "blue" and "green" might be distinguished, but a single term might be used for both if the color is dark. Furthermore, green might be associated with yellow, and blue with black or gray.
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Brent_Berlin" title="Brent Berlin">Brent Berlin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kay" title="Paul Kay">Paul Kay</a>'s 1969 study <i><a href="/wiki/Basic_Color_Terms:_Their_Universality_and_Evolution" title="Basic Color Terms: Their Universality and Evolution">Basic Color Terms: Their Universality and Evolution</a></i>, distinct terms for <a href="/wiki/Brown" title="Brown">brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Purple" title="Purple">purple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink" title="Pink">pink</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orange_(colour)" title="Orange (colour)">orange</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grey" title="Grey">grey</a> will not emerge in a language until the language has made a distinction between green and blue. In their account of the development of color terms the first terms to emerge are those for <a href="/wiki/White" title="White">white</a>/<a href="/wiki/Black" title="Black">black</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dark" title="Dark">dark</a>), <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a>/<a href="/wiki/Yellow" title="Yellow">yellow</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Afro-Asiatic">Afro-Asiatic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Afro-Asiatic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Amazigh">Amazigh</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amazigh">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The word for blue in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazigh" title="Amazigh">Amazigh</a> language is <i>Azerwal</i>. In some dialects of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazigh" title="Amazigh">Amazigh</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Shilha_language" title="Shilha language">Shilha</a>, the word <i>Azegzaw</i> is used for both green and blue.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic">Arabic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arabic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The color of the sky is sometimes referred to as "the green" in Classical Arabic poetry, in which it is al-khaḍrā' (<span dir="rtl" lang="ar" title="Arabic language text">الخضراء</span>). In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> the word for blue is generally <i>azraq</i> (<span dir="rtl" lang="ar" title="Arabic language text">أزرق</span>). The Arabic word for green is  <i>akhḍar</i> (<span dir="rtl" lang="ar" title="Arabic language text">أخضر</span>).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Moroccan_Arabic" title="Moroccan Arabic">Moroccan Arabic</a>, the word for light blue is <i>šíbi</i>, whereas <i>zraq</i> (<span class="script-arabic script-Arab" dir="rtl" style="font-family:Scheherazade,Lateef,LateefGR,Amiri,'Noto Naskh Arabic','Droid Arabic Naskh',Harmattan,'Arabic Typesetting','Traditional Arabic','Simplified Arabic','Times New Roman',Arial,'Sakkal Majalla','Microsoft Uighur',Calibri,'Microsoft Sans Serif','Segoe UI',serif,sans-serif; font-size: 125%; font-weight:normal;">زرق</span>‬) stands for blue and <i>khḍar</i> (<span class="script-arabic script-Arab" dir="rtl" style="font-family:Scheherazade,Lateef,LateefGR,Amiri,'Noto Naskh Arabic','Droid Arabic Naskh',Harmattan,'Arabic Typesetting','Traditional Arabic','Simplified Arabic','Times New Roman',Arial,'Sakkal Majalla','Microsoft Uighur',Calibri,'Microsoft Sans Serif','Segoe UI',serif,sans-serif; font-size: 125%; font-weight:normal;">خضر</span>‬) for green. The word <i>zrag</i> (<span class="script-arabic script-Arab" dir="rtl" style="font-family:Scheherazade,Lateef,LateefGR,Amiri,'Noto Naskh Arabic','Droid Arabic Naskh',Harmattan,'Arabic Typesetting','Traditional Arabic','Simplified Arabic','Times New Roman',Arial,'Sakkal Majalla','Microsoft Uighur',Calibri,'Microsoft Sans Serif','Segoe UI',serif,sans-serif; font-size: 125%; font-weight:normal;">زرڭ</span>‬) is used to describe the color of <a href="/wiki/Cyanosis" title="Cyanosis">a suffocated person</a>, and is also used pejoratively as synonym to "dumb".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Egyptian">Egyptian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Egyptian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ancient <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">Egyptian</a> word <i><a href="/wiki/Wadjet" title="Wadjet">wadjet</a></i> covers the range of blue, blue-green, and green. On the one hand, it was the name of a goddess, the patroness of Lower Egypt, represented as a cobra called Wadjet, "the green one," or as the <a href="/wiki/Eye_of_Horus" title="Eye of Horus">Eye of Horus</a>, also called by the same name. On the other hand, <i>wedjet</i> was the word used for <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_blue" title="Egyptian blue">Egyptian blue</a> in faience ceramics.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hebrew">Hebrew</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hebrew">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Hebrew, the word "<span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', 'Keter Aram Tsova', 'Taamey Ashkenaz', 'Taamey David CLM', 'Taamey Frank CLM', 'Frank Ruehl CLM', 'Keter YG', 'Shofar', 'David CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', 'Simple CLM', 'Nachlieli', 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', Cardo, Alef, 'Noto Serif Hebrew', 'Noto Sans Hebrew', 'David Libre', David, 'Times New Roman', Gisha, Arial, FreeSerif, FreeSans;">כחול</span>‬" (pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/kaˈχol/</span>) means blue, while "<span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', 'Keter Aram Tsova', 'Taamey Ashkenaz', 'Taamey David CLM', 'Taamey Frank CLM', 'Frank Ruehl CLM', 'Keter YG', 'Shofar', 'David CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', 'Simple CLM', 'Nachlieli', 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', Cardo, Alef, 'Noto Serif Hebrew', 'Noto Sans Hebrew', 'David Libre', David, 'Times New Roman', Gisha, Arial, FreeSerif, FreeSans;">ירוק</span>‬" (pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/jaˈʁok/</span>) means green and has the same <a href="/wiki/Semitic_root" title="Semitic root">root</a>, <span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', 'Keter Aram Tsova', 'Taamey Ashkenaz', 'Taamey David CLM', 'Taamey Frank CLM', 'Frank Ruehl CLM', 'Keter YG', 'Shofar', 'David CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', 'Simple CLM', 'Nachlieli', 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', Cardo, Alef, 'Noto Serif Hebrew', 'Noto Sans Hebrew', 'David Libre', David, 'Times New Roman', Gisha, Arial, FreeSerif, FreeSans;">י־ר־ק</span>‬ (j-r-q), as the word for vegetables (<span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', 'Keter Aram Tsova', 'Taamey Ashkenaz', 'Taamey David CLM', 'Taamey Frank CLM', 'Frank Ruehl CLM', 'Keter YG', 'Shofar', 'David CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', 'Simple CLM', 'Nachlieli', 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', Cardo, Alef, 'Noto Serif Hebrew', 'Noto Sans Hebrew', 'David Libre', David, 'Times New Roman', Gisha, Arial, FreeSerif, FreeSans;">ירקות</span>‬, <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">jeʁaˈkot</span>). However, in classical Hebrew, <span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', 'Keter Aram Tsova', 'Taamey Ashkenaz', 'Taamey David CLM', 'Taamey Frank CLM', 'Frank Ruehl CLM', 'Keter YG', 'Shofar', 'David CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', 'Simple CLM', 'Nachlieli', 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', Cardo, Alef, 'Noto Serif Hebrew', 'Noto Sans Hebrew', 'David Libre', David, 'Times New Roman', Gisha, Arial, FreeSerif, FreeSans;">ירוק</span>‬ can mean both green and yellow, giving rise to such expressions as <span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', 'Keter Aram Tsova', 'Taamey Ashkenaz', 'Taamey David CLM', 'Taamey Frank CLM', 'Frank Ruehl CLM', 'Keter YG', 'Shofar', 'David CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', 'Simple CLM', 'Nachlieli', 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', Cardo, Alef, 'Noto Serif Hebrew', 'Noto Sans Hebrew', 'David Libre', David, 'Times New Roman', Gisha, Arial, FreeSerif, FreeSans;">ירוק כרישה</span>‬ (pronounced <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">/jaˈʁok kriʃ'ʔa/</span>), "leek green", to specify green to the exclusion of yellow. 
Like Russian and Italian, Hebrew has a separate name for light blue (<span class="script-hebrew" dir="rtl" style="font-size: 115%; font-family: 'Ezra SIL', 'Ezra SIL SR', 'Keter Aram Tsova', 'Taamey Ashkenaz', 'Taamey David CLM', 'Taamey Frank CLM', 'Frank Ruehl CLM', 'Keter YG', 'Shofar', 'David CLM', 'Hadasim CLM', 'Simple CLM', 'Nachlieli', 'SBL BibLit', 'SBL Hebrew', Cardo, Alef, 'Noto Serif Hebrew', 'Noto Sans Hebrew', 'David Libre', David, 'Times New Roman', Gisha, Arial, FreeSerif, FreeSans;">תכלת</span>‬, "<a href="/wiki/Tekhelet" title="Tekhelet">t'khelet</a>")—the color of the sky and of fringes on the ritual garment <i><a href="/wiki/Tzitzit" title="Tzitzit">Tzitzit</a></i>. This color has <a href="/wiki/Blue_in_Judaism" title="Blue in Judaism">special symbolic significance</a> in <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> and Jewish culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indo-European">Indo-European</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Indo-European">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Albanian">Albanian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Albanian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Albanian has two major words for "blue": <i>kaltër</i> refers to a light blue, such as that of the sky, but it is derived from Vulgar Latin <i>calthinus</i>, itself derived from <i>caltha</i>, a loan from Ancient Greek that meant "marigold" a small and in fact yellow flower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The other word, <i>blu</i>, refers to a darker shade of blue, and like many similar words across many European languages, derives ultimately from Germanic (see also: Italian <i>blu</i>). There is a separate word for green, <i>gjelbër</i>, which derives from the Latin <i>galbinus</i>, which originally meant "yellow"; the original Latin word for green on the other hand, <i>viridis</i>, is the source of the Albanian word for "yellow", <i>verdhë</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Baltic">Baltic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Baltic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>There are separate words for green (<i>zaļš</i>) and blue (<i>zils</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a>. Both <i>zils</i> and <i>zaļš</i> stem from the same Proto-Indo-European word for yellow (<i>*ghel</i>). Several other words in Latvian have been derived from these colors, namely grass is called <i>zāle</i> (from <i>zaļš</i>), while the name for iris is <i>zīlīte</i> (from <i>zils</i>).
</p><p>The now archaic word <i>mēļš</i> was used to describe both dark blue and black (probably indicating that previously <i>zils</i> was used only for lighter shades of blue). For instance, blueberries and blackberries are called <i>mellenes</i>.
</p><p>In Latvian black is "melns" (in some local dialects "mells").
</p><p>In Lithuanian <i>žalias</i> is green, <i>mėlynas</i> is blue and <i>žilas</i> is grey (hair), grizzled.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slavic">Slavic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Slavic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Slavic_language" title="South Slavic language">South Slavic language</a>, makes a clear distinction between blue (синьо, <i>sinyo</i>), green (зелено, <i>zeleno</i>), and black (черно, <i>cherno</i>).
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish language</a>, blue (<i>niebieski</i> from <i>niebo</i> – sky) and green (<i>zielony</i>) are treated as separate colors. The word for <a href="/wiki/Sky_blue" title="Sky blue">sky blue</a> or <a href="/wiki/Azure_(color)" title="Azure (color)">azure</a>—<i>błękitny</i>—might be considered either a basic color or a <a href="/wiki/Shades_of_blue" title="Shades of blue">shade of blue</a> by different speakers. Similarly dark blue or <a href="/wiki/Navy_blue" title="Navy blue">navy blue</a> (<i>granatowy</i>—deriving from the name of <a href="/wiki/Pomegranate" title="Pomegranate">pomegranate</a> (granat), some <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> of which are dark purplish blue in color) can be considered by some speakers as a separate basic color. Black (<i>czarny</i>) is completely distinguished from blue. As in English, Polish distinguishes pink ("różowy") from red ("czerwony").
</p><p>The word <i>siwy</i> means blue-gray in Polish (literally it means the color of gray hair). The word <i>siny</i> refers to violet-blue and is used to describe the color of bruises ("siniaki"), <a href="/wiki/Hematoma" title="Hematoma">hematoma</a>, and the blue skin discoloration that can result from moderate <a href="/wiki/Hypothermia" title="Hypothermia">hypothermia</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> does not have a single word referring to the whole range of colors denoted by the English term "blue". Instead, it traditionally treats light blue (голубой, <i>goluboy</i>) as a separate color independent from plain or dark blue (синий, <i>siniy</i>), with all seven "basic" colors of the spectrum (red–orange–yellow–green–<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%B3%D0%BE%D0%BB%D1%83%D0%B1%D0%BE%D0%B9#Russian" title="wikt:голубой">голубой</a> / <i>goluboy</i> (sky blue, light azure, but <i>does not equal</i> <a href="/wiki/Cyan" title="Cyan">cyan</a>)–<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D1%81%D0%B8%D0%BD%D0%B8%D0%B9#Russian" title="wikt:синий">синий</a> / <i>siniy</i> ("true" deep <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a>, like synthetic <a href="/wiki/Ultramarine" title="Ultramarine">ultramarine</a>)–violet) while in English the light blues like azure and cyan are considered mere shades of "blue" and not different colors.<br/>
To better understand this, consider that English makes a similar distinction between "<a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a>" and light red (<a href="/wiki/Pink" title="Pink">pink</a>, which is considered a different color and not merely a kind of red), but such a distinction is unknown in several other languages; for example, both "red" (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">红</span> / <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">紅</span>, <i>hóng</i>) and "pink" (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">粉红</span>, <i>fěn hóng</i>, lit. "powder red") have traditionally been considered varieties of a single color in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>. The Russian language also distinguishes between red (красный, krasniy) and pink (розовый, rozoviy).
</p><p>Similarly English descriptions of rainbows have often distinguished between blue or <a href="/wiki/Turquoise_(color)" title="Turquoise (color)">turquoise</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Indigo" title="Indigo">indigo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> the latter of which is often described as dark blue or <a href="/wiki/Ultramarine" title="Ultramarine">ultramarine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">Serbo-Croatian</a> color system makes a distinction between blue, green and black:
</p>
<ul><li>Blue: <i>plava</i> (indicates any blue) or <i>modra</i>.
<ul><li>Navy blue: <i>teget</i></li></ul></li>
<li>Green: <i>zelena</i></li>
<li>Black: <i>crna</i></li></ul>
<p><i>Modra</i> may also mean dark purple and is used to describe a bruise, <i>modrica</i>. Native speakers cannot pinpoint a color on the spectrum which would correspond to <i>modra</i>.
</p><p><i>Sinje</i>, analogue to Bulgarian синьо, <i>sinyo</i>/Russian синий, <i>siniy</i>, is archaic, and denotes blue-gray, usually used to describe dark skies or seas.
</p><p><i>Mrka</i> "dusky" can refer either to dark grey or dark brown, or even black. It is etymologically derived from the word for "darkness" (<i>mrak</i>), but it is distinct from "dark" (<i>taman</i>).
</p><p>Turquoise is usually described as "tirkizna", and similarly, azure will use a loan word "azurna".  There is no specific word for cyan. Blond hair is called <i>plava</i> ('blue'), reflecting likely the archaic use of "plav" for any bright white/blue colors (like the sky).
</p><p>Shades are defined with a prefix (e.g. "tamno-" for dark, or "svetlo-" for light), for example, dark blue = "tamnoplava".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Celtic">Celtic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Celtic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> word <i>glas</i> is usually translated as "blue"; however, it can also refer, variously, to the color of the sea, of grass, or of silver (cf. greek γλαυκός). The word <i>gwyrdd</i> (a borrowing from Latin <i>viridis</i>) is the standard translation for "green". In traditional <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> (and related languages), <i>glas</i> could refer to certain shades of green and grey as well as blue, and <i>llwyd</i> could refer to various shades of grey and brown; however, modern Welsh is tending toward the <a href="/wiki/Color_term" title="Color term">11-color Western scheme</a>, restricting <i>glas</i> to blue and using <i>gwyrdd</i> for green, <i>llwyd</i> for grey and <i>brown</i> for brown.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Modern Irish</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a> the word for "blue" is <i>gorm</i> (whence the name <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cairngorm_mountains" title="Cairngorm mountains">Cairngorm mountains</a> derives) – a borrowing of the now obsolete Early Welsh word <i>gwrm</i>, meaning "dark blue" or "dusky". A relic of the original meaning ("dusky") survives in the Irish term <i>daoine gorma</i>, meaning "<a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">Black people</a>".
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Colours_in_Irish.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2828" data-file-width="4000" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Colours_in_Irish.png/300px-Colours_in_Irish.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Colours_in_Irish.png/450px-Colours_in_Irish.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Colours_in_Irish.png/600px-Colours_in_Irish.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Colours_in_Irish.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Colour wheel in Irish with notes on their usage</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">Old</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_Irish" title="Middle Irish">Middle Irish</a>, like in Welsh, <i>glas</i> was a blanket term for colors ranging from green to blue to various shades of grey (e.g. the <i>glas</i> of a sword, the <i>glas</i> of stone, etc.). In Modern Irish, it has come to mean various shades of green, with specific reference to plant hues,  and grey (like the sea); other shades of green<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (January 2015)">vague</span></a></i>]</sup> would be referred to in Modern Irish as <i>uaine</i> or <i>uaithne</i>, while <i>liath</i> is grey proper (like a stone).
</p><p>Scottish Gaelic uses the term <i>uaine</i> for "green". However, the dividing line between it and <i>gorm</i> is somewhat different than between the English "green" and "blue", with <i>uaine</i> signifying a light green or yellow-green, and <i>gorm</i> extending from dark blue (what in English might be navy blue) to include the dark green or blue-green of vegetation. Grass, for instance, is <i>gorm</i>, rather than <i>uaine</i>. In addition, <i>liath</i> covers a range from light blue to light grey. However, the term for a green apple, such as a Granny Smith, would be <i>ubhal glas</i>.
</p><p>The boundary between colors varies much more than the "focal point": e.g. an island known in <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a> as <i>Enez glas ("the blue island")</i> is called in French <i>l'Île Verte ("the green island")</i>, in both cases referring to the greyish-green color of its bushes, even though both languages distinguish green (as in lawn grass) from blue (as in a cloudless midday sky).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romance">Romance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Romance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Romance terms for "green" (Catalan <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verd#Catalan" title="wikt:verd">verd</a></i></i>, French <i><i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vert#French" title="wikt:vert">vert</a></i></i>, Italian, Portuguese, Romanian and Spanish <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verde" title="wikt:verde">verde</a></i>) are all from Latin <i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/viridis#Latin" title="wikt:viridis">viridis</a></i></i>. The terms for "blue", on the other hand, vary: Catalan <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blau#Catalan" title="wikt:blau">blau</a></i></i>, Occitan <i><i lang="oc" title="Occitan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blau#Occitan" title="wikt:blau">blau</a></i></i>, French <i><i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bleu#French" title="wikt:bleu">bleu</a></i></i> and Italian <i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blu#Italian" title="wikt:blu">blu</a></i></i> come from a Germanic root, whereas the Spanish and Portuguese <i>azul</i> is likely to come from Arabic. French <i>bleu</i> was in turn loaned into many other languages, including English. Latin itself did not have a word covering all shades of blue, which may help explain these borrowings. It did, however, recognise <i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caeruleus#Latin" title="wikt:caeruleus">caeruleus</a></i></i> (dark blue, sometimes greenish), and <i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lividus#Latin" title="wikt:lividus">lividus</a></i></i> (greyish blue, like lead).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, as most Romance languages, makes roughly the same distinctions as English and has a specific term for each of blue ("<i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bleu#French" title="wikt:bleu">bleu</a></i>"), green ("<i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vert#French" title="wikt:vert">vert</a></i>") and grey ("<i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gris#French" title="wikt:gris">gris</a></i>"). For all three, different shades can be indicated with different (compound) terms, none of them being considered as basic color terms: "bleu <i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/clair#French" title="wikt:clair">clair</a></i>" (light blue), "bleu <i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ciel#French" title="wikt:ciel">ciel</a></i>" (sky blue), "bleu <i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/marine#French" title="wikt:marine">marine</a></i>" (Navy blue), "bleu <i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/roi#French" title="wikt:roi">roi</a></i>" (royal blue); "vert clair" (light green), "vert <i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pomme#French" title="wikt:pomme">pomme</a></i>" (literally: apple green); "gris <i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/anthracite#French" title="wikt:anthracite">anthracite</a></i>" (very dark grey), "gris <i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/souris#French" title="wikt:souris">souris</a></i>" (literally: mouse grey). French also uses "<i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azur#French" title="wikt:azur">azur</a></i>" for the lighter shade of blue of the sunny sky, that was in turn loaned to English as "azure".
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a> distinguishes blue (<i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blau#Catalan" title="wikt:blau">blau</a></i></i>) from green (<i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verd#Catalan" title="wikt:verd">verd</a></i></i>) and grey (<i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gris#Catalan" title="wikt:gris">gris</a></i></i>). Other basic or common colors by its own right are <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/porpra#Catalan" title="wikt:porpra">porpra</a></i></i> "purple", <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/groc#Catalan" title="wikt:groc">groc</a></i></i> "yellow", <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/taronja#Catalan" title="wikt:taronja">taronja</a></i></i> "orange", <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vermell#Catalan" title="wikt:vermell">vermell</a></i></i> "red", <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rosa#Catalan" title="wikt:rosa">rosa</a></i></i> "pink", <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/marr%C3%B3#Catalan" title="wikt:marró">marró</a></i></i> "brown", <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gris#Catalan" title="wikt:gris">gris</a></i></i> "grey", <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/negre#Catalan" title="wikt:negre">negre</a></i></i> "black" and <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blanc#Catalan" title="wikt:blanc">blanc</a></i></i> "white". For all these colors except black and white it is possible to indicate different shades using <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/clar#Catalan" title="wikt:clar">clar</a></i></i> "light" and <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fosc#Catalan" title="wikt:fosc">fosc</a></i></i> "dark"; for blue, though, it generally is <i>blau cel</i> "sky blue" and <i>blau marí</i> "sea blue". Other words and compounds are common to indicate more elaborated shades (<i>verd llimona</i> "lemon green", <i>rosa pàlid</i> "pale pink", <i>lila</i> "lilac", <i>granat</i> "carmine", <i>ocre</i> "ochre", <i>verd oliva</i> "olive green", etc.). Catalan actually distinguishes two reds with different and common words: while <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vermell#Catalan" title="wikt:vermell">vermell</a></i></i> refers to the color of arterial blood, <i><i lang="ca" title="Catalan language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/roig#Catalan" title="wikt:roig">roig</a></i></i> is a red tending towards yellow or the color of clay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> distinguishes blue (<i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blu#Italian" title="wikt:blu">blu</a></i></i>), green (<i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verde#Italian" title="wikt:verde">verde</a></i></i>) and grey (<i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/grigio#Italian" title="wikt:grigio">grigio</a></i></i>). There are also common words for light blue (e.g. the color of the cloudless sky): <i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azzurro#Italian" title="wikt:azzurro">azzurro</a></i></i> and <i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/celeste#Italian" title="wikt:celeste">celeste</a></i></i>, and other for darker shades, e.g. <i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/indaco#Italian" title="wikt:indaco">indaco</a></i></i>, indigo. <i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azzurro#Italian" title="wikt:azzurro">Azzurro</a></i></i>, the English equivalent of <a href="/wiki/Azure_(color)" title="Azure (color)">azure</a>, is usually considered a separate basic color rather than a shade of <i>blu</i> (similar to  the distinction in English between red and pink). Some sources even go to the point of defining <i>blu</i> as a darker shade of <i>azzurro</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <i>Celeste</i> literally means '(the color) of the sky' and can be used as synonym of <i>azzurro</i>, although it will more often be considered a less saturated hue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Acquamarina#Italian" title="wikt:Acquamarina">Acquamarina</a></i></i> (aquamarine) literally "sea water", indicates an even lighter, almost transparent, shade of blue. To indicate a mix of green and blue, Italians might say <i>verde <i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/acqua#Italian" title="wikt:acqua">acqua</a></i></i>, literally <i>water green</i>. The term <i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/glauco#Italian" title="wikt:glauco">glauco</a></i></i>, not common in standard italian and perceived as a literary term, is used in scientific contexts (esp. botany) to indicate a mix of blue, green and gray.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Other similar terms are <i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ceruleo#Italian" title="wikt:ceruleo">ceruleo</a></i></i> and <i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/turchese#Italian" title="wikt:turchese">turchese</a></i></i> (turquoise/teal); they are more saturated hues (especially turchese) and differ in context of use: the first is a literary or bureaucratic term (used for example to indicate light green eyes in identity cards); the second is more common in any informal speech, along with the variant <i>turchino</i> (for instance, the fairy of <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Pinocchio" title="The Adventures of Pinocchio">The Adventures of Pinocchio</a> is called <i>fata turchina</i>).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, the word "<i lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azul#Portuguese" title="wikt:azul">azul</a></i>" means blue and the word "<i lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verde#Portuguese" title="wikt:verde">verde</a></i>" means green. Furthermore, "azul-<i lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/claro#Portuguese" title="wikt:claro">claro</a></i>" means light-blue, and "azul-<i lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/escuro#Portuguese" title="wikt:escuro">escuro</a></i>" means dark-blue. More distinctions can be made between several hues of blue. For instance, "azul-<i lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/celeste#Portuguese" title="wikt:celeste">celeste</a></i>" means sky blue, "azul-<i lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/marinho#Portuguese" title="wikt:marinho">marinho</a></i>" means navy-blue and "azul-<i lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/turquesa#Portuguese" title="wikt:turquesa">turquesa</a></i>" means turquoise-blue. One can also make the distinction between "verde-claro" and "verde-escuro", meaning light and dark-green respectively, and more distinctions between several qualities of green: for instance, "verde-<i lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/oliva#Portuguese" title="wikt:oliva">oliva</a></i>" means olive-green and "verde-<i lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/esmeralda#Portuguese" title="wikt:esmeralda">esmeralda</a></i>" means emerald-green. <a href="/wiki/Cyan" title="Cyan">Cyan</a> is usually called "azul-celeste" (sky blue) or "azul-<i lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/piscina#Portuguese" title="wikt:piscina">piscina</a></i>", meaning pool blue, but also less commonly "<i lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ciano#Portuguese" title="wikt:ciano">ciano</a></i>", and "verde-<i lang="pt"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%C3%A1gua#Portuguese" title="wikt:água">água</a></i>", meaning water green.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a> clearly distinguishes between the colors green (<i><i lang="ro" title="Romanian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verde#Romanian" title="wikt:verde">verde</a></i></i>) and blue (<i><i lang="ro" title="Romanian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/albastru#Romanian" title="wikt:albastru">albastru</a></i></i>). It also uses separate words for different hues of the same color, e.g. light blue (<i><i lang="ro" title="Romanian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bleu#Romanian" title="wikt:bleu">bleu</a></i></i>), blue (<i>albastru</i>), dark-blue (<i>bleu-<i lang="ro" title="Romanian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/marin#Romanian" title="wikt:marin">marin</a></i></i> or <i><i lang="ro" title="Romanian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bleomarin#Romanian" title="wikt:bleomarin">bleomarin</a></i></i>), along with a word for turquoise (<i><i lang="ro" title="Romanian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/turcoaz#Romanian" title="wikt:turcoaz">turcoaz</a></i></i>) and azure (<i><i lang="ro" title="Romanian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azur#Romanian" title="wikt:azur">azur</a></i></i> or <i><i lang="ro" title="Romanian language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azuriu#Romanian" title="wikt:azuriu">azuriu</a></i></i>).
</p><p>Similarly to French, Romanian, Italian and Portuguese, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> distinguishes blue (<i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/azul#Spanish" title="wikt:azul">azul</a></i></i>) and green (<i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verde#Spanish" title="wikt:verde">verde</a></i></i>) and has an additional term for the tone of blue visible in the sky, namely "<i lang="es" title="Spanish language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/celeste#Spanish" title="wikt:celeste">celeste</a></i>", which is nonetheless considered a shade of blue.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germanic">Germanic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Germanic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a>, the word <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bl%C3%A1r" title="wikt:blár">blár</a></i> "blue" (from <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">proto-Germanic</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bl%C4%93waz" title="wikt:blēwaz">blēwaz</a>) was also used to describe <a href="/wiki/Black" title="Black">black</a> (and the common word for people of African descent was thus <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bl%C3%A1menn" title="wikt:blámenn">blámenn</a></i> 'blue/black men'). In <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>, <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bl%C3%A5" title="wikt:blå">blå</a></i>, the modern word for blue, was used this way until the early 20th century, and it still is to a limited extent in modern <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">Faroese</a>.
</p><p>German and Dutch distinguish blue (respectively <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blau" title="wikt:blau">blau</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blauw" title="wikt:blauw">blauw</a></i>) and green (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gr%C3%BCn" title="wikt:grün">grün</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/groen" title="wikt:groen">groen</a></i>) very similarly to English. There are (compound) terms for light blue (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hellblau" title="wikt:hellblau">hellblau</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lichtblauw" title="wikt:lichtblauw">lichtblauw</a></i>) and darker shades of blue (<i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dunkelblau" title="wikt:dunkelblau">dunkelblau</a></i> and <i><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/donkerblauw" title="wikt:donkerblauw">donkerblauw</a></i>). In addition, adjective forms of most traditional color names are inflected to match the corresponding noun's case and gender.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek">Greek</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Greek">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The terms for "blue" and "green" have changed completely in the transition from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> to <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">Modern Greek</a>.  Ancient Greek had γλαυκός (<i>glaukós</i>) "clear light blue", also with a turquoise/teal/green hue (it was a typical term for goddess Athena's eyes, portrayed in green or light blue) contrasting with χλωρός (<i>khlōrós</i>) "bright green" but also "acid yellow" (cf. words such as chlorophyll). κυανός (<i>kuanós</i>, which turned into "cyan" in English) meant either a "dark blue/green substance" or just "blue".
</p><p>We know the terms changed in Byzantine Era through the name of two political factions of Constantinople: the Green ones (Πράσινοι) and the Blue ones (Bένετοι); we don't know if the names of those groups influenced the change or if they have been named from the new color terms.
</p><p>Modern Greek has πράσινο (<i>prásino</i>) for green and γαλάζιο (<i>galázio</i>) or θαλασσί (<i>thalassí</i>, 'sea colored') for light blue/sea blue: the previous term for blue is therefore disappeared. It's interesting to note that both galázio and véneto probably came from the eye colour of two barbaric populations, the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Veneti" title="Adriatic Veneti">Veneti</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Galatians_(people)" title="Galatians (people)">Galatians</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
The recent loan μπλε (<i>mple</i>, from French <i>bleu</i>) is used for blue.
In the Modern Greek language, there are additional names for light and dark blues/greens:
</p>
<ol><li>τυρκουάζ (<i>tyrkouáz</i>) for turquoise</li>
<li>κυανό (<i>kyanó</i>) for azure</li>
<li>λαχανί (<i>lachaní</i> 'cabbage colored') for lime green</li>
<li>λαδί (<i>ladí</i>) for olive green</li>
<li>χακί (<i>chakí</i>) for dark <a href="/wiki/Khaki" title="Khaki">khaki</a></li>
<li>κυπαρισσί (<i>kyparissí</i> 'cypress colored') for brownish green.</li></ol>
<p>As a rule, the first two words of the list are accepted as shades of blue, and the rest as shades of green. Also βιολέ (<i>violé</i>) / βιολετί (<i>violetí</i>) for violet blue (which is, however, usually considered as a shade of purple, rather than blue).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iranian">Iranian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Iranian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ossetian_language" title="Ossetian language">Ossetian</a> has one word for blue, light blue and green—цъæх (tsəh), which also means "grey" and "glaucous"—but it also has a separate word for green, кæрдæгхуыз (kərdəghuɨz), literally "grassy" (from кæрдæг "grass"). The latter derives from кæрдын (kərdɨn) "to mow" (like in German <i>Heu</i> (<i>hau</i>) &lt; <i>hauen</i> (to mow)).
</p><p>Ossetian also has separate words for these colors:
</p>
<ul><li>light blue: æрвхуыз (ərvhuɨz) from æрвон "sky"</li>
<li>glaucous: бæлонхуыз (bəlonhuɨz) from бæлон "pigeon" (a <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">calque</a> from Russian, cf. голубой (light blue) &lt; голубь (pigeon)); also фæздæгхуыз (fəzdəghuɨz) from <i>fazdag</i> "smoke", from Old Persian *pazdaka-, cognate of Latin <i>pedis</i> (louse) and Russian (obscene) <i>пизда</i> (cunt), because of its stinking.</li>
<li>blue: копрадзхуыз (kopradzhuɨz), from копрадз (kopradz) - bluing for laundering, transliteration of Russian купорос "vitriol" from Latin <i>cuprum</i> "copper"</li>
<li>grey: фæныкхуыз (fənykhuɨz), from фæнык "ashes", originating from Persian *pa(s)nu-ka, cognate or Russian песок "sand"</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pashto" title="Pashto">Pashto</a> uses the word <i>shīn</i> to denote blue as well as green. <i>Shinkay</i>, a word derived from <i>shīn</i>, means 'greenery' but <i>shīn āsmān</i> means 'blue sky'. When there is ambiguity, it is common to ask (as in Vietnamese), "<i>Shīn</i> like the sky? Or <i>shīn</i> like plants?"
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> words for blue include <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" title="Persian language text">آبی</span> <i>ābi</i> (literally the color of water, from <i>āb</i> 'water'), for blue generally; <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" title="Persian language text">نیلی</span> <i>nili</i> (from <i>nil</i>, '<a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo dye</a>'), for deeper shades of blue such as the color of rain clouds; <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" title="Persian language text">فیروزه</span> <i>fayruzeh</i> '<a href="/wiki/Turquoise" title="Turquoise">turquoise</a> stone', used to describe the color of blue eyes; <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" title="Persian language text">لاجوردی</span> <i>lājvardi</i> or <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" title="Persian language text">لاژوردی</span> <i>lāzhvardi</i> '<a href="/wiki/Lapis_lazuli" title="Lapis lazuli">lapis lazuli</a> color', source of the words <i>lazuli</i> and <i>azure</i>; <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" title="Persian language text">نیلوفری</span> <i>nilufari</i> '<a href="/wiki/Nymphaeaceae" title="Nymphaeaceae">water lily</a> color'; and <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" title="Persian language text">کبود</span> <i>kabud</i>, an old literary word for 'blue'.
</p><p>The Persian word for green is <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" title="Persian language text">سبز</span> <i>sabz</i>. As in <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a>, dark-skinned people may be described as "green".
</p><p>The color of the sky is variously described in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_poetry" title="Persian poetry">Persian poetry</a> using the words <i>sabz</i>, <i>fayruzeh</i>, <i>nil</i>, <i>lājvardi</i>, or <i>nilufari</i>— literally "green", "indigo", "turquoise", "azure" or "the color of water lilies". For example, <i>sabz-ākhor</i> "green stable", <i>sabz-āshyāneh</i> "green ceiling", <i>sabz-ayvān</i> "green balcony", <i>sabz-bādbān</i> "green sail", <i>sabz-bāgh</i> "green garden", <i>sabz-farsh</i> "green carpet", <i>sabz-golshan</i> "green flower-garden", <i>sabz-kārgāh</i> "green workshop", <i>sabz-kh</i><sup><i>v</i></sup><i>ān</i> "green table", <i>sabz-manzareh</i> "green panorama", <i>sabz-maydān</i> "green field" <i>sabz-pol</i> "green bridge", <i>sabz-tāq</i> "green arch", <i>sabz-tasht</i> "green bowl", and <i>sabz-tā'us</i> "green peacock" are poetic epithets for the sky—in addition to similar compounds using the words for blue, e.g. <i>lājvardi-saqf</i> "lapis lazuli-colored roof" or <i>fayruzeh-tasht</i> "turquoise bowl". Moreover, the words for green of Arabic origin <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" title="Persian language text">اخضر</span> <i>akhzar</i> and <span dir="rtl" lang="fa" title="Persian language text">خضرا</span> <i>khazrā</i> are used for epithets of the sky or heaven, such as <i>charkh-e akhzar</i> "green wheel".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indo-Aryan">Indo-Aryan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Indo-Aryan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">Indo-Aryan languages</a> distinguish blue from green. In Urdu, blue is نیلا (<i>nīlā</i>) and green is سبز (<i>sabz</i>). There are some names of shades of blue as well, like فیروزی (<i>ferozī</i>) "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turquoise_(colour)" title="Turquoise (colour)">turquoise</a>".
In Hindi, blue is नीला (<i>nīlā</i>) and green is हरा (<i>harā</i>).
In Marathi, blue is निळा (<i>niḽā</i>) and green is हिरवा (<i>hiravā</i>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basque">Basque</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Basque">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the Basque language there are three native color words derived from "ur" (water). <i>Urdin</i>, is nowadays used in most cases for blue. "Ubel", originally meant flash flood and, with respect to colors, is often used in relation to bruises. "Begi ubela" would be translated into English by "a black and blue eye". But in Basque, unlike English, ubel remains in use after the hit skin has lost its purple color and become pale, why this word is used for both purple in particular and pale hue in general. "Uher", originally meant dirty, still water or rusty and is used for gray or sienna tones or impure, dark color in general. Green is usually expressed with the loan-word <i>berde</i> from Spanish "verde"/ French "vert". The authenticity of the less common Basque terms for green <i>(h)orlegi</i> and <i>musker</i> is disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uralic">Uralic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Uralic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finnish">Finnish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Finnish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> makes a distinction between <i>vihreä</i> (green) and <i>sininen</i> (blue). <a href="/wiki/Turquoise_(color)" title="Turquoise (color)">Turquoise</a> or <a href="/wiki/Teal" title="Teal">teal</a> (<i>turkoosi</i> or <i>sinivihreä</i>) is considered to be a separate, intermediate color between green and blue, and black (<i>musta</i>) is also differentiated from blue.
</p><p>The name for blue, <i>sininen</i>, is shared with other <a href="/wiki/Finnic_languages" title="Finnic languages">Finnic languages</a> and is thus dated to the era of the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Finnic_language" title="Proto-Finnic language">Proto-Finnic language</a> (ca. 5000 years old). However, it is also shared with the unrelated Russian language (синий, <i>siniy</i>), suggesting that it is a loanword. (Alternatively, the Russian word синий could represent Finnic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substratum" title="Substratum">substratum</a>). The word <i>vihreä</i> (<i>viher-</i>, archaic <i>viheriä</i>, <i>viheriäinen</i>) is related to <i>vehreä</i> "verdant" and <i>vihanta</i> "green", and <i>viha</i> "hate", originally "poison". It is not shared with Estonian, in which it is <i>roheline</i>, probably related with the Estonian word <i>rohi</i> "grass". However, the form <i>viha</i> does have correspondences in related languages as far as <a href="/wiki/Permic_languages" title="Permic languages">Permic languages</a>, where it means not only "poison" but "bile" or "green or yellow". It has been originally loaned from an Indo-Iranian protolanguage and is related to Latin <i>virus</i> "poison". Furthermore, the word <i>musta</i> "black" is also of Finnic origin.
</p><p>The differentiation of several colors by <a href="/wiki/Hue" title="Hue">hue</a> is at least <a href="/wiki/Finnic_languages" title="Finnic languages">Finnic</a> (a major subgroup of Uralic) in origin. Before this, only red (<i>punainen</i>) was clearly distinguished by hue, with other colors described in terms of brightness (<i>valkea</i> vs. <i>musta</i>), using non-color adjectives for further specificity. Alternatively, it appears that the distinction between <i>valkea</i> and <i>musta</i> was in fact "clean, shining" vs. "dirty, murky". The original meaning of <i>sini</i> was possibly either "black/dark" or "green". Mauno Koski's theory is that dark colors of high saturation—both blue and green—would be <i>sini</i>, while shades of color with low saturation, such as dark brown or black, would be <i>musta</i>. Although it is theorized that originally <i>vihreä</i> was not a true color name and was used to describe plants only, the occurrence of <i>vihreä</i> or <i>viha</i> as a name of a color in several related languages shows that it was probably <a href="/wiki/Polysemy" title="Polysemy">polysemic</a> (meaning both "green" and "verdant") already in early Baltic-Finnic. However, whatever the case with these theories, differentiation of blue and green must be at least as old as the Baltic-Finnic languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungarian">Hungarian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Hungarian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a> makes the distinction between green (<i>zöld</i>) and blue (<i>kék</i>), and also distinguishes black (<i>fekete</i>). Intermediate colors between green and blue are commonly referred to as <i>zöldeskék</i> (literally greenish-blue) or <i>kékeszöld</i> (bluish-green), but names for specific colors in this continuum—like turquoise (<i>türkiz</i>)—also exist. Particular shades of a color can also have separate names, such as azure (<i>azúr</i>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkic">Turkic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Turkic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Kazakh_language" title="Kazakh language">Kazakh language</a>, like many <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">Turkic languages</a>, distinguishes between <i>kök</i> for blue and <i>jasâl</i> for green. In Kazakh, many adjectival variations can be found referring to perceived gradations in saturation level of "blue", such as <i>kögildir</i>, <i>kökshil</i>, and <i>kökboz</i>, which respectively denominate the gradual decrease in the intensity, <i>kökboz</i> being often used as a color referent in its own right. <i>Kök</i> is occasionally used to denote green plants (e.g. 'kök' shöp'), but such usage is mostly confined to poetic utterances or certain localized dialects.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> treats dark or navy blue (<i>lacivert</i>, from the same Persian root as English <i>azure</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Lapis_lazuli" title="Lapis lazuli">lapis lazuli</a></i>) as a separate color from plain or light blue (<i>mavi</i>). <i>Mavi</i> is derived from the Arabic word مائي <i>mā'ī</i> 'like water' (ماء <i>mā'</i> being the Arabic word for water) and <i>lacivert</i> is derived from Persian لاجورد <i>lājvard</i> '<a href="/wiki/Lapis_lazuli" title="Lapis lazuli">lapis lazuli</a>', a semiprecious stone with the color of navy blue. In the <a href="/wiki/Tengrism" title="Tengrism">pre-Islamic religion of the Turks</a>, blue is the color that represented the east, as well as the zodiac sign Aquarius (the Water Bearer). A characteristic tone of blue, <a href="/wiki/Turquoise_(color)" title="Turquoise (color)">turquoise</a>, was much used by the Turks for their traditional decorations and jewelry.
</p><p>In traditional pre-Islamic Turkic culture, both blue and green were represented by the same name, <i>gök</i> 'sky'. The name is still in use in many rural areas. For instance, in many regions of Turkey, when mold is formed on cheese, the phenomenon is called <i>gögermek</i> 'turning into the color of <i>gök</i>/sky'.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dravidian">Dravidian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Dravidian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil language</a> clearly distinguishes between the colors <span lang="ta" title="Tamil language text">பச்சை</span> green (<i>paccai</i>), <span lang="ta" title="Tamil language text">நீலம்</span> blue (<i>neelam</i>) and <span lang="ta" title="Tamil language text">கருப்பு</span> black (<i>karuppu</i>). The prefix <i>karu-</i> would indicate dark colors while the suffix <i>iḷam</i> would indicate light colors. Thus <span lang="ta" title="Tamil language text">கரும்பச்சை</span> <i>karumpaccai</i> would be dark green.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu language</a> uses a single word, <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a>: <span lang="te">పచ్చ</span> <i>pacca</i>, for green and yellow. To differentiate between the two shades, another word is prefixed in some cases. For example, green will be called <span lang="te" title="Telugu language text">ఆకుపచ్చ</span> <i>ākupacca</i> "leaf-<i>pacca</i>" and yellow <span lang="te" title="Telugu language text">పసుపుపచ్చ</span> <i>pasupupacca</i> "<a href="/wiki/Turmeric" title="Turmeric">turmeric</a>-<i>pacca</i>".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asian_languages">East Asian languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: East Asian languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese">Chinese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Chinese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The modern <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese language</a> has the blue–green distinction (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%93%9D" title="wikt:蓝">蓝</a>/ <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%97%8D" title="wikt:藍">藍</a> <i>lán</i> for blue and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%BB%BF" title="wikt:绿">绿</a> / <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%B6%A0" title="wikt:綠">綠</a> <i>lǜ</i> for green); however, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%E9%9D%92" title="青">another word</a> that predates the modern vernacular, <i>qīng</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92" title="wikt:青">青</a></span>), is also used. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_character" title="Chinese character">character</a> depicts the budding of a young plant and it could be understood as "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verdant" title="wikt:verdant">verdant</a>", but the word is used to describe colors ranging from light and yellowish green through deep blue all the way to black, as in <i>xuánqīng</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%8E%84%E9%9D%92" title="wikt:玄青">玄青</a></span>). For example, the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Republic_of_China" title="Flag of the Republic of China">Flag of the Republic of China</a> is today still referred to as <i>qīng tiān, bái rì, mǎn dì hóng</i> ("'Blue' Sky, White Sun, Whole Ground Red"—<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92" title="wikt:青">青</a>天，白日，滿地紅</span>); whereas <i>qīngcài</i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92%E8%8F%9C" title="wikt:青菜">青菜</a></span>) is the Chinese word for "green <a href="/wiki/Bok_choy" title="Bok choy">bok choy</a>". A <a href="/wiki/Cucumber" title="Cucumber">cucumber</a> is known as either huángguā (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%BB%83%E7%93%9C" title="wikt:黃瓜">黃瓜</a></span>) "yellow melon" while (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92%E7%93%9C" title="wikt:青瓜">青瓜</a></span>) meaning "green melon" is more commonly used in Cantonese. <i>Qīng</i> 青 was the traditional designation of both blue and green for much of the history of the Chinese language, while 蓝 <i>lán</i> ('blue') originally referred to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigo_plant" title="Indigo plant">indigo plant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  However, the character 绿 <i>lǜ</i> ('green'), as a particular 'shade' of <i>qīng</i> applied to cloth and clothing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> has been attested since the <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_of_Poetry" title="Classic of Poetry">Book of Odes</a></i> (1000 to 600 B.C.) (e.g., the title of Ode 27 《邶风·绿衣》 'Green Upper Garment' in the <i>Airs of Bei</i>).  As a part of the adoption of modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernacular_Chinese" title="Vernacular Chinese">Vernacular Chinese</a> as the social norm, replacing <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">Classical Chinese</a>, the modern terms for blue and green are now more commonly used than <i>qīng</i> as standalone color terms, although <i>qīng</i> is still part of many common noun phrases. The two forms can also be encountered combined as 青蓝 and 青绿, with 青 being used as an <a href="/wiki/Intensifier" title="Intensifier">intensifier</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese">Japanese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Japanese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:278px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arrows_in_Okazaki_edit.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="158" data-file-width="276" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/af/Arrows_in_Okazaki_edit.jpeg" width="276"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arrows_in_Okazaki_edit.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A billboard in <a href="/wiki/Okazaki,_Aichi" title="Okazaki, Aichi">Okazaki, Aichi</a>, depicting <a href="/wiki/Traffic_light" title="Traffic light">traffic lights</a> with a blue, rather than green, 'go' signal. The distinction of blue and green (<i><a href="/wiki/Ao_(color)" title="Ao (color)">ao</a></i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92" title="wikt:青">青</a></span>)</span>) in Japanese is fluid enough to allow these variances in the depiction of the color.</div></div></div>
<p>The Japanese word <i><a href="/wiki/Ao_(color)" title="Ao (color)">ao</a></i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92" title="wikt:青">青</a></span>, <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">n.</a>, <i>aoi</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%92" title="wikt:青">青</a>い</span>, <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adj.</a>)</span>)</span>, the same <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> character as the Chinese <i>qīng</i>, can refer to either blue or green depending on the situation. Modern <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> has a word for green<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%B7%91" title="wikt:緑">緑</a></span><span style="display:none">,</span> <i>midori</i>)</span>, but it is a relatively recent usage. Ancient Japanese did not have this distinction: the word <i>midori</i> only came into use in the <a href="/wiki/Heian_period" title="Heian period">Heian period</a>, and at that time (and for a long time thereafter) <i>midori</i> was still considered a shade of <i>ao</i>. Educational materials distinguishing green and blue only came into use after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> thus, even though most Japanese consider them to be green, the word <i>ao</i> is still used to describe certain <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apples</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">vegetation</a>. <i>Ao</i> is also the word used to refer to the color on a <a href="/wiki/Traffic_light" title="Traffic light">traffic light</a> that signals one to "go".  However, most other objects—a green <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">car</a>, a green <a href="/wiki/Sweater" title="Sweater">sweater</a>, and so forth—will generally be called <i>midori</i>. Japanese people also sometimes use the word <i>gurīn</i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">グリーン</span>)</span>, based on the English word "green", for colors. The language also has several other words meaning specific shades of green and blue.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korean">Korean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Korean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The native <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a> words <span lang="ko" title="Korean language text">푸르다</span> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization" title="Revised Romanization">Revised Romanization</a>: <i>pureu-da</i>) or 파랗다 (parah-ta) may mean either blue or green, or bluish green. These adjectives <span lang="ko" title="Korean language text">푸르다</span> and  파랗다 are used for blue as in <span lang="ko" title="Korean language text">푸른 하늘</span> (<i>pureu-n haneul</i>)  or 파란 하늘 (para-n haneul) meaning 'blue sky', or for green as in <span lang="ko" title="Korean language text">푸른 숲</span> (<i>pureu-n sup</i>, green forest) or 파란 잔디 (parah-n jandi,  green grass). 파란 (paran) is also used for the traffic green light meaning 'go' as in 파란 불 (paran pul). In general, however, 파랗다 "parah-ta" is more associated with blue, and 푸르다 "pureu-da" with green. 푸른 (pur-n) and 파란 (parah-n) are noun-modifying forms, and 파랗다 "parah-ta" can also be used in a noun form; (<span lang="ko" title="Korean language text">파랑</span> <i>parang</i> <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">n.</a>). There are Sino-Korean expressions that refer to green and blue. <span lang="ko" title="Korean language text">초록/草綠</span> (<i>chorok</i> <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adj.</a>/<a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">n.</a>), <span lang="ko" title="Korean language text">초록색/草綠色</span> (<i>choroksaek</i> <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">n.</a> or for short, <span lang="ko" title="Korean language text">녹색/綠色</span> <i>noksaek</i> <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">n.</a>) is used for green. <i>Cheong</i> <span lang="ko" title="Korean language text">청/靑</span>, another expression borrowed from Chinese (<a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i>qing</i>), is mostly used for blue, as in 청바지/靑-- (cheong-baji, blue jean" and Cheong Wa Dae (<span lang="ko" title="Korean language text">청와대</span> or <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko" title="Korean language text"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%9D%91" title="wikt:靑">靑</a>瓦臺</span>), the <a href="/wiki/Blue_House" title="Blue House">Blue House</a>, which is the executive office and official residence of the President of the Republic of Korea, but is also used for green as well, as in 청과물/靑果物 (cheong-gwamul, fruits and vegetables) and 청포도/靑葡萄 (cheong-podo, green grape).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tibetan">Tibetan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Tibetan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Classical_Tibetan" title="Classical Tibetan">Tibetan</a>, སྔོན་པོ། (<a href="/wiki/Wylie_transliteration" title="Wylie transliteration">Wylie</a> sngon po) is the term traditionally given for the color of the sky and of grass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> This term also falls into the general pattern of naming colors by appending the suffix "po", as in "mar po" (red); "ser po" (yellow); "nag po" (black); and "dkar po" (white). Conspicuously, the term for "green" is "ljang khu", likely related to "ljang bu", and defined as—"the grue (sngon po) sprout of wheat or barley".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnamese">Vietnamese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Vietnamese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a> usually does not use separate words for green and refers to that color using a word that can also refer to <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a>. In Vietnamese, blue and green are denoted by <i>xanh</i> (青). This is a colloquial rendering of <i>thanh</i> (青), as with Chinese and Japanese. Blue is specifically described as <i>xanh</i>, as in the skin of the sky (<i>xanh da trời</i>) or "<i>xanh</i>, as in the ocean" (<i>xanh dương, xanh nước biển</i>) and green as "<i>xanh</i>, as in the leaves" (<i>xanh lá cây</i>).
</p><p>Modern Vietnamese occasionally does employ the terms <i>xanh lam</i> and <i>xanh lục</i> (in which the second syllables derive from the Chinese: <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%97%8D" title="wikt:藍">藍</a> and <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%B6%A0" title="wikt:綠">綠</a>) for <i>blue</i> and <i>green</i>, respectively.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mongolian">Mongolian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Mongolian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Mongolian</a>, the word for green is ногоон (<i>nogoon</i>). Mongolian distinguishes between dark and light blue. The word for light blue is цэнхэр (<i>tsenher</i>) and the word for dark blue is хөх (<i>höh</i>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Austronesian_languages">Austronesian languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Austronesian languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Filipino_.28Tagalog.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Filipino_(Tagalog)">Filipino (Tagalog)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Filipino (Tagalog)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Speakers of <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog</a> most commonly use the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Philippines" title="Spanish language in the Philippines">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_loanwords_in_Tagalog" title="List of loanwords in Tagalog">loanwords</a> for blue and green—<i>asul</i> (from Spanish <i>azul</i>) and <i>berde</i> (from Spanish <i>verde</i>), respectively. Although these words are much more common in spoken use, Tagalog has native terms: <i>bugháw</i> for blue and <i>lunti(án)</i> for green, which are seen as archaic and more flowery. These are mostly confined to formal and academic writings, alongside artistic fields such literature, music, and poetry.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Cebuano_language" title="Cebuano language">Cebuano</a>, another major <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_the_Philippines" title="Languages of the Philippines">Philippine language</a>, the native words for "blue" and "green" end in the same syllable: <i>pughaw</i> and <i>lunhaw</i>, respectively. Pughaw means sky blue, while lunhaw is fresh leaf green (i.e. neither brownish nor yellowish).
</p><p>Humour and jokes of a sexual or derogatory nature are called "green" in <a href="/wiki/Philippine_English" title="Philippine English">Philippine English</a> while they would otherwise be described as "blue" in English (e.g. "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_comedy" title="Blue comedy">blue comedy</a>", "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dirty_joke" title="Dirty joke">blue joke</a>"). This is a <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">calque</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Hispanidad" title="Hispanidad">Hispanic</a> term <i>chiste verde</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Javanese">Javanese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Javanese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Javanese_language" title="Javanese language">Modern Javanese</a> has distinct words for blue <i>biru</i> and green <i>ijo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> These words are derived from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Javanese_language" title="Old Javanese language">Old Javanese</a> <i>birū</i> and <i>hijo</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> However, in Old Javanese <i>birū</i> could mean pale blue, greyish blue, greenish blue or even turquoise, while <i>hijo</i> which means green, could also means the blue-green colour of clear water. <i>Biru</i> and <i>ijo</i> in Modern Javanese are cognates of <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay</a>/<a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a> <i>biru</i> and <i>hijau</i> which have the same meaning.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Niger-Congo">Niger-Congo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Niger-Congo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_language" title="Yoruba language">Yoruba</a> word for green is <i>àwö ewéko</i>, which translates to "color of leaves", and blue is <i>àwö sánmà</i>, which translates to "color of sky".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bantu">Bantu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Bantu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a> word for blue is <i>buluu</i>, which is derived directly from English and has been in the language for a relatively short time. For other colors, Swahili uses either <i>rangi ya ___</i> (<i>the color of ___</i>) or a shortened version, <i>-a ___</i>. For example, green is <i>rangi ya kijani</i> or <i>rangi ya majani</i>, which means <i>the color of grass/leaves</i>. Sky blue is <i>rangi ya samawati</i>, or <i>the color of the sky</i> from the Arabic word for sky. (Note: all of these can be written as <i>-a kijani</i>, <i>-a majani</i>, <i>-a samwati</i>, etc.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">Zulu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xhosa_language" title="Xhosa language">Xhosa</a> use the word <i>-luhlaza</i> (the prefix changes according to the class of the noun) for <i>blue/green</i>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tswana_language" title="Tswana language">Tswana</a> uses the same word <i>tala</i> to refer to both blue and green. One has to deduce from the context and prior knowledge, of what is being talked about, to be able to pinpoint exactly the color in question.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Himba_people" title="Himba people">Himba people</a> use a single word for shades of green and blue: <i>buru</i>. They curiously have only three other colour names, and their color perception has been studied by anthropologists.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="American_languages">American languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue%E2%80%93green_distinction_in_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: American languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The language of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohawk_Nation" title="Mohawk Nation">Kanien'kehá:ka</a> Nation at <a href="/wiki/Akwesasne" title="Akwesasne">Akwesasne</a> is at Stage VII on the <a href="/wiki/Basic_Color_Terms:_Their_Universality_and_Evolution" title="Basic Color Terms: Their Universality and Evolution">Berlin–Kay Scale</a>, and thus possesses distinct terms for a broad range of <a href="/wiki/Spectral_color" title="Spectral color">spectral</a> and non-spectral colours, including blue (<i>oruía</i>), green (<i>óhute</i>), black (<i>kahúji</i>), white (<i>karákA'</i>), and grey (<i>atakArókwa</i>). According to one researcher, the Nation's term for purple (<i>arihwawakunéha</i>), which translates to 'bishop['s colour]', a recent coinage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frisch_Mohawk_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frisch_Mohawk-25">[25]</a></sup> The way in which purple was categorized and referenced prior to the addition of the latter term is not clear.
</p><p>In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lakota_Sioux" title="Lakota Sioux">Lakota Sioux</a> <a href="/wiki/Lakota_language" title="Lakota language">language</a>, the word <i>tȟó</i> is used for both blue and green, though the word <i>tȟózi</i> (a mixture of the words <i>tȟó</i> meaning "blue (green)," and <i>zí</i> meaning "yellow") has become common (<i>zítȟo</i> can also be used). This is in line with common practice of using <i>zíša/šázi</i> for Orange (<i>šá</i> meaning "red"), and <i>šátȟo/tȟóša</i> for "purple/violet."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Choctaw" title="Choctaw">Choctaw</a> <a href="/wiki/Choctaw_language" title="Choctaw language">language</a> has two words, <i>okchʋko</i> and <i>okchʋmali</i>, which have different meanings depending on the source. In 1852 <i>okchakko</i> is translated variously as pale blue or pale green, <i>okchakko chohmi</i> (<i>somewhat okchakko</i>) is given as swarthy, and <i>okchamali</i> is defined as deep blue, gray, green, or sky blue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In 1880 <i>okchakko</i> and <i>okchʋmali</i> are both given as blue, and green is not specifically listed as a color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In an 1892 dictionary, <i>okchamali</i> is deep blue or green, <i>okchakko</i> is pale blue or bright green, and a third word <i>kili̱koba</i> is bright green (resembling a <i>kili̱kki</i>, a species of parrot).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> By 1915, the authoritative Byington dictionary gives <i>okchako</i> as blue and <i>okchamali</i> as green, blue, gray, verdant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> A current coursebook differentiates based on brightness, giving <i>okchʋko</i> as bright blue/green and <i>okchʋmali</i> as pale or dull blue/green.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Modern usage in the <a href="/wiki/Choctaw_Nation_of_Oklahoma" title="Choctaw Nation of Oklahoma">Choctaw Nation of Oklahoma</a> language school is to use <i>okchʋko</i> for blue and <i>okchʋmali</i> for green, with no distinction for brightness.
</p><p>Single words for blue/green are also found in <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan languages</a>; for example in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yukatek_Maya_language" title="Yukatek Maya language">Yukatek Maya language</a> <i>blue/green</i> is <i>yax</i>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tupian_languages" title="Tupian languages">Tupian languages</a> did not originally differ between the two colors, though they may now as a result of interference of Spanish (in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guaran%C3%AD_language" title="Guaraní language">Guaraní</a>) or Portuguese (in the case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nheengatu_language" title="Nheengatu language">Nheengatu</a>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Tupi_language" title="Old Tupi language">Tupi</a> word <i>oby</i> (<small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[ɔˈβɨ]</a></span>) meant both as does the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guaran%C3%AD_language" title="Guaraní language">Guaraní</a> <i>hovy</i> (<small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[hɔʋɨ]</a></span>). In modern tupi (<a href="/wiki/Nheengatu" title="Nheengatu">nheengatu</a>) you can use the word "suikiri" for green and "iakira/akira" for blue, but they are still interchangeable, because "iakira/akira" also means immature, as in "pakua akira" (green banana/immature banana), and "suikiri" can also mean blue. In modern <a href="/wiki/Guarani_language" title="Guarani language">guarani</a>, they use "hovy" for blue and "hovy'û" (which literally means "dark green/blue) for green. So "hovy" can still be used for green" and "hovy'û" can be used for dark blue shades. They also have the word "aky", cognate with nheengatu "akira", and it means green/immature too.
</p><p>The Yebamasa of the Rio Piraparana region in <a href="/wiki/Vaup%C3%A9s_Department" title="Vaupés Department">Vaupés Department</a>, southeastern <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, use the term <i>sumese</i> for blue/green. The letter "u" is pronounced like the German "ü".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mapuche_language" title="Mapuche language">Mapudungun</a> distinguishes between black (<i>kurü</i>), blue (<i>kallfü</i>) and green (<i>karü</i>, also meaning "raw" or "immature"). The word <i>payne</i> was formerly used to refer to a sky blue, and also refers to the bluish color of stones (Zúñiga, 2006).
</p>
