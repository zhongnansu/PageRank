<p><b>Julien Green</b> (September 6, 1900 – August 13, 1998) was an American writer who authored several novels (<i>The Dark Journey</i>, <i>The Closed Garden</i>, <i>Moira</i>, <i>Each Man in His Darkness</i>, the <i>Dixie</i> trilogy, etc.), a four-volume autobiography (<i>The Green Paradise</i>, <i>The War at Sixteen</i>, <i>Love in America</i> and <i>Restless Youth</i>) and his famous <i>Diary</i> (in nineteen volumes, 1919–1998). He wrote primarily in French and was the first non-French national to be elected to the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_fran%C3%A7aise" title="Académie française">Académie française</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julien_Green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Julian Hartridge Green was born to American parents in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, a descendant on his mother's side of a Confederate Senator, <a href="/wiki/Julian_Hartridge" title="Julian Hartridge">Julian Hartridge</a> (1829–1879), who later served as a Democratic Representative from Georgia to the US Congress, and who was Julien Green's namesake. (Green was christened "Julian"; his French publisher changed the spelling to "Julien" in the 1920s).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The youngest of eight children born to Protestant parents, he had a puritanical and overprotective upbringing, his mother being  sexually repressive. Green received a Calvinist education in his religious education as a child,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> but became a Roman Catholic in 1916, two years after his mother's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The following year, still only 16, he volunteered his services as an ambulanceman in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Field_Service" title="American Field Service">American Field Service</a>. When his age was discovered his enlistment was annulled. He immediately signed up with an ambulance unit of the <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a>, and when that six-month term of service ended in 1918, he enlisted in the French Army, in which he served as a second lieutenant of artillery until 1919.  After the war, he spent three years (1919–22) at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a> at the invitation of his uncle—his mother’s brother—Walter Hartridge. It was his first direct encounter with the United States. He discovered the South, where both his parents had been born. He returned to France in 1922, where, after a false start as a painter, he began his career as a French writer, and by 1927 had established himself in the world of French literature.  His career as a major figure of 20th-century French literature began soon after his return from the United States with the novel <i>Mont-Cinère</i> (1926), which was well received by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernanos" title="Bernanos">Georges Bernanos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> 
In July 1940, after France's defeat, he went back to America. In 1942, he was mobilized and sent to New York to work at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Office_of_War_Information" title="United States Office of War Information">United States Office of War Information</a>.  From there, for almost a year, five times a week, he would address France as part of the radio broadcasts of <a href="/wiki/Voice_of_America" title="Voice of America">Voice of America</a>, working <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inter_alia" title="Inter alia">inter alia</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Breton" title="André Breton">André Breton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yul_Brynner" title="Yul Brynner">Yul Brynner</a>.  Green returned to France after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.
</p><p>Julien Green died in Paris shortly before his 98th birthday and is entombed in a chapel designed for him in St. Egid Church, <a href="/wiki/Klagenfurt" title="Klagenfurt">Klagenfurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His name on the tomb uses the original English spelling "Julian" instead of the French "Julien".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remgreen_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-remgreen-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julien_Green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of Green's books focused on the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Faith" title="Faith">faith</a> and <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a> as well as hypocrisy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Several dealt with the southern United States, and he strongly identified with the fate of the Confederacy, characterizing himself throughout his life as a "Sudiste" (southerner). He inherited this version of <a href="/wiki/Patriotism" title="Patriotism">patriotism</a> from his mother, who came from a distinguished southern family. Some years before Julien's birth, when Julien's father was offered a choice of posts (with his bank) in either Germany or France, Julien's mother urged the choice of France on the grounds that the French were "also a proud people, recently defeated in war, and we shall understand one another." The reference was to France's 1871 defeat in the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In France, both during his life and today, Green's reputation rests principally not on his <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novels</a>, but on his <a href="/wiki/Diary" title="Diary">diary</a>, published in nineteen volumes, and spanning the years 1919 to 1998. These volumes provide a chronicle of his literary and religious life, and a unique window on the artistic and literary scene in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> over a span of eighty years. Green's style, austere and employing to great effect the <a href="/wiki/Pass%C3%A9_simple" title="Passé simple">passé simple</a>, a literary tense nearly abandoned by many of his French contemporaries, found favor with the Académie française. Green resigned from the Académie shortly before his death, citing his American heritage and loyalties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remgreen_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-remgreen-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>While Green wrote primarily in the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French language</a>, he also wrote in English. He translated some of his own works from French to English, sometimes with the help of his sister, <a href="/wiki/Anne_Green" title="Anne Green">Anne Green</a>, an author herself. A collection of some of his translations is published in <i>Le langage et son double</i>, with a side-by-side English–French format, facilitating direct comparison. Despite his being bilingual, Green's texts remain largely unknown in the English-speaking world. Thus far three of his books have been turned into films: <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Leviathan_(1962_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Leviathan (1962 film) (page does not exist)">Leviathan</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> (<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leviathan_(film,_1962)" title="fr:Leviathan (film, 1962)">fr</a>)</span></i> (1962), for which he wrote the <a href="/wiki/Screenplay" title="Screenplay">screenplay</a>, is the most famous. <i>Adrienne Mesurat</i> (1953), and<i> La Dame de pique</i> (1965) were also adapted to film.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> His stage play <i>South</i> (<i>Sud</i>, 1953) was adapted for a British television production in 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships_and_legacy">Relationships and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Julien_Green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relationships and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For many years Green was the companion of Robert de Saint-Jean, a journalist, whom he had met in the 1920s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remgreen_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-remgreen-6">[6]</a></sup> In his later years Green formally adopted gay fiction writer <a href="/wiki/%C3%89ric_Jourdan" title="Éric Jourdan">Éric Jourdan</a>. According to Jourdan, Green decided to move to a house which formerly belonged to <a href="/wiki/Caterina_Sforza" title="Caterina Sforza">Caterina Sforza</a> in <a href="/wiki/Forl%C3%AC" title="Forlì">Forlì</a>, Italy, in 1994. However, Green did not move to this house because his health was failing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_fran%C3%A7aise" title="Académie française">Académie française</a>, Green succeeded <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mauriac" title="François Mauriac">François Mauriac</a>, taking chair number 22 on June 3, 1971. In 1996, he resigned from the Académie which caused a minor scandal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remgreen_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-remgreen-6">[6]</a></sup> However, he was only formally replaced upon his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was commonly believed he had dual citizenship, but in fact, although born in Paris and writing almost exclusively in the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French language</a>, he had never become a French citizen. President <a href="/wiki/Georges_Pompidou" title="Georges Pompidou">Georges Pompidou</a> reportedly offered him French citizenship in 1972 but Green declined.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
