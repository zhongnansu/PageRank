<p><b>Operation Greenhouse</b> was the fifth American nuclear test series, the second conducted in 1951 and the first to test principles that would lead to developing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teller-Ullam" title="Teller-Ullam">thermonuclear weapons</a> (<i>hydrogen bombs</i>). Conducted at the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Proving_Ground" title="Pacific Proving Ground">Pacific Proving Ground</a>, specifically on islands of the <a href="/wiki/Enewetak_Atoll" title="Enewetak Atoll">Enewetak Atoll</a>, all of the devices were mounted in large steel towers, to simulate air bursts. This series of nuclear weapons tests was preceded by <a href="/wiki/Operation_Ranger" title="Operation Ranger">Operation Ranger</a> and succeeded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Buster-Jangle" title="Operation Buster-Jangle">Operation Buster-Jangle</a>.
</p><p>Operation Greenhouse showcased new and aggressive designs for nuclear weapons. The main idea was to reduce the size, weight, and most importantly, reduce the amount of fissile material necessary for nuclear weapons, while increasing the destructive power. With the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>'s first nuclear test a year and half earlier, the United States had begun stockpiling the new designs before they were actually proven. Thus the success of Operation Greenhouse was vital before the development of thermonuclear weapons could continue.
</p><p>A number of target buildings, including bunkers, houses and factories were built on Mujinkarikku Islet to test nuclear weapon effects.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="George">George</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: George">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <i>George</i> explosion was the world's first thermonuclear burn, though it was just a test design, unsuitable for weaponization. Shaped like a <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">torus</a>, the <i>George</i> device had a small amount of heavy <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a> (<a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tritium" title="Tritium">tritium</a>) placed at its center. The vast majority of its yield derived from fission. The energy output from the thermonuclear fusion in this test was insignificant in comparison. The "George" device was more like a "boosted" atomic bomb than a thermonuclear one. The small amount of heavy  deuterium and tritium in this test fused, but its role was to generate a strong flurry of fast <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> - ones that sparked more <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">fissions</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> nuclei that were present, and which also caused fission in <a href="/wiki/Uranium-238" title="Uranium-238">uranium-238</a> - which does not fission under bombardment with slow neutrons, as does <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">uranium-235</a>.
</p><p>The George design was a '<a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Teller%E2%80%93Ulam_design" title="History of the Teller–Ulam design">Classical Super</a>' prototype with a binary triggering device using <a href="/wiki/Radiation_implosion" title="Radiation implosion">radiation implosion</a> upon a cylinder. The design of the triggering system in this test was based on the one patented by <a href="/wiki/Klaus_Fuchs" title="Klaus Fuchs">Klaus Fuchs</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Von_Neumann" title="Von Neumann">von Neumann</a> in 1946.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Its success played a vital role in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Teller%E2%80%93Ulam_design" title="History of the Teller–Ulam design">history of the Teller–Ulam design</a>.
</p><p>The <i>George</i> test validated the principles which would be used for the first full-scale thermonuclear bomb test, <i><a href="/wiki/Ivy_Mike" title="Ivy Mike">Ivy Mike</a></i>, one year later, on 1 November 1952, at <a href="/wiki/Enewetak_Atoll" title="Enewetak Atoll">Enewetak Atoll</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Item">Item</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Item">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Conducted on May 25, 1951, <i><a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_Item" title="Greenhouse Item">Item</a></i> was the first test of an actual <a href="/wiki/Boosted_fission_weapon" title="Boosted fission weapon">boosted fission weapon</a>, nearly doubling the normal yield of a similar non-boosted weapon. In this test, deuterium-tritium (D-T) gas was injected into the enriched uranium core of a nuclear fission bomb. The extreme heat of the fissioning bomb produced thermonuclear fusion reactions within the D-T gas. While not enough to be considered a full nuclear fusion bomb, the large number of high-energy neutrons released nearly doubled the efficiency of the nuclear fission reaction.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dog">Dog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Dog</i> explosion is most popularly known for an image taken of those viewing it than the actual explosion itself; the photograph depicts numerous VIPs wearing safety goggles sitting on <a href="/wiki/Adirondack_chair" title="Adirondack chair">Adirondack chairs</a> while being illuminated by the flash of the detonation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This photograph takes up the bottom portion of the cover of the 1995 documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Trinity_and_Beyond" title="Trinity and Beyond">Trinity and Beyond</a></i> by Peter Kuran. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safety_goggles" title="Safety goggles">safety goggles</a> worn by all those viewing the test in the picture have become somewhat of a museum collectors item, with a possibility that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_F._Ramsey" title="Norman F. Ramsey">Norman F. Ramsey</a> may have been present.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Cynthia Miller claims that her father, Van Dine, is the first man on the left in the photo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Blast_wave" title="Blast wave">blast wave</a> safely arrived at the location of the VIPs some 45 seconds after the initially silent flash of the detonation as observed from their position on Parry island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
