<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Rose Fitzgerald Kennedy Greenway</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>The <b>Rose Fitzgerald Kennedy Greenway</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Linear_park" title="Linear park">linear park</a> located in several <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Boston" title="Downtown Boston">Downtown Boston</a> neighborhoods.  It consists of landscaped gardens, promenades, plazas, fountains, art, and specialty lighting systems that stretch over one mile through the <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Boston" title="Chinatown, Boston">Chinatown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Financial_District,_Boston" title="Financial District, Boston">Financial District</a>, Waterfront, and <a href="/wiki/North_End,_Boston" title="North End, Boston">North End</a> neighborhoods.  Officially opened in October 2008, the 17-acre Greenway sits on land created from demolition of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_F._Fitzgerald_Expressway" title="John F. Fitzgerald Expressway">John F. Fitzgerald Expressway</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Big_Dig" title="Big Dig">Big Dig</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Rose Fitzgerald Kennedy Greenway is named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy" title="Rose Fitzgerald Kennedy">Rose Fitzgerald Kennedy</a>, the matriarch of the Kennedy Family who was born in the neighboring North End neighborhood. Her son, Senator <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Edward M. Kennedy</a>, played an important role in establishing the Greenway.
</p><p>The Rose Fitzgerald Kennedy Greenway Conservancy was established as an independently incorporated non-profit organization in 2004 to guide the emerging park system and raise funds for an endowment and operations. In 2008, the State Legislature confirmed the Conservancy as the designated steward of the Rose Kennedy Greenway;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the Conservancy operates with a lease from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Turnpike_Authority" title="Massachusetts Turnpike Authority">Massachusetts Turnpike Authority</a> (now Massachusetts Department of Transportation).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  Since February 2009, the Conservancy has operated the park, leading the maturation of this new civic space, strengthening its physical beauty, and encouraging a sense of a shared community in Boston.
</p><p>The 2008 legislation established a 50%-50% public/private funding model. Through a multi-party funding agreement announced in June 2017, public funds from the State and City will represent ~20% of the operating budget, a new Greenway Business Improvement District will supply ~20% of the operating budget, and the Greenway Conservancy will generate ~60%. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1940s, planning began for a "Highway in the Skies" that would alleviate traffic congestion and provide a direct route for moving goods in and out of Boston. Construction of the elevated Central Artery began in 1951 and was completed in 1959, displacing more than 10,000 residents and demolishing some 1,000 buildings. The limitations of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Artery" title="Central Artery">Central Artery</a> soon became painfully clear, however. In 1991, after almost a decade of planning, construction began on the Central Artery/Tunnel Project, more widely known as the "<a href="/wiki/Big_Dig" title="Big Dig">Big Dig</a>", which was recognized as one of the largest, most complex, and technologically challenging highway projects in the history of the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the elevated highway to be relocated underground, Boston would be rich in prime urban land. Community and political leaders seized the opportunity to enhance Boston's city life by providing additional parks and gardens to connect some of its oldest, most diverse, and vibrant neighborhoods. The creation of the <a href="/wiki/Greenway_(landscape)" title="Greenway (landscape)">Greenway</a> – a joint effort of the Massachusetts Turnpike Authority (MTA), the Commonwealth of Massachusetts, the City of Boston, and various civic groups.
</p><p>On October 4, 2008, tens of thousands of visitors came together for the parks’ Inaugural Celebration with the Rose Kennedy Greenway Conservancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  The following year, on February 23, 2009 the Rose Kennedy Greenway Conservancy assumed operational responsibility for the parks. Today, the Greenway encompasses gardens, plazas, and tree-lined promenades. The Greenway is a key feature of the modern reinvention of Boston, Boston Harbor, the South Boston Waterfront, and the Harbor Islands.  MassDOT's obligation under state law to provide 50% of the funding for the greenway ended in 2012, and was replaced by year-to-year agreements until a proposed six-year agreement in 2017.  This would create a Business Improvement District, and also receive funding from the City of Boston (from a fund established with proceeds from the sale of the Winthrop Square garage) and MassDOT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parks">Parks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Parks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinatown_Park">Chinatown Park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chinatown Park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chinatown_Park,_Boston.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3648" data-file-width="5472" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Chinatown_Park%2C_Boston.JPG/220px-Chinatown_Park%2C_Boston.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Chinatown_Park%2C_Boston.JPG/330px-Chinatown_Park%2C_Boston.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Chinatown_Park%2C_Boston.JPG/440px-Chinatown_Park%2C_Boston.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chinatown_Park,_Boston.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chinatown Park</div></div></div>
<p>Located at the southern end of the Greenway, this one-acre linear park contains design elements drawn from Asian traditions and art work. Designed by Carol R. Johnson and Associates<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/May_Sun" title="May Sun">May Sun</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Chinatown Park has a serpentine walkway edged by bamboo within bright red sculptural elements and a fountain that suggests a waterfall and shallow riverbed.
</p><p>In 2011, the Rose Fitzgerald Kennedy Greenway Conservancy partnered with local community groups, residents, and abutters to add the plaza tables, chairs and shade umbrellas,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> while the City of Boston renovated neighboring Mary Soo Hoo Park to the south. Large festivals, such as the Chinatown Main Street Festival, the August Moon Festival, and Films at the Gate, are hosted in the plaza. In 2016, the PlayCubes children's climbing structure was added to the plaza temporarily as part of the Design Museum Boston's Extraordinary Playscapes exhibit.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dewey_Square_Park">Dewey Square Park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dewey Square Park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dewey_Square_Farmers_Market_Boston.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2432" data-file-width="4320" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Dewey_Square_Farmers_Market_Boston.jpg/220px-Dewey_Square_Farmers_Market_Boston.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Dewey_Square_Farmers_Market_Boston.jpg/330px-Dewey_Square_Farmers_Market_Boston.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Dewey_Square_Farmers_Market_Boston.jpg/440px-Dewey_Square_Farmers_Market_Boston.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dewey_Square_Farmers_Market_Boston.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Farmers market in Dewey Square</div></div></div>
<p>Located between Congress and Summer Streets along Atlantic Avenue, <a href="/wiki/Dewey_Square" title="Dewey Square">Dewey Square Park</a> joins the major transportation hub of <a href="/wiki/South_Station" title="South Station">South Station</a> to the Financial District. The park has gardens, lawn areas, and the adjacent plaza, which was designed to draw in commuters and nearby workers and residents. The tables, chairs, and cluster of food trucks have made Dewey Square Park a popular lunch destination, especially in the warm seasons when the plaza features the twice-weekly Boston Public Market Association’s farmers market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Food truck and other major festivals are hosted here.
</p><p>In 2011, Dewey Square was the site of the <a href="/wiki/Occupy_Boston" title="Occupy Boston">Occupy Boston</a> movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The subsequent redesign of the planting includes pollinator garden, raised beds planted with edibles, a <a href="/wiki/Rain_garden" title="Rain garden">rain garden</a>, and a small orchard; produce harvested from the edible garden is donated to hunger-relief charities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fort_Point_Channel_Parks">Fort Point Channel Parks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fort Point Channel Parks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Fort Point Channel Parks are located between Oliver and Congress Streets along Atlantic Avenue, they are often referred to as the "New American Gardens" for their wide variety of trees and flowers that are often found in gardens of typical New England homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Fort Point Channel Parks, designed by Halvorson Design Associates,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> were planted in 2008 by the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Horticultural_Society" title="Massachusetts Horticultural Society">Massachusetts Horticultural Society</a>, with help from many volunteers including the Massachusetts Master Gardener Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Additional planting and ongoing care are the responsibility of the Greenway Conservancy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wharf_District_Parks">Wharf District Parks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wharf District Parks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wharf_District_Park_fountain,_Boston.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2432" data-file-width="4320" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Wharf_District_Park_fountain%2C_Boston.jpg/220px-Wharf_District_Park_fountain%2C_Boston.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Wharf_District_Park_fountain%2C_Boston.jpg/330px-Wharf_District_Park_fountain%2C_Boston.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Wharf_District_Park_fountain%2C_Boston.jpg/440px-Wharf_District_Park_fountain%2C_Boston.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wharf_District_Park_fountain,_Boston.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rings Fountain in the Wharf District Parks</div></div></div>
<p>The Wharf District Parks connect <a href="/wiki/Faneuil_Hall" title="Faneuil Hall">Faneuil Hall</a> and the Financial District with Boston Harbor. Designed by <a href="/wiki/EDAW" title="EDAW">EDAW</a> and Copley Wolff Design Group,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> the parks contain areas of paved surfaces for active public use and a gathering space for public events known as the Great Room.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  Three open lawn areas, surrounded with plantings, provide informal space for gatherings. During the warmer months, the Wharf District Parks host the Greenway Open Market,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> food vendors, concerts and fitness classes. Large events also occur across the Wharf District Parks, such as the Boston Local Food Fest and the FIGMENT participatory art festival. The Wharf District Parks are home to the Mothers’ Walk, a curving pathway that consists of pavers engraved with names and personal messages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Greenway Carousel at the Tiffany &amp; Co. Foundation Grove, inspired by the imaginations of Boston’s school children, opened on August 31, 2013. Instead of horses, <a href="/wiki/File:Greenway_Carousel_-_Rose_Kennedy_Greenway_-_Boston,_MA_-_20141226_111556.jpg" title="File:Greenway Carousel - Rose Kennedy Greenway - Boston, MA - 20141226 111556.jpg">the hand-carved creatures of this carousel</a> are the wildlife found in and around Boston and its harbor; the animals are carved by local sculptor Jeff Briggs from the drawings of Boston school children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> NY Carousels, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Ride_Entertainment_Group" title="Ride Entertainment Group">Ride Entertainment Group</a> handles the day-to-day operation of the ride in partnership with the Greenway Conservancy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Harbor_Islands_Pavilion">Harbor Islands Pavilion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Harbor Islands Pavilion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>One of the new additions to the Wharf District Parks, the Harbor Islands Pavilion is an open-air structure staffed by National Park Service rangers to welcome visitors and provide information about the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Harbor_Islands_National_Recreation_Area" title="Boston Harbor Islands National Recreation Area">Boston Harbor Islands National Recreation Area</a>. Ferry tickets and Boston Harbor Islands park merchandise are available for purchase at the pavilion. The Pavilion was designed by Utile<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and opened on June 2, 2011<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Armenian_Heritage_Park">Armenian Heritage Park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Armenian Heritage Park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greenway_Armenian_Heritage_Park_2012.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Greenway_Armenian_Heritage_Park_2012.JPG/220px-Greenway_Armenian_Heritage_Park_2012.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Greenway_Armenian_Heritage_Park_2012.JPG/330px-Greenway_Armenian_Heritage_Park_2012.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Greenway_Armenian_Heritage_Park_2012.JPG/440px-Greenway_Armenian_Heritage_Park_2012.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Greenway_Armenian_Heritage_Park_2012.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Armenian Heritage Park</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Heritage_Park" title="Armenian Heritage Park">Armenian Heritage Park</a> is dedicated to the victims of the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Genocide" title="Armenian Genocide">Armenian Genocide</a> and acknowledges the history of Boston as a port of entry for immigrants worldwide, and celebrates those who have migrated to Massachusetts shores and contributed to American life and culture. The Park consists of two key features surrounded by seating, brick paving and landscaping. The Abstract Sculpture, a split dodecahedron, is mounted on a Reflecting Pool, represents the immigrant experience.  <a href="/wiki/File:Boston_Armenian_Heritage_Park_panorama_view_2015.jpg" title="File:Boston Armenian Heritage Park panorama view 2015.jpg">The Labyrinth</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> a circular winding path paved in granite and set in lawn, celebrates life's journey.  The Park and its endowed programs are a key initiative of the Armenian Heritage Foundation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The park opened in May 2012<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The park is maintained by the Rose Kennedy Greenway Conservancy under contract with the Armenian Heritage Fountain.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_End_Parks">North End Parks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: North End Parks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2008_Greenway_Boston_2739303146.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/2008_Greenway_Boston_2739303146.jpg/220px-2008_Greenway_Boston_2739303146.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/2008_Greenway_Boston_2739303146.jpg/330px-2008_Greenway_Boston_2739303146.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/2008_Greenway_Boston_2739303146.jpg/440px-2008_Greenway_Boston_2739303146.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2008_Greenway_Boston_2739303146.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>North End Parks</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/North_End_Parks" title="North End Parks">North End Parks</a> reflect the scale of the adjacent North End neighborhood. Designed by Crosby, Schlessinger, Smallridge LLC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and Gustafson Guthrie Nichol Ltd.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>  lawns surrounded by densely planted perimeter beds are designed to evoke a formal feel of past European style gardens with boxwood hedges enclosing an array of perennials. Along the eastern edge, a pergola covers a long "front porch" that provides both a place for sitting and an overlook for the lawn and the historic architecture beyond.  A shallow water "canal" runs the length with vertical water jets adding a fountain like scene.  This fountain reflects a period approximately a century ago when a canal connected the harbor to now defunct industrial operations. During the warmer months, the North End Parks host numerous free fitness classes and other events.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_use">Land use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Land use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Quincy_Market_and_Parcel_12,_Boston.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Quincy_Market_and_Parcel_12%2C_Boston.JPG/220px-Quincy_Market_and_Parcel_12%2C_Boston.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Quincy_Market_and_Parcel_12%2C_Boston.JPG/330px-Quincy_Market_and_Parcel_12%2C_Boston.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Quincy_Market_and_Parcel_12%2C_Boston.JPG/440px-Quincy_Market_and_Parcel_12%2C_Boston.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Quincy_Market_and_Parcel_12,_Boston.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Greenway open space and development parcels</div></div></div>
<p>Big Dig planners debated the mix between open space and buildings on the land to be freed up by the removal of the elevated highway. Proposals in 1985 by the state and in 1988 by the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Society_of_Architects" title="Boston Society of Architects">Boston Society of Architects</a> suggested that most of the land should be used for mid-rise buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luberoff_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Luberoff-36">[36]</a></sup> But in 1991, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Redevelopment_Authority" title="Boston Redevelopment Authority">Boston Redevelopment Authority's</a> "Boston 2000" plan called for 75% of the land "to be developed as a series of parks and urban plazas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1991 environmental certificate for the project adopted this idea, mandating that not more than 25% of the surface could be developed, with the rest maintained as public open space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlassAct_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-GlassAct-39">[39]</a></sup> The certificate specifically required that three parcels near South Station, encompassing about four acres of land, be turned over to the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Horticultural_Society" title="Massachusetts Horticultural Society">Massachusetts Horticultural Society (MassHort)</a> for outdoor gardens and an enclosed winter garden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luberoff_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Luberoff-36">[36]</a></sup> And the certificate required that the three ramps at Parcels 6, 12, and 18 be covered "to mitigate their impacts on the surface environment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luberoff_36-2"><a href="#cite_note-Luberoff-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2001 Central Artery Corridor Master Plan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> followed the 1991 mandate by designating specific parcels for open space or for development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> A map showed the location of each open space and development parcel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Parcels 19, 21, and 22 were assigned to MassHort. The three parcels containing highway ramps were designated for development.  All of the other parcels directly above the underground highway from Parcel 17 north to Parcel 7 were designated as parkland.  All of the parcels not located directly above the new tunnel were designated for development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>A surface road carrying local traffic existed underneath most of the length of the elevated Central Artery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>  Big Dig plans called for the creation of a pair of one-way surface roads extending the length of the Greenway, to handle local traffic. Greenway parks and development sites would be bordered by these surface roads as well as by cross streets separating each parcel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Massachusetts_Horticultural_Society_parcels">Massachusetts Horticultural Society parcels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Massachusetts Horticultural Society parcels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1991, the Turnpike Authority authorized the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Horticultural_Society" title="Massachusetts Horticultural Society">Massachusetts Horticultural Society (MassHort)</a> to develop gardens and an indoor "Garden Under Glass" on Big Dig Parcels 19, 21, and 22, near <a href="/wiki/Dewey_Square" title="Dewey Square">Dewey Square</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GlassAct_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-GlassAct-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>  But MassHort was unable to raise sufficient funds for the $100 million project, and cancelled it in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>  The parcels were then turned over to the Greenway Conservancy, which created Dewey Square Park (Parcel 22) and Fort Point Channel Parks (Parcels 19 &amp; 21).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_park_parcels">Other park parcels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other park parcels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Parcels 8 and 10 became the <a href="/wiki/North_End_Parks" title="North End Parks">North End Parks</a>.  Parcel 13 became the <a href="/wiki/Armenian_Heritage_Park" title="Armenian Heritage Park">Armenian Heritage Park</a>.  Parcels 14 through 17 became the Wharf District Parks.  Parcel 23D became the Chinatown Park.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ramp_parcels">Ramp parcels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ramp parcels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At three locations along the Greenway, ramps to and from the underground highway occupy some of the surface.  The Big Dig's 1991 environmental approval certificate committed the state to cover the ramps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Luberoff_36-3"><a href="#cite_note-Luberoff-36">[36]</a></sup> Nonprofit organizations were designated to develop buildings on the three sites, and the Massachusetts Legislature authorized $31 million to help pay the costs of building on top of the ramps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> However, all three proposals were abandoned due to high costs between 2009 and 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conti_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conti-51">[51]</a></sup>  In 2014, the Boston Redevelopment Authority initiated a review of the three parcels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Preliminary studies have proposed landscaping and partial coverage of some of the ramps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Big_Dig_Parcel_6_Panorama,_Boston.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3181" data-file-width="8810" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Big_Dig_Parcel_6_Panorama%2C_Boston.jpg/300px-Big_Dig_Parcel_6_Panorama%2C_Boston.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Big_Dig_Parcel_6_Panorama%2C_Boston.jpg/450px-Big_Dig_Parcel_6_Panorama%2C_Boston.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Big_Dig_Parcel_6_Panorama%2C_Boston.jpg/600px-Big_Dig_Parcel_6_Panorama%2C_Boston.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Big_Dig_Parcel_6_Panorama,_Boston.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Panoramic view of Parcel 6</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Parcel_6">Parcel 6</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Parcel 6">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/File:I-93_tunnels_pass_under_the_Rose_Kennedy_Greenway.jpg" title="File:I-93 tunnels pass under the Rose Kennedy Greenway.jpg">Parcel 6</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> is located at the northern end of the Greenway, adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/North_End_Parks" title="North End Parks">North End Parks</a>.  The entire site is covered by highway ramps. In 2003, the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Young_Men%27s_Christian_Association" title="Boston Young Men's Christian Association">YMCA of Greater Boston</a> was designated to build a community center and gym on the parcel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The estimated cost of the building, designed by <a href="/wiki/Childs_Bertman_Tseckares_Inc." title="Childs Bertman Tseckares Inc.">Childs Bertman Tseckares</a>, was $70 million.  In 2011, the YMCA abandoned its plans for the site, citing the high cost of building over the active highway ramps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vision_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Vision-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Parcel_12">Parcel 12</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Parcel 12">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/File:Big_Dig_Parcel_12_Panorama_2009.jpg" title="File:Big Dig Parcel 12 Panorama 2009.jpg">Parcel 12</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>  is located just south of the <a href="/wiki/North_End_Parks" title="North End Parks">North End Parks</a>.  In 2005, <a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Museum" title="The Boston Museum">The Boston Museum</a> was awarded the rights to build an $89 million museum designed by <a href="/wiki/Moshe_Safdie" title="Moshe Safdie">Moshe Safdie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>  The Museum gave up its plans in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vision_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Vision-57">[57]</a></sup>  It made two unsuccessful bids for Parcel 9,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> one of the development parcels, before dissolving in 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>  The Museum's first proposal for Parcel 9 also included a plan to build a pedestrian bridge extending the length of Parcel 12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/File:Big_Dig_Parcel_12_fence,_Boston,_2016.JPG" title="File:Big Dig Parcel 12 fence, Boston, 2016.JPG">Fencing keeps pedestrians off of the ramp structure of Parcel 12.</a> The fencing on the west side of the ramps has been used by the Greenway Conservancy as the supporting structure for several temporary art exhibits, including "MAY THIS NEVER END",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> a 4 foot (1.2 m) by 319 foot (97 m) work by Matthew Hoffman that was installed in 2016.
</p><p>A seasonal <a href="/wiki/Zip-line" title="Zip-line">zip-line</a> opened on Parcel 12 in June 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Parcel_18">Parcel 18</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Parcel 18">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/File:Boston_cityscape_(5238285410).jpg" title="File:Boston cityscape (5238285410).jpg">Parcel 18</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> is located between the Wharf District Parks and the Fort Point Channel Parks.  In 2004, The New Center for Arts and Culture proposed construction of a $40 million art and cultural center to be designed by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Libeskind" title="Daniel Libeskind">Daniel Libeskind</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>  The center was designated as the developer of this site, but abandoned the plans in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conti_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-Conti-51">[51]</a></sup>  Unlike Parcels 6 and 12, Parcel 18 has a relatively small portion of its area devoted to the highway ramps.  The rest of the parcel <a href="/wiki/File:Big_Dig_Parcel_18_pedestrian_path,_Boston,_2016.JPG" title="File:Big Dig Parcel 18 pedestrian path, Boston, 2016.JPG">was intensively landscaped</a>, and has a pedestrian walkway that extends its full length.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_development_parcels">Other development parcels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other development parcels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All of the parcels in the <a href="/wiki/Bulfinch_Triangle_Historic_District" title="Bulfinch Triangle Historic District">Bulfinch Triangle</a> (Parcels 1, 1a/b/c, 2, and 2a/b) at the northern end of the Greenway are being developed with mixed-use, predominantly residential buildings, including The Victor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> Parcel 1B,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> One Canal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> and Avenir Apartments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> As part of the Big Dig, a mixed use structure was built on Parcel 7<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> that contains a ventilation tower for the tunnel as well as a parking garage, state agency offices, part of the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haymarket_(MBTA_station)" title="Haymarket (MBTA station)">Haymarket MBTA Station</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Public_Market" title="Boston Public Market">Boston Public Market</a>. Development of a hotel and market building on Parcel 9,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parcel9_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parcel9-77">[77]</a></sup> adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Haymarket_-_Boston" title="Haymarket - Boston">the Haymarket produce market</a>, was approved in June 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>  No action has been taken on Parcel 11a or Parcel 11b;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> the latter is used as a parking lot for city employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> Parcel 24, near the southern end of the Greenway, is the site of the mixed-income residential building One Greenway,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> which opened in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>  Parcels 25, 26, and 27, at the southern end of the tunnel, were the subject of unsuccessful requests for proposals in 2004<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> and in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Plans to develop the land, now called the South Bay Kneeland Street Parcels,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> were revived in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_art">Public art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Public art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>
A permanent installation, Harbor Fog,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> by Boston artist <a href="/wiki/Ross_Miller_(artist)" title="Ross Miller (artist)">Ross Miller</a>, is located in the Wharf District Parks. </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Harbor_fog_backlit_sq.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="3000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ef/Harbor_fog_backlit_sq.jpg/220px-Harbor_fog_backlit_sq.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ef/Harbor_fog_backlit_sq.jpg/330px-Harbor_fog_backlit_sq.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ef/Harbor_fog_backlit_sq.jpg/440px-Harbor_fog_backlit_sq.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Harbor_fog_backlit_sq.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Harbor Fog water feature sculpture</div></div></div>
<p>The Conservancy has a program to install rotating exhibitions of contemporary public art on the Greenway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Echelman_Boston_2015_from_above.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2432" data-file-width="4320" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Echelman_Boston_2015_from_above.jpg/220px-Echelman_Boston_2015_from_above.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Echelman_Boston_2015_from_above.jpg/330px-Echelman_Boston_2015_from_above.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Echelman_Boston_2015_from_above.jpg/440px-Echelman_Boston_2015_from_above.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Echelman_Boston_2015_from_above.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Janet Echelman aerial installation, "As If It Were Already Here"</div></div></div>
<p>Commissions by the Greenway Conservancy have won 7 awards from Americans for the Arts’ Public Art Network, among the most in the country since 2012. Annually, PAN recognizes the best 50 new public art installations and has recognized Os Gemeos, “The Giant of Boston” (2012); Shinique Smith, “Seven Moon Junction” (2014); Kyu Seok Oh, “Wandering Sheep” (2015); Matthew Hoffman, “MAY THIS NEVER END” (2016); Mehdi Ghadyanloo, “Spaces Of Hope” (2017); Meredith James, “Far from this setting in which I now find myself” (2017); and Mark Reigelman, “The Meeting House” (2017).
</p><p>A series of temporary murals are rotated each fall on the 70' x 76' wall of the air intake structure overlooking Dewey Square Park. In 2012, Brazilian twin brothers Otavio and Gustavo Pandolfo (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Os_G%C3%AAmeos" title="Os Gêmeos">Os Gêmeos</a>) painted a mural on the wall, in conjunction with their first solo exhibition in the United States at Boston’s <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Contemporary_Art,_Boston" title="Institute of Contemporary Art, Boston">Institute of Contemporary Art</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> In fall 2013, a new mural, "Remanence: Salt and Light (Part II)" by <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Ritchie" title="Matthew Ritchie">Matthew Ritchie</a> replaced the Os Gemeos mural.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The third mural on the Greenway Wall was "Seven Moon Junction"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Shinique_Smith" title="Shinique Smith">Shinique Smith</a>, installed in 2014.  "A TRANSLATION FROM ONE LANGUAGE TO ANOTHER"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Weiner" title="Lawrence Weiner">Lawrence Weiner</a> was installed on the Greenway Wall in 2015 and remained through September 2016. "Spaces of Hope," by Iranian artist <a href="/wiki/Mehdi_Ghadyanloo" title="Mehdi Ghadyanloo">Mehdi Ghadyanloo</a> was installed on the wall in October 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> In May 2018, 
Shara Hughes’ “Carving Out Fresh Options” was installed on the Greenway Wall.
</p><p>In 2015, the Conservancy commissioned the installation of a work called "As If It Were Already Here"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Janet_Echelman" title="Janet Echelman">Janet Echelman</a>. The installation was attached to three adjacent high-rise buildings, so that it was suspended above the Fort Point Channel parks.
</p><p>An exhibit of sculptures called "Circle of Animals/Zodiac Heads,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> by Chinese artist and activist <a href="/wiki/Ai_Weiwei" title="Ai Weiwei">Ai Weiwei</a>, was installed in April 2016 at the Rings Fountain in the Wharf District Parks, and remained on exhibit until October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, an exhibit of 8 historic neon signs from across Massachusetts is on exhibit. Numerous other works are also exhibited.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_programs">Public programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Public programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rose_Kennedy_Greenway_fog_P1010925.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3648" data-file-width="5472" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Rose_Kennedy_Greenway_fog_P1010925.jpg/220px-Rose_Kennedy_Greenway_fog_P1010925.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Rose_Kennedy_Greenway_fog_P1010925.jpg/330px-Rose_Kennedy_Greenway_fog_P1010925.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Rose_Kennedy_Greenway_fog_P1010925.jpg/440px-Rose_Kennedy_Greenway_fog_P1010925.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rose_Kennedy_Greenway_fog_P1010925.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boston Local Food Festival, 2017</div></div></div><p>Public programs and events on the Greenway, organized by the Greenway Conservancy in partnership with cultural institutions and businesses, have included art festivals, food truck competitions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> concerts, markets, and more, and are geared toward multi-generational and multi-cultural audiences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup>  Trackable visitation of the Greenway has increased from 96,000 visitors in 2009<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> to 800,000 in 2013<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> and to 1.38 million in 2016;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> many additional visitors enjoy the fountains, gardens, public art, and pathways. A free Wi-Fi network running the length of the park was added by the Conservancy in 2010.
</p><p>Over 400 free events are held on the Greenway annually. Recurring events include the FIGMENT participatory art festival in July,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-112"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> the Boston Local Food Festival in September,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup> the Greenway Open Market from May through early October,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> World Labyrinth Day in May,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup> the Berklee College of Music Summer Concerts at the Greenway,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Coolidge_Corner_Theatre" title="Coolidge Corner Theatre">Coolidge Corner Theatre's</a> "Coolidge at the Greenway" movie series,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> and the "Greenway Mobile Eats" program,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> which has brought an increasing number of food trucks to sites along the Greenway since 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Conservancy has partnered in 2017 and 2018 with the <a href="/wiki/Trillium_Brewing_Company" title="Trillium Brewing Company">Trillium Brewing Company</a> on a seasonal outdoor <a href="/wiki/Beer_garden" title="Beer garden">beer garden</a>, located at Atlantic Avenue at High Street on the Greenway, with a half-dozen beers on tap and Westport Rivers Winery on offer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> A second beer garden, operated by Downeast Cider in partnership with the Greenway Conservancy, opened for the 2018 season on Dewey Square Plaza on the Greenway.
</p><p><br/>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_horticulture">Sustainable horticulture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Sustainable horticulture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Conservancy uses organic and sustainable landscape management programs to maintain the parks and features of the Greenway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> The Greenway is Boston’s only organically maintained Public Park and one of a handful of organically maintained urban parks in the United States. Organic maintenance means no expenditures for toxic chemicals, and lower expenditures for watering. Plants are healthier, more resilient, and better able to withstand the wear of public use. The Conservancy’s practice of using composting and compost tea instead of herbicides and toxins also ensures that run-off from the parks will not pollute Boston Harbor or harm the delicate marine life. Children and pets can freely and safely play on the park lawns without the worry of pesticides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup>
</p>
