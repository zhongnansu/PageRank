<p><b>Little Sugar Creek Greenway</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Linear_park" title="Linear park">linear park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stream_restoration" title="Stream restoration">stream restoration</a> project in <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg_County,_North_Carolina" title="Mecklenburg County, North Carolina">Mecklenburg County, North Carolina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-connects_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-connects-1">[1]</a></sup> When completed it will consist of twenty miles of trails and paved walkways running from <a href="/wiki/Cordelia_Park_(Charlotte,_North_Carolina)" title="Cordelia Park (Charlotte, North Carolina)">Cordelia Park</a> just north of <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_center_city" title="Charlotte center city">uptown Charlotte</a>, then south through midtown <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte</a>, and continuing all the way to the <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> state line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sugarcreek_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-sugarcreek-2">[2]</a></sup> The Little Sugar Creek Greenway will be a key part of the Carolina Thread Trail, a regionwide network of trails that will ultimately pass through 15 counties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Greenway_(landscape)" title="Greenway (landscape)">Greenways</a> are narrow strips of land, planted and managed to provide both human recreation and wildlife habitat.  Greenways along streams, such as Little Sugar Creek, improve water quality and help control flooding.   There are about 37 miles of developed greenways in Mecklenburg County, of which this is only one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenways_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenways-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The two urban sections of the Little Sugar Creek Greenway were championed by Central Piedmont Community College president Tony Zeiss and together provide five unbroken miles of paved walkway from East 7th Street southward to Brandywine Road.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artsy_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-artsy-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-map_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-map-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Sugar_Creek_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Little_Sugar_Creek">Little Sugar Creek</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Sugar_Creek_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Little Sugar Creek">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike many large cities, Charlotte is not sited on or near a sea, lake, or significant river; its hydrogeography is based on small streams and creeks, Little Sugar Creek being perhaps the most prominent. The name of Little Sugar Creek (which has also been called Sugar Creek, a name that has also been applied to what is now called Irwin Creek) derives from the Sugaree tribe indigenous to the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsom_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newsom-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Running through some of Charlotte's oldest neighborhoods, over time parts of the stream became hidden by houses, factories, parking lots, <a href="/wiki/Riprap" title="Riprap">riprap</a>, highways, and culverts. Over time, Little Sugar Creek also became very polluted by runoff from factories and sewers, and litter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsom_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newsom-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Little_Sugar_Creek_flooding_on_12-31-15.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="1080" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Little_Sugar_Creek_flooding_on_12-31-15.jpg/220px-Little_Sugar_Creek_flooding_on_12-31-15.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Little_Sugar_Creek_flooding_on_12-31-15.jpg/330px-Little_Sugar_Creek_flooding_on_12-31-15.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Little_Sugar_Creek_flooding_on_12-31-15.jpg/440px-Little_Sugar_Creek_flooding_on_12-31-15.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Little_Sugar_Creek_flooding_on_12-31-15.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Controlled flooding on Little Sugar Creek in December 2015.  In the 1990s floods caused extensive damage along the Sugar Creek watershed.</div></div></div>
<p>As recently as 2000 Little Sugar Creek was "the most polluted stream in Charlotte-Mecklenburg, a fetid stew of runoff and industrial waste trickling beneath parking decks and buildings on the fringe of downtown."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artsy_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-artsy-5">[5]</a></sup> Businesses dumped waste straight into its water, the fumes so noxious they were said to peel the paint off nearby buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sweet_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sweet-8">[8]</a></sup> Moreover, flood-prone buildings had to be razed, and much of the stream was covered by concrete which had to be removed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stormwater_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-stormwater-9">[9]</a></sup> The pollution problem began to be solved when straight-piping of wastes was outlawed in 1998; nevertheless Little Sugar Creek Greenway became the most expensive stream restoration in Mecklenburg county.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sweet_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-sweet-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Greenway">The Greenway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Sugar_Creek_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Greenway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1968 Charlotte city council member Jerry Tuttle, inspired by <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio_River_Walk" title="San Antonio River Walk">San Antonio's River Walk</a>, proposed an urban "Charlotte waterfront" on Sugar Creek.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artsy_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-artsy-5">[5]</a></sup>  In 1974 the Mecklenburg County Park and Recreation Commission first studied the feasibility of a greenway system.  A master plan was created in 1980 involving 20 creeks and streams.  The final report was based on the graduate thesis for Joan Sigmon, a geography major at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Charlotte" title="University of North Carolina at Charlotte">University of North Carolina at Charlotte</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-landmark_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-landmark-10">[10]</a></sup> The idea of a comprehensive greenway system was also promoted by Mecklenburg county commissioner Tom Ray who authored the concept known as "Sugar Creek Projection 70".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Demographics provided further impetus for preserving green space in the center city.  Bucking the general trend, Charlotte experienced growth in the central core while other cities were losing population to the suburbs.  As a result "The Mecklenburg County Parks and Recreation Department embarked on an ambitious update to its earlier 1980 and 1991 Greenways Plans to not only extend and create new greenway corridors, but also improve established corridors."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Finally, in 1999 a bond plan was approved; the money was used to buy property along the stream bed, begin construction or paved walkways, restore <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>, build <a href="/wiki/Water_garden" title="Water garden">water gardens</a>, and generally launch a major stream restoration project. In the end, the urban section of the Little Sugar Creek Greenway cost $43 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sugarcreek_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-sugarcreek-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artsy_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-artsy-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the master plan remained the guideline, little was done until 2000 when a 10-year growth plan was formulated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sweet_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-sweet-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Department_of_Transportation" title="North Carolina Department of Transportation">North Carolina Department of Transportation</a> announced the completion of the Charlotte portion of the Little Sugar Creek Greenway at a celebration on April 24, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdot_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-ncdot-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Finished_sections">Finished sections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Sugar_Creek_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Finished sections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The completed part of the Little Sugar Creek Greenway can be divided into four sections going downstream from north to south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fourgreens_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-fourgreens-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Cordelia Park to 12th Street</dt>
<dd>Paved walkway follows Little Sugar Creek and extends for .95 miles from Parkwood Avenue on the southern border of <a href="/wiki/Cordelia_Park_(Charlotte,_North_Carolina)" title="Cordelia Park (Charlotte, North Carolina)">Cordelia Park</a> to Greenway Crescent Lane and East 12th street just south of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Street_Park_(Charlotte,_North_Carolina)" title="Alexander Street Park (Charlotte, North Carolina)">Alexander Street Park</a>.  Gulf Coast <a href="/wiki/Spiny_softshell_turtle" title="Spiny softshell turtle">spiny softshell turtles</a> can be seen in this part of the creek.  This section does not currently connect with the rest of the greenway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></dd></dl>

<dl><dt>East 7th Street to Morehead Street</dt>
<dd>The "urban section" of the greenway runs for 1.29 miles along Sugar Creek in uptown Charlotte and was completed in April 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-finished_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-finished-16">[16]</a></sup>  It begins at 7th Street, goes southward past <a href="/wiki/Central_Piedmont_Community_College" title="Central Piedmont Community College">Central Piedmont Community College</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> passes through <a href="/wiki/Thompson_Park_(Charlotte,_North_Carolina)" title="Thompson Park (Charlotte, North Carolina)">Thompson Park</a>, connects to the showpiece <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Park_(Charlotte,_North_Carolina)" title="Midtown Park (Charlotte, North Carolina)">Midtown Park</a> at Pearl Park Way,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-midtown_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-midtown-18">[18]</a></sup> and ends at the Morehead Street underpass.  It is the most highly developed section of the greenway and incorporates paved walkways, stonework walls and plazas, fountains, dining, many newly planted trees, large decorative rocks, gardens, public event areas, numerous statues and sculptures, and a clock tower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> There are overpasses and underpasses to facilitate unimpeded walking and biking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Included also are the usual park amenities such as benches, bike racks, restrooms, decorative lampposts, and a snack stand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jack_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-jack-21">[21]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Liz_Hair_Nature_Walk_1,_Charlotte,_NC.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="188" data-file-width="250" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Liz_Hair_Nature_Walk_1%2C_Charlotte%2C_NC.jpg/220px-Liz_Hair_Nature_Walk_1%2C_Charlotte%2C_NC.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c7/Liz_Hair_Nature_Walk_1%2C_Charlotte%2C_NC.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Liz_Hair_Nature_Walk_1,_Charlotte,_NC.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Liz Hair Nature Walk</div></div></div>
<dl><dt>Morehead Street to Brandywine Road</dt>
<dd>This section connects with the urban section through the Morehead Street underpass.  The first part going south is a straight section directly on the banks of Sugar Creek known as the Liz Hair Nature Walk, completed in September 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  It extends for .65 miles from Morehead Street to East Boulevard and is adjacent to the <a href="/wiki/Carolinas_Medical_Center" title="Carolinas Medical Center">Carolinas Medical Center</a> complex.  The next section runs from East Boulevard to Princeton Avenue, passing by the <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Place#Charlotte_Nature_Museum" title="Discovery Place">Charlotte Nature Museum</a> and through <a href="/wiki/Freedom_Park_(Charlotte,_North_Carolina)" title="Freedom Park (Charlotte, North Carolina)">Freedom Park</a>.  Next is a so-called overland connector which utilizes existing sidewalk for .42 miles along Jameston Drive.  The paved part of the greenway resumes again at Hillside Avenue and continues on to Brandywine Road, ending behind the <a href="/wiki/Park_Road_Shopping_Center" title="Park Road Shopping Center">Park Road Shopping Center</a>.  This last section features wetland filtration ponds and explanatory signage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Little_Sugar_Creek_Greenway_in_Huntingtowne_Farms_Park,_Charlotte,_NC.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="188" data-file-width="250" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Little_Sugar_Creek_Greenway_in_Huntingtowne_Farms_Park%2C_Charlotte%2C_NC.jpg/220px-Little_Sugar_Creek_Greenway_in_Huntingtowne_Farms_Park%2C_Charlotte%2C_NC.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/Little_Sugar_Creek_Greenway_in_Huntingtowne_Farms_Park%2C_Charlotte%2C_NC.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Little_Sugar_Creek_Greenway_in_Huntingtowne_Farms_Park,_Charlotte,_NC.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Greenway trail in Huntingtowne Farms Park</div></div></div>
<dl><dt>Huntingtowne Farms Park Section</dt>
<dd>The southernmost existing section of the Little Sugar Creek Greenway is .79 miles long and is almost entirely contained in <a href="/wiki/Huntingtowne_Farms_Park_(Charlotte,_North_Carolina)" title="Huntingtowne Farms Park (Charlotte, North Carolina)">Huntingtowne Farms Park</a>.  It is more than 5 miles from the rest of the completed greenway.  This part of the greenway known as a haven for hawks and <a href="/wiki/Barred_owl" title="Barred owl">barred owls</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reviews">Reviews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Sugar_Creek_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reviews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gene_Conti" title="Gene Conti">Gene Conti</a> of the North Carolina Department of Transportation praised the greenway "for providing Charlotteans with an accessible place to run, walk and bike," and called it "an example for the rest of the state."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ncdot_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-ncdot-13">[13]</a></sup>  The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charlotte_Observer" title="Charlotte Observer">Charlotte Observer</a> called the Little Sugar Creek Greenway a "sweet resurrection".  Fish have returned to the stream; and <a href="/wiki/Duck" title="Duck">ducks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canada_geese" title="Canada geese">Canada geese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Heron" title="Heron">herons</a> are often seen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artsy_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-artsy-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maschal_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-maschal-25">[25]</a></sup>  The mixture of natural restoration and urban redevelopment draws "gushing praise"—although some people, such as Mecklenburg County commissioner Bill James, have criticized the project for its cost.  James compared the project to proposed uptown baseball stadiums, asserting that the county overpaid for land for stream restoration and helped "well-connected developers". However, one business owner said that sites along the greenway offer the "best view of Charlotte from Charlotte—a view that will remain unencumbered forever."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sweet_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-sweet-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite being a vast improvement over the toxic pollution of the past, trash–particularly plastic bags-remains a problem along Sugar Creek. In 2015 Richard Maschal of the Charlotte Observer reported, "The creek is festooned with such detritus, particularly near the bridges spanning Morehead Street and Kings Drive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maschal_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-maschal-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">Activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Little_Sugar_Creek_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Since 2011, the annual <i>Kings Drive Art Walk</i> has been held on the greenway between Midtown Park and Morehead Street.  It features artists and craftspeople and is held in late April.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-artwalk_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-artwalk-28">[28]</a></sup></li>
<li>Every Thursday evening starting in late April, MetLive presents <i>Music on the Greenway</i> on the nearby Metlive stage at 1224 Metropolitan Avenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>The popular <i>Kings Drive Farmers Market</i>, open from April to October, is right by the greenway at S Kings Drive and E Morehead Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li></ul>
