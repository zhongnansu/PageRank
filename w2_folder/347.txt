<p><b>The Greens</b> (French: <i lang="fr">Les Verts</i>, <small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[le vɛʁ]</a></span>; VEC or LV) was a <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecology_movement" title="Ecology movement">ecologist</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AnttiroikoMälkiä2007_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-AnttiroikoMälkiä2007-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_France" title="List of political parties in France">political party in France</a>. The Greens had been in existence since 1984, but their spiritual roots could be traced as far back as <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Dumont" title="René Dumont">René Dumont</a>'s <a href="/wiki/French_presidential_election,_1974" title="French presidential election, 1974">candidacy for the presidency in 1974</a>. On 13 November 2010, The Greens merged with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europe_%C3%89cologie" title="Europe Écologie">Europe Ecology</a> to become <a href="/wiki/Europe_Ecology_%E2%80%93_The_Greens" title="Europe Ecology – The Greens">Europe Ecology – The Greens</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lansford2014_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lansford2014-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_(France)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_(France)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 1974, the environmentalist movement has been a permanent feature of the French political scene, contesting every election: municipal, national &amp; European.
</p><p>In the years following Dumont's challenge for the presidency, and prior to the formal confirmation of les Verts as political party, environmentalists contested elections under such banners as <i>Ecology 78</i>, <i>Ecology Europe</i> and <i>Ecology Today</i>. When, in 1982, <i>the Ecologist Party</i> merged with <i>the Ecologist Confederation</i>, les Verts were born. Under the ideological guidance of <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Waechter" title="Antoine Waechter">Antoine Waechter</a>, the party in 1986 signalled a break with the traditional divide in French politics, declaring that environmental politics could not be "married" to either the left or the right (which gave rise to its famous slogan "ni droite, ni gauche" – "neither right, nor left"). Antoine Waechter ran in the <a href="/wiki/French_presidential_election,_1988" title="French presidential election, 1988">1988 presidential elections</a>, capturing 1,150,000 ballots (or 3.8%) in the first round of voting. But the major breakthrough came the following year when – again under the leadership of Waechter – the Greens polled 10.6% in the European parliamentary elections.
</p><p>However, the party faced with another ecologist party: <a href="/wiki/Ecology_Generation" title="Ecology Generation">Ecology Generation</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Brice_Lalonde" title="Brice Lalonde">Brice Lalonde</a>, environment minister of President <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mitterrand" title="François Mitterrand">François Mitterrand</a> and allied with the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(France)" title="Socialist Party (France)">Socialist Party</a> (PS). In this, if the ecologist parties benefited from the electoral decline of the PS in the beginning of the 1990s, the Greens competed for the leadership of the French ecologist movement. In the <a href="/wiki/French_regional_elections,_1992" title="French regional elections, 1992">1992 regional elections</a>, the Greens obtained 6.8% of votes and the presidency of <a href="/wiki/Nord-Pas-de-Calais" title="Nord-Pas-de-Calais">Nord-Pas-de-Calais</a> region. The next year, it scored 4.1% in the <a href="/wiki/French_legislative_election,_1993" title="French legislative election, 1993">legislative election</a> while all of the ecologist votes represented 11%. But, without political allies in the second round, they failed to gain a parliamentary seat.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Participation_in_government">Participation in government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_(France)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Participation in government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Waechter's influence was called into question in 1994 when the Greens decided to break with his policy of non-alignment, instead deciding to adopt a markedly left-wing stance. The move prompted Waechter to leave the Greens. He went on to found the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Ecological_Movement" title="Independent Ecological Movement">Independent Ecological Movement</a>. In the following presidential election of 1995, <a href="/wiki/Dominique_Voynet" title="Dominique Voynet">Dominique Voynet</a> polled a modest 3.8% but, in due to the marginalisation of <a href="/wiki/Ecology_Generation" title="Ecology Generation">Ecology Generation</a>, the Greens captured the leadership into the family of the French political ecology.
</p><p>Component of <a href="/wiki/Plural_Left" title="Plural Left">Plural Left</a> coalition, the Greens obtained for the first time a parliamentary representation in <a href="/wiki/French_legislative_election,_1997" title="French legislative election, 1997">1997</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dominique_Voynet" title="Dominique Voynet">Dominique Voynet</a> was to lead the party into government for the first time, joining <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Jospin" title="Lionel Jospin">Lionel Jospin</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(France)" title="Socialist Party (France)">Socialist Party</a> (PS) and the <a href="/wiki/French_Communist_Party" title="French Communist Party">Communist Party</a> (PCF). Voynet was rewarded with the cabinet position of Minister for the Environment and Regional Planning, before being replaced by <a href="/wiki/Yves_Cochet" title="Yves Cochet">Yves Cochet</a> in 2001.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Cohn-Bendit" title="Daniel Cohn-Bendit">Daniel Cohn-Bendit</a> (or "Danny the Red"), a leader of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/May_1968_in_France" title="May 1968 in France">1968 student uprising</a>, spearheaded the party's <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1999_(France)" title="European Parliament election, 1999 (France)">1999 European campaign</a>, obtaining 9.7% of votes cast, enough to return seven deputies to <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Alain_Lipietz" title="Alain Lipietz">Alain Lipietz</a> was first selected to represent the Greens in the <a href="/wiki/French_presidential_election,_2002" title="French presidential election, 2002">2002 presidential elections</a> but his public outings proved awkward and he was soon replaced by <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Mam%C3%A8re" title="Noël Mamère">Noël Mamère</a> who had initially lost the <a href="/wiki/Primary_election" title="Primary election">primary elections</a>. Mamère's 5.25% represents the strongest Green challenge for the presidency to date. However, the legislative elections were a major disappointment: with just 4.51% of votes cast nationally, the Greens’ representation fell from six to just three deputies (out of a total of 577) in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_National_Assembly" title="French National Assembly">National Assembly</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Greens_today">The Greens today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_(France)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Greens today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the return to opposition benches in 2002, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gilles_Lemaire&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gilles Lemaire (page does not exist)">Gilles Lemaire</a> assumed the position of national secretary. His tenure is marked by a period of internal strife in the party. Lemaire was in turn replaced by <a href="/wiki/Yann_Wehrling" title="Yann Wehrling">Yann Wehrling</a>, who seemingly united a majority of the membership under a text outlining the future direction that the party hoped to pursue. He was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9cile_Duflot" title="Cécile Duflot">Cécile Duflot</a> in 2006, who was the party's youngest National Secretary at the age of 31. She announced her resignation in May 2012 after being appointed to the new cabinet appointed by President <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Hollande" title="François Hollande">François Hollande</a>.
</p><p>Les Verts had six <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">MEPs</a> elected in the 2004 European Election with 8.43% of the vote.
</p><p>In the hugely divisive 2005 referendum on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Constitution" title="European Constitution">European Constitution</a>, the Greens campaigned for a Yes vote.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/French_presidential_election,_2007" title="French presidential election, 2007">French presidential election, 2007</a>, les Verts nominated <a href="/wiki/Dominique_Voynet" title="Dominique Voynet">Dominique Voynet</a>. Her low score of 1.57% in the first round was the party's worst electoral result, and the French ecologist's worst showing since <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Dumont" title="René Dumont">René Dumont</a> in the <a href="/wiki/French_presidential_election,_1974" title="French presidential election, 1974">1974</a>. The party refused an electoral deal with the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(France)" title="Socialist Party (France)">Socialists</a> for the <a href="/wiki/French_legislative_election,_2007" title="French legislative election, 2007">June legislative election</a>. However, the three Green incumbents, <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Mam%C3%A8re" title="Noël Mamère">Noël Mamère</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yves_Cochet" title="Yves Cochet">Yves Cochet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Martine_Billard" title="Martine Billard">Martine Billard</a> had no PS opposition in their respective constituencies. While the Green's vote share was down from 2002, it won a fourth seat in <a href="/wiki/Nantes" title="Nantes">Nantes</a> where <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_de_Rugy" title="François de Rugy">François de Rugy</a> defeated a conservative UMP incumbent. The Greens now had four seats in the Assembly and sat with the <a href="/wiki/French_Communist_Party" title="French Communist Party">PCF</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_and_Republican_Left" title="Democratic and Republican Left">Democratic and Republican Left</a> group.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2009_(France)" title="European Parliament election, 2009 (France)">2009 European Parliament election</a>, the party was an integral part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europe_%C3%89cologie" title="Europe Écologie">Europe Écologie</a> coalition, led by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Cohn-Bendit" title="Daniel Cohn-Bendit">Daniel Cohn-Bendit</a>, which gained 8 seats for a total of 14 on a 16.3% of the vote. Since November 2010, it merged with the coalition to become <a href="/wiki/Europe_Ecology_%E2%80%93_The_Greens" title="Europe Ecology – The Greens">Europe Ecology – The Greens</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Skandrani_Affair">The Skandrani Affair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_(France)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Skandrani Affair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the party's co-founders, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ginette_Skandrani&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ginette Skandrani (page does not exist)">Ginette Skandrani</a>, had long attracted criticism due to her involvement with <a href="/wiki/Holocaust_denial" title="Holocaust denial">Holocaust deniers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auffray_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-auffray-3">[3]</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Roth_Institute" title="Stephen Roth Institute">Stephen Roth Institute</a> criticized the Green Party in 2004, calling its record "tainted by abortive attempts to expel from within its ranks notorious anti-Jewish activist Ginette Skandrani herself ethnically Jewish<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www.proche-orient.info_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-www.proche-orient.info-4">[4]</a></sup> who has close contacts with Holocaust deniers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roth_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roth-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other critics, such as <a href="/wiki/Roger_Cukierman" title="Roger Cukierman">Roger Cukierman</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Conseil_Repr%C3%A9sentatif_des_Institutions_juives_de_France" title="Conseil Représentatif des Institutions juives de France">Representative Council of French Jewish Institutions</a> did not attack the party as a whole, but rather its <a href="/wiki/Anti-Zionism" title="Anti-Zionism">anti-Zionist</a> wing, claiming that it promoted a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red-green-brown_alliance" title="Red-green-brown alliance">brown-green alliance</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auffray_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-auffray-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2005, the Greens voted to permanently expel Skandrani. Among the reasons for her definitive expulsion were her participation in the holocaust-denial website <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AAARGH" title="AAARGH">AAARGH</a></i> (Association des anciens amateurs de récits de guerres et d'holocaustes).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auffray_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-auffray-3">[3]</a></sup> Patrick Farbiaz, a Green leader involved in her expulsion, argued that "although she has not written [anti-Semitic texts] herself, she looks like a kingpen of holocaust deniers and avowed antisemites".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auffray_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-auffray-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The party had previously expelled another co-founder (in 1991), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Bri%C3%A8re&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jean Brière (page does not exist)">Jean Brière</a>, for signing a text addressing the alleged "war-causing role" of Israel and "the <a href="/wiki/Zionism" title="Zionism">zionist</a> lobby in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auffray_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-auffray-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Call_to_lift_sanctions_against_Cypriot_Turks">Call to lift sanctions against Cypriot Turks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_(France)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Call to lift sanctions against Cypriot Turks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Green MEP <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helene_Flautre" title="Helene Flautre">Helene Flautre</a> has attracted controversy by calling for the lifting of sanctions against Turkish Cypriots imposed by the United Nations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Youth_wing">Youth wing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_(France)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Youth wing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The youth branch of the Greens, founded in Strasbourg in 2001, is called <i>Les Jeunes Verts – la Souris verte</i> (Young Greens – the Green mouse). It has been part of the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_Young_European_Greens" title="Federation of Young European Greens">Federation of Young European Greens</a> since 2006.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Factions">Factions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_(France)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Factions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most internal divisions within the party concern the party's political position (neither right nor left, or left-wing) and electoral strategy (alliance with the PS, the centre or the alternative left).
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Antoine_Waechter" title="Antoine Waechter">Neo-Waechterians</a></b> (<a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">social liberals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centrism" title="Centrism">centrists</a>): Followers of former Green leader <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Waechter" title="Antoine Waechter">Antoine Waechter</a>, a large part has joined the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Ecological_Movement" title="Independent Ecological Movement">Independent Ecological Movement</a> or, more recently, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Movement_(France)" title="Democratic Movement (France)">MoDem</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jean-Luc_Bennahmias" title="Jean-Luc Bennahmias">Jean-Luc Bennahmias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yann_Wehrling" title="Yann Wehrling">Yann Wehrling</a>)</li>
<li><b>Green left</b> (<a href="/wiki/Eco-socialism" title="Eco-socialism">eco-socialists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Democratic_socialism" title="Democratic socialism">democratic socialists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maoism" title="Maoism">Maoists</a>): Including members such as <a href="/wiki/Jean_Desessard" title="Jean Desessard">Jean Desessard</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yves_Contassot&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yves Contassot (page does not exist)">Yves Contassot</a> and, until recently, <a href="/wiki/Martine_Billard" title="Martine Billard">Martine Billard</a></li></ul>
<p>The party's final leadership, led by <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9cile_Duflot" title="Cécile Duflot">Cécile Duflot</a>, and including <a href="/wiki/Dominique_Voynet" title="Dominique Voynet">Dominique Voynet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yves_Cochet" title="Yves Cochet">Yves Cochet</a> and <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Mam%C3%A8re" title="Noël Mamère">Noël Mamère</a> were positioned between the two aforementioned factions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elected_officials">Elected officials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_(France)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Elected officials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><b>Deputies:</b> <a href="/wiki/Yves_Cochet" title="Yves Cochet">Yves Cochet</a>, <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Mam%C3%A8re" title="Noël Mamère">Noël Mamère</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_de_Rugy" title="François de Rugy">François de Rugy</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_and_Republican_Left" title="Democratic and Republican Left">GDR</a> Group). <a href="/wiki/Martine_Billard" title="Martine Billard">Martine Billard</a>, elected as a Green in 2007 joined the <a href="/wiki/Left_Party_(France)" title="Left Party (France)">Left Party</a> in July 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Senators:</b> <a href="/wiki/Marie-Christine_Blandin" title="Marie-Christine Blandin">Marie-Christine Blandin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alima_Boumediene-Thiery" title="Alima Boumediene-Thiery">Alima Boumediene-Thiery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Desessard" title="Jean Desessard">Jean Desessard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Muller" title="Jacques Muller">Jacques Muller</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dominique_Voynet" title="Dominique Voynet">Dominique Voynet</a> (<a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(France)" title="Socialist Party (France)">Socialist</a> Group).</li>
<li><b>MEPs:</b> <a href="/wiki/Malika_Benarab-Attou" title="Malika Benarab-Attou">Malika Benarab-Attou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pascal_Canfin" title="Pascal Canfin">Pascal Canfin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Cohn-Bendit" title="Daniel Cohn-Bendit">Daniel Cohn-Bendit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karima_Delli" title="Karima Delli">Karima Delli</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A9l%C3%A8ne_Flautre" title="Hélène Flautre">Hélène Flautre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Gr%C3%A8ze" title="Catherine Grèze">Catherine Grèze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicole_Kiil-Nielsen" title="Nicole Kiil-Nielsen">Nicole Kiil-Nielsen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mich%C3%A8le_Rivasi" title="Michèle Rivasi">Michèle Rivasi</a> (6 of the 14 MEPs from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europe_%C3%89cologie" title="Europe Écologie">Europe Écologie</a> are not members of the party).</li></ul>
<p>The Greens held 41 town halls,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the largest city being <a href="/wiki/Montreuil,_Seine-Saint-Denis" title="Montreuil, Seine-Saint-Denis">Montreuil</a> (<a href="/wiki/Seine-Saint-Denis" title="Seine-Saint-Denis">Seine-Saint-Denis</a>). Other cities held by the Greens include <a href="/wiki/Wattwiller" title="Wattwiller">Wattwiller</a>, <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A8gles" title="Bègles">Bègles</a> and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A8ze" title="Mèze">Mèze</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The party also claims 168 regional councillors and 14 general councillors (plus 9 Parisian councillors).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_support_and_electoral_record">Popular support and electoral record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_(France)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Popular support and electoral record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Greens were strong electorally in urban areas, specifically in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele-de-France_(region)" title="Île-de-France (region)">Greater Paris</a> area, <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">Brittany</a> and western France, parts of the <a href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B4ne-Alpes" title="Rhône-Alpes">Rhône-Alpes</a> region and <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a>. In the 2009 European elections, the Greens won their best result outside of <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>, where their result was due to the support of the <a href="/wiki/Party_of_the_Corsican_Nation" title="Party of the Corsican Nation">Party of the Corsican Nation</a> (PNC), in the city of Paris (27.41%),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interieur.gouv.fr_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-interieur.gouv.fr-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Haute-Savoie" title="Haute-Savoie">Haute-Savoie</a> (20.26%),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-interieur.gouv.fr_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-interieur.gouv.fr-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dr%C3%B4me" title="Drôme">Drôme</a> (21.75%),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Is%C3%A8re" title="Isère">Isère</a> (21.64%), <a href="/wiki/Hauts-de-Seine" title="Hauts-de-Seine">Hauts-de-Seine</a> (20.74%), <a href="/wiki/Ille-et-Vilaine" title="Ille-et-Vilaine">Ille-et-Vilaine</a> (20.59%), and <a href="/wiki/Loire-Atlantique" title="Loire-Atlantique">Loire-Atlantique</a> (20.16%). It also did very well in large, wealthy urban centres such as <a href="/wiki/Rennes" title="Rennes">Rennes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grenoble" title="Grenoble">Grenoble</a>. It does more poorly in rural areas, notably areas where its rival, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunting,_Fishing,_Nature,_Tradition" title="Hunting, Fishing, Nature, Tradition">CPNT</a>, is strong. It also did poorly in industrial or poorer urban areas; for example it won only 9.33% in the <a href="/wiki/Pas-de-Calais" title="Pas-de-Calais">Pas-de-Calais</a>, a department formerly dominated by coal mining, in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
