<p><b>Deep Green Resistance</b> (<b>DGR</b>) is a radical environmental movement that views mainstream <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental activism</a> as being largely ineffective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBay_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-McBay-1">[1]</a></sup> DGR is also used by group members to refer to their particular political strategies. The group believes that <a href="/wiki/Industrial_civilization" title="Industrial civilization">industrial civilization</a>, as they define it, is endangering all life on the planet and that a broad range of tactics are needed to achieve <a href="/wiki/Environmental_justice" title="Environmental justice">environmental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>. They argue for a radical shift in societal structures and functions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBay_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-McBay-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beliefs">Beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Green_Resistance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the book <i>Deep Green Resistance</i>, the authors state that civilization, particularly <a href="/wiki/Industrialisation" title="Industrialisation">industrial civilization</a>, is fundamentally unsustainable and must be actively (and urgently) dismantled in order to secure a livable future for all species on the planet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBay_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-McBay-1">[1]</a></sup> Civilization can be defined as the development of agriculture and the growth of cities. Deep Green Resistance argues that agriculture is detrimental to land fertility and cities necessarily surpass the natural carrying capacity of land bases. Civilization relies heavily on industry, which operates largely on non-renewable and unclean fossil fuels.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>DGR's principles stem from the concept of <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">deep ecology</a> and state that all species are inherently equal, and thus humans are not superior to any other form of life. Deep ecology attributes the current environmental crisis to the anthropocentrism that is embedded in Western perspectives. The term, first used by the Norwegian philosopher <a href="/wiki/Arne_N%C3%A6ss" title="Arne Næss">Arne Næss</a>, was quickly taken up by a variety of radical environmental groups, such as Earth First!.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Deep ecology provides a foundation upon which to base the cross-cultural nature-based spirituality that is often associated with radical environmental movements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The movement differentiates itself from <a href="/wiki/Bright_green_environmentalism" title="Bright green environmentalism">bright green environmentalism</a>, which is characterized by a focus on personal, technological, or government and corporate solutions, in that it holds these solutions as inadequate. DGR believes that lifestyle changes, such as using travel mugs and reusable bags and taking shorter showers, are too small for the large-scale environmental problems the world faces. It also states that the recent surge in environmentalism has become commercial in nature, and thus in itself has been industrialized. The movement asserts that per capita industrial waste produced is orders of magnitude greater than personal waste produced; therefore, it is industrialism that must be ended, and with that, lifestyle changes will follow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBay_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-McBay-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_advocates">Origins and advocates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Green_Resistance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins and advocates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term was created for a conference entitled "Deep Green Resistance. Confronting Industrial Culture" in April, 2007 in Deerfield, Massachusetts. <a href="/wiki/Lierre_Keith" title="Lierre Keith">Lierre Keith</a> was the main organizer.
</p><p>Deep Green theory draws on elements of <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phenomenology_(philosophy)" title="Phenomenology (philosophy)">phenomenology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">deep ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecofeminism" title="Ecofeminism">ecofeminism</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Derrick_Jensen" title="Derrick Jensen">Derrick Jensen</a> is a prominent member of the Deep Green Resistance Advisory Board. In addition to being a radical activist, he is also a writer, philosopher and teacher. Lierre Keith, another board member, is a feminist activist, writer and small farmer. Stephanie McMillan, and <a href="/wiki/Jack_D._Forbes" title="Jack D. Forbes">Jack D. Forbes</a> are also prominent influences on the movement. DGR draws on the philosophy and writings of many authors, including <a href="/wiki/Arne_N%C3%A6ss" title="Arne Næss">Arne Næss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Manning" title="Richard Manning">Richard Manning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrea_Dworkin" title="Andrea Dworkin">Andrea Dworkin</a>, Peggy Reeves Sanday, <a href="/wiki/David_Abram" title="David Abram">David Abram</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chellis_Glendinning" title="Chellis Glendinning">Chellis Glendinning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Hedges" title="Chris Hedges">Chris Hedges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Tainter" title="Joseph Tainter">Joseph Tainter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Heinberg" title="Richard Heinberg">Richard Heinberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Quinn" title="Daniel Quinn">Daniel Quinn</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pablo_Servigne&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pablo Servigne (page does not exist)">Pablo Servigne</a>, Tracie Marie Park (a.k.a. Zoe Blunt) and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Mander" title="Jerry Mander">Jerry Mander</a>, among others.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resistance_tactics">Resistance tactics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Green_Resistance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Resistance tactics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Deep Green perspective argues that the dominant culture, a term that encompasses all the cultures within globalized civilization, will not undergo a voluntary transformation to a sustainable way of living. This includes the dismissal of a possible success of a slow and soft shift to <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>. Members of the Deep Green movement believe that industrial civilization will inevitably collapse. This notion is based on historical examples of the collapse of major civilizations such as Rome or the Mayan civilization, and statistics regarding the current system's unsustainability. DGR maintains that humans must act decisively before the collapse to ensure the Earth that remains inhabitable for all organisms and that humans build a more sustainably structured society following the collapse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBay_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-McBay-1">[1]</a></sup> Deep Green Resistance supports an active resistance movement with the objective of accelerating the collapse of industrial civilization.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Within the Deep Green theory, lifestyle or personal changes are not considered effective methods of creating meaningful change. The mainstream environmental movement is seen as being distracted by its emphasis on individual lifestyle changes and technological solutions instead of confronting systems of power and holding individuals, industries, and institutions accountable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The founders of the Deep Green movement view technological solutions, no matter how well-intentioned, as unsatisfactory and warn that they could even lead to accelerated ecological destruction and pollution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBay_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-McBay-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Advocating_for_militant_resistance">Advocating for militant resistance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Green_Resistance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Advocating for militant resistance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Decisive Ecological Warfare:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGRDEW_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-DGRDEW-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>Well-organized underground militants would make coordinated attacks on energy infrastructure around the world … actions against pipelines, power lines, tankers, and refineries, perhaps using electromagnetic pulses. ….<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C&amp;C_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-C&amp;C-7">[7]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Proponents of Deep Green Resistance encourage strategies for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_action" title="Social action">social action</a> that range from violent to non-violent. DGR's support for militant action (described as a potentially necessary means by which to invoke drastic environmental change) is rejected by many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacifist" title="Pacifist">pacifists</a>. Some have called this a potential deterrent to environmentalists who believe in nonviolent activism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  The strategy described by Jensen, McBay and Keith largely centers around hard-hitting infrastructural sabotage, such as forcible <a href="/wiki/Dam_removal" title="Dam removal">dam removal</a>, rather than any kind of personal violence.
</p><p>Jensen predicts, regarding the movement, that acts of personal violence in the future will be almost completely offensive by those countering the resistance movement (i.e. those currently in power) and self-defensive by those enacting the resistance movement. When asked, "If we dismantle civilization, won't that kill millions of people in cities? What about them?," Jensen has responded that "No matter what you do, your hands will be blood red... because the global economy is [already] murdering humans and non-humans the planet over...." and "As this culture continues to collapse, those who are doing the exploiting will continue.... Don’t blame those who want to stop that exploitation. Instead, help to stop the exploitation that is killing people in the first place."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Jensen has also clarified such ideas on <i><a href="/wiki/Democracy_Now!" title="Democracy Now!">Democracy Now!</a></i>: "I get accused of being the 'violence guy'... but I don’t ever think that's really fair, because I really consider myself the 'everything guy', that I want to put everything on the table and talk about all forms of resistance.... We can certainly parse out cases where we think it's appropriate to have militant response or non-militant response."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DN112610_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-DN112610-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Keith has discussed in depth the "complexity of the issue of violence" in the organization's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eponymous" title="Eponymous">eponymous</a> book, and how it is "understandable that people who care about justice want to reject violence; many of us are survivors of it." However, she urges the following distinctions, favoring only the second in each dichotomy: "the violence of hierarchy vs. the violence of self-defense, violence against actual people vs. violence against property, and the [use of] violence as self-actualization vs. the violence of political resistance. It is difficult to find someone who is against all of these."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBay_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-McBay-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>While Deep Green Resistance maintains that it is a strictly aboveground movement, Keith and McBay have openly encouraged an additional element of underground action, and DGR provides the theoretical and aboveground framework to support such action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The organization's book points to historical examples of resistance movements that relied on an interdependence between aboveground and underground networks in order to accomplish mutual goals.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature_and_references">Literature and references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Green_Resistance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Literature and references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A book titled <i>Deep Green Resistance: Strategy to Save the Planet</i> by Aric McBay, <a href="/wiki/Lierre_Keith" title="Lierre Keith">Lierre Keith</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Derrick_Jensen" title="Derrick Jensen">Derrick Jensen</a> was released in May 2011, from Seven Stories Press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The book is divided into four sections: Resistance, Organization, Strategy and Tactics, and the Future. Each section contains several essays written by McBay or Keith and concludes with Jensen's answers to common questions about resistance. The book intends to provide a framework from which readers can build upon in order to begin fighting for environmental change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McBay_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-McBay-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_issues">Gender issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Green_Resistance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Gender issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The organization's official website states that "DGR strives to be a radical organization in every respect, including our approach to feminist struggles."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radfem_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-radfem-13">[13]</a></sup> DGR's <a href="/wiki/Radical_feminism" title="Radical feminism">radical feminism</a> views <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">gender</a>, by definition, as a social system created by <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">patriarchy</a> to coercively and violently control behavior of both males and females; however, within this system, men and masculinity are privileged, while women are trained into subordinated behavior and routinely targeted for institutionalized and socially sanctioned violence due to their sex and/or gender role.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>DGR's stance on gender has led some to interpret DGR as transphobic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The controversy especially swelled when a newly <a href="/wiki/Transitioning_(transgender)" title="Transitioning (transgender)">transitioning</a> member of DGR, self-identifying as a woman, requested to join a women-only communal sleeping and showering space and, later, a women-only caucus space; the women's caucus discussed the issue collectively and ultimately denied the applicant.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A year after this, Aric McBay claimed he "left the organization at the beginning of 2012 after a trans inclusive policy was cancelled by Derrick Jensen and Lierre Keith. Many good people and good activists left the organization for that reason. I find these transphobic attitudes to be disgusting and deeply troubling...."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> DGR released a note in response to McBay's statement, claiming that McBay actually departed the organization by mutual agreement, after unsuccessfully trying to force Derrick Jensen and Lierre Keith from DGR. Keith and Jensen reiterated that the women of DGR made the decision to deny the applicant for themselves. In this statement, DGR also affirmed that "the right of women to define their own spaces" is central to how the organization operates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The organization has denied these claims,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deepgreenresistance.org_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-deepgreenresistance.org-19">[19]</a></sup> explaining that "DGR does not condone dehumanization or violence against anyone, including people who describe themselves as trans."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deepgreenresistance.org_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-deepgreenresistance.org-19">[19]</a></sup> The website asserts that DGR holds "a difference of opinion about the definition of gender" with <a href="/wiki/Genderism" title="Genderism">genderists</a>, and states that "genderists think gender is an identity, an internal set of feelings people might have. Radical feminists think gender is a caste system."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deepgreenresistance.org_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-deepgreenresistance.org-19">[19]</a></sup> DGR's view of gender as a caste system implies that gender is an institution that cannot be improved or remedied, but, rather, requires complete dismantling so that egalitarian, spontaneous, and free behavior can emerge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radfem_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-radfem-13">[13]</a></sup> Because DGR inherently defines gender as "a brutal and corrupt arrangement of power," they want to dismantle the gender system, rather than reform it, celebrate it, or make it more inclusive. DGR, as self-described radical feminists, are "critical of gender itself. We are not gender reformists—we are gender abolitionists."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radfem_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-radfem-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deep_Green_Resistance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology#Misconceptions" title="Deep ecology">Deep ecology § Misconceptions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anarcho-primitivism#Criticism" title="Anarcho-primitivism">Anarcho-primitivism § Criticism</a></div>
<p>Critics of Deep Green Resistance have made claims similar to critics of <a href="/wiki/Anarcho-primitivism" title="Anarcho-primitivism">anarcho-primitivism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">deep ecology</a>. DGR's ideas have been variously criticized by online publications as polarizing solutions to environmental reform, "elitist", "too radical", or "out of step."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-climateandcapitalism.com_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-climateandcapitalism.com-20">[20]</a></sup> Founder Derrick Jensen, for example, has concluded in his earlier work <i>Endgame</i> that "The mass of civilized people will never be on our side.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> This has led to the criticism of organizing a strategy that is pessimistic about popular involvement, and therefore aims at forcing change without the majority's consensus. Some critics have, therefore, argued that "radical tactics tend to be counterproductive to the goal of increasing awareness and concern in the general public."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Opponents of Deep Green Resistance further claim that DGR, which predicts massive numbers of human deaths during the forthcoming "crash" of civilization, does not offer a feasible or desirable alternative human lifestyle in seeking to accelerate this crash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Keith and Jensen have both acknowledged this unpleasant reality; however, they have repeatedly claimed that the crash is inevitable (and will only result in more suffering the longer it is delayed) and that they are merely warning of—and trying to prepare the world for—these forthcoming deaths and possible wide-scale violence. Jensen has stated that if activists do not wish to participate in the physical work of accelerating this crash, they, at the very least, should locally prepare and "set up committees to eliminate or, if appropriate, channel the (additional) violence that might break out."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>Anarchists who believe that all hierarchically structured relations, whatever the purpose, are oppressive, disagree with DGR's promotion of the use of hierarchical organization within any underground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EF_Critique_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-EF_Critique-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> McBay and Keith have both promoted the idea of underground networks organized along hierarchical lines. Keith has advocated for a disciplined "command structure" regarding frontline activists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> McBay, in <i>Deep Green Resistance</i>, has argued that hierarchies are an efficient means of organizing underground resistance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sproutdistro1_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-sproutdistro1-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Anarchist writers—among them anarcho-primitivists like <a href="/wiki/John_Zerzan" title="John Zerzan">John Zerzan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Tucker_(anarchist)" title="Kevin Tucker (anarchist)">Kevin Tucker</a>—have criticized DGR's Code of Conduct, their historical understanding of revolution and radical history, and the perceived "cult of personality" around Jensen and Keith.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EF_Critique_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-EF_Critique-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sproutdistro1_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-sproutdistro1-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
