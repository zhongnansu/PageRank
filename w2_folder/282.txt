<p><b>The Evergreen State College</b> is a public <a href="/wiki/Liberal_arts_college" title="Liberal arts college">liberal arts college</a> in <a href="/wiki/Olympia,_Washington" title="Olympia, Washington">Olympia, Washington</a>. Founded in 1967, Evergreen was formed to be an experimental and non-traditional college. Full-time students enroll in interdisciplinary academic programs instead of classes. Programs typically offer students the opportunity to study several disciplines in a coordinated manner. Faculty write <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Narrative_evaluations" title="Narrative evaluations">narrative evaluations</a> of students' work in place of issuing grades.
</p><p>Evergreen offers a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> in Liberal Arts and <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a>, Master of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Studies" title="Environmental Studies">Environmental Studies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Public_Administration" title="Master of Public Administration">Master of Public Administration</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Master_in_Teaching" title="Master in Teaching">Master in Teaching</a>. As of  2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, there were 3,907 students, 3,610 of whom were undergraduates, and 223 faculty members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, protests over claims of racial discrimination and free speech issues repeatedly shut down the school, and led to the resignation of two professors, <a href="/wiki/Bret_Weinstein" title="Bret Weinstein">Bret Weinstein</a> and his wife Heather Heying.  Evergreen later paid the couple $500,000 to settle a lawsuit alleging that the college failed to protect them from race-based threats and hostility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Recentism" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article or section <b>appears to be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Recentism" title="Wikipedia:Recentism">slanted towards recent events</a>.</b><span class="hide-when-compact"> Please try to keep recent events in historical perspective and add more content related to non-recent events.</span> <small><i>(February 2018)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>In 1964, a report was issued by the Council of Presidents of Washington State baccalaureate institutions stating that another college was needed in the state to balance the geographical distribution of the existing state institutions.  This report spurred the 1965 Washington legislature to create the Temporary Advisory Council on Public Higher Education to study the need and possible location for a new state college.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Evergreen_State_College_Developmental_Aspects_Prior_to_Appointment_of_the_President_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Evergreen_State_College_Developmental_Aspects_Prior_to_Appointment_of_the_President-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1965–66, the Temporary Advisory Council on Public Higher Education (assisted by Nelson Associates of New York) concluded "at the earliest possible time a new college should be authorized", to be located at a suburban site in Thurston County within a radius of approximately 10 miles (16 km) from Olympia. Evergreen's enabling legislation – HB 596 (Chapter 47, Laws of 1967) – stated that the campus should be no smaller than 600 acres (240 ha), making it then the largest campus in the state as well as the first public four-year college created in Washington in the 20th century.
</p><p>On January 24, 1968, <i>The Evergreen State College</i> was selected from 31 choices as the name of the new institution. On November 1, 1968, <a href="/wiki/Charles_J._McCann" title="Charles J. McCann">Charles J. McCann</a> assumed the first presidency of the college. McCann and the founding faculty held the first day of classes October 4, 1971 with 1128 students. McCann served from 1968 until stepping down to join the faculty June 6, 1977 when former Governor <a href="/wiki/Daniel_J._Evans" title="Daniel J. Evans">Daniel J. Evans</a>, who signed the legislation creating Evergreen, assumed the presidency. Evans left the president's office abruptly in 1983 when he was appointed to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> to fill the vacancy created by the death of <a href="/wiki/Henry_M._Jackson" title="Henry M. Jackson">Senator Henry M. Jackson</a>. The largest building on campus is named in honor of Evans, the Daniel J. Evans Library Building. The entrance to the campus bears McCann's name, the Charles J. McCann plaza.
</p><p>In 2004, the college completed the 170,000-square-foot (16,000 m<sup>2</sup>) Seminar II building, and a significant remodel of the Daniel J. Evans Library was completed.
</p><p>In 1999, <a href="/wiki/Mumia_Abu-Jamal" title="Mumia Abu-Jamal">Mumia Abu-Jamal</a> was invited to deliver the keynote address by audiotape for the graduating class at the college. The event was protested by some.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2017_protests">2017 protests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 2017 protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Every April since the 1970s until 2018, Evergreen has held a daylong event called <i>Day of Absence</i>, inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Turner_Ward" title="Douglas Turner Ward">Douglas Turner Ward</a> play of the same name, during which minority students and faculty members voluntarily stay off campus to raise awareness of the contributions of minorities and to discuss racial and campus issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dayofabsence_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-dayofabsence-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InsideHigherEd-20170530_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-InsideHigherEd-20170530-7">[7]</a></sup> As of  2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, approximately 25% of Evergreen students were racial minorities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-8">[8]</a></sup> Since 1992, the <i>Day of Absence</i> has been followed by the <i>Day of Presence</i>, when the campus community reunites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dayofabsence_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-dayofabsence-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="Bret_Weinstein"></span>In 2017, the <i>Day of Absence</i> was altered after students of color voiced concerns about feeling unwelcome on campus following the 2016 U.S. Presidential election. For that year's event, white students, staff, and faculty were invited to stay off campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Events for students of color were held on the Evergreen campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dayofabsence_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-dayofabsence-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InsideHigherEd-20170530_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-InsideHigherEd-20170530-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dayofabsencearticle_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-dayofabsencearticle-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bret_Weinstein" title="Bret Weinstein">Bret Weinstein</a>, a professor of biology at Evergreen, wrote a letter in March to Evergreen faculty explaining his objections to the change in format: "On a college campus, one’s right to speak — or to be — must never be based on skin color."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-8">[8]</a></sup> and "There is a huge difference between a group or coalition deciding to voluntarily absent themselves from a shared space to highlight their vital and under-appreciated roles [...] and a group or coalition encouraging another group to go away."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Weinstein's emails were leaked and widely shared among conservative media outlets such as <i><a href="/wiki/Breitbart_News" title="Breitbart News">Breitbart</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Heat_Street" title="Heat Street">Heat Street</a></i>, which led to harassment and threats towards school officials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stranger_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-stranger-13">[13]</a></sup> In late May 2017, student protests—focused in part on the comments by Weinstein—disrupted the campus and called for a number of changes to the college.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InsideHigherEd-20170530_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-InsideHigherEd-20170530-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashingtonTimes-20170525_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashingtonTimes-20170525-14">[14]</a></sup> Weinstein says he was told that campus police could not protect him and that they encouraged him not to be on campus, which caused Weinstein to hold his biology class in a public park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>  Weinstein and his wife, Professor Heather Heying, later resigned and reached a $500,000 settlement with the university after having sued it for failing to "protect its employees from repeated provocative and corrosive verbal and written hostility based on race, as well as threats of physical violence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>A June 1 threat to "execute" people at Evergreen led to an evacuation and two-day closure of the campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Vandals with sticks and baseball bats caused approximately $10,000 in damage to the campus and forced closure of the school for an additional day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A June 15 protest on campus by the group <a href="/wiki/Patriot_Prayer" title="Patriot Prayer">Patriot Prayer</a> led to campus being closed early.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  The following day, Evergreen's 2017 commencement ceremony was also moved off-campus because of safety concerns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>A report from the college suggested<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style_(words_to_watch)#Editorializing" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style (words to watch)"><span title="A statement that draws an independent conclusion. (October 2018)">editorializing</span></a></i>]</sup> that the protests may adversely affect Evergreen's enrollment, which has been declining over the last decade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olympian_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Olympian-22">[22]</a></sup> Consequently, the college cut its budget by 10% and increased student fees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Olympian_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Olympian-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Programs">Programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Undergraduate">Undergraduate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Undergraduate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Evergreen is unique<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> in that undergraduate students select one 16-credit program for the entire quarter rather than multiple courses. Full-time programs will encompass a quarter's worth of work in everything related to that program concentration, by up to three professors. There are no "majors": students have the freedom to choose what program to enroll in each quarter for the entire duration of their undergraduate education, and are never required to follow a specific set of programs. Evergreen is on the <a href="/wiki/Academic_quarter_(year_division)#United_States" title="Academic quarter (year division)">"quarter" system</a>, with programs lasting one, two, or three quarters.  Three quarter programs are generally September through June.
</p><p>At the end of the program the professor writes a one-page report ("Evaluation") about the student's activity in the class rather than a letter grade, and has an end-of-program Evaluation conference with each student. The professor also determines how many credits should be awarded to the student, and students can lose credit.
</p><p>In order to obtain a Bachelor of Arts, a student must complete 180 credits.
</p><p>In order to obtain a Bachelor of Science, a student must complete 180 credits, 72 of which need to be in science, with 48 of those noted as upper division. This requirement can be satisfied by one year of upper-division science.
</p><p>Evergreen's Evening and Weekend Studies Program makes it possible for students who cannot attend full-time, day classes to get a Bachelor of Arts degree by attending on a part-time basis through night and weekend interdisciplinary programs.
</p>

<p>Unlike the undergraduate programs, the graduate programs require that students have taken a certain rotation of courses. Evergreen graduate studies consist of the following three programs:
</p>
<ul><li>Master of Environmental Studies</li>
<li>Master in Teaching</li>
<li>Master of Public Administration</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rankings">Rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/U.S._News_%26_World_Report" title="U.S. News &amp; World Report">U.S. News &amp; World Report</a>'s 2018 college rankings, Evergreen is ranked #33 in Regional Universities West, and #1 in Regional West Best Undergraduate Teaching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>  US News ranked Evergreen as #33 in the nation for offering first-year experiences to freshmen, which entails building into the curriculum first-year seminars or other programs that bring small groups of students together with faculty and staff on a regular basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Author and former <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> education editor <a href="/wiki/Loren_Pope" title="Loren Pope">Loren Pope</a> cites Evergreen as one of two public colleges in the United States in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Colleges_That_Change_Lives" title="Colleges That Change Lives">Colleges That Change Lives</a></i>.
</p><p>The Evergreen State College has an admission rate of 97%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The campus contains a large tract of undeveloped land along the Puget Sound waterfront, much of it being second growth evergreen forest, which is of use to the students and faculty of the campus as a research and natural area.  The entire campus comprises about 408 hectares (1,010 acres), much of it forested land. This total includes 11 hectares (27 acres) of southern Puget Sound tidelands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evergreen1_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-evergreen1-27">[27]</a></sup> In addition to the main campus, there is also an organic farm as well as an urban campus located in <a href="/wiki/Tacoma,_Washington" title="Tacoma, Washington">Tacoma, Washington</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Daniel_J._Evans_Library">Daniel J. Evans Library</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Daniel J. Evans Library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main library on The Evergreen State College campus is the Daniel J. Evans Library, named after the former governor who signed the legislation that founded Evergreen, and was also the school's second president. The library is home to some 428,000 volumes and 750,000 print and media items overall. The library hosts a number of small viewing rooms and also maintains special collections of rare books, archival material, and government documents.  The Quantitative and Symbolic Reasoning Center (QuaSR), a tutoring center for the sciences, is located on the first floor of the library. The library is located in the Information Technology wing of the Daniel J. Evans Library Building. This wing is also the home for Media Services and a large Academic Computing center.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_reserve_and_beach">Environmental reserve and beach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Environmental reserve and beach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Evergreen State College has 1,000 acres (400 ha) of land that is mostly second growth forest. The entire campus serves as a natural laboratory for scientific field research and provides inspiration for creative work. Throughout the 1,000-acre (400 ha) forest there are multiple trails leading to a variety of locations throughout the reserve and to Evergreen Beach. The coastal habitat is characterized by steep bluffs, gravelly beaches with many washed-up logs, and the marine intertidal zone which extends up to 150 feet (46 m) out into Puget Sound's Eld Inlet during low tides. Evergreen has approximately 3,300 feet (1.0 km) of untouched beach. Students use the beach for scientific study and as a place to get away from their studies and relax. There are multiple trails leading to the beach and a small road that leads to the only building at the beach and small boat ramp. The bluffs range from 15 to 60 feet (5 to 20 m) in height.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evergreen1_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-evergreen1-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_farm">Organic farm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Organic farm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Evergreen Organic Farm annual crop bed space comprises 38,000 square feet (0.35 ha), slightly less than 1 acre (0.40 ha). The farm also produces apples and other perennial food crops, and tends to a flock of hens. Produce is sold to the Evergreen community through CSA (<a href="/wiki/Community-supported_agriculture" title="Community-supported agriculture">Community Supported Agriculture</a>) shares, or from a farm-stand on Red Square every Tuesday and Thursday from 11am to 5pm during the growing season.  Produce is sometimes sold to campus food services Aramark and the Flaming Eggplant Cafe. Excess produce is available to students in the interdisciplinary program, titled "The Practice of Sustainable Agriculture".
</p><p>Proceeds from the sale of the crops are used to finance farm projects, as well as purchase seeds and equipment. Two of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouses" title="Greenhouses">greenhouses</a>, the cooler, <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compost shed</a>, farm fencing and orchard are just a few projects made possible from farm sales. Another use for money generated on the farm is to fund student projects. Many of these projects are related to horticultural aspects of food crops.
</p><p>The farm production area is divided into sections that are used to delineate cropping areas for specific types of crops. The farm practices a strict five year crop rotation. The rotating of crops creates plant diversity over time as opposed to plant diversity in space. The rotation has four general crop categories with each category occupying a given space for one growing season.
</p><p>Crop rotation is just one method the farm uses to maintain diversity in the field. Other methods employed are the use of undersown ground covers and inter-cropping different types of vegetable crops. Creating diversity in the field is one of the cornerstones of sustainable agriculture. Diversity provides non-toxic, sustainable crop protection against plant diseases and insect pests.
</p><p>The Evergreen Organic Farm hosts a large composting facility that composts all compostables from the campus. It also hosts a Biodiesel facility, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Community_garden" title="Community garden">community garden</a>, demeters garden, and a large farmhouse that was partially built by students.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_service_centers">Public service centers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Public service centers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Evergreen State College is the home of the Longhouse Education and Cultural Center. The Longhouse exists to provide service and hospitality to students, the college, and surrounding Native communities. With a design based on the Northwest Indigenous Nations' philosophy of hospitality, its primary functions are to provide a gathering place for hosting cultural ceremonies, classes, conferences, performances, art exhibits and community events. The Longhouse provides the opportunity to build a bridge of understanding between the regions' tribes and visitors of all cultures. The public service mission of the Longhouse is to promote indigenous arts and cultures through education, cultural preservation, and economic development.
</p><p>It is also the administrative home for the <a href="/wiki/Washington_State_Institute_for_Public_Policy" title="Washington State Institute for Public Policy">Washington State Institute for Public Policy</a>. The Institute's mission is to carry out practical, non-partisan research—at legislative direction—on issues of importance to Washington State. The Institute conducts research using its own policy analysts and economists, specialists from universities, and consultants. Institute staff work closely with legislators, legislative and state agency staff, and experts in the field to ensure that studies answer relevant policy questions.
</p><p>Other notable public service centers on campus are:
</p>
<ul><li>Washington Center for Improving the Quality of Undergraduate Education</li>
<li>Center for Community-Based Learning and Action</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_State_College&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Evergreen teams, nicknamed athletically as the Geoducks, are part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Intercollegiate_Athletics" title="National Association of Intercollegiate Athletics">National Association of Intercollegiate Athletics</a> (NAIA), primarily competing in the <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Collegiate_Conference" title="Cascade Collegiate Conference">Cascade Collegiate Conference</a> (CCC). Men's sports include basketball, cross country, soccer, crew, and track &amp; field; while women's sports include basketball, cross country, soccer, crew, track &amp; field and volleyball. A <a href="/wiki/Geoduck" title="Geoduck">geoduck</a> is a clam native to the region.
</p><p>Former men's soccer star <a href="/wiki/Joey_Gjertsen" title="Joey Gjertsen">Joey Gjertsen</a>, who led the Geoducks to the 2004 <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Intercollegiate_Athletics" title="National Association of Intercollegiate Athletics">NAIA</a> National quarterfinals, has gone on to have professional success with the <a href="/wiki/San_Jose_Earthquakes" title="San Jose Earthquakes">San Jose Earthquakes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Soccer" title="Major League Soccer">Major League Soccer</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Shawn_Medved" title="Shawn Medved">Shawn Medved</a> previously had success in the MLS, playing for <a href="/wiki/D.C._United" title="D.C. United">D.C. United</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Jose_Clash" title="San Jose Clash">San Jose Clash</a>.  Medved scored the tying goal in the 1996 MLS Cup as D.C. went on to the championship.
</p><p>Evergreen also had a strong run in men's basketball during the first decade of the 21st Century, winning the 2002 <a href="/wiki/Cascade_Collegiate_Conference" title="Cascade Collegiate Conference">Cascade Collegiate Conference</a> championship and reaching the NAIA national tournament in '02, '09 and '10.  Forward Mike Parker from the '02 team has become one of the top professional players in Japan and several other basketball players have gone on to professional careers overseas.
</p>
