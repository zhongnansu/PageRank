<p>A <b>green belt</b> or <b>greenbelt</b> is a policy and land use zone designation used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_use_planning" title="Land use planning">land use planning</a> to retain areas of largely undeveloped, wild, or agricultural <a href="/wiki/Landscape" title="Landscape">land</a> surrounding or neighbouring urban areas. Similar concepts are <b><a href="/wiki/Greenway_(landscape)" title="Greenway (landscape)">greenways</a></b> or <b>green wedges</b> which have a linear character and may run through an urban area instead of around it. In essence, a green belt is an invisible line designating a border around a certain area, preventing development of the area and allowing wildlife to return and be established.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purposes">Purposes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Purposes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In those countries which have them, the stated objectives of green belt policy are to:
</p>
<ul><li>Protect natural or semi-<a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environments</a>;</li>
<li>Improve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_quality" title="Air quality">air quality</a> within <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> areas;</li>
<li>Ensure that urban dwellers have access to countryside, with consequent educational and recreational opportunities; and</li>
<li>Protect the unique character of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rural" title="Rural">rural</a> communities that might otherwise be absorbed by expanding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suburbs" title="Suburbs">suburbs</a>.</li></ul>
<p>The green belt has many benefits for people:
</p>
<ul><li>Walking, camping, and biking areas close to the cities and towns.</li>
<li>Contiguous <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> network for wild <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>.</li>
<li>Cleaner air and water</li>
<li>Better land use of areas within the bordering cities.</li></ul>
<p>The effectiveness of green belts differs depending on location and country. They can often be eroded by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urban_rural_fringe" title="Urban rural fringe">urban rural fringe</a> uses and sometimes, development 'jumps' over the green belt area, resulting in the creation of "satellite towns" which, although separated from the city by green belt, function more like suburbs than independent communities.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> outlines a proposal for a green belt around the <a href="/wiki/Levite" title="Levite">Levite</a> towns in the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Israel" title="Land of Israel">Land of Israel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Moses <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a> expounded that the greenbelt plan from the Old Testament referred to all towns in ancient Israel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In the 7th century, <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> established a green belt around <a href="/wiki/Medina" title="Medina">Medina</a>. He did this by prohibiting any further removal of trees in a 12-mile long strip around the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1580 <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I of England</a> banned new building in a 3-mile wide belt around the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> in an attempt to stop the spread of plague. However, this was not widely enforced and it was possible to buy dispensations which reduced the effectiveness of the proclamation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In modern times, the term emerged from continental Europe where broad boulevards were increasingly used to separate new development from the centre of historic towns; most notably the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ringstra%C3%9Fe" title="Ringstraße">Ringstraße</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. Green belt policy was then pioneered in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> confronted with ongoing <a href="/wiki/Rural_flight" title="Rural flight">rural flight</a>. Various proposals were put forward from 1890 onwards but the first to garner widespread support was put forward by the <a href="/wiki/London_Society" title="London Society">London Society</a> in its "Development Plan of Greater London" 1919. Alongside the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_to_Protect_Rural_England" title="Campaign to Protect Rural England">CPRE</a> they lobbied for a continuous belt (of up to two miles wide) to prevent urban sprawl, beyond which new development could occur.
</p><p>There are fourteen green belt areas in the UK covering 16,716 km² or 13% of England, and 164 km² of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>; for a detailed discussion of these, see <a href="/wiki/Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)" title="Green belt (United Kingdom)">Green belt (UK)</a>. Other notable examples are the <a href="/wiki/Greenbelt_(Ottawa)" title="Greenbelt (Ottawa)">Ottawa Greenbelt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenbelt_(Golden_Horseshoe)" title="Greenbelt (Golden Horseshoe)">Golden Horseshoe Greenbelt</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada. Ottawa's 20,350-hectare (78.6 sq mi) instance is managed by the <a href="/wiki/National_Capital_Commission" title="National Capital Commission">National Capital Commission</a> (NCC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  The more general term in the United States is <b>green space</b> or <b>greenspace</b>, which may be a very small area such as a <a href="/wiki/Park" title="Park">park</a>.
</p><p>The dynamic <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_Park_Lands" title="Adelaide Park Lands">Adelaide Park Lands</a>, measuring approximately 7.6 km² surround, unbroken, the city centre of <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a>. On the fringe of the eastern suburbs, an expansive natural greenbelt in the <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_Hills" title="Adelaide Hills">Adelaide Hills</a> acts as a growth boundary for Adelaide and cools the city in the hottest months.
</p><p>The concept of "green belt" has evolved in recent years to encompass not only "Greenspace" but also "Greenstructure" which comprises all urban and peri-urban greenspaces, an important aspect of sustainable development in the 21st century. The <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>'s <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20060329125020/http://www.map21ltd.com/COSTC11-book/index.htm" rel="nofollow">COST Action C11</a>
(<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/COST" title="COST">COST</a> – European Cooperation in Science and Technology) is undertaking
<a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20060329125428/http://www.map21ltd.com/COSTC11-book/pdfs/b-Cases.pdf" rel="nofollow">"Case studies in Greenstructure Planning"</a> involving 15 European countries.
</p><p>An act of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Sweden" title="Parliament of Sweden">Swedish parliament</a> from 1994 has declared a series of parks in  <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> and the adjacent municipality of <a href="/wiki/Solna_Municipality" title="Solna Municipality">Solna</a> to its north a "national city park" called <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_City_Park" title="Royal National City Park">Royal National City Park</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="House_prices">House prices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: House prices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When paired with a city which is economically prospering, homes in a Green belt may have been motivated by or result in considerable premiums. They may also be more economically resilient as popular among the retired and less attractive for short-term renting of modest homes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harford_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-harford-7">[7]</a></sup> Where in the city itself demand exceeds supply in housing, green belt homes compete directly with much city housing wherever such green belt homes are well-connected to the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harford_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-harford-7">[7]</a></sup> Further, they in all cases attract a future-guaranteed premium for protection of their views, recreational space and for the preservation/conservation value itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harford_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-harford-7">[7]</a></sup> Most also benefit from higher rates of urban <a href="/wiki/Gardening" title="Gardening">gardening</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farming" title="Farming">farming</a>, particularly when done in a <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> setting,  which have positive effects on <a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">nutrition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physical_fitness" title="Physical fitness">fitness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Self-esteem" title="Self-esteem">self-esteem</a>, and happiness, providing a benefit for both physical and mental health, in all cases easily provided or accessed in a green belt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Government planners also seek to protect the green belt as its local farmers are engaged in <a href="/wiki/Peri-urban_agriculture" title="Peri-urban agriculture">peri-urban agriculture</a> which augments <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>, reduces the urban <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_island" title="Heat island">heat island</a> effect, and provides a <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organisms" title="Organisms">organisms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Peri-urban agriculture may also help <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recycle" title="Recycle">recycle</a> urban <a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">greywater</a> and other products of <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a>, helping to conserve water and reduce waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The housing market contrasts with more uncertainty and <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">economic liberalism</a> inside and immediately outside of the belt:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harford_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-harford-7">[7]</a></sup> Green Belt homes have by definition nearby protected landscapes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harford_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-harford-7">[7]</a></sup> Local residents in affluent parts of a Green Belt, as in parts of the city, can be assured of preserving any localised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bourgeois" title="Bourgeois">bourgeois</a> <a href="/wiki/Status_quo" title="Status quo">status quo</a> present and so assuming the Green Belt is not from the outset an area of more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_housing" title="Social housing">social housing</a> proportionately than the city, it naturally tends toward greater economic wealth. In a protracted housing shortage, reduction of the Green Belt is one of the possible solutions.  All such solutions may be resisted however by private <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Landlords" title="Landlords">landlords</a> who profit from a scarcity of housing, for example by lobbying to restrain new housing across the city. The stated motivation and benefits of the green belt might be well-intentioned (public health, social gardening and agriculture, environment), but inadequately realised relative to other solutions.  
</p><p>Inherently partial critics include <a href="/wiki/Mark_Pennington" title="Mark Pennington">Mark Pennington</a> and the economics-heavy think tanks such as the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Economic_Affairs" title="Institute of Economic Affairs">Institute of Economic Affairs</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-11">[11]</a></sup> who would see a reduction in many Green Belts. Such studies focus on widely inherent limitations of Green Belts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-11">[11]</a></sup>  In most examples only a small fraction of the population uses the green belt for leisure purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-11">[11]</a></sup> The IEA study claims that a green belt is not strongly causally linked to clean air and water.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-11">[11]</a></sup> Rather, they view the ultimate result of the decision to green-belt a city as one to prevent housing demand within the zone to be met with supply,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-11">[11]</a></sup> thus exacerbating high housing prices and stifling competitive forces in general. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Increasing_urban_sprawl">Increasing urban sprawl</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Increasing urban sprawl">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another area of criticism comes from the fact that, since a greenbelt does not extend indefinitely outside a city, it spurs the growth of areas much further away from the city core than if it had not existed, thereby actually <i>increasing</i> urban sprawl.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Examples commonly cited are the <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a> suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Kanata,_Ontario" title="Kanata, Ontario">Kanata</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orl%C3%A9ans,_Ontario" title="Orléans, Ontario">Orleans</a>, both of which are outside the city's greenbelt, and are currently undergoing explosive growth (see <a href="/wiki/Greenbelt_(Ottawa)" title="Greenbelt (Ottawa)">Greenbelt (Ottawa)</a>). This leads to other problems, as residents of these areas have a longer commute to work places in the city and worse access to <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a>. It also means people have to commute through the green belt, an area not designed to cope with high levels of transportation. Not only is the merit of a green belt subverted, but the green belt may heighten the problem and make the city unsustainable.
</p><p>There are many examples whereby the actual effect of green belts is to act as a land reserve for future freeways and other highways. Examples include sections of the 407 highway north of Toronto and the Hunt Club Rd./Richmond Rd. south of Ottawa. Whether they are originally planned as such, or the result of a newer administration taking advantage of land that was left available by its predecessors is debatable.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)" title="Green belt (United Kingdom)">Green belt (United Kingdom)</a></div>
<p>In the UK, greenbelt around the major conurbations has been criticized as one of the main protectionist bars to building housing, the others being other planning restrictions (Local Plans and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Restrictive_covenant" title="Restrictive covenant">restrictive covenants</a>) and developers' <a href="/wiki/Land_banking" title="Land banking">land banking</a>. Local Plans and land banking are to be relaxed for home building in the 2015-2030 period by law and the green belt will be reduced by some local authorities as each local authority must now consider it among the available shortlisted options in drawing development plans to meet higher housing targets. Critics argue that the greenbelts defeat their stated objective of saving the countryside and open spaces. Such criticism falls shorts when considering the other, broader benefits such as <a href="/wiki/Peri-urban_agriculture" title="Peri-urban agriculture">peri-urban agriculture</a> which includes gardening and carries many benefits, especially to the retired. It also ignores the strategic aims of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attlee_Ministry" title="Attlee Ministry">Attlee Ministry</a> in 1946, just as in France, of shifting capital away from the capital (addressing regional disparity) and avoiding intra-urban gridlock.  The restrictions of the Green Belt were particularly in the 1940s-1980s mitigated with planned, government-supported, new towns under the <a href="/wiki/New_Towns_Act_1946" title="New Towns Act 1946">New Towns Act 1946</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Towns_Act_1981" title="New Towns Act 1981">New Towns Act 1981</a>. These saw establishment beyond the greenbelts of new homes, <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a>, businesses and other facilities.  Without large scale sustainable development, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infill_development" title="Infill development">infill development</a> sees urban green space lost. A chronic housing shortage with inadequate new settlements and/or extension of those outside of the green belt and/or no green belt reduction has seen many brownfield sites, often well-suited to industry and commerce, lost in existing conurbations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a>'s Central Business District is completely encircled by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adelaide_Parklands" title="Adelaide Parklands">Adelaide Parklands</a>, as was <a href="/wiki/Light%27s_Vision" title="Light's Vision">initially planned</a> in 1837.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Shire_of_Nillumbik" title="Shire of Nillumbik">Nillumbik Shire Council</a> which is located approximately 30 km (19 miles) north-east of <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> is considered as "The Green Wedge Shire" because of the agreement with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victorian_Government" title="Victorian Government">Victorian Government</a> which prevents high-density infrastructure to be built.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=S%C3%A3o_Paulo_City_Green_Belt_Biosphere_Reserve&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="São Paulo City Green Belt Biosphere Reserve (page does not exist)">São Paulo City Green Belt Biosphere Reserve</a> – GBBR, an integral part of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Forest_Biosphere_Reserve" title="Atlantic Forest Biosphere Reserve">Atlantic Forest Biosphere Reserve</a>, was created in 1994 stemming from a people's movement that collected 150 thousand signatures. It extends throughout 73 municipalities including <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a> metro and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santos_(S%C3%A3o_Paulo)" title="Santos (São Paulo)">Santos</a> area. With approximately 17,000 km², it is inhabited by about 23 million people, corresponding to more than 10% of the country's total population in an area equivalent to 2 thousandth of the Brazilian territory. There are over 6,000 km² of forests and other Atlantic Forest ecosystems at the Reserve, one of the planet's most threatened biomes. In addition to a spectacular biological diversity, the GBBR's ecosystems render valuable <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_services" title="Ecosystem services">ecosystem services</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Greenbelt_(Ottawa)" title="Greenbelt (Ottawa)">Ottawa Greenbelt</a> – Surrounds the Capital city of Ottawa</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Greenbelt_(Golden_Horseshoe)" title="Greenbelt (Golden Horseshoe)">Greenbelt (Golden Horseshoe)</a>, is a 7300 km² band of land that encompasses the rural and agricultural land surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Toronto_Area" title="Greater Toronto Area">Greater Toronto Area</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Peninsula" title="Niagara Peninsula">Niagara Peninsula</a>, and parts of the <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Peninsula" title="Bruce Peninsula">Bruce Peninsula</a>. It is larger than Canada's smallest province of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">Prince Edward Island</a>. Most of the land consists of the <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridges_Moraine" title="Oak Ridges Moraine">Oak Ridges Moraine</a>, an environmentally sensitive land that is a major <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a> for the region, and the <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Escarpment" title="Niagara Escarpment">Niagara Escarpment</a>, a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biosphere_reserve" title="Biosphere reserve">Biosphere Reserve</a>. In an effort to restrain urban sprawl that has encroched on these lands, the Ontario government created the Greenbelt Act in February 2005 to protect this greenspace from all future development, with the exception of limited agricultural use.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> – the <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_Land_Reserve" title="Agricultural Land Reserve">Agricultural Land Reserve</a> protects agricultural land throughout this mountainous province from urban development, including around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vancouver,_British_Columbia" title="Vancouver, British Columbia">Vancouver</a>. This protection is strict and urban development of agricultural land is only allowed if no reasonable alternative exists. However, it does not protect non-agricultural land, particularly hillsides, leading to substantial, and highly visible, leapfrog-type hillside sprawl.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> – The <i>Commission de protection du territoire agricole du Québec</i> asserts its mission, namely to keep a territory (the agricultural zones) that is favorable for the practice and the development of agricultural activities. In so doing, the commission safeguards the agricultural territory and helps make its protection a local priority. The agricultural zones cover an area of 63 000 square kilometres in 952 local municipalities.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominican_Republic">Dominican Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Dominican Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

It has largely been affected by uncontrolled urbanization, but other parts remain unaffected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tochal_from_Modarres_Expressway.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="465" data-file-width="700" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Tochal_from_Modarres_Expressway.jpg/280px-Tochal_from_Modarres_Expressway.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Tochal_from_Modarres_Expressway.jpg/420px-Tochal_from_Modarres_Expressway.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Tochal_from_Modarres_Expressway.jpg/560px-Tochal_from_Modarres_Expressway.jpg 2x" width="280"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tochal_from_Modarres_Expressway.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>afforestation beyond an expressway in <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li>Green Belt in <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a> has always been a consequential matter due to the rapid increase of the city size, population, cars, factories, pollution and other expansions which hardened breathing. By a big project, the green belt of Tehran increased from 29 square kilometres in 1979 to 530 square kilometres in 2017 and the number of parks also increased from 75 in 1979 to 2,211 in 2017 in total urban and suburb areas. Such actions and afforestation increased the humidity level and precipitation chance in the city which cools the summer's temperatures down by up to 4 °C. Tehran municipality announced to increase the green belt by 10 square kilometres each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_2">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> states of <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> require cities to establish <a href="/wiki/Urban_growth_boundary" title="Urban growth boundary">urban growth boundaries</a> (UGBs).</li>
<li>Notable U.S. cities which have adopted UGBs include <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland, Oregon</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twin_Cities,_Minnesota" title="Twin Cities, Minnesota">Twin Cities, Minnesota</a>; <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Beach,_Virginia" title="Virginia Beach, Virginia">Virginia Beach, Virginia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky" title="Lexington, Kentucky">Lexington, Kentucky</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">Miami-Dade County, Florida</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Public_Works_Administration" title="Public Works Administration">Public Works Administration</a> of the <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a> created three Greenbelt communities based on the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Ebenezer_Howard" title="Ebenezer Howard">Ebenezer Howard</a> which are now the municipalities of <a href="/wiki/Greenbelt,_Maryland" title="Greenbelt, Maryland">Greenbelt, Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenhills,_Ohio" title="Greenhills, Ohio">Greenhills, Ohio</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Greendale,_Wisconsin" title="Greendale, Wisconsin">Greendale, Wisconsin</a>.</li>
<li>More than 20 cities in the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a> have UGBs (see <a href="/wiki/Greenbelt_Alliance" title="Greenbelt Alliance">Greenbelt Alliance</a>, a Bay Area organization that has been involved in establishing these boundaries).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Staten_Island_Greenbelt" title="Staten Island Greenbelt">Staten Island Greenbelt</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooklyn-Queens_Greenway" title="Brooklyn-Queens Greenway">Brooklyn-Queens Greenway</a> in New York City</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barton_Creek_Greenbelt" title="Barton Creek Greenbelt">Barton Creek Greenbelt</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austin" title="Austin">Austin</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ann_Arbor,_Michigan" title="Ann Arbor, Michigan">Ann Arbor, Michigan</a> is acquiring conservation easements on agricultural land around the city without the establishment of an urban growth boundary. While the city's initial plan did not include the participation of surrounding townships, at least four townships have participated directly or have initiated their own efforts to protect agricultural land surrounding the city.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boise_Greenbelt" title="Boise Greenbelt">Boise Greenbelt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boise,_Idaho" title="Boise, Idaho">Boise, Idaho</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Jungle_(Seattle)" title="The Jungle (Seattle)"><i>The Jungle</i>, Seattle</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Emerald_Necklace" title="Emerald Necklace">Emerald Necklace</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> is halfway between a green belt and a greenway, nearly ringing central Boston. The final link in the chain, the Dorchesterway, was never constructed.</li></ul>
