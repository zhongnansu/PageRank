<p><b>Bitar Mansion</b>, also known as <b>Harry A. Green House</b> or the <b>Harry A. and Ada Green House</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Mansion" title="Mansion">mansion</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Laurelhurst,_Portland,_Oregon" title="Laurelhurst, Portland, Oregon">Laurelhurst neighborhood</a> of <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Oregon" title="Portland, Oregon">Portland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, United States.   The 12,000-square-foot (1,100 m<sup>2</sup>) and 17-room structure was designed by architect <a href="/wiki/Herman_Brookman" title="Herman Brookman">Herman Brookman</a> and built in 1927 for $410,000, equivalent to $5.78 million today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-US_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-US-2">[2]</a></sup>  The Mediterranean-style house contains a grand ballroom and many elaborate details.  The mansion has views of the <a href="/wiki/Tualatin_Mountains" title="Tualatin Mountains">Tualatin Mountains</a> and adjacent <a href="/wiki/Laurelhurst_Park" title="Laurelhurst Park">Laurelhurst Park</a>.
</p><p>Robert and Mable Bitar purchased the house in 1951. Robert later became an honorary <a href="/wiki/Consul_(representative)" title="Consul (representative)">consul</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a> and lived in the mansion until his death in 2000. The house went on the real estate market for the first time in 55 years in 2006 and was purchased in December 2006 for $1.825 million. As of 2011, the house remains the most expensive home sold in southeast Portland. The house was put on the market again and is up for auction in August 2011.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bitar_Mansion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Mediterranean-style mansion is on a property "the equivalent of seven standard city lots" adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Laurelhurst_Park" title="Laurelhurst Park">Laurelhurst Park</a> and offers views of the <a href="/wiki/Tualatin_Mountains" title="Tualatin Mountains">Tualatin Mountains</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaquiss_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jaquiss-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Otto_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Otto-4">[4]</a></sup> The house contains a round tower, multiple chimneys, a red-tiled roof, <a href="/wiki/Bronzing" title="Bronzing">bronzed</a> iron gates, and <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> accents with a peacock motif.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-King-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Classic_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Classic-6">[6]</a></sup> A bell-cast entrance tower, which contains a curved stairway and a <a href="/wiki/Vestibule_(architecture)" title="Vestibule (architecture)">vestibule</a>, anchors "sweeping and curved low-pitched roofs".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Classic_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Classic-6">[6]</a></sup> Next to the tower is the living room's fireplace chimney. To the right of the entrance hall is the formal living room that opens to the parterre overlooking Laurelhurst Park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Classic_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Classic-6">[6]</a></sup> To the entrance hall's left is the dining room, followed by a curved wing housing the kitchen, service areas and garages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Classic_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Classic-6">[6]</a></sup> The interior features a marble-floored ballroom, heated pool, servants wing, and elaborate woodwork, tile, metalwork and sculpture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaquiss_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jaquiss-3">[3]</a></sup> Surrounding the French doors leading outside from the entrance hall are columns supporting paired peacocks cast in stone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Classic_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Classic-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The house plan is influenced by English Arts and Crafts tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Classic_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-Classic-6">[6]</a></sup> Italian influences are evidenced by the garden parterre in the backyard as well as the "medieval style" columns and arches at the breakfast room windows. Decorative embellishments along the main garden door suggest Art Deco and Spanish Plasteresque styles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Classic_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-Classic-6">[6]</a></sup> Stuccoed walls, tiled roofs and metal-framed windows showcase Mediterranean style. The windows are three-paned vertically within each casement; feature windows have columns supporting Moorish-arched openings. The living and dining rooms contain large <a href="/wiki/Bay_window" title="Bay window">bay windows</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Classic_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-Classic-6">[6]</a></sup> Outside, an <a href="/wiki/Arcade_(architecture)" title="Arcade (architecture)">arcade</a> leads to the swimming pool, where privacy from the street is provided by a pyramid-roofed bath house and garden walls. According to William J. Hawkins, III and William F. Willingham, these elements "add to the unity of the entire architectural composition, giving a handsome facade to the public street, yet providing a great variety of private spaces behind the house and walled gardens to the rear."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Classic_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-Classic-6">[6]</a></sup> The architect, <a href="/wiki/Herman_Brookman" title="Herman Brookman">Herman Brookman</a>, also designed the <a href="/wiki/Congregation_Beth_Israel_(Portland,_Oregon)" title="Congregation Beth Israel (Portland, Oregon)">Congregation Beth Israel</a> and Fir Acres, the <a href="/wiki/M._Lloyd_Frank_Estate" title="M. Lloyd Frank Estate">M. Lloyd Frank Estate</a> that became <a href="/wiki/Lewis_%26_Clark_College" title="Lewis &amp; Clark College">Lewis &amp; Clark College</a>, both in Portland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaquiss_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Jaquiss-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walks_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Walks-7">[7]</a></sup> Brookman mixed architectural styles, as evidenced by the many influences seen in Bitar Mansion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Classic_6-9"><a href="#cite_note-Classic-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bitar_Mansion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bitar_Mansion,_Portland,_Oregon,_April_2012.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Bitar_Mansion%2C_Portland%2C_Oregon%2C_April_2012.JPG/220px-Bitar_Mansion%2C_Portland%2C_Oregon%2C_April_2012.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Bitar_Mansion%2C_Portland%2C_Oregon%2C_April_2012.JPG/330px-Bitar_Mansion%2C_Portland%2C_Oregon%2C_April_2012.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Bitar_Mansion%2C_Portland%2C_Oregon%2C_April_2012.JPG/440px-Bitar_Mansion%2C_Portland%2C_Oregon%2C_April_2012.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bitar_Mansion,_Portland,_Oregon,_April_2012.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The mansion following <a href="/wiki/Foreclosure" title="Foreclosure">foreclosure</a>, April 2012</div></div></div>
<p>Robert and Mable Bitar purchased the house in 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walks_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Walks-7">[7]</a></sup> Robert recalled that as a young boy he vowed to one day own the house. A grocery store owner and <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a> investor, Robert later became an honorary <a href="/wiki/Consul_(representative)" title="Consul (representative)">consul</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a> and lived in the mansion until his death in 2000. While owned by the Bitar family, the home hosted First Lady <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Roosevelt" title="Eleanor Roosevelt">Eleanor Roosevelt</a>, pianist <a href="/wiki/Van_Cliburn" title="Van Cliburn">Van Cliburn</a> and many <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Oregon" title="Governor of Oregon">state governors</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">U.S. senators</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaquiss_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Jaquiss-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The house was placed on the real estate market in 2006, the first time in 55 years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walks_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Walks-7">[7]</a></sup> and sold for $1.825 million after being valued at $3.99 million by an assessor for Multnomah County.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Best_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Best-8">[8]</a></sup> The new owners began to restore the mansion to its original condition with building renovations and searching for furnishings in the original Doernbecher style. Since 2010, the house has fallen into disrepair and been foreclosed. On August 22, 2011, the house was auctioned off at the Multnomah County Courthouse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaquiss_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Jaquiss-3">[3]</a></sup> The property was purchased by ReconTrust Company, an affiliate of Bank of America, for $1.605 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In December 2011 the home sold for $870,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Best_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Best-8">[8]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Willamette_Week" title="Willamette Week">Willamette Week</a></i> included the house on their "Best of Portland 2012" list as the "Best Barometer of the Real Estate Market", signifying the bottom of the economic market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Best_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Best-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2011, the house remains the most expensive sold in Southeast Portland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jaquiss_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Jaquiss-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
