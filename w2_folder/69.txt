<p>A <b>seawater greenhouse</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> structure that enables the growth of crops in <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> regions, using  <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">seawater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a>. The technique involves pumping seawater (or allowing it to gravitate if below sea level) to an arid location and then subjecting it to two processes: first, it is used to humidify and cool the air, and second, it is evaporated by solar heating and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distilled" title="Distilled">distilled</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a>. Finally, the remaining humidified air is expelled from the greenhouse and used to improve growing conditions for outdoor plants. The technology was introduced by British inventor Charlie Paton in the early 1990s and is being developed by his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a> company Seawater Greenhouse Ltd. The more concentrated salt water may either be further evaporated for the production of salt and other elements, or discharged back to the sea. The seawater greenhouse is a response to the global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_crisis" title="Water crisis">water crisis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peak_water" title="Peak water">peak water</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seawater_greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The seawater greenhouse concept was first researched and developed in 1991 by Charlie Paton's company Light Works Ltd, now Seawater Greenhouse Ltd. The first pilot project commenced in 1992 with the search for a test site that was eventually found on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canary_Island" title="Canary Island">Canary Island</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">Tenerife</a>. A prototype seawater greenhouse was assembled in the UK and constructed on the site in Tenerife. The results from this pilot project validated the concept and demonstrated the potential for other arid regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The original pilot design evolved into a lower cost solution using a lighter steel structure, similar to a multi-span polytunnel. This structure was designed to be cost effective and suitable for local sourcing. The design was first tested and validated through a second seawater greenhouse that was constructed on <a href="/wiki/Al-Aryam_Island" title="Al-Aryam Island">Al-Aryam Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi" title="Abu Dhabi">Abu Dhabi</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
The year 2004 saw the completion of a third pilot seawater greenhouse near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscat,_Oman" title="Muscat, Oman">Muscat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/Sultan_Qaboos_University" title="Sultan Qaboos University">Sultan Qaboos University</a>, providing an opportunity to develop a sustainable horticultural sector on the <a href="/wiki/Al_Batinah_Region" title="Al Batinah Region">Batinah coast</a>. These projects have enabled the validation of a thermodynamic simulation model which, given appropriate meteorological data, accurately predict and quantify how the seawater greenhouse
will perform in other parts of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TechAwards_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-TechAwards-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, Seawater Greenhouse built a new commercial installation in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. The enterprise is now independently operating with an evolved set of technologies as <a class="external text" href="http://www.sundropfarms.com" rel="nofollow">Sundrop Farms Pty Ltd</a>
.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process">Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seawater_greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A seawater greenhouse uses the sun, the sea and the atmosphere to produce fresh water and
cool air. The process recreates the natural <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">hydrological cycle</a> within a controlled environment. The front wall of the building is a seawater evaporator. It consists of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honeycomb_lattice" title="Honeycomb lattice">honeycomb lattice</a> and faces the prevailing wind. Fans control air movement. Seawater trickles down over the lattice, cooling and humidifying the air passing through into the planting area.
Sunlight is filtered through a specially constructed roof. The roof traps <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> heat, while allowing visible light through to promote photosynthesis. This creates optimum growing conditions – cool and humid with high light intensity. Seawater that has been heated in the roof passes through a second evaporator creating hot, saturated air which then flows through a condenser.
The condenser is cooled by incoming seawater. The temperature difference causes fresh water to condense out of the air stream. The volume of fresh water is determined by air temperature, relative humidity, solar radiation and the airflow rate. These conditions can be modeled with appropriate meteorological data, enabling the design and process to be optimized for any suitable location.
</p>

<p>A seawater greenhouse evaporates much more water than it condenses back into freshwater. This humid air is `lost´ due to high rates of ventilation to keep the crops cool and supplied with CO2. The higher humidity exhaust air provides some benefit to the cultivation of more hardy crops downwind of the greenhouse.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applicability">Applicability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seawater_greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applicability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The technique is applicable to sites in arid regions near the sea. The distance and elevation from the sea must be evaluated considering the energy required to pump water to the site.  There are numerous suitable locations on the coasts; others are below sea level, such as the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Qattara_Depression" title="Qattara Depression">Qattara Depression</a>, where hydro schemes have been proposed to exploit the hydraulic pressure to generate power, e.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_Sea-Dead_Sea_canal" title="Red Sea-Dead Sea canal">Red Sea–Dead Sea Canal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Sundrop_Farms.2C_Australia"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sundrop_Farms,_Australia">Sundrop Farms, Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Seawater_greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sundrop Farms, Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The seawater greenhouse technology was adopted and subsequently modified by <a href="/wiki/Sundrop_Farms" title="Sundrop Farms">Sundrop Farms</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Augusta,_South_Australia" title="Port Augusta, South Australia">Port Augusta</a>, Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In particular, Sundrop Farms uses a more robust desalination technique in favour of the water vapour technique employed by Seawater Greenhouse, thus allowing them to construct larger scale facilities.  This has been a success, allowing Sundrop Farms to grow crops such as tomatoes in arid climates with only the use of salt water and sunlight, which they use to produce heat, water, and electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> An expansion of 20 hectares was <a href="/wiki/Project_commissioning" title="Project commissioning">commissioned</a> in October 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
