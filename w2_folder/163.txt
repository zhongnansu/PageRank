<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Greenspan put</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The "<b>Greenspan put</b>" refers to the monetary policy approach that <a href="/wiki/Alan_Greenspan" title="Alan Greenspan">Alan Greenspan</a>, the former Chairman of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_of_America" title="United States of America">United States</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Board" title="Federal Reserve Board">Federal Reserve Board</a>, and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_System" title="Federal Reserve System">Fed</a> members exercised from late 1987 to 2000.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenspan_put&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "put" refers to a <a href="/wiki/Put_option" title="Put option">put option</a>, a contractual obligation giving its holder the right to sell an asset at a particular price to a counterparty. The put option can be exercised if asset prices decline below that put price, protecting the holder from further losses. During Greenspan's chairmanship, when a crisis arose and the stock market fell more than about 20%, the Fed would lower the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fed_Funds_Rate" title="Fed Funds Rate">Fed Funds rate</a>, often resulting in a negative real yield. In essence, the Fed added monetary liquidity and encouraged risk-taking in the financial markets to avert further deterioration.
</p><p>The Fed did so after the <a href="/wiki/Black_Monday_(1987)" title="Black Monday (1987)">1987 stock market crash</a>, which prompted traders to coin the term Greenspan Put, later termed <a href="/wiki/Moral_hazard" title="Moral hazard">moral hazard</a>. In 2000, Alan Greenspan raised interest rates several times. These actions were believed by some to have caused the bursting of the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">dot-com bubble</a>. The Fed also injected funds to avert further market declines associated with the <a href="/wiki/Savings_and_loan_crisis" title="Savings and loan crisis">savings and loan crisis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1994_economic_crisis_in_Mexico" title="1994 economic crisis in Mexico">Mexican crisis</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1997_Asian_Financial_Crisis" title="1997 Asian Financial Crisis">Asian financial crisis</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Long-Term_Capital_Management" title="Long-Term Capital Management">LTCM</a> crisis, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Y2K" title="Y2K">Y2K</a>, the burst of the <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">internet bubble</a>, the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">9/11 attacks</a>, and repeatedly from the early stages of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_Financial_Crisis" title="Global Financial Crisis">Global Financial Crisis</a> to the present.
</p><p>The Fed's pattern of providing ample liquidity resulted in the investor perception of put protection on asset prices. Investors increasingly believed that in a crisis or downturn, the Fed would step in and inject liquidity until the problem got better.  Invariably, the Fed did so each time, and the perception became firmly embedded in asset pricing in the form of higher valuation, narrower credit spreads, and excess risk taking.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stiglitz" title="Joseph Stiglitz">Joseph Stiglitz</a> criticized the put as privatizing profits and socializing losses and implicates it in inflating a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speculative_bubble" title="Speculative bubble">speculative bubble</a> in the lead-up to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Financial_crisis_of_2007%E2%80%932010" title="Financial crisis of 2007–2010">2008 financial crisis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bernanke_Put">Bernanke Put</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenspan_put&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bernanke Put">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2007 and early 2008, the financial press had begun discussing the <i>Bernanke Put</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> as new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Board" title="Federal Reserve Board">Federal Reserve Board</a> chairman, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Bernanke" title="Ben Bernanke">Ben Bernanke</a> continued the practice of reducing interest rates to fight market falls.  The decision by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Board" title="Federal Reserve Board">Fed</a> to lower short-term interest rates to 50 basis points (0.5%) on October 8, 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NY_Times_Oct_08_2008_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times_Oct_08_2008-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NY_Times2_Oct_08_2008_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NY_Times2_Oct_08_2008-8">[8]</a></sup> and thereafter a range from 0.00-0.25% rate in December 2008 suggests attempts to create a Bernanke put similar to the Greenspan put.  New steps in <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_easing" title="Quantitative easing">quantitative easing</a> further illustrate the Fed's attempt to moderate the <a href="/wiki/Business_cycle" title="Business cycle">business cycle</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.  Recent (post March 2011) declines in measures of velocity and related declines in monetary growth measures suggest there is a limit to <a href="/wiki/Market_manipulation" title="Market manipulation">market manipulation</a>.
</p>
