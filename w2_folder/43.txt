</p>

<h2><span id="Indian_Green_Building_Council_.28IGBC.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_Green_Building_Council_(IGBC)">Indian Green Building Council (IGBC)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Indian Green Building Council (IGBC)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Indian Green Building Council (IGBC) was formed by the Confederation of Indian Industry (CII) in 2001. The council is based out of the CII Green Business Centre, Hyderabad which is India’s 1st Platinum rated green building. The vision of the council is to enable ‘Sustainable built environment for all. 
</p><p>IGBC is the country's premier body for green building certification and allied services. Today, with strong support from various stakeholders, we have achieved the following significant milestones:
</p>
<ul><li>4,400+ projects registered with IGBC from various parts of India and abroad, amounting to a total footprint of 4.72 billion sq. ft.</li>
<li>22 IGBC green building ratings to cover all typologies of projects -  residential, commercial, industrial, healthcare, etc</li>
<li>2,100+ IGBC Member Organizations comprising developers, corporates, architects, consultants, institutes, government, etc</li>
<li>2,800+ qualified IGBC Accredited Green Building Professionals ; more than 30,000 stakeholders have been trained by IGBC till date</li></ul>
<p>IGBC certifies the green projects which are conceptualized, designed, constructed and operated as per <a class="external text" href="https://igbc.in/igbc/redirectHtml.htm?redVal=showratingSysnosign" rel="nofollow">IGBC Ratings</a>. Till date, more than 1,250 projects (approx 400 million sq ft) have been rated by IGBC. These IGBC-rated projects, as compared to conventional structures, have demonstrated tremendous savings to the extent of up to:
</p>
<ul><li>15,000 MWh of Energy per million sq ft. per annum</li>
<li>45,000 KL of Water per million sq ft. per annum</li>
<li>Installation of 30 MW of Renewable Energy in IGBC certified projects</li>
<li>Reduced CO2 emissions by 12,000 tons per million sq ft. per annum</li>
<li>Diverted 500 Tons of construction waste from landfill per million sq ft.</li></ul>
<p>Green projects rated by IGBC fall under one of the following levels (in ascending order): Certified, Silver, Gold and Platinum. For further details, please refer the IGBC website, <a class="external text" href="https://igbc.in/igbc/" rel="nofollow">www.igbc.in</a>
</p><p><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="IGBC_Green_league_.28IGL.29_by_IGBC_Mumbai_Chapter"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="IGBC_Green_league_(IGL)_by_IGBC_Mumbai_Chapter">IGBC Green league (IGL) by IGBC Mumbai Chapter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: IGBC Green league (IGL) by IGBC Mumbai Chapter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The foremost objective of IGL is "Learning &amp; Growing : Together". The particular initiative has taken by IGBC Mumbai Chapter.
</p><p>Residential buildings in India:
The residential building sector is one of the largest consumers of electricity in India. Continuous urbanisation and the growth of population result in increasing power consumption in buildings. Thus, while experts express the huge potential for energy conservations in this sector, the belief still predominates among stakeholders that energy-efficient buildings are more expensive than conventional buildings, which adversely affects the “greening” of the building sector. This belief is contested by studies finding evidence for the opposite being the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="EDGE_Program_in_India">EDGE Program in India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: EDGE Program in India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Finance_Corporation" title="International Finance Corporation">IFC</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank_Group" title="World Bank Group">World Bank Group</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CREDAI" title="CREDAI">Confederation of Real Estate Developers' Associations of India (CREDAI)</a>, apex body of private real estate developers, have partnered to promote green buildings in the country through IFC’s <a class="external text" href="https://www.edgebuildings.com" rel="nofollow">EDGE certification</a>. An MoU was signed in the presence of Minister for Environment and Forests <a href="/wiki/Prakash_Javadekar" title="Prakash Javadekar">Prakash Javadekar</a> on<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> November 25, 2014.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="BEE_certification">BEE certification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: BEE certification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Indian <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Energy_Efficiency" title="Bureau of Energy Efficiency">Bureau of Energy Efficiency</a> (BEE) launched the Energy Conservation Building Code (ECBC). The code is set for energy efficiency standards for design and construction with any building of minimum conditioned area of 1,000 m<sup>2</sup> and a connected demand of power of 500 KW or 600 KVA.  The energy performance index of the code is set from 90 kW·h/sqm/year to 200 kW·h/sqm/year where any buildings that fall under the index can be termed as <b>"ECBC Compliant Building"</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Moreover, the BEE launched a five-star rating scheme for office buildings operated only in the day time in three climatic zones, composite, hot&amp;dry, warm&amp;humid on 25 February 2009. IGBC rated green buildings are also able to meet or exceed the ECBC compliance. The CII Sohrabji Godrej Green Business Centre is a BEE 5 star-rated building.
</p><p>The Reserve Bank of India's buildings in <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a>, Bhubaneshwar in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orissa,_India" title="Orissa, India">Orissa</a> and in Kerala have been star rated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Tamil Nadu 11 buildings were star rated by BEE, in the year 2010, including RBI buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_houses">Green houses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Green houses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Tamil Nadu, the government is planning to build solar-powered green houses for rural poor. It has allotted Rs.1058
crore for construction of 60,000 houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Maharashtra, near Mumbai in the Thane District, Govardhan Eco Village, a community in India, has built buildings with compressed stabilized Earth blocks, Rammed Earth Technique, Cob Houses(ADOBE Bricks) with traditional thatched roofs. These buildings have received a five-star rating from GRIHA, an Indian Nationwide Green Standards for Buildings, a wing of the famous TERI.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_buildings">Traditional buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_building_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Traditional buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditional buildings were energy efficient because architecture depended on the places. Buildings in the hot and dry regions, had corridors directing the wind to cool naturally. In wet regions, structures using natural light and breeze, were used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Some examples are
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Hawa_Mahal" title="Hawa Mahal">Hawa Mahal</a> - Articulated windows provides cool breeze in a desert area<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Golkonda" title="Golkonda">Golkonda</a> - Ventilation is designed to let in fresh cool breeze, in spite of summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The traditional building practices were utilized in constructing the <a href="/wiki/Dhyanalinga" title="Dhyanalinga">Dhyanalinga</a>. Mud mortar stabilized with lime, sand, alum and some herbal additives was used.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
