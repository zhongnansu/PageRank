<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NASA_earth_energy_budget.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="556" data-file-width="744" height="239" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/NASA_earth_energy_budget.gif/320px-NASA_earth_energy_budget.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/NASA_earth_energy_budget.gif/480px-NASA_earth_energy_budget.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/NASA_earth_energy_budget.gif/640px-NASA_earth_energy_budget.gif 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NASA_earth_energy_budget.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Earth's energy budget</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>greenhouse effect</b> is the process by which radiation from a planet's atmosphere warms the planet's surface to a temperature above what it would be without its atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipccar4syr_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipccar4syr-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc-AR4WG1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc-AR4WG1-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>If a planet's atmosphere contains radiatively active gases (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>) they will radiate energy in all directions. Part of this radiation is directed towards the surface, warming it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
The intensity of the downward radiation – that is, the strength of the greenhouse effect – will depend on the atmosphere's temperature and on the amount of greenhouse gases that the atmosphere contains.
</p><p>Earth’s natural greenhouse effect is critical to supporting life. Human activities, mainly the burning of fossil fuels and clearing of forests, have strengthened the greenhouse effect and caused <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "greenhouse effect" arose from a faulty analogy with the effect of sunlight passing through glass and warming a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a>. The way a greenhouse retains heat is fundamentally different, as a greenhouse works mostly by reducing airflow so that warm air is kept inside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ipcc-AR4WG1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc-AR4WG1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schroeder_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schroeder-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wood1909_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wood1909-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_climate_change_science" title="History of climate change science">History of climate change science</a></div>
<p>The existence of the greenhouse effect was argued for by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fourier" title="Joseph Fourier">Joseph Fourier</a> in 1824. The argument and the evidence were further strengthened by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Pouillet" title="Claude Pouillet">Claude Pouillet</a> in 1827 and 1838 and reasoned from experimental observations by <a href="/wiki/John_Tyndall" title="John Tyndall">John Tyndall</a> in 1859, who measured the radiative properties of specific greenhouse gases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The effect was more fully quantified by <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a> in 1896, who made the first quantitative prediction of global warming due to a hypothetical doubling of atmospheric carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anrev_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-anrev-8">[8]</a></sup> However, the term "greenhouse" was not used to refer to this effect by any of these scientists; the term was first used in this way by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nils_Ekholm" title="Nils Ekholm">Nils Gustaf Ekholm</a> in 1901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span></h2>
<p>Earth receives energy from the Sun in the form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV" title="UV">ultraviolet</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">visible</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-infrared" title="Near-infrared">near-infrared</a> radiation. About 26% of the incoming solar energy is reflected to space by the atmosphere and clouds, and 19% is absorbed by the atmosphere and clouds. Most of the remaining energy is absorbed at the surface of Earth. <a href="/wiki/Wien%27s_displacement_law" title="Wien's displacement law">Because the Earth's surface is colder</a> than the Sun, it radiates at <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">wavelengths that are much longer</a> than the wavelengths that were absorbed. Most of this thermal radiation is absorbed by the atmosphere and warms it. The atmosphere also gains heat by sensible and latent heat fluxes from the surface. The atmosphere radiates energy both upwards and downwards; the part radiated downwards is absorbed by the surface of Earth. This leads to a higher <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equilibrium_temperature" title="Equilibrium temperature">equilibrium temperature</a> than if the atmosphere were absent.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Spectrum.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="595" data-file-width="800" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Solar_Spectrum.png/320px-Solar_Spectrum.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Solar_Spectrum.png/480px-Solar_Spectrum.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Solar_Spectrum.png/640px-Solar_Spectrum.png 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Spectrum.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> spectrum for direct light at both the top of Earth's atmosphere and at sea level</div></div></div>
<p>An ideal thermally conductive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blackbody" title="Blackbody">blackbody</a> at the same distance from the Sun as Earth would have a temperature of about 5.3 °C (41.5 °F). However, because Earth reflects about 30%<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harvard_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-harvard-12">[12]</a></sup> of the incoming sunlight, this idealized planet's <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">effective temperature</a> (the temperature of a blackbody that would emit the same amount of radiation) would be about −18 °C (0 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC4_ch01_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC4_ch01-14">[14]</a></sup>  The surface temperature of this hypothetical planet is 33 °C (91 °F) below Earth's actual surface temperature of approximately 14 °C (57 °F).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The basic mechanism can be qualified in a number of ways, none of which affect the fundamental process. The atmosphere near the surface is largely opaque to thermal radiation (with important exceptions for "window" bands), and most heat loss from the surface is by <a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">sensible heat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> transport. Radiative energy losses become increasingly important higher in the atmosphere, largely because of the decreasing concentration of water vapor, an important greenhouse gas. It is more realistic to think of the greenhouse effect as applying to a "surface" in the mid-<a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, which is effectively coupled to the surface by a <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rate</a>. The simple picture also assumes a steady state, but in the real world, there are variations due to the <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_cycle" title="Diurnal cycle">diurnal cycle</a> as well as the seasonal cycle and weather disturbances. Solar heating only applies during daytime. During the night, the atmosphere cools somewhat, but not greatly, because its emissivity is low. <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">Diurnal temperature changes</a> decrease with height in the atmosphere.
</p><p>Within the region where radiative effects are important, the description given by the <a href="/wiki/Idealized_greenhouse_model" title="Idealized greenhouse model">idealized greenhouse model</a> becomes realistic. Earth's surface, warmed to a temperature around 255 K, radiates long-wavelength, <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> heat in the range of 4–100 μm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell-1989_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell-1989-16">[16]</a></sup> At these wavelengths, greenhouse gases that were largely transparent to incoming solar radiation are more absorbent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell-1989_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell-1989-16">[16]</a></sup> Each layer of atmosphere with greenhouses gases absorbs some of the heat being radiated upwards from lower layers. It reradiates in all directions, both upwards and downwards; in equilibrium (by definition) the same amount as it has absorbed. This results in more warmth below. Increasing the concentration of the gases increases the amount of absorption and reradiation, and thereby further warms the layers and ultimately the surface below.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC4_ch01_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC4_ch01-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Greenhouse gases—including most diatomic gases with two different atoms (such as carbon monoxide, CO) and all gases with three or more atoms—are able to absorb and emit infrared radiation. Though more than 99% of the dry atmosphere is IR transparent (because the main constituents—<span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>, and Ar—are not able to directly absorb or emit infrared radiation), intermolecular collisions cause the energy absorbed and emitted by the greenhouse gases to be shared with the other, non-IR-active, gases.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gases">Greenhouse gases</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">Greenhouse gas</a></div>
<p>By their percentage contribution to the greenhouse effect on Earth the four major gases are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-realclimate.org_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-realclimate.org-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kiehl197_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-kiehl197-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:722px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="107" data-file-width="720" height="107" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/57/CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png" width="720"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CO2_H2O_absorption_atmospheric_gases_unique_pattern_energy_wavelengths_of_energy_transparent_to_others.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Atmospheric gases only absorb some wavelengths of energy but are transparent to others. The absorption patterns of water vapor (blue peaks) and carbon dioxide (pink peaks) overlap in some wavelengths. Carbon dioxide is not as strong a greenhouse gas as water vapor, but it absorbs energy in longer wavelengths (12–15 micrometers) that water vapor does not, partially closing the "window" through which heat radiated by the surface would normally escape to space. (Illustration NASA, Robert Rohde)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_climate_forcings_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_climate_forcings-19">[19]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a>, 36–70%</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, 9–26%</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, 4–9%</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, 3–7%</li></ul>
<p>It is not possible to assign a specific percentage to each gas because the absorption and emission bands of the gases overlap (hence the ranges given above). <a href="/wiki/Cloud_forcing" title="Cloud forcing">Clouds</a> also absorb and emit infrared radiation and thus affect the radiative properties of the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kiehl197_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-kiehl197-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_climate_change">Role in climate change</span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="708" data-file-width="708" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg/320px-Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg/480px-Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg/640px-Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg.png 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Keeling_Curve" title="Keeling Curve">Keeling Curve</a> of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> concentrations measured at <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa_Observatory" title="Mauna Loa Observatory">Mauna Loa Observatory</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Strengthening of the greenhouse effect through human activities is known as the enhanced (or <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a>) greenhouse effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>  This increase in <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> from human activity is attributable mainly to increased atmospheric carbon dioxide levels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> According to the latest Assessment Report from the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>, "<i>atmospheric concentrations of carbon dioxide, methane and nitrous oxide are unprecedented in at least the last 800,000 years. Their effects, together with those of other anthropogenic drivers, have been detected throughout the climate system and are extremely likely to have been the dominant cause of the observed warming since the mid-20th century</i>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>CO<sub>2</sub> is produced by fossil fuel burning and other activities such as <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> production and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tropical_deforestation" title="Tropical deforestation">tropical deforestation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC_deforestation_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_deforestation-23">[23]</a></sup>  Measurements of CO<sub>2</sub> from the Mauna Loa observatory show that concentrations have increased from about 313 parts per million (ppm)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> in 1960 to about 389 ppm in 2010. It reached the 400 ppm milestone on May 9, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>  The current observed amount of CO<sub>2</sub> exceeds the geological record maxima (~300 ppm) from ice core data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The effect of combustion-produced carbon dioxide on the global climate, a special case of the greenhouse effect first described in 1896 by <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a>, has also been called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Callendar_effect" title="Callendar effect">Callendar effect</a>.
</p><p>Over the past 800,000 years,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide#Variation_in_the_past" title="Carbon dioxide">ice core data</a> shows that carbon dioxide has varied from values as low as 180 ppm to the pre-industrial level of 270 ppm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleoclimatologists" title="Paleoclimatologists">Paleoclimatologists</a> consider variations in carbon dioxide concentration to be a fundamental factor influencing climate variations over this time scale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowen_(2005),_Thin_Ice_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bowen_(2005),_Thin_Ice-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Real_greenhouses">Real greenhouses</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RHSGlasshouse.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1953" data-file-width="2628" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/RHSGlasshouse.JPG/320px-RHSGlasshouse.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/RHSGlasshouse.JPG/480px-RHSGlasshouse.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/RHSGlasshouse.JPG/640px-RHSGlasshouse.JPG 2x" width="320"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RHSGlasshouse.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A modern <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">Greenhouse</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wisley_Garden" title="Wisley Garden">RHS Wisley</a></div></div></div>
<p>The "greenhouse effect" of the atmosphere is named by analogy to <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouses</a> which become warmer in sunlight. However, a greenhouse is not primarily warmed by the "greenhouse effect".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
"Greenhouse effect" is actually a misnomer since heating in the usual greenhouse is due to the reduction of <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> while the "greenhouse effect" works by preventing absorbed heat from leaving the structure through <a href="/wiki/Radiative_transfer" title="Radiative transfer">radiative transfer</a>.
</p><p>A greenhouse is built of any material that passes sunlight usually glass, or plastic. The sun warms the ground and contents inside just like the outside, which then warms the air. Outside, the warm air near the surface rises and mixes with cooler air aloft, keeping the temperature lower than inside, where the air continues to heat up because it is confined within the greenhouse. This can be demonstrated by opening a small window near the roof of a greenhouse: the temperature will drop considerably. It was demonstrated experimentally (<a href="/wiki/Robert_W._Wood" title="Robert W. Wood">R. W. Wood</a>, 1909) that a (not heated) "greenhouse" with a cover of <a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">rock salt</a> (which is transparent to infrared) heats up an enclosure similarly to one with a glass cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wood1909_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-wood1909-6">[6]</a></sup> Thus greenhouses work primarily by preventing <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> cooling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schroeder_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schroeder-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Heated greenhouses are yet another matter, having an internal source of heating they leak heat out, which must be prevented. So it again makes sense to try to prevent radiative cooling through the use of adequate glazing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_effects">Related effects</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-greenhouse_effect">Anti-greenhouse effect</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Anti-greenhouse_effect" title="Anti-greenhouse effect">Anti-greenhouse effect</a></div>
<p>The anti-greenhouse effect is a mechanism similar and symmetrical to the greenhouse effect: greenhouse effect is about atmosphere letting radiation in, while not letting thermal radiation out, which warms the body surface; anti-greenhouse effect is about atmosphere NOT letting radiation in, while letting thermal radiation out, which lowers the equilibrium surface temperature. Such an effect has been cited about <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Runaway_greenhouse_effect">Runaway greenhouse effect</span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect" title="Runaway greenhouse effect">runaway greenhouse effect</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect" title="Runaway greenhouse effect">runaway greenhouse effect</a> occurs if <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedbacks</a> lead to the evaporation of all greenhouse gases into the atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasting_1991_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kasting_1991-35">[35]</a></sup> A runaway greenhouse effect involving carbon dioxide and water vapor has long ago been hypothesized to have occurred on Venus,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">this idea is still largely accepted</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bodies_other_than_Earth">Bodies other than Earth</span></h2>
<p>The greenhouse effect on Venus is particularly large because its dense atmosphere consists mainly of carbon dioxide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
"Venus experienced a runaway greenhouse in the past, and we expect that Earth will in about 2 billion years as solar luminosity increases".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Titan has an anti-greenhouse effect, in that its atmosphere absorbs solar radiation but is relatively transparent to outgoing infrared radiation.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Pluto" title="Atmosphere of Pluto">Pluto</a> is also colder than would be expected because evaporation of nitrogen cools it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
