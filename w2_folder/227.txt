<p><b>Evergreen Park</b> is a village in <a href="/wiki/Cook_County,_Illinois" title="Cook County, Illinois">Cook County, Illinois</a>, United States. In 2010, the population of Evergreen Park was 19,852, according to that year's census.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_DP_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010_DP-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Park,_Illinois&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As early as 1828, a German farming family had settled in the area of what is now Evergreen Park. In the succeeding decades, other German immigrants arrived. Kedzie Avenue and 95th Street crisscrossed the farmland and provided access to markets.
</p><p>The first railroad (now the Grand Trunk Railroad) came through the area in 1873. In 1875, the community built its first school just west of 95th and Kedzie. The school and the stores that began to cluster around this intersection defined the community's main business area. Nearby, a real-estate developer, with a vision of the Arc de Triomphe area of Paris, laid out a star-shaped park with eight streets radiating from it. The evergreen trees planted in the park inspired the village's name. The location and layout of the park was intended to be the center of town, but 95th St and Kedzie Ave. later proved a more accurate midpoint.  After the death of Mayor Henry Klein shortly after the village's 75th anniversary, the park was renamed Klein Park in his honor. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1888 St. Mary's Cemetery opened, and mourners traveled by train from Chicago. Restaurants and taverns were created to provide meals for cemetery visitors. Within five years, the village had become a recreation center that attracted hundreds of Chicagoans to its picnic groves, beer gardens, and dance halls.  The first of the village's 13 churches was established in 1893. 
</p><p>As a result of the financial panic of the 1890s, several surrounding communities voted to be annexed by Chicago.  Realizing the current and future potential of its strong business district, and  in order to avoid annexation during the serious economic crisis, The Village of Evergreen Park declared its independence and was incorporated on December 20, 1893.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evergreenpark-ill.com_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-evergreenpark-ill.com-5">[5]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup> Prior to its incorporation, the village was sustained by approximately 500 regional residents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evergreenpark-ill.com_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-evergreenpark-ill.com-5">[5]</a></sup> The final decision to incorporate as its own entity separate from the City of Chicago was made by 41 out of 50 residents that showed up to vote on the matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evergreenpark-ill.com_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-evergreenpark-ill.com-5">[5]</a></sup>  John M. Foley, a real estate and insurance agent, became the village's first mayor. On that day, the Village of Evergreen Park officially occupied an area of four square miles; it now covers an area of the same size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evergreenpark-ill.com_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-evergreenpark-ill.com-5">[5]</a></sup>   In 1899, telephone service was introduced to the community.  In 1910, gas and electric lines were extended into homes and street lights were erected.   By 1920, most of the village's homes had indoor plumbing, although some residents still used a well located behind the village hall as their water source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 20th century, many residents still farmed and there were many open fields within the town limits.  As a result, fire was a constant threat and the water supply was scarce.  In July of 1918, a spark from a passing train set the original village hall on fire.  Despite villagers attempts to douse the flames, the village hall was destroyed.  In 1920, a new village hall was built and the village's population grew to 800.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1930, Little Company of Mary Hospital was opened at 95th and California.  Within the first year of its inception, 232 babies were born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>While the village remains small in size, it is only seventeen miles southwest of the Loop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-evergreenpark-ill.com_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-evergreenpark-ill.com-5">[5]</a></sup> The Village is also currently surrounded by Chicago on the north, south, and east sides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopedia.chicagohistory.org_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia.chicagohistory.org-6">[6]</a></sup> Evergreen Park is also known as the “Village of Churches” because of its thirteen established religious congregations within close proximity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encyclopedia.chicagohistory.org_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-encyclopedia.chicagohistory.org-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_successful_organ_transplant">First successful organ transplant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Park,_Illinois&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First successful organ transplant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Little_Company_of_Mary_Hospital_(Evergreen_Park)" title="Little Company of Mary Hospital (Evergreen Park)">Little Company of Mary Hospital</a>, located at 2800 W. 95th St. in Evergreen Park, was the site of the world's first successful organ transplant.  The pioneering procedure took place on June 17, 1950.   Dr. Richard H. Lawler (1896–1982), a highly regarded general surgeon, led a team of doctors that performed the highly risky, controversial, and experimental operation.   Dr. Lawler spent a number of years researching the possibility of organ transplantation and concluded the most probable means of achieving success for a patient would involve the kidney.   After successfully practicing the procedure with dogs and realizing he had the opportunity to "get it all started", Lawler decided to attempt the medical-first on a desperate patient.  The recipient of Lawler's first organ transplantation was Ruth Tucker, a 49-year-old Chicago-area woman who suffered from terminal <a href="/wiki/Polycystic_kidney_disease" title="Polycystic kidney disease">polycystic kidney disease.</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_landmarks">Historical landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Park,_Illinois&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dt>Evergreen Plaza, "The Plaza"</dt></dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/The_Plaza_(mall)" title="The Plaza (mall)">Evergreen Plaza</a>, located on 95th and Western, was an indoor shopping mall originating from the early 1950s. In 1952, real estate developer <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rubloff" title="Arthur Rubloff">Arthur Rubloff</a> debuted the Evergreen Plaza in the heart of the southwest Chicago suburbs. A few years after the shopping mall’s debut, Rubloff decided to enclose the mall thereby making it the first indoor shopping mall in the Chicago area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theplazamall.org_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-theplazamall.org-11">[11]</a></sup> As a result, Rubloff changed shopping by allowing people the opportunity to pull up, park, and shop for various goods all in one place. Since the 1950s the Evergreen Park Plaza had seen more than $8 million in major internal &amp; external improvements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theplazamall.org_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-theplazamall.org-11">[11]</a></sup> Evergreen Plaza was shortened to be acknowledged as, “The Plaza”. The Plaza covered 1,200,000 square feet (110,000 m<sup>2</sup>), and two stories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-12">[12]</a></sup> As of 2006, The Plaza had an annual visitor count of roughly 7 million people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-12">[12]</a></sup> The Plaza closed on May 31, 2013, after 61 years of operation and is presently under construction for re-development. The Plaza, in its new form, will re-open on October 18, 2018 and will be renamed Eddie Maloney Plaza of Evergreen Park.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Park,_Illinois&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Evergreen Park is located at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Evergreen_Park,_Illinois&amp;params=41_43_12_N_87_42_9_W_region:US_type:city"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">41°43′12″N</span> <span class="longitude">87°42′9″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">41.72000°N 87.70250°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">41.72000; -87.70250</span></span></span></a></span> (41.719933, −87.702499).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GR1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-GR1-13">[13]</a></sup> The suburb is surrounded by the city of Chicago on three of its sides, while <a href="/wiki/Oak_Lawn,_Illinois" title="Oak Lawn, Illinois">Oak Lawn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hometown,_Illinois" title="Hometown, Illinois">Hometown</a> border it on the west. Chicago's <a href="/wiki/Ashburn,_Chicago" title="Ashburn, Chicago">Ashburn</a> community is to its north, <a href="/wiki/Beverly,_Chicago" title="Beverly, Chicago">Beverly</a> is to its east, and Beverly and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Greenwood,_Chicago" title="Mount Greenwood, Chicago">Mount Greenwood</a> are to its south.
</p><p>According to the 2010 census, Evergreen Park has a total area of 3.16 square miles (8.18 km<sup>2</sup>), all land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-census-g001_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-census-g001-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a> of 2010, there were 19,852 people, 7,192 households, and 5,130 families residing in the village.  The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 6,282.3 inhabitants per square mile (2,425.6/km²). There were 7,559 housing units at an average density of 2,392.1 per square mile (921.8/km²). The racial makeup of the village was 74.1% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 18.7% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 0.2% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 1.2% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.02% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 3.8% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" title="Race (United States Census)">some other race</a>, and 2.0% from two or more races. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 10.3% of the population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_DP_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010_DP-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>There were 7,192 households, out of which 36.4% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 49.9% were headed by married couples living together, 16.6% had a female householder with no husband present, and 28.7% were non-families. 25.1% of all households were made up of individuals, and 10.7% were someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.74, and the average family size was 3.31.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_DP_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010_DP-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the village, the population comprised 25.3% under the age of 18, 8.8% from 18 to 24, 24.3% from 25 to 44, 28.6% from 45 to 64, and 13.1% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 38.6 years. For every 100 females, there were 91.2 males. For every 100 females aged 18 or over, there were 86.6 males.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Census_2010_DP_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010_DP-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>For the period 2007–11, the estimated median annual income for a household in the village was $64,602, and the median income for a family was $80,395. Male full-time workers had a median income of $60,928 versus $43,884 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the village was $28,499. About 5.6% of families and 6.9% of the population were below the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_line" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 9.1% of those under age 18 and 5.3% of those age 65 or over.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_politics">Government and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Park,_Illinois&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Government and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Evergreen Park is in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Illinois%27_1st_congressional_district" title="Illinois' 1st congressional district">Illinois' 1st congressional district</a>, and its congressman is Democrat <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Rush" title="Bobby Rush">Bobby Rush</a>. The village backed <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> by a margin of 61.25% to 37.40% over <a href="/wiki/John_McCain" title="John McCain">John McCain</a> in 2008. Evergreen Park leans moderately Democratic as <a href="/wiki/John_Kerry" title="John Kerry">John Kerry</a> beat <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> here 55.77% to 43.40% in 2004. This is slightly more Democratic than in 2000 when Bush lost to <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> 51.13% to 45.60% in the village. The most Republican area of the village is the Southwest quadrant which went for Bush both years. (In 2000 Bush won 51.29% to 45.24%, and in 2004, Bush won 49.91% to 49.31%.).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
