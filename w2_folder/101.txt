<p><b>Linda Joyce Greenhouse</b> (born January 9, 1947) is the Knight Distinguished Journalist in Residence and Joseph M. Goldstein Lecturer in Law at Yale Law School.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> She is a <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>-winning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reporter" title="Reporter">reporter</a> who covered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> for nearly three decades for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT20080714_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT20080714-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Linda_Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greenhouse was born to a <a href="/wiki/American_Jews" title="American Jews">Jewish</a> family<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, to H. Robert Greenhouse, a physician and professor of psychiatry at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Medical_School" title="Harvard Medical School">Harvard Medical School</a>, and Dorothy (née Greenlick). She received her <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> degree in government from <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_College" title="Radcliffe College">Radcliffe College</a> in 1968, where she was elected to <a href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa" title="Phi Beta Kappa">Phi Beta Kappa</a>. She received her Master of Studies in Law<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YaleMSL_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-YaleMSL-5">[5]</a></sup> from <a href="/wiki/Yale_Law_School" title="Yale Law School">Yale Law School</a> in 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pulitzer_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pulitzer-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>She married lawyer <a href="/wiki/Eugene_R._Fidell" title="Eugene R. Fidell">Eugene R. Fidell</a> on January 1, 1981 in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, in a <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jewish</a> ceremony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Together they have one daughter, filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Hannah_Fidell" title="Hannah Fidell">Hannah Margalit Fidell</a> (born October 7, 1985).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Linda_Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greenhouse began her 40-year career at <i>The New York Times</i> covering state government in the paper's bureau in <a href="/wiki/Albany,_New_York" title="Albany, New York">Albany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT20080714_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT20080714-2">[2]</a></sup> After completing her master's degree on a <a href="/wiki/Ford_Foundation" title="Ford Foundation">Ford Foundation</a> fellowship, she returned to the <i>Times</i> and covered 29 sessions of the Supreme Court from 1978 to 2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT20080713_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT20080713-9">[9]</a></sup> with the exception of two years during the mid-1980s during which she covered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_United_States" title="Congress of the United States">Congress</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pulitzer_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-pulitzer-6">[6]</a></sup> Since 1981, she has published over 2,800 articles in the <i>Times.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> She has been a regular guest on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Broadcasting_Service" title="Public Broadcasting Service">PBS</a> program <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Week" title="Washington Week">Washington Week</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, Greenhouse accepted an offer from <i>The Times</i> for an early retirement at the end of the Supreme Court session in the summer of 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LegalTimes20080612_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-LegalTimes20080612-13">[13]</a></sup> Seven of the nine sitting Justices attended a goodbye party for Greenhouse on June 12, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LegalTimes20080612_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-LegalTimes20080612-13">[13]</a></sup>  She continues to blog for <i>The Times</i> in the "Opinionator" section.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, Greenhouse and co-author <a href="/wiki/Reva_Siegel" title="Reva Siegel">Reva Siegel</a> put out a book on the development of the abortion debate prior to the 1973 <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a> ruling on the subject: <i>Before Roe v. Wade</i>. This was largely a selection of primary documents, though with some commentary.
</p><p>Greenhouse criticized US policies and actions at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_detainment_camp" title="Guantanamo Bay detainment camp">Guantanamo Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abu_Ghraib" title="Abu Ghraib">Abu Ghraib</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Haditha" title="Haditha">Haditha</a> in a 2006 speech at Harvard University.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-folkenflik_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-folkenflik-15">[15]</a></sup> In it, Greenhouse said she started crying a few years back at a <a href="/wiki/Simon_%26_Garfunkel" title="Simon &amp; Garfunkel">Simon &amp; Garfunkel</a> concert because her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baby_Boomers" title="Baby Boomers">generation</a> hadn't done a better job of running the country than previous generations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radcliffe_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-radcliffe-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_prizes">Awards and prizes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Linda_Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Awards and prizes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Greenhouse was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulitzer_prize" title="Pulitzer prize">Pulitzer Prize in Journalism</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beat_Reporting" title="Beat Reporting">Beat Reporting</a>) in 1998 "for her consistently illuminating coverage of the United States Supreme Court."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pulitzer_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-pulitzer-6">[6]</a></sup>  In 2004, she received the Goldsmith Career Award for Excellence in Journalism<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/John_Chancellor_Award" title="John Chancellor Award">John Chancellor Award</a> for Excellence in Journalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> She was a Radcliffe Institute Medal winner in 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>When she was at Radcliffe, she said in a speech given in 2006, "I was the Harvard stringer for the <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Herald" title="Boston Herald">Boston Herald</a></i>, which regularly printed, and paid me for, my accounts of student unrest and other newsworthy events at Harvard. But when it came time during my senior year to look for a job in journalism, the <i>Herald</i> would not even give me an interview, and neither would the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Globe" title="Boston Globe">Boston Globe</a></i>, because these newspapers had no interest in hiring women."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-radcliffe_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-radcliffe-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Greenhouse has expressed her personal views as an outspoken advocate for abortion rights and critic of conservative religious values,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-folkenflik_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-folkenflik-15">[15]</a></sup> and a 2006 report on <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR</a> questioned whether this compromised the appearance that she maintains journalistic neutrality on such matters. <i>New York Times</i> public editor <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Okrent" title="Daniel Okrent">Daniel Okrent</a> said that he has never received a single complaint of bias in Greenhouse's coverage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-folkenflik_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-folkenflik-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Journalism_conference.2C_2007"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Journalism_conference,_2007">Journalism conference, 2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Linda_Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Journalism conference, 2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On August 9, 2007, a television crew from <a href="/wiki/C-SPAN" title="C-SPAN">C-SPAN</a> was forbidden to film a panel discussion at a meeting of the Association for Education in Journalism and Mass Communication. Greenhouse had told organizers that she would not be able answer questions as fully and frankly if the session were filmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BandC_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-BandC-20">[20]</a></sup> The vice president of programming at C-SPAN, Terence Murphy, questioned the decision: "If professors of journalism and working journalists taking part in a journalism education conference don't stand up for open media access to public policy discussions, who will?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Suggestions_of_conflict_of_interest.2C_2008"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Suggestions_of_conflict_of_interest,_2008">Suggestions of conflict of interest, 2008</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Linda_Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Suggestions of conflict of interest, 2008">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M._Edward_Whelan_III" title="M. Edward Whelan III">Ed Whelan</a>, writing in a blog associated with <i><a href="/wiki/National_Review" title="National Review">National Review</a></i>, suggested that Greenhouse had an obligation to her readers to inform them when reporting on a Supreme Court case that her husband <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_Fidell" title="Eugene Fidell">Eugene Fidell</a> had submitted an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amicus_curae" title="Amicus curae">amicus</a> brief:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bench_Memos_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bench_Memos-22">[22]</a></sup> He had submitted an amicus brief in the <a href="/wiki/Hamdan_v._Rumsfeld" title="Hamdan v. Rumsfeld"><i>Hamdan</i></a> case. Fidell also submitted an amicus brief in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boumediene_et_al_v._Bush" title="Boumediene et al v. Bush"><i>Boumediene</i></a> case when it was at the D.C. Circuit level before it went to the Supreme Court. <a href="/wiki/Clark_Hoyt" title="Clark Hoyt">Clark Hoyt</a>, the public editor of the <i>New York Times</i> at the time, opined that the paper "should have clued in readers" to Greenhouse's conflict, but defended the neutrality of her coverage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTPubEd_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTPubEd-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Emily_Bazelon" title="Emily Bazelon">Emily Bazelon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dahlia_Lithwick" title="Dahlia Lithwick">Dahlia Lithwick</a>, writing in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slate_magazine" title="Slate magazine">Slate magazine</a></i>, complained that the <i>New York Times</i> "had failed to stand up" for Greenhouse and defended Greenhouse from Whelan's criticism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate20080122_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slate20080122-24">[24]</a></sup> 
They quoted <a href="/wiki/Yale_Law_School" title="Yale Law School">Yale Law School</a> professor Judith Resnik who pointed out that Whelan had been unable to point to any actual sign of bias.
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Unable to point to any actual bias, Whelan resorts to the petulant claim that the effect of Fidell's involvement in the detainee cases "would be impossible to separate … from the broader political bias that pervades so much of Greenhouse's reporting.
</p></blockquote>
<p>In a rebuttal in the <i>National Review</i>, Whelan asserted both that Bazelon and Lithwick had resorted to "baseless ad hominem attacks and to (literally) catty comments about 'right-wing kitty cats.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bench_Memos_2_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bench_Memos_2-25">[25]</a></sup>  He then refutes the claim that he did not provide any actual examples of bias, and points readers to a previous article in his series on Greenhouse's alleged conflict of interest.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.nationalreview.com/bench-memos/51211/response-nyts-public-editor-greenhouse-conflict-mdash-part-1/ed-whelan" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>
</p>
