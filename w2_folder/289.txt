<p>The <b>Green Party of Ontario</b> (<b>GPO</b>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Parti vert de l'Ontario</i>) is a political party in <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada. The party is led by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Schreiner" title="Mike Schreiner">Mike Schreiner</a>. Until 2018, it had never held any seats in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontario_Legislative_Assembly" title="Ontario Legislative Assembly">Ontario Legislative Assembly</a>. Beforehand, the party did see significant gains in the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_2007" title="Ontario general election, 2007">2007 provincial election</a>, earning 8% of the popular vote with some candidates placing second and third in their ridings. Previous polling has identified support to be between 6% and 12% of decided voters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and on 14 June 2008, the GPO hit a milestone of 13% support in polling, matching the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_New_Democratic_Party" title="Ontario New Democratic Party">Ontario New Democratic Party</a> for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> On 7 June 2018 <a href="/wiki/Mike_Schreiner" title="Mike Schreiner">Mike Schreiner</a> was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Ontario" title="Legislative Assembly of Ontario">Legislative Assembly of Ontario</a> in the riding of <a href="/wiki/Guelph" title="Guelph">Guelph</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TorStarSch01_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-TorStarSch01-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPOSch01_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-GPOSch01-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Elections_Ontario" title="Elections Ontario">Elections Ontario</a> records that in the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_1999" title="Ontario general election, 1999">1999 provincial election</a>, the GPO fielded 58 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Party_candidates,_1999_Ontario_provincial_election" title="Green Party candidates, 1999 Ontario provincial election">candidates</a>, and became the fourth largest party in the province. In 2003, the party fielded its first nearly full slate, 102 out of 103 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Party_candidates,_2003_Ontario_provincial_election" title="Green Party candidates, 2003 Ontario provincial election">candidates</a>, and received 2.8% of the vote. In 2007, in what many consider the breakthrough election for the GPO, the party fielded a full slate of 107 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Party_candidates,_2007_Ontario_provincial_election" title="Green Party candidates, 2007 Ontario provincial election">candidates</a>, receiving over 8.0% and nearly 355,000 votes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The GPO had gained the most in the 2007 election and was one of only two parties that gained a significant amount of support. The rise in its political fortunes coincided with the national rise in support for the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">Green Party of Canada</a> during the same period.  Subsequently, the party's popularity declined in the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_2011" title="Ontario general election, 2011">2011</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_2014" title="Ontario general election, 2014">2014</a> elections during tightly contested races between the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Conservative_Party_of_Ontario" title="Progressive Conservative Party of Ontario">Progressive Conservatives</a> and ruling <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Liberal_Party" title="Ontario Liberal Party">Liberals</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_2018" title="Ontario general election, 2018">2018 election</a>, the party received 4.6% of the vote. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The late 1960s is widely seen as the start of the global ecological movement, however it wasn't until the 1970s that this movement began to gain political and economic legitimacy, with advances such as the founding of the world's first green party (New Zealand's <a href="/wiki/Values_Party" title="Values Party">Values Party</a>), and the entry of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/West_German" title="West German">West German</a> Greens (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Die Grünen</a>) into that country's legislature. The tiny, short-lived Small Party, named after <a href="/wiki/E._F._Schumacher" title="E. F. Schumacher">E. F. Schumacher</a>'s book <a href="/wiki/Small_Is_Beautiful" title="Small Is Beautiful">Small Is Beautiful</a>, formed in the Maritimes in the mid to late 1970s, and was the first party in the Western Hemisphere related to the green movement. This party was founded by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_May" title="Elizabeth May">Elizabeth May</a>, now the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">Green Party of Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the early 1980s, the idea of organized Green politics began to gain in international popularity, and in 1983 the Green Party of Ontario was registered with <a href="/wiki/Elections_Ontario" title="Elections Ontario">Elections Ontario</a>. Shortly after the GPO was registered it contested its first election, fielding nine candidates who collected a combined 5,300 votes or 0.14%. In the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_1987" title="Ontario general election, 1987">1987 election</a> the party again ran nine candidates who fared worse, collecting 3,400 votes or 0.09%. In 1990, to the surprise of many, the GPO captured a much higher result, with 40 candidates capturing 30,400 votes or 0.75%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GPO_logo.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="120" data-file-width="120" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/d7/GPO_logo.png" width="120"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GPO_logo.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The previous GPO logo</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frank_de_Jong_years">Frank de Jong years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Frank de Jong years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It wasn't until 1993, however, that the party began to properly organize itself, electing <a href="/wiki/Frank_de_Jong" title="Frank de Jong">Frank de Jong</a> as its first official leader. The GPO and their newly selected leader ran in their first election as an organized party in the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_1995" title="Ontario general election, 1995">1995 provincial election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However, even with a new leader and just three candidates less than 1990, the party lost more than half their support, falling to just 14,100 or 0.34%.
</p><p>De Jong led the party through three election campaigns, gradually building party support from less than one percent in the early 1990s to just over 8% in the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_2007" title="Ontario general election, 2007">2007 provincial election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_1999" title="Ontario general election, 1999">1999 provincial election</a> the party ran 57 candidates<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and collected 0.70% or 30,800 votes. With 17 more candidates the party fell 0.05% short of their 1990 result; however, this was a large increase compared to the previous election (1995). In addition, increased organization resulted in the addition of a Deputy Leader and a Shadow Cabinet. The first Deputy Leader of the Green Party of Ontario was Judy Greenwood-Speers. She served the party in this role from 1999 to 2002. Ms Greenwood-Speers was also the party's first Issue Advocate, continuously serving as the Advocate for Health and Long Term Care, and in the Senior's Secretariat from 1999 to today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontario_provincial_election,_2003" title="Ontario provincial election, 2003">2003 provincial election</a> was what many consider to be the first breakthrough for the Greens. Running 102 out of a possible 103 candidates the GPO was able to capture 126,700 votes, or 2.82%. The GPO placed ahead of the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_New_Democratic_Party" title="Ontario New Democratic Party">Ontario New Democratic Party</a> (NDP) in two ridings, and took fourth place in 92 others. Just eight candidates fared worse than fourth place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>De Jong announced his resignation as leader on 16 May 2009, at the Green Party of Ontario Annual General Meeting.  A leadership and policy convention was held 13–15 November 2009 in London, Ontario.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Ontario_leadership_election,_2009" title="Green Party of Ontario leadership election, 2009">Green Party of Ontario leadership election, 2009</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_history">Recent history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recent history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ontario_general_election,_2014_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3692" data-file-width="4811" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Ontario_general_election%2C_2014_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg/300px-Ontario_general_election%2C_2014_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Ontario_general_election%2C_2014_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg/450px-Ontario_general_election%2C_2014_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Ontario_general_election%2C_2014_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg/600px-Ontario_general_election%2C_2014_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ontario_general_election,_2014_results_by_riding_-_Green_Party_strength.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Results of the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_2014" title="Ontario general election, 2014">2014 Ontario general election</a> showing support for Green candidates by riding</div></div></div>
<p>Throughout 2006 there was a move toward major constitutional changes in the party, led by Executive Council Member-at-Large (former GPO President) Ron Yurick. During the May Annual Policy Conference in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, and the September 2006 AGM in Lion's Head, Ontario, sweeping changes were approved to the party's governance structures. It was described as "the culmination of hundreds of hours of work that evolved out of a directive passed at the 2004 (AGM) in Cambridge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Included in the changes were the formation of a much larger Provincial Executive, which included two gender paritied representatives from each of six regions, gender paritied Deputy Leaders, and the creation of multiple functionary roles (a quasi civil service) separated from the Provincial Executive.
</p><p>At the Party's 2006 Annual General Meeting (AGM) the Party adopted further changes to the existing Constitution that, amongst other things, reduced the size of the Provincial Council and renamed it the Provincial Council Executive. One of the first acts of the new Provincial Executive was to strike a hiring committee to bring on a full-time campaign manager in response to mounting internal pressures to ensure the party was ready for the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_2007" title="Ontario general election, 2007">October 2007 provincial election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the run-up to the 2007 provincial election, the Greens' support climbed into the double-digits for the first time in party history. Although the party did not elect a member to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontario_Legislative_Assembly" title="Ontario Legislative Assembly">provincial legislature</a>, they did increase their share of the popular vote to 8.1% (a gain of 5.3% from the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_2003" title="Ontario general election, 2003">2003 election</a>), placed second in one riding (<a href="/wiki/Bruce%E2%80%94Grey%E2%80%94Owen_Sound_(provincial_electoral_district)" title="Bruce—Grey—Owen Sound (provincial electoral district)">Bruce—Grey—Owen Sound</a>, with 33.1% compared to the PC incumbent winner's 46.7%), and took third place in a number of other ridings, ahead of candidates from previously elected parties.  <a href="/wiki/Shane_Jolley" title="Shane Jolley">Shane Jolley</a>, the Green candidate for Bruce-Grey-Owen Sound, earned more votes than any Green candidate in Canadian history at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mike_Schreiner_and_Frank_de_Jong_Hart_House_2009.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="454" data-file-width="600" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Mike_Schreiner_and_Frank_de_Jong_Hart_House_2009.jpg/200px-Mike_Schreiner_and_Frank_de_Jong_Hart_House_2009.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Mike_Schreiner_and_Frank_de_Jong_Hart_House_2009.jpg/300px-Mike_Schreiner_and_Frank_de_Jong_Hart_House_2009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Mike_Schreiner_and_Frank_de_Jong_Hart_House_2009.jpg/400px-Mike_Schreiner_and_Frank_de_Jong_Hart_House_2009.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mike_Schreiner_and_Frank_de_Jong_Hart_House_2009.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frank_De_Jong" title="Frank De Jong">Frank De Jong</a> (right), with his successor as GPO Leader, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Schreiner" title="Mike Schreiner">Mike Schreiner</a> (left)</div></div></div>
<p>The party had its 2007 AGM at an <a href="/wiki/Easter_Seals_(Canada)" title="Easter Seals (Canada)">Easter Seals</a> camp near Perth on 23 November to the 25. It was the largest AGM in GPO history at that time with over 120 delegates and over 400 proxy votes. The GPO adopted changes to the constitution, many involving the provincial executive. A few directives to the executive also discussed at the AGM included party bilingualism and fundraising. The party voted in the new executive including a new president <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lawson_Hunter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lawson Hunter (page does not exist)">Lawson Hunter</a>, while former president Ron Yurick was voted in as Northern male rep.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Over 70% of the voting membership had also voted to re-elect party leader <a href="/wiki/Frank_de_Jong" title="Frank de Jong">Frank de Jong</a> for another two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>De Jong resigned as leader in 2009 and was replaced by Toronto entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Mike_Schreiner" title="Mike Schreiner">Mike Schreiner</a> who was the sole candidate in the party's leadership race.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2018_Election">2018 Election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2018 Election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 2018 election, the party ran on a platform of investing in green jobs and clean energy, rolling out a universal basic income, shifting away from nuclear power, lowering payroll taxes for small businesses and implementing road tolls to fund transit infrastructure. The party ran a full slate of candidates including over 50% women for the first time. Mike Schreiner was excluded from the televised leaders debates, which led to an unsuccessful campaign by Fair Debates to encourage media to reverse the decisions. The GPO finally broke through on 7 June 2018, with leader Mike Schreiner winning a seat in Guelph and becoming the first Green MPP in Ontario history. He was sworn into the Ontario Legislature on 5 June 2018. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies">Policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green Party of Ontario shares the same <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ten_Key_Values" title="Ten Key Values">Ten Key Values</a> as other <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North American</a> <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">Greens</a>. Although the party has generally been perceived as being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-wing" title="Left-wing">left-wing</a>, the party combines ecologically and socially reformist policies with strong respect for the free market and entrepreneurship. Many key members are recruits from the former centre-right <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Conservative_Party_of_Canada" title="Progressive Conservative Party of Canada">Progressive Conservative Party of Canada</a>, including Elio Di Iorio, who was a protégé of former Canadian Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Joe_Clark" title="Joe Clark">Joe Clark</a>, and Peter Elgie, son of former Ontario Progressive Conservative cabinet minister <a href="/wiki/Robert_Elgie" title="Robert Elgie">Robert Elgie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  The party's newly chosen Chief Financial Officer, <a href="/wiki/David_Scrymgeour" title="David Scrymgeour">David Scrymgeour</a>, was the National Director of the former Progressive Conservative Party of Canada.
</p><p>Under Frank de Jong, the GPO has emphasized policies typical of both left- and right-wing parties. In the words of de Jong, the GPO tends to favour policies that are "socially progressive, fiscally conservative, and environmentally aware".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> As such, policies in areas such as education, health, environmental protection and social equity are notably progressive, while policies on income &amp; property taxation, market regulation, and industrial subsidization are more conservative in nature. Contrary to most other parties, the Greens prefer a model of decentralization, where administration of local programs/services (for example, local schools, hospitals, housing, and transport) are left to local government which is more responsive to local needs/realities, but where costs are not simply downloaded without the ability to raise local revenue (such as with previous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontario_Progressive_Conservative_Party" title="Ontario Progressive Conservative Party">Progressive Conservative governments</a>). The party emphasizes interconnectivity between various policy areas (for example, health and the environment, or the environment and the economy).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxation">Taxation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Taxation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>GPO policy proposes a concept known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_tax_shifting" title="Green tax shifting">green tax shifting</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> which it classifies under the broad context of ecological fiscal reform. In general, the party proposes gradual but significant reductions in all income and corporate taxes (or taxes on so-called "earned income"), funded by the introduction of new resource-based taxes applied at the point of entry into the economy (for example, carbon taxes). The Green Party also proposes introduction of a system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_value_taxation" title="Land value taxation">land value taxation</a>, which would replace the current value assessment-based property tax system and would be meant to discourage <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a> and increase land use efficiency. Central to the GPO's tax policies is the concept of revenue neutrality, in which any new taxes (i.e., those on resources and consumption) are complemented by a reduction in other forms of taxation (generally income and corporate taxes). 
</p><p>This mixture of <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarian</a> and free-market income tax policies with a shift towards consumption/resource taxation is one of the clear differences between the Ontario Greens and the three main provincial parties. For example, while the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_New_Democratic_Party" title="Ontario New Democratic Party">NDP</a> and to a lesser extent the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Ontario" title="Liberal Party of Ontario">Liberals</a> aim to create social equity through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progressive_taxation" title="Progressive taxation">progressive taxation</a> of already-earned income, and the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Conservative_Party_of_Ontario" title="Progressive Conservative Party of Ontario">Conservatives</a> do not necessarily view social equity to be the role of government, the Greens prefer allowing individuals/businesses to contribute by paying more for what they use (resources, energy) and the pollution/side-effects that they create. 
</p><p>The GPO claims that this system is more fair and more economically desirable, because it only punishes individuals and businesses who operate without regard for society and the environment, while accentuating the ability of truly efficient and responsible businesses to prosper without hindrance. Critics of these policies, however, oppose these taxation methods because they view them as examples of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regressive_taxation" title="Regressive taxation">regressive taxation</a> due to the fact that they would have a bigger economic impact that would be felt in the everyday lives of lower-income groups. They point out that since these policies don't take into account the person's income/ability to pay, they would naturally create greater economic hardship the lower down the income ladder one is (as in any amount of money that is set to a flat rate would represent a greater portion of one's income the less money they make, hence placing the tax burden on the shoulders of the poorest in society because although everyone would be paying the same amount, "x" amount of dollars is far more significant to a lower-income person than a higher-income person).
</p><p>Greens have historically supported tax relief for small businesses, generally funded by modest increases to the corporate tax rate. They have also proposed road pricing (including tolls, parking levies and land-value taxes near subways) to pay for public transit. 
</p><p>The party favours a revenue neutral <a href="/wiki/Fee_and_dividend" title="Fee and dividend">carbon fee-and-dividend</a> approach to pollution pricing. Under the scheme, emitters would be charged at the source of pollution and all revenues collected would be returned to citizens in the form of dividend cheques. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Greens base their health policies on prevention, and claim to consider health in areas such as organic agriculture, active transportation, urban planning, and education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In particular, party policy closely links the areas of disease prevention and environmental health, with policies such a ban on cosmetic pesticides, a <i>Cancer Prevention Act</i>, a Lyme Disease Strategy, and doubling funding for the Ministry of Health Promotion. Policies on health care include expanding the Community Care Access Centre (CCAC) system, increasing support for multidisciplinary clinics (those with doctors, nurses, nurse practitioners, psychologists, dieticians, and other health care professionals), and increasing support for midwifery, along with a number of administrative reforms. GPO policy emphasizes a reduction in health care costs through avoidance of illness and expansion of alternative access models (such as CCACs), rather than simply closing facilities or increasing expenditures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>In its 2007 platform, the Green Party of Ontario advocated a full phasing-out of the Ontario Health Premium tax.
</p><p>In its 2018 platform, the party proposed a major increase in funding for mental health services, a first step towards transitioning to full coverage under OHIP+. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_programs">Social programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Social programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Green Party of Ontario believes in modernizing the social safety net to account for present-day challenges. It has been an advocate for a universal <a href="/wiki/Basic_income" title="Basic income">Basic Income</a> for all Ontarians, in order to provide economic security while at the same time cutting red tape and bureaucracy. In 2017, Ontario introduced a Basic Income pilot program, which the Greens wanted to see rolled out across the province. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_general_election,_2007" title="Ontario general election, 2007">2007 provincial election</a>, education, and specifically the funding of religious schools, was a central issue. GPO policy calls for an end to the <a href="/wiki/Separate_school" title="Separate school">publicly funded Catholic school system</a>, a merger that it claimed would save millions of dollars in duplicate administrative costs. Other items include giving local school boards a say in funding allocation, ending standardized testing of students, and encouraging programmes such as physical education, environmental education, and a mandatory course in world religions. At the post-secondary level, the GPO proposes a tuition cap of $3 000 per year for university studies and $700 per year for college and increased funding for apprenticeship programmes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electoral_reform">Electoral reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Ontario&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Electoral reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The GPO is a strong supporter of <a href="/wiki/Electoral_reform" title="Electoral reform">electoral reform</a>. It is in favour of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mixed_Member_Proportional" title="Mixed Member Proportional">Mixed Member Proportional</a> representation system recommended by the <a href="/wiki/Citizens%27_Assembly_on_Electoral_Reform_(Ontario)" title="Citizens' Assembly on Electoral Reform (Ontario)">Citizens' Assembly on Electoral Reform</a> in May 2007<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and defeated in the <a href="/wiki/Ontario_electoral_reform_referendum,_2007" title="Ontario electoral reform referendum, 2007">Ontario referendum</a> in October 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> This system would make the number of seats attributed to the party in a "members-at-large" section of the legislature approximately equal to the percentage of the vote won by the party in separate party vote. 
</p>
