<p><b>Cobalt green</b> is an ambiguous term for either of two families of green inorganic <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigments</a>.  Both are obtained by doping cobalt(II) oxide into colorless host oxides.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spinel-based_cobalt_green">Spinel-based cobalt green</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cobalt_green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spinel-based cobalt green">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Doping Co(II) into Mg(II) and Zn(II) sites of Mg<sub>2</sub>TiO<sub>4</sub> and Zn<sub>2</sub>TiO<sub>4</sub>, respectively gives one family of cobalt greens.  These materials adopt the <a href="/wiki/Spinel" title="Spinel">spinel</a> structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Rinman.27s_green"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Rinman's_green">Rinman's green</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cobalt_green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rinman's green">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rinman's green, also referred to as Rinmann's green, is obtained by doping cobalt(II) oxide into zinc oxide. <a href="/wiki/Sven_Rinman" title="Sven Rinman">Sven Rinman</a>, a Swedish chemist, discovered this compound in 1780.  <a href="/wiki/Zinc_oxide" title="Zinc oxide">Zinc oxide</a>–derived pigments have been used in many industries and processes.  It is rarely used because it is a weak chromophore and relatively expensive compared to <a href="/wiki/Chromium(III)_oxide" title="Chromium(III) oxide">chromium(III) oxide</a>.
</p><p>The structure and color of compositions Zn<sub>1−x</sub>Co<sub>x</sub>O depends on the value of x.  For x ≤ 0.3, the material adopts the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wurzite" title="Wurzite">Wurzite</a> structure (of ZnO) and is intensely green.  For x ≥ 0.7, the material has the <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> structure (of CoO) and is pink.  Intermediate values of x give a mixture of the two phases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cobalt green has been tested for use in "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spintronic" title="Spintronic">spintronic</a>" devices. Cobalt green is attractive in this application because it is magnetic at room temperature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
