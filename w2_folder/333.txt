<p><b>London Greenpeace</b> was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anarchist" title="Anarchist">anarchist</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_activist" title="Environmental activist">environmentalist activist</a> collective that existed between 1972 and 2001. They were based in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, and came to international prominence when two of their activists refused to capitulate to <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> in the landmark libel case known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McDonald%27s_Restaurants_v_Morris_%26_Steel" title="McDonald's Restaurants v Morris &amp; Steel">"McLibel"</a>. It was not affiliated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenpeace_International" title="Greenpeace International">Greenpeace International</a> or with Greenpeace UK.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1972 a group of activists loosely associated with the <i><a href="/wiki/Peace_News" title="Peace News">Peace News</a></i> newspaper formed a new group committed to environmentalism and anarchism. Initially the group campaigned for the ending of atmospheric testing of nuclear weapons by France at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mururoa" title="Mururoa">Mururoa</a> atoll in the south Pacific. In support of this, in 1973 the group held a 60-strong protest march from London to Paris, via <a href="/wiki/Dover" title="Dover">Dover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ostend" title="Ostend">Ostend</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wattrelos" title="Wattrelos">Wattrelos</a>, ending in a demonstration at Notre Dame cathedral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcspotlight.org_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcspotlight.org-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>London Greenpeace was not affiliated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenpeace_International" title="Greenpeace International">Greenpeace International</a>. Greenpeace International was formed out of a rough coalition of various environmentalist groups in 1971, many of whom were already using the name "Greenpeace". London Greenpeace emphatically wanted to remain independent of this new and larger Greenpeace, which it saw as being too "centralized and mainstream for their tastes".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_affiliation">Political affiliation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political affiliation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>London Greenpeace's politics have primarily been informed by <a href="/wiki/Anarchism" title="Anarchism">anarchism</a>. They have been linked, ideologically and in their activism, with <a href="/wiki/Radical_environmentalism" title="Radical environmentalism">radical environmentalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_anarchism" title="Green anarchism">green anarchism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacifism" title="Pacifism">pacifism</a>. They have been officially affiliated with <a href="/wiki/War_Resisters%27_International" title="War Resisters' International">War Resisters' International</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Peace_Council" title="National Peace Council">National Peace Council</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcspotlight.org_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-mcspotlight.org-1">[1]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Campaign_Against_Arms_Trade" title="Campaign Against Arms Trade">Campaign Against Arms Trade</a>, and supportive of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_liberation_movement" title="Animal liberation movement">Animal Liberation</a> movement. In the 1980s they were involved with the <a href="/wiki/Stop_the_City" title="Stop the City">Stop the City</a> campaigns,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> whilst the 1990s saw them helping to initiate the London-wide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reclaim_The_Streets" title="Reclaim The Streets">Reclaim The Streets</a> Network. They are viewed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2017)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> as one of the first anarchist groups to promote a specifically environmentalist message.
</p><p>During the second half of the 1970s the group pioneered the campaign against nuclear power, and worked with a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear" title="Anti-nuclear">anti-nuclear</a> alliances such as Stop Urenco, the Torness Alliance, and the Nuclear Information network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcspotlight.org_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-mcspotlight.org-1">[1]</a></sup> London Greenpeace was also involved in the opposition to the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a>, and co-founded the Anti-Falkland War Support network.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>London Greenpeace gained public attention with the McLibel case, which became well known as one of the first <a href="/wiki/Strategic_lawsuit_against_public_participation" title="Strategic lawsuit against public participation">SLAPP suits</a> against freedom of expression. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McDonald%27s_Restaurants" title="McDonald's Restaurants">McDonald's Restaurants</a> sued London Greenpeace, which later morphed into "McDonald's vs Steel and Morris". The case lasted for 15 years and was finally settled in 2005. The McLibel case became famous because McDonald's lost the public relations case in the public mind.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="McLibel">McLibel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: McLibel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McLibel" title="McLibel">McLibel</a></div>
<p>In 1990 McDonald's issued proceedings against five London Greenpeace supporters, Paul Gravett, Andrew Clarke and Jonathan O'Farrell, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helen_Steel" title="Helen Steel">Helen Steel</a> and <a href="/wiki/McLibel_case" title="McLibel case">David Morris</a>, for libel. The company offered to withdraw actions against each individual in return for an apology and an undertaking not to repeat the claims. The activists had been distributing a pamphlet throughout London containing allegations regarding starvation in the Third World, destruction of rainforest, the use of recycled paper, links between the company's food and heart disease &amp; breast/bowel cancer, false advertising, the rearing and slaughter of animals, food poisoning, and employment practices. Of the five defendants, Gravett, Clarke and O'Farrell apologised to McDonald's, while Steel and Morris (often referred to as "The McLibel Two") refused.
</p><p>Almost all of London Greenpeace's resources and efforts went to helping the pair over the years the case was heard, but in 1997 both defendants lost and were ordered to pay McDonald's £60,000. However, the extended court battle was a public relations failure for McDonald's; the company decided not to pursue the two defendants for the money.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissolution">Dissolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dissolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2001 London Greenpeace issued a public statement announcing their dissolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Animal_Rights_News_in_UK_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Animal_Rights_News_in_UK-8">[8]</a></sup> While the McLibel action brought fresh energy, publicity and urgency to the organisation, this did not last long, and the group felt it best to permanently suspend their efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Animal_Rights_News_in_UK_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Animal_Rights_News_in_UK-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Undercover_police">Undercover police</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=London_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Undercover police">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During October 2011 activists from the group exposed <a href="/wiki/Bob_Lambert_(undercover_police_officer)" title="Bob Lambert (undercover police officer)">Robert Lambert</a>, whom they had known as Bob Robinson, as being a former undercover police officer who had infiltrated the group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Following a number of court cases that centred on Lambert and other undercover police officers, in 2014 and 2015 the Metropolitan Police apologised and paid substantial compensation to eight women who had discovered they had had intimate relationships with undercover officers, including Lambert. The police admitted that the relationships had been “abusive, deceitful, manipulative and wrong”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
