<p><b>Evergreen International Airlines</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Air_charter" title="Air charter">charter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cargo_airline" title="Cargo airline">cargo airline</a> based in <a href="/wiki/McMinnville,_Oregon" title="McMinnville, Oregon">McMinnville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, United States. It operated contract freight services, offering charters and scheduled flights, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wet_lease" title="Wet lease">wet lease</a> services. It operated services for the U.S. military and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postal_Service" title="United States Postal Service">United States Postal Service</a>, as well as ad hoc charter flights. Its crew base was at <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_International_Airport" title="John F. Kennedy International Airport">John F. Kennedy International Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FI-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Evergreen also maintained a large aircraft maintenance and storage facility at the <a href="/wiki/Pinal_Airpark" title="Pinal Airpark">Pinal Air Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Marana,_Arizona" title="Marana, Arizona">Marana, Arizona</a> that the company acquired from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIA" title="CIA">CIA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Air_America_(airline)" title="Air America (airline)">Air America</a> fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time86_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time86-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oregonian88_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Oregonian88-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_International_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The airline was established by Delford Smith (founder and owner) and began operations in 1960 as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evergreen_Helicopters" title="Evergreen Helicopters">Evergreen Helicopters</a>. It acquired the operating certificate of Johnson Flying Service and merged it with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intermountain_Airlines" title="Intermountain Airlines">Intermountain Airlines</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Corporation" title="Pacific Corporation">Pacific Corporation</a> (a CIA front company) in 1975 to form Evergreen International Airlines. The holding company, <a href="/wiki/Evergreen_International_Aviation" title="Evergreen International Aviation">Evergreen International Aviation</a>, formed in 1979, wholly owns the airline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FI_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FI-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">One of Evergreen's <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a> airplanes (registered N473EV, which suffered an in-flight engine separation in 1993) starred in the 1990 action film <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Hard_2" title="Die Hard 2">Die Hard 2</a></i>. Additionally, Evergreen <a href="/wiki/Boeing_727" title="Boeing 727">Boeing 727s</a> were featured in numerous films including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Donnie_Brasco" title="Donnie Brasco">Donnie Brasco</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Al_Pacino" title="Al Pacino">Al Pacino</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rookie_(1990_film)" title="The Rookie (1990 film)">The Rookie</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Clint_Eastwood" title="Clint Eastwood">Clint Eastwood</a>.</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>On November 9, 2013, it was announced that Evergreen Airlines would close on November 30, 2013 due to financial troubles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This information was initially denied by Evergreen, but shortly afterwards admitted: <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> "Evergreen International Airlines flew its last flight Monday [December 2, 2013] Mike Hines, chairman of its parent company board, acknowledged..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Evergreen also owned a museum and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterpark" title="Waterpark">waterpark</a>, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evergreen_Wings_and_Waves_Waterpark" title="Evergreen Wings and Waves Waterpark">Evergreen Wings and Waves Waterpark</a>. This remains open, despite the company's <a href="/wiki/Bankruptcy" title="Bankruptcy">bankruptcy</a>. One of Evergreen's 747-100s (registration N481EV) is mounted on the roof of the waterpark, serving as the housing for the top of four of the waterpark's slides.
</p><p>On December 31, 2013, Evergreen International Airlines filled a Chapter 7 petition in federal bankruptcy court in Delaware. The bankruptcy filing lists seven entities as submitting the Chapter 7 petition: Evergreen Aviation Ground Logistics Enterprise, Evergreen Defense and Security Services, Evergreen International Airlines, Evergreen International Aviation, Evergreen Systems Logistics, Evergreen Trade, and Supertanker Services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Evergreen previously operated three <a href="/wiki/Boeing_Dreamlifter" title="Boeing Dreamlifter">Boeing Dreamlifters</a> to transport the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_787" title="Boeing 787">Boeing 787</a> parts to Boeing, but the contract was given to <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Air" title="Atlas Air">Atlas Air</a> in September 2010. This was due to Boeing's rescheduled delivery of the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747-8" title="Boeing 747-8">Boeing 747-8</a>Fs ordered by <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Air" title="Atlas Air">Atlas Air</a> to increase their current fleet. The "Dreamlifter" is the logistic support aircraft for Boeing's global <a href="/wiki/Boeing_787_Dreamliner" title="Boeing 787 Dreamliner">Boeing 787 Dreamliner</a> production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The company was also scheduled to operate the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SOFIA" title="SOFIA">SOFIA</a> <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747</a>SP aircraft for NASA and the <a href="/wiki/German_Aerospace_Center" title="German Aerospace Center">German Aerospace Center</a> (DLR) at <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ames_Research_Center" title="Ames Research Center">Ames Research Center</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moffett_Field,_CA" title="Moffett Field, CA">Moffett Field, CA</a> (in the silicon valley near <a href="/wiki/San_Jose,_California" title="San Jose, California">San Jose</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The airline modified a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_747-100" title="Boeing 747-100">Boeing 747-100</a> for <a href="/wiki/Aerial_firefighting" title="Aerial firefighting">aerial firefighting</a>, receiving final certification from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FAA" title="FAA">FAA</a> in October 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Compared to existing large water bombers and airtankers, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evergreen_Supertanker" title="Evergreen Supertanker">Evergreen Supertanker</a> was planned to offer at least seven times more <a href="/wiki/Fire_retardant" title="Fire retardant">fire retardant</a> capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> In December, 2010, Israel hired Evergreen's fire-fighting aircraft to assist in firefighting efforts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010_Mount_Carmel_forest_fire" title="2010 Mount Carmel forest fire">2010 Mount Carmel forest fire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2007, Evergreen announced that it had ordered 3 <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747-400" title="Boeing 747-400">Boeing 747-400</a>BCF to upgrade its commercial operations, with deliveries in summer 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In March 2010, the orders had not yet been delivered.  In December 2012, Evergreen ceased operations of the last three 747-200s; they are parked at <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_International_Airport_at_Pease" title="Portsmouth International Airport at Pease">Portsmouth International Airport at Pease</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_International_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>March 18, 1989: Evergreen International Airlines Flight 17, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DC-9" title="DC-9">DC-9</a> was on a cargo flight from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelly_Air_Force_Base" title="Kelly Air Force Base">Kelly Air Force Base</a> outside San Antonio to <a href="/wiki/Tinker_Air_Force_Base" title="Tinker Air Force Base">Tinker Air Force Base</a> outside of Oklahoma City, with a stop at <a href="/wiki/Carswell_Air_Force_Base" title="Carswell Air Force Base">Carswell Air Force Base</a> in Fort Worth. As the plane was departing, the cargo door on the plane opened; the pilot immediately requested emergency return to Carswell. As the plane was approaching on base leg, the cargo door fully opened, which caused the plane to yaw to the left and right, and then roll, until crashing near <a href="/wiki/Saginaw,_Texas" title="Saginaw, Texas">Saginaw</a> in an inverted position. Both pilots on board were killed. The investigation found that when closing the cargo door, the copilot did not close it fully, but since the locked and latched indicators were applied incorrectly, the copilot thought the door was fully locked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li>March 31, 1993: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lee_waves" title="Lee waves">lee waves</a> were believed responsible for the in-flight separation of the #2 engine on an Evergreen <a href="/wiki/Boeing_747" title="Boeing 747">Boeing 747-100</a>, registration: N473EV, near <a href="/wiki/Anchorage,_Alaska" title="Anchorage, Alaska">Anchorage, Alaska</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The plane involved was used in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Hard_2" title="Die Hard 2">Die Hard 2</a></i>. The plane was since repaired, then flew until 2001.</li>
<li>September 1997; fines were imposed by the Australian civil aviation authority for dumping fuel while flying over Sydney</li></ul>
