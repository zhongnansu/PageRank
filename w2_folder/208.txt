<p><b>Nathanael Greene</b> (August 7 [<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> July 27] 1742 – June 19, 1786, sometimes misspelled <b>Nathaniel</b>) was a <a href="/wiki/Major_general" title="Major general">major general</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> (1775–1783). He emerged from the war with a reputation as commanding General <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>'s most gifted and dependable officer, and is known for his successful command in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War" title="Southern theater of the American Revolutionary War">Southern theater</a> of the war.
</p><p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Warwick,_Rhode_Island" title="Warwick, Rhode Island">Warwick, Rhode Island</a>, Greene was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island_General_Assembly" title="Rhode Island General Assembly">Rhode Island General Assembly</a> and ran his family's foundry. He came to oppose British rule in <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a> and formed a militia in 1774. The <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Second Continental Congress</a> appointed Greene to the rank of brigadier general in the Continental Army in 1775, and promoted Greene to major general in 1776. He served as Washington's subordinate in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_and_New_Jersey_campaign" title="New York and New Jersey campaign">New York and New Jersey campaign</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_campaign" title="Philadelphia campaign">Philadelphia campaign</a>, and was the Continental Army's <a href="/wiki/Quartermaster_General_of_the_United_States_Army" title="Quartermaster General of the United States Army">Quartermaster General</a> from 1778 to 1780.
</p><p>In December 1780, Greene was appointed to command the Continental Army in the southern theater of the Revolutionary War, replacing General <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Gates" title="Horatio Gates">Horatio Gates</a>. He engaged in a successful campaign to harass the British forces under General <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Charles Cornwallis</a>, limiting British control of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">South</a> to the coastal areas. After the war, he declined appointment as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_War" title="United States Secretary of War">Secretary of War</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">Articles of Confederation and Perpetual Union</a> central government and received land grants from the several Southern states. He died at age 43 at his <a href="/wiki/Mulberry_Grove_Plantation" title="Mulberry Grove Plantation">Mulberry Grove Plantation</a>  in <a href="/wiki/Chatham_County,_Georgia" title="Chatham County, Georgia">Chatham County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> in 1786. Many places in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> are <a href="/wiki/List_of_places_named_for_Nathanael_Greene" title="List of places named for Nathanael Greene">named after Greene</a>.
</p>

<p>Greene was the son of Nathanael Greene (4 November 1707 – October 1768), a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaker" title="Quaker">Quaker</a> farmer and smith, and he was descended from <a href="/wiki/John_Greene_(settler)" title="John Greene (settler)">John Greene, Sr.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Gorton" title="Samuel Gorton">Samuel Gorton</a>, both of whom were <a href="/wiki/List_of_early_settlers_of_Rhode_Island#Founders_of_Warwick" title="List of early settlers of Rhode Island">founding settlers</a> of <a href="/wiki/Warwick,_Rhode_Island" title="Warwick, Rhode Island">Warwick, Rhode Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Nathanael was born on <a href="/wiki/Forge_Farm" title="Forge Farm">Forge Farm</a> at <a href="/wiki/Potowomut,_Rhode_Island" title="Potowomut, Rhode Island">Potowomut</a> in the township of <a href="/wiki/Warwick,_Rhode_Island" title="Warwick, Rhode Island">Warwick, Rhode Island</a> on  August 7, 1742 [<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">N.S.</a>]. His mother Mary Mott was his father's second wife. His father's sect discouraged "literary accomplishments," so Greene educated himself with a special study of mathematics and law. The Rev. <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Stiles" title="Ezra Stiles">Ezra Stiles</a>, later <a href="/wiki/President" title="President">president</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>, was a strong influence in the young Nathanael's life.
</p><p>In 1770, Greene moved to <a href="/wiki/Coventry,_Rhode_Island" title="Coventry, Rhode Island">Coventry, Rhode Island</a> to take charge of the family-owned foundry, just prior to his father's death. There he was the first to urge the establishment of a public school and was chosen as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island_General_Assembly" title="Rhode Island General Assembly">Rhode Island General Assembly</a> in the same year, to which he was re-elected in 1771, 1772, and 1775.  He sympathized strongly with the Whig or <a href="/wiki/Patriot_(American_Revolution)" title="Patriot (American Revolution)">Patriot</a> element among the colonists.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In July 1774, he married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catherine_Littlefield_Greene" title="Catherine Littlefield Greene">Catharine Littlefield</a>, also known as Caty or Kitty, who was a dozen or so years younger than he. They had six children who survived infancy. He was Quaker by birth, and struggled to reconcile the faith's commitment to <a href="/wiki/Pacifism" title="Pacifism">pacifism</a> throughout his military career. He continued to serve despite this, as did many other <a href="/wiki/Quakers_in_the_American_Revolution" title="Quakers in the American Revolution">Quakers in the American Revolution</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Militia">Militia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Militia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 1774, Greene helped organize a local <a href="/wiki/Militia_(United_States)" title="Militia (United States)">militia</a> which was chartered as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kentish_Guards" title="Kentish Guards">Kentish Guards</a> that October.  His participation in the group was challenged because he had a pronounced <a href="/wiki/Limp" title="Limp">limp</a>. At this time, he began to acquire many expensive volumes on military tactics and began to teach himself the art of war. In December 1774, he was on a committee appointed by the assembly to revise the militia laws.  His zeal in fighting the British and organizing the militia led to his expulsion from the pacifistic Quakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_War_of_Independence">American War of Independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: American War of Independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Boston">Boston</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Boston">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Boston" title="Siege of Boston">Siege of Boston</a></div>
<p>On May 8, 1775, he was promoted from private to <a href="/wiki/Major_general" title="Major general">major general</a> of the Rhode Island Army of Observation formed in response to the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Boston" title="Siege of Boston">Siege of Boston</a>.  He was appointed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brigadier_General" title="Brigadier General">brigadier</a> of the Continental Army by the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a> on June 22, 1775. <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> assigned Greene the command of the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a> after it was evacuated by the British in March 1776.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York">New York</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: New York">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_York_and_New_Jersey_campaign" title="New York and New Jersey campaign">New York and New Jersey campaign</a></div>
<p>On August 9, 1776, he was promoted to be one of the four new major generals and was put in command of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> troops on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>; he chose the place for fortifications, and supervised the construction of redoubts and entrenchments (the site of current day <a href="/wiki/Fort_Greene_Park" title="Fort Greene Park">Fort Greene Park</a>) east of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Heights,_Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn Heights, Brooklyn">Brooklyn Heights</a>. Severe illness prevented him from taking part in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Long_Island" title="Battle of Long Island">Battle of Long Island</a>. Greene was also a Rhode Island <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freemason" title="Freemason">Freemason</a> and bore a masonic jewel, the gift of his Masonic Brother the Marquis de Lafayette, on his person throughout the whole of the revolution.
</p><p>Greene was prominent among those who advised a retreat from <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. He also advocated the burning of the city so that the British might not use it. He justified this by asserting that the majority of property was owned by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loyalists_(American_Revolution)" title="Loyalists (American Revolution)">Loyalists</a>. While Washington agreed with this, the proposal was rejected by Congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He was placed in command of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lee_Historic_Park#Fort_Lee_.28American_Revolutionary_War.29" title="Fort Lee Historic Park">Fort Constitution</a> (later renamed <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lee_Historic_Park" title="Fort Lee Historic Park">Fort Lee</a>) on the <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> side of the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a>. On October 25, 1776, he succeeded General <a href="/wiki/Israel_Putnam" title="Israel Putnam">Israel Putnam</a> in command of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Washington_(New_York)" title="Fort Washington (New York)">Fort Washington</a>, across the river from Fort Constitution. He received orders from Washington to defend Fort Washington to the last extremity, and on October 11, 1776, the Congress passed a resolution to the same effect; but later Washington wrote to him to use his own discretion. Greene ordered Colonel <a href="/wiki/Robert_Magaw" title="Robert Magaw">Magaw</a>, who was in immediate command, to defend the place until he should hear from him again, and reinforced it to meet General Howe's attack. Nevertheless, the blame for the losses of Forts Washington and Constitution was put upon Greene, but apparently without his losing the confidence of Washington, who himself assumed the responsibility.
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trenton" title="Battle of Trenton">Battle of Trenton</a>, Greene commanded one of the two American columns. After the victory there, he urged Washington to push on immediately to <a href="/wiki/Princeton,_New_Jersey" title="Princeton, New Jersey">Princeton</a>, but was overruled by a council of war.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Philadelphia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Campaign" title="Philadelphia Campaign">Philadelphia Campaign</a></div>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Brandywine" title="Battle of Brandywine">Battle of Brandywine</a>, Greene commanded the reserve. At <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Germantown" title="Battle of Germantown">Germantown</a>, Greene's command failed to arrive in good time, having a greater distance to march than the right wing under <a href="/wiki/John_Sullivan_(general)" title="John Sullivan (general)">Sullivan</a> – a failure which Greene himself thought would cost him Washington's trust. But when they arrived at length, Greene and his troops distinguished themselves.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nathanael_Greene_by_C.W._Peale.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1478" data-file-width="1038" height="313" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Nathanael_Greene_by_C.W._Peale.jpg/220px-Nathanael_Greene_by_C.W._Peale.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Nathanael_Greene_by_C.W._Peale.jpg/330px-Nathanael_Greene_by_C.W._Peale.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Nathanael_Greene_by_C.W._Peale.jpg/440px-Nathanael_Greene_by_C.W._Peale.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nathanael_Greene_by_C.W._Peale.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Painting by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Willson_Peale" title="Charles Willson Peale">Charles Willson Peale</a></div></div></div>
<p>At the urgent request of Washington on March 2, 1778, at <a href="/wiki/Valley_Forge" title="Valley Forge">Valley Forge</a>, he accepted the office of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quartermaster_general_(USA)" title="Quartermaster general (USA)">Quartermaster General</a>. His conduct in this difficult office, of which Washington heartily approved, was said to be "as good as was possible under the circumstances of that fluctuating uncertain force". However, he had become Quartermaster General on the understanding that he should retain the right to command troops in the field. Thus, he became head of the right wing at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monmouth" title="Battle of Monmouth">Monmouth</a> on June 28, 1778.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhode_Island">Rhode Island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rhode Island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Rhode_Island" title="Battle of Rhode Island">Battle of Rhode Island</a></div>
<p>In August, Greene and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marquis_de_la_Fayette" title="Marquis de la Fayette">Lafayette</a> commanded the land forces sent to Rhode Island to co-operate with French admiral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_Hector,_comte_d%27Estaing" title="Charles Hector, comte d'Estaing">d'Estaing</a>, in an expedition (the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Rhode_Island" title="Battle of Rhode Island">Battle of Rhode Island</a>) which proved unsuccessful. In June 1780, Greene was in command at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Springfield_(Revolutionary_War)" title="Battle of Springfield (Revolutionary War)">Battle of Springfield</a>. In August, he resigned the office of Quartermaster General after a long and bitter struggle with Congress over the interference in army administration by the Treasury Board and by commissions appointed by Congress.  Greene had vehemently argued with Congress over how to supply the Continental Army.  Congress was in favor of having the individual states provide equipment, which had already proven to be ineffective since the federal government held little to no power over the states.   A month before Washington appointed him commander of West Point, it fell to Greene to preside over the court which condemned Major <a href="/wiki/John_Andr%C3%A9" title="John André">John André</a> to death on September 29, 1780. Greene spent the winter of 1780-81 at <a href="/wiki/John_Tillinghast_House" title="John Tillinghast House">the house</a> of John Tillinghast, an important Newport merchant.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Command_in_the_South">Command in the South</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Command in the South">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Southern_theater_of_the_American_Revolutionary_War" title="Southern theater of the American Revolutionary War">Southern theater of the American Revolutionary War</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mount_vernon_stamp.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="196" data-file-width="300" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f5/Mount_vernon_stamp.JPG" width="300"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">Washington &amp; Nathanael Greene</div></div></div>
<p>The Congress had been unfortunate in the selection of commanders in the South. It had chosen <a href="/wiki/Robert_Howe_(Continental_Army_officer)" title="Robert Howe (Continental Army officer)">Robert Howe</a>, and he had lost Savannah. It had chosen <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Lincoln" title="Benjamin Lincoln">Benjamin Lincoln</a>, and he had lost Charleston. The British attacked  <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Gates" title="Horatio Gates">Horatio Gates</a>'  army in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Camden" title="Battle of Camden">Battle of Camden</a>, South Carolina on August 16, 1780, which broke and ran in wild confusion. This defeat effectively ended the American Southern army as a cohesive fighting force. It left the way clear for  <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Cornwallis</a> to pursue his goals of gathering southern Loyalists and taking the war to Virginia. He planned then to use his captured Southern <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">ports</a> to move men and <a href="/wiki/Materiel" title="Materiel">materiel</a> into the interior of North and South Carolina.
</p><p>When Gates' successor was to be chosen, the Congress decided to entrust the choice to General Washington. On October 5, it resolved "that the Commander-in-Chief be and is hereby directed to appoint an officer to command the southern army, in the room of Major General Gates". Washington delayed not at all in making his selection. On the day after he received a copy of the resolution, he wrote to Nathanael Greene at West Point, "It is my wish to appoint You."  The Congress approved the appointment, gave Greene command over all troops from <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> to <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> with extraordinarily full powers, "subject to the control of the Commander-in-Chief", effectively making him the second-in-command of the entire Continental Army. Greene took command at <a href="/wiki/Hillsborough,_North_Carolina" title="Hillsborough, North Carolina">Hillsborough, North Carolina</a>, on December 3, 1780. <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Huger" title="Isaac Huger">Brig. Gen. Isaac Huger</a> of the South Carolina Continentals was appointed his second in command. He was one of the dependable leaders in the state.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_strategic_retreat">The strategic retreat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The strategic retreat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The American army was weak and badly equipped and was opposed by a superior force under <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Cornwallis</a>. Greene decided to divide his own troops, thus forcing the division of the British, as well, and creating the possibility of a strategic interplay of forces.  The campaign changed, starting with the success at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kings_Mountain" title="Battle of Kings Mountain">Battle of Kings Mountain</a> in 1780 under Colonel <a href="/wiki/William_Campbell_(general)" title="William Campbell (general)">William Campbell</a> (he was later appointed as a Brigadier General in 1781).  The entire British force was captured or killed.  A new strategy led to General <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Morgan" title="Daniel Morgan">Daniel Morgan</a>'s victory of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cowpens" title="Battle of Cowpens">Cowpens</a> on January 17, 1781, where nearly nine-tenths of the entire British force were killed or captured.  Many of the same forces who were at Kings Mountain also came to Cowpens.
</p><p>With over 800 prisoners, Morgan began a strategic retreat, moving north towards the <a href="/wiki/Salisbury_District,_North_Carolina" title="Salisbury District, North Carolina">Salisbury District</a> where he was joined by Greene at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cowan%27s_Ford" title="Battle of Cowan's Ford">Cowan's Ford</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Catawba_River" title="Catawba River">Catawba River</a> where a force of Patriot Militia fought a small engagement against <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Cornwallis</a>'s forces. Greene then wrote to Huger to direct his troop movement from <a href="/wiki/Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park" title="Guilford Courthouse National Military Park">Guilford Courthouse</a>. Arriving on February 9 at Guilford, Greene summoned his field officers to a council of war and put forward the question of whether the army should give battle. It was voted that for the time being, the army should continue retreating to gather more forces, and defer engagement with Cornwallis.
On the tenth he wrote to  <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Henry" title="Patrick Henry">Patrick Henry</a> requesting troops, "If it is possible for you to call forth fifteen hundred Volunteers &amp; march them immediately to my assistance, the British Army will be exposed to a very critical and dangerous situation."
</p><p>"In all probability you will find me on the North side of Dan River. I must repeat it, the present moment is big with the most important consequences, &amp; requires the greatest &amp; most spirited exertions."
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_race_to_the_Dan_River">The race to the Dan River</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The race to the Dan River">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CouncilofWarLetter.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2703" data-file-width="1569" height="345" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/CouncilofWarLetter.jpg/200px-CouncilofWarLetter.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/CouncilofWarLetter.jpg/300px-CouncilofWarLetter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/CouncilofWarLetter.jpg/400px-CouncilofWarLetter.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CouncilofWarLetter.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Council of War letter (this copy made contemporaneously with original) that Greene sent of the proceedings to Samuel Huntington, the president of Congress. Written at Guilford Court House on February 9. 1781. This is a scan of the photograph from the National Archives.</div></div></div>
<p>Greene at this same time formed a special light corps to be commanded by Col. Otho Williams to cover the main army's retreat. In a letter to George Washington on February 9, he described the "light army" he had formed under Williams as composed of: "cavalry of the 1st and 3rd Regiments and the Legion amounting to 240, a detachment of 280 Infantry under Lieut. Col. Howard, the Infantry of Lieut. Col. Lee's Legion and 60 Virginia Riflemen making in their whole 700 men which will be ordered with the Militia to harass the enemy in their advance, check their progress and if possible give us opportunity to retire without general action." Also saying "I called a Council, who unanimously advised to avoid an action, and to retire beyond the Roanoke immediately. A copy of the proceedings I have the honor to inclose." The reunited army only numbered two thousand and thirty-six men, including fourteen hundred and twenty-six regulars. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Carrington" title="Edward Carrington">Col. Edward Carrington</a> joined the command, with the report that boats had been secured, and secreted along the <a href="/wiki/Dan_River" title="Dan River">Dan River</a> in Virginia, so as to be collected on a few hours' warning. The British army was at Salem, only twenty-five miles from Guilford. This was on the tenth of February.
</p><p>By the fourteenth, Greene's army had outrun the British and crossed the Dan River at Irvine's ferry in Halifax County, Virginia with boats being delivered from Boyd's ferry in Halifax and from Dix's ferry in Pittsylvania County, Virginia. Cornwallis got the news in the course of the evening. The river was too high to cross without boats, and every boat was on the farther shore. Greene had won the race.
</p>
<blockquote><p>This American retreat, which extended across the breadth of North Carolina, is considered one of the masterful military achievements of all time.  -- Dennis M. Conrad, Project Director and Editor, <i>The Papers of General Nathanael Greene</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In a letter to General John Butler, Greene writes "I have some expectation of collecting a force sufficient in this County to enable me to act offensively and in turn race Lord Cornwallis as he has done me."
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House">Battle of Guilford Court House</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Battle of Guilford Court House">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House" title="Battle of Guilford Court House">Battle of Guilford Court House</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monument_to_General_Nathanael_Greene_Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2815" data-file-width="2856" height="271" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Monument_to_General_Nathanael_Greene_Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park.JPG/275px-Monument_to_General_Nathanael_Greene_Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Monument_to_General_Nathanael_Greene_Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park.JPG/413px-Monument_to_General_Nathanael_Greene_Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Monument_to_General_Nathanael_Greene_Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park.JPG/550px-Monument_to_General_Nathanael_Greene_Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park.JPG 2x" width="275"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monument_to_General_Nathanael_Greene_Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Monument to General Nathanael Greene, <a href="/wiki/Guilford_Courthouse_National_Military_Park" title="Guilford Courthouse National Military Park">Guilford Courthouse National Military Park</a></div></div></div>
<p>After only a week's encampment at Halifax Court House, Greene had sufficient promises and reports of help on the way to recross the river. Greene and the main army re-crossed the Dan River into North Carolina on the 22nd, then pursued Cornwallis and gave battle on March 15, 1781, at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Guilford_Court_House" title="Battle of Guilford Court House">Battle of Guilford Court House</a> in North Carolina, on ground which he had chosen. Greene's army engaged Cornwallis's Army.  At the height of the battle, as the Continentals started to turn the British flank, Cornwallis ordered his artillery to fire grapeshot into the thick of the battle, killing as many of his own men as Greene's.  Greene ordered his army to execute a tactical retreat and left the field to Cornwallis, but inflicted a great loss of men to the British. Three days after this battle, with his army battered and exhausted, Cornwallis withdrew toward <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>. Greene's generalship and judgment were again conspicuously illustrated in the next few weeks, in which he allowed Cornwallis to march north to <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> and himself turned swiftly to the reconquest of the inner country of South Carolina. This he achieved by the end of June, in spite of a reverse sustained at <a href="/wiki/Francis_Rawdon-Hastings,_1st_Marquess_of_Hastings" title="Francis Rawdon-Hastings, 1st Marquess of Hastings">Francis Rawdon</a>'s hands at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hobkirk%27s_Hill" title="Battle of Hobkirk's Hill">Hobkirk's Hill</a> (2 miles north of <a href="/wiki/Camden,_South_Carolina" title="Camden, South Carolina">Camden</a>) on April 25.  From May 22 – June 19, 1781, Greene led the Siege of <a href="/wiki/Ninety_Six,_South_Carolina" title="Ninety Six, South Carolina">Ninety-Six</a>, which ended unsuccessfully.  These actions helped force the British to the coast.
</p><p>Greene then gave his forces a six weeks rest on the High Hills of the <a href="/wiki/Santee_River" title="Santee River">Santee River</a>, and on September 8, with 2,600 men, engaged the British under Lieutenant Colonel <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Stewart_(British_Army_officer)" title="Alexander Stewart (British Army officer)">Alexander Stewart</a> at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Eutaw_Springs" title="Battle of Eutaw Springs">Eutaw Springs</a>. Americans who fell in this battle were immortalized by American author <a href="/wiki/Philip_Freneau" title="Philip Freneau">Philip Freneau</a> in his 1781 poem "To the Memory of Brave Americans". The battle, although tactically a draw, so weakened the British that they withdrew to <a href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina" title="Charleston, South Carolina">Charleston</a>, where Greene pinned them down during the remaining months of the war.
</p><p>Greene's Southern Campaign showed remarkable strategic features. He excelled in dividing, eluding, and tiring his opponent by long marches, and in actual conflict forcing the British to pay heavily for a temporary advantage, a price that they could not afford. However, he was defeated in every pitched battle which he fought against the British during his time as southern commander. He was greatly assisted by able subordinates, including Polish engineer <a href="/wiki/Tadeusz_Ko%C5%9Bciuszko" title="Tadeusz Kościuszko">Tadeusz Kościuszko</a>, brilliant cavalry officers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_Horse_Harry_Lee" title="Light Horse Harry Lee">Henry ("Light-Horse Harry") Lee</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Washington" title="William Washington">William Washington</a>, and partisan leaders <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Sumter" title="Thomas Sumter">Thomas Sumter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Pickens_(congressman)" title="Andrew Pickens (congressman)">Andrew Pickens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elijah_Clarke" title="Elijah Clarke">Elijah Clarke</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Marion" title="Francis Marion">Francis Marion</a>.  In the end, Greene and his forces liberated the southern states from British control.  When the Treaty of Paris ended the war, British forces controlled a couple of southern coastal cities, but Greene controlled the rest.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life_and_death">Later life and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Later life and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greene was an original member of the Rhode Island <a href="/wiki/Society_of_the_Cincinnati" title="Society of the Cincinnati">Society of the Cincinnati</a> and served as the Society's president from its founding in 1783 until his death in 1786.  He was succeeded by his son George Washington Greene, his grandson Dr. Nathanael Greene (who served as president of the Rhode Island Society) and then by his great great grandson George Washington Greene Carpenter.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> all voted Greene liberal grants of lands and money, including an estate called "Boone's Barony" which was south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edisto,_South_Carolina" title="Edisto, South Carolina">Edisto</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bamberg_County,_South_Carolina" title="Bamberg County, South Carolina">Bamberg County, South Carolina</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mulberry_Grove_Plantation" title="Mulberry Grove Plantation">Mulberry Grove Plantation</a> near <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He sold Boone's Barony to pay bills for the rations of his Southern army.
</p><p>After twice refusing the post of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_War" title="United States Secretary of War">Secretary of War</a>, Greene settled in 1785 on his Georgia estate at Mulberry Grove. He died there at age 43, on June 19, 1786. For 114 years his remains were interred at The Graham Vault in Colonial Park Cemetery of Savannah, alongside <a href="/wiki/John_Maitland_(British_Army_officer)" title="John Maitland (British Army officer)">John Maitland</a>, his arch-rival in the conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hungry_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-hungry-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  On November 14, 1902,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hungry_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-hungry-6">[6]</a></sup> through the efforts of Rhode Island Society of the Cincinnati President <a href="/wiki/Asa_Bird_Gardiner" title="Asa Bird Gardiner">Asa Bird Gardiner</a>, his remains were moved to a monument in <a href="/wiki/Squares_of_Savannah,_Georgia#Johnson_Square" title="Squares of Savannah, Georgia">Johnson Square</a> in Savannah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greene was singularly able and, like other prominent generals on the American side, a self-trained soldier. He was second only to Washington among the officers of the American army in military ability, and the only general, other than Washington and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Knox" title="Henry Knox">Henry Knox</a>, to serve the entire eight years of the war. Like Washington, he had the great gift of using small means to the utmost advantage. His attitude towards the British was humane and even kindly. He even generously defended <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Gates" title="Horatio Gates">Horatio Gates</a>, who had repeatedly intrigued against him, when Gates' conduct of the campaign in the South was criticized.
</p><p>For his actions at Eutaw Springs, the Continental Congress awarded him a <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Gold_Medal" title="Congressional Gold Medal">Gold Medal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials">Memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nathanael_Greene&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NathanGreene_Monument.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1484" data-file-width="1140" height="286" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/NathanGreene_Monument.JPG/220px-NathanGreene_Monument.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/NathanGreene_Monument.JPG/330px-NathanGreene_Monument.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/NathanGreene_Monument.JPG/440px-NathanGreene_Monument.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NathanGreene_Monument.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Greene's grave in Johnson Square, <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a></div></div></div>
<p>There are many cities, counties, and parks named in honor of Nathanael Greene across America.
</p><p>Four <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard_Cutter" title="United States Coast Guard Cutter">Coast Guard revenue cutters</a> named for him.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/USS_Nathanael_Greene_(SSBN-636)" title="USS Nathanael Greene (SSBN-636)">USS <i>Nathanael Greene</i></a>, a James Madison-class nuclear submarine (decommissioned in 1986).
</p><p>Other vessels include an Army cargo ship, hull number 313 (1904), Liberty class steam merchant (1942), which was sunk by a U-boat during World War II, and a 128-foot Army tug, USAV <i>MG Nathanael Greene</i> (LT 801), which is still in service today.
</p><p>A large oil portrait of Nathanael Greene hangs in the State Room in the <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island_State_House" title="Rhode Island State House">Rhode Island State House</a>, and a statue stands outside the building's south facade.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Cenotaph" title="Cenotaph">cenotaph</a> to him stands in the Old Forge Burial Ground in Warwick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nathanael_Greene_(Brown)" title="Nathanael Greene (Brown)">His statue</a>, with that of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roger_Williams_(theologian)" title="Roger Williams (theologian)">Roger Williams</a>, represents the state of Rhode Island in the <a href="/wiki/National_Statuary_Hall" title="National Statuary Hall">National Hall of Statuary</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">Capitol</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a>; in the same city there is a <a href="/wiki/Major_General_Nathanael_Greene_(Brown)" title="Major General Nathanael Greene (Brown)">bronze equestrian statue</a> of him by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kirke_Brown" title="Henry Kirke Brown">Henry Kirke Brown</a> at the center of <a href="/wiki/Stanton_Park" title="Stanton Park">Stanton Park</a>.
</p><p>A small statue of Greene by Lewis Iselin, Jr. is part of the Terrace of Heroes outside the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Museum_of_Art" title="Philadelphia Museum of Art">Philadelphia Museum of Art</a>.
</p><p>He is also memorialized by an equestrian statue designed by Francis H. Packer at the site of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Guilford_Courthouse" title="Battle of Guilford Courthouse">Battle of Guilford Courthouse</a> near what is now <a href="/wiki/Greensboro,_North_Carolina" title="Greensboro, North Carolina">Greensboro, North Carolina</a>, the city named after him.
</p><p>Another statue stands in the middle of the traffic circle between Greene Street and McGee Street in downtown Greensboro.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Greeneville,_Tennessee" title="Greeneville, Tennessee">Greeneville, Tennessee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greene_County,_Tennessee" title="Greene County, Tennessee">Greene County, Tennessee</a> are also named after him. In 2006, the city of <a href="/wiki/Greenville,_South_Carolina" title="Greenville, South Carolina">Greenville, South Carolina</a>, also named for him, unveiled a statue of Greene designed by T. J. Dixon and James Nelson at the corner of South Main and Broad Streets.
</p><p>In 2000, a six-foot tall, bronze statue of Greene by sculptor <a href="/wiki/Chas_Fagan" title="Chas Fagan">Chas Fagan</a> was unveiled in St. Clair Park, in <a href="/wiki/Greensburg,_Pennsylvania" title="Greensburg, Pennsylvania">Greensburg, Pennsylvania</a>.
</p><p>On August 7, 2015, his 273rd birthday, the <a href="/wiki/Sons_of_the_Revolution" title="Sons of the Revolution">General Society of the Sons of the Revolution</a> dedicated a statue to General Greene on the Washington Memorial Chapel grounds located in <a href="/wiki/Valley_Forge" title="Valley Forge">Valley Forge</a> National Military Park, Pennsylvania.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nathanael_Greene_Homestead" title="Nathanael Greene Homestead">Nathanael Greene Homestead</a> in <a href="/wiki/Coventry,_Rhode_Island" title="Coventry, Rhode Island">Coventry, Rhode Island</a> features Spell Hall which was General Greene's home built in 1774.
</p>
