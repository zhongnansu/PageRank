<p>The <b>Scottish Green Party</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">Scottish Gaelic</a>: <i lang="gd">Pàrtaidh Uaine na h-Alba</i>; <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>: <i lang="sco">Scots Green Pairty</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Green_party" title="Green party">green</a> <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. The party has six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Members_of_the_Scottish_Parliament" title="Members of the Scottish Parliament">MSPs</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a> as of 2016. After the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_local_elections,_2017" title="Scottish local elections, 2017">2017 local elections</a>, the party also have 19 councillors in six of the 32 <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_Scotland" title="Local government in Scotland">Scottish local councils</a>.
</p><p>The Scottish Green Party was created in 1990 when the former <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(UK)" title="Green Party (UK)">Green Party</a> split into separate, independent parties, for Scotland, <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Green Party in Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">England and Wales</a>. The party is affiliated to the <a href="/wiki/Global_Greens" title="Global Greens">Global Greens</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Party" title="European Green Party">European Green Party</a>.
</p><p>Party membership increased dramatically following the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">Scottish independence referendum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> As of May 2016, the Scottish Green Party has become the fourth biggest party by membership in Scotland, overtaking the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Liberal_Democrats" title="Scottish Liberal Democrats">Scottish Liberal Democrats</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation">Organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Scottish Green Party is fully independent, but works closely with the other green parties of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party of England and Wales</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Green Party in Northern Ireland">Green Party in Northern Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Ireland)" title="Green Party (Ireland)">Green Party of Ireland</a>. It is a full member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Party" title="European Green Party">European Green Party</a>. The party currently has six MSPs and nineteen councillors. At the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2005" title="United Kingdom general election, 2005">2005 Westminster election</a>, the party contested 19 seats and polled 25,760 votes, they returned no MPs. Its highest share of the vote was 7.7% of the vote in <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_North_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Glasgow North (UK Parliament constituency)">Glasgow North</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> election of 2004, it polled 6.8% of the vote and did not return any MEPs. The party lost five of their seven seats in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2007_Scottish_Parliament_election" title="2007 Scottish Parliament election">2007 Scottish Parliament election</a>.
</p><p>According to accounts filed with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electoral_Commission_(UK)" title="Electoral Commission (UK)">Electoral Commission</a> for the year ending 31 December 2009, the party had an income of about £90,230 that year, an expenditure of £61,165 and a membership of 1,072.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  Within days of the Scottish Independence referendum being held, the membership swelled to more than 5,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Launching its manifesto for the 2015 General Election, the Scottish Green Party stated a membership of over 8,500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> By October 2015 the party were holding their biggest ever conference, with their membership standing at more than 9,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conferences">Conferences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conferences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Scottish Green Party hold conferences every Spring and Autumn, where members from throughout Scotland attend to deal with all the business of the Scottish Green Party, with delegates voting on conference motions and policy motions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Branches">Branches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Branches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The party is made up of branches, who cover a specific geographical area, and meet on a regular basis.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Groups">Groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Scottish Green Party have four self-organised groups:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Scottish_Young_Greens" title="Scottish Young Greens">Scottish Young Greens</a></li>
<li>Women’s Network</li>
<li>Rainbow Greens, which is The LGBTI+ (Rainbow) movement</li>
<li>Disabled Network</li></ul>
<p>The groups have additional meetings and discussions which are separate from the main party meetings.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Scottish Green Party originated as the Scottish branch of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecology_Party_(UK)" title="Ecology Party (UK)">Ecology Party</a>, founded in 1978 by <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Spoor" title="Leslie Spoor">Leslie Spoor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cockburn_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-cockburn-17">[17]</a></sup>  The Ecology Party became the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(UK)" title="Green Party (UK)">Green Party</a> and it remained a constituent party until 1990, when the Scottish Green Party became a separate entity. The separation was entirely amicable, as part of the green commitment to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decentralisation" title="Decentralisation">decentralisation</a>: the Scottish Green Party supported the referendum on <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish independence</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and opposed Britain's entry into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Common_Market" title="European Common Market">European Common Market</a> in its 1989 European election manifesto, claiming that the Common Market would cause <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_unemployment" title="Mass unemployment">mass unemployment</a> for Scottish workers, force Scotland to move towards a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tourist" title="Tourist">tourist</a>-based economy, enable the destruction of local food markets and cause catastrophic environmental damage – for this reason, the party campaigned for a Europe-wide <a href="/wiki/Confederation" title="Confederation">confederation</a> of individuals on global issues affecting the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Scottish Green Party has its most prominent presence in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>, which is elected using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Additional_member_system_(Scottish_Parliament)" title="Additional member system (Scottish Parliament)">additional member system</a> of <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a>. In the first election to this Parliament, in 1999, the Scottish Green Party got one <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Members_of_the_Scottish_Parliament" title="Members of the Scottish Parliament">Member of the Scottish Parliament</a> (MSP) elected by proportional representation, <a href="/wiki/Robin_Harper" title="Robin Harper">Robin Harper</a>, the UK's first elected Green parliamentarian (<a href="/wiki/George_MacLeod" title="George MacLeod">George MacLeod</a> had previously represented the UK Green Party in the House of Lords).  On 1 May 2003 the Scottish Greens added six new MSPs to their previous total.
</p><p>In the 2007 elections, the Party lost five seats in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Holyrood</a>. However, in the council elections, taking place under the new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_Transferable_Vote" title="Single Transferable Vote">Single Transferable Vote</a> voting system, they gained three Councillors on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_of_Edinburgh_Council" title="City of Edinburgh Council">City of Edinburgh Council</a> and five Councillors on <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_City_Council" title="Glasgow City Council">Glasgow City Council</a>. On 11 May, the Greens signed an agreement<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> with the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a>, which meant that the Greens voted for Alex Salmond as First Minister and supported his initial Ministerial appointments. In return, the Nationalists backed a <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> bill as an early measure and promised to legislate against ship-to-ship oil transfers in the Firth of Forth. The SNP also agreed to nominate <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Harvie" title="Patrick Harvie">Patrick Harvie</a>, one of the Green MSPs, to convene one of the Holyrood committees: Transport, Infrastructure and Climate Change.
</p><p>On 28 January 2009, the two Green MSPs were instrumental in the defeat of the Government's budget,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> though a slightly amended version was passed easily the following week. On 31 May, Cllr Martin Ford, formerly a <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Liberal_Democrats" title="Scottish Liberal Democrats">Liberal Democrat</a>, joined the Scottish Green Party in protest against the plans by <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> to develop on an important environmental site at <a href="/wiki/Balmedie#Menie_estate" title="Balmedie">Menie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  On 13 October 2009, he was joined by fellow former Liberal Democrat Cllr Debra Storr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>  Both Councillors continued to serve on Aberdeenshire Council as members of the Democratic Independent group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> At the 2012 Scottish local elections Councillor Debra Storr stood down to concentrate on her professional career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Councillor Martin Ford was re-elected, this time standing as a Scottish Green Party candidate.
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> announced the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">referendum on Scottish independence</a>, a campaign group called <a href="/wiki/Yes_Scotland" title="Yes Scotland">Yes Scotland</a> was established to promote a vote for independence. Leading members of the Scottish Green Party actively supported and became involved with the campaign from its foundation, with Patrick Harvie among the members of Yes Scotland's Advisory Board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In November 2013, Edinburgh councillor <a href="/wiki/Maggie_Chapman" title="Maggie Chapman">Maggie Chapman</a> succeeded Martha Wardrop as the party's female co-convenor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In December, former convenor <a href="/wiki/Robin_Harper" title="Robin Harper">Robin Harper</a> said that he would "absolutely vote No" in the independence referendum and offered his backing to the <a href="/wiki/Better_Together_(campaign)" title="Better Together (campaign)">Better Together</a> campaign, putting himself at odds with official party policy and its present leadership. Going on to say that he would like to help the <a href="/wiki/Better_Together_(campaign)" title="Better Together (campaign)">Better Together</a> and that there was a "significant minority" of Greens who were opposed to independence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Uniquely amongst the parties in the Scottish Parliament, the Scottish Green Party is open about and comfortable with the differences of opinion in the party on the constitutional issue, with co-convenor Patrick Harvie pointing out that "even the very firm supporters of independence within the Greens tend to be more strongly motivated by other aspects of our political agenda..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2005 the party announced plans to field candidates in 19 seats in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2005_(Scotland)" title="United Kingdom general election, 2005 (Scotland)">2005 Westminster elections</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In February 2015, the party announced that it would field candidates in 32 seats for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2015" title="United Kingdom general election, 2015">2015 United Kingdom general election</a> with 40% of their candidates being women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In 2017 the party generated some controversy by standing only 3 candidates at the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2017" title="United Kingdom general election, 2017">general election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy">Policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While associated mainly with <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalist</a> policies, the party has a history of support for communitarian economic policies, including well-funded, locally controlled public services within the confines of a <a href="/wiki/Steady-state_economy" title="Steady-state economy">steady-state economy</a>, is supportive of <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">proportional representation</a> and takes a progressive approach to social policies. The party is also strongly opposed to both <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trident_nuclear_programme" title="Trident nuclear programme">Trident nuclear programme</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> It is the only party other than the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a> (SNP) to both support <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">Scottish independence</a> and have representation in the Scottish Parliament. However, unlike the position taken by the SNP in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence_referendum,_2014" title="Scottish independence referendum, 2014">2014 independence referendum</a>, the Scottish Green Party supports Scotland having its own currency if it were to become an independent country. The party has said this would be in order to establish full economic independence, rather than being tied to the <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">pound sterling</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the party's website, the Scottish Greens are committed to forming a <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development_in_Scotland" title="Sustainable development in Scotland">sustainable society</a> and are guided by four interconnected principles:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">Ecology</a>: Our <a href="/wiki/Environment_of_Scotland" title="Environment of Scotland">environment</a> is the basis upon which every society is formed. Whenever we damage our environment, we damage ourselves. Respect for our environment is therefore essential.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Egalitarianism" title="Egalitarianism">Equality</a>: A society that is not socially and economically just cannot be sustainable. Only when released from immediate <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> can individuals be expected to take responsibility for wider issues. Our society must be founded on cooperation and respect. We campaign hard against <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a> on grounds of <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" title="Race (classification of human beings)">race</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sexual_orientation" title="Sexual orientation">sexuality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disability</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aging" title="Aging">age</a> or <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Radical_democracy" title="Radical democracy">Radical Democracy</a>: Politics is too often conducted in a polarised, confrontational atmosphere and in a situation remote from those that it affects. We must develop decentralised, participative systems that encourage individuals to control the decisions that affect their own lives.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peace" title="Peace">Peace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">Nonviolence</a>: Violence at all levels of human interaction must be rejected and succeeded by relations characterised by flexibility, respect and fairness.</li></ul>
<p>The party claims that, taken together, these principles give the party a <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holistic</a> view that is in common with all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_parties" title="Green parties">Green parties</a> around the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="MSPs">MSPs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: MSPs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All of the Scottish Green Party's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Members_of_the_Scottish_Parliament" title="Members of the Scottish Parliament">Members of the Scottish Parliament</a> (MSPs) have been elected under the list or "top-up" system of representation in the Parliament.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_MSPs">Current MSPs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Current MSPs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/John_Finnie" title="John Finnie">John Finnie</a> is an MSP for the <a href="/wiki/Highlands_and_Islands_(Scottish_Parliament_electoral_region)" title="Highlands and Islands (Scottish Parliament electoral region)">Highlands and Islands</a> region. He sat in the <a href="/wiki/4th_Scottish_Parliament" title="4th Scottish Parliament">previous session of parliament</a> both as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_National_Party" title="Scottish National Party">Scottish National Party</a>, and after leaving that party over its <a href="/wiki/NATO_debate_in_the_Scottish_National_Party" title="NATO debate in the Scottish National Party">change in policy on NATO membership</a>, as an independent.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ross_Greer_(politician)" title="Ross Greer (politician)">Ross Greer</a> is an MSP for the <a href="/wiki/West_Scotland_(Scottish_Parliament_electoral_region)" title="West Scotland (Scottish Parliament electoral region)">West Scotland</a> region.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Patrick_Harvie" title="Patrick Harvie">Patrick Harvie</a> is an MSP for the <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_(Scottish_Parliament_electoral_region)" title="Glasgow (Scottish Parliament electoral region)">Glasgow</a> region and was elected male Co-Convenor of the Party in November 2008.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alison_Johnstone" title="Alison Johnstone">Alison Johnstone</a> is an MSP for the <a href="/wiki/Lothian_(Scottish_Parliament_electoral_region)" title="Lothian (Scottish Parliament electoral region)">Lothian</a> region.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Previous_MSPs">Previous MSPs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Previous MSPs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Shiona_Baird" title="Shiona Baird">Shiona Baird</a> for <a href="/wiki/North_East_Scotland_(Scottish_Parliament_electoral_region)" title="North East Scotland (Scottish Parliament electoral region)">North East Scotland</a>. Baird served as party co-convener from 2004–07.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chris_Ballance" title="Chris Ballance">Chris Ballance</a> for the <a href="/wiki/South_of_Scotland_(Scottish_Parliament_electoral_region)" title="South of Scotland (Scottish Parliament electoral region)">South of Scotland</a>.</li>

He later served as one of its co-conveners from 2004–08.</li>

He stood for the Greens in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament_election,_2016" title="Scottish Parliament election, 2016">2016 election</a>, but was unsuccessful.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Councillors">Councillors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Councillors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_local_elections,_2007" title="Scottish local elections, 2007">2007</a> elections, the Party had only ever elected one councillor at local level: in May 1990, Roger (aka Rory) Winter, representing the Highland Green Party (<i>Uainich na Gàidhealtachd</i>), was elected in <a href="/wiki/Nairn" title="Nairn">Nairn</a> as Scotland's first Green regional councillor to the then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highland_Regional_Council" title="Highland Regional Council">Highland Regional Council</a>. Cllr Winter broke away from the Greens in 1991 and continued his four-year term as an Independent Green Highlander.
</p><p>The party made its first major breakthroughs at council level in the 2007 local elections, electing 8 councillors between Glasgow City and City of Edinburgh Councils.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_local_elections,_2012" title="Scottish local elections, 2012">2012</a> local elections this was increased to 14. The party elected councillors for the first time to Aberdeenshire, Stirling &amp; <a href="/wiki/Midlothian_Council" title="Midlothian Council">Midlothian Councils</a>.
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_local_elections,_2017" title="Scottish local elections, 2017">2017</a> local elections, the party returned a record 19 councillors, including elected councillors to <a href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands_Council" title="Orkney Islands Council">Orkney Islands Council</a> for the first time. However the party lost its sole councillor on Midlothian Council.
</p>
