<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Green Building (MIT)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Campus_of_the_Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Campus of the Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Campus of the Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Green_Building,_MIT,_Cambridge,_Massachusetts.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2560" data-file-width="1920" height="333" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Green_Building%2C_MIT%2C_Cambridge%2C_Massachusetts.JPG/250px-Green_Building%2C_MIT%2C_Cambridge%2C_Massachusetts.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Green_Building%2C_MIT%2C_Cambridge%2C_Massachusetts.JPG/375px-Green_Building%2C_MIT%2C_Cambridge%2C_Massachusetts.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Green_Building%2C_MIT%2C_Cambridge%2C_Massachusetts.JPG/500px-Green_Building%2C_MIT%2C_Cambridge%2C_Massachusetts.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Green_Building,_MIT,_Cambridge,_Massachusetts.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Green Building, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Cecil and Ida Green Building</b>, also called the <b>Green Building</b> or <b>Building 54</b>, is an academic and research building at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and houses the MIT Department of Earth, Atmospheric and Planetary Sciences. It was designed by <a href="/wiki/Araldo_Cossutta" title="Araldo Cossutta">Araldo Cossutta</a> and <a href="/wiki/I._M._Pei" title="I. M. Pei">I. M. Pei</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Pei, among the world's most noted architects, had received his bachelor's degree from MIT in 1940. Principal donor <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Howard_Green" title="Cecil Howard Green">Cecil Howard Green</a> received a bachelor's degree and master's degree from MIT and was a co-founder of <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments" title="Texas Instruments">Texas Instruments</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Building_(MIT)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green Building was constructed during 1962–1964, in <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">reinforced concrete</a>. It has 18 floors, equivalent to 21 stories or 295 feet (90 m) tall, with a concrete facade that more or less matches the <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and concrete of the older MIT buildings near it. The basement of the building is below <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> and connects to the MIT <a href="/wiki/Tunnel" title="Tunnel">tunnel</a> system.  Three <a href="/wiki/Elevator" title="Elevator">elevators</a> operate in the Green Building. There are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staircases" title="Staircases">staircases</a> at both the east and west sides, whose exterior facades present a vast windowless expanse relieved only by one-story tall concrete recessed panels. On the "LL" level (actually one story above ground level), is Room 54-100, a large <a href="/wiki/Lecture_hall" title="Lecture hall">lecture hall</a>. The second floor formerly housed the Lindgren Library, part of MIT's library system, but this separate facility was consolidated into another library in 2009.
</p><p>The Green Building is the tallest building in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge,_MA" title="Cambridge, MA">Cambridge</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Emporis_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Emporis-3">[3]</a></sup> When it was built, there was a limit on the number of floors.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Thus, it was designed to be on stilts, with the first occupied floor approximately 30 feet above grade in order to "circumvent" this law.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The footprint of every floor measures only 60 by 120 feet (18 by 36 m), which research groups quickly outgrew, forcing some of them to disperse elsewhere on campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Simha_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Simha-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The tower's height has some functional purpose, since its roof supports <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological instruments</a> and radio communications equipment, plus a white spherical <a href="/wiki/Radome" title="Radome">radome</a> enclosing long-distance <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> apparatus.  This technical equipment all requires a <a href="/wiki/Line-of-sight_propagation" title="Line-of-sight propagation">line-of-sight</a> vantage point for optimum range and accuracy, and would have required construction of some kind of tower to function as intended.  To minimize interference with radio signals, other buildings on MIT's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT_campus" title="MIT campus">central campus</a> are less than half the height of the Green Building, and the dormitory towers of Eastgate, Westgate, and MacGregor House are at least 1,500 feet (450 m) away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MITMap_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-MITMap-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupancy">Occupancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Building_(MIT)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Occupancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green Building is the main facility of the Department of Earth, Atmospheric, and Planetary Science (EAPS), also known as Course 12.  The departmental headquarters is on the 9th floor of the building.  The lower floors of the building contain the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_Science" title="Planetary Science">Planetary Science</a> section.  The middle floors have the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_Science" title="Earth Science">Earth Science</a> section (<a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">Geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">Geophysics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">Geochemistry</a>).  The upper floors house the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_Science" title="Atmospheric Science">Atmospheric Science</a> section (which also includes <a href="/wiki/Oceanography" title="Oceanography">Oceanography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">Climatology</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems">Problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Building_(MIT)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the Green Building was first opened, the isolated prominence of the building and its relative proximity to the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River</a> basin increased wind speeds in the high open archway at its base, preventing people from entering or leaving the building through the hinged main doors on windy days, necessitating use of a tunnel connecting to the other buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dickson_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dickson-6">[6]</a></sup>  Large wood panels were temporarily erected in the open concourse to block the wind, and <a href="/wiki/Revolving_door" title="Revolving door">revolving doors</a> were later installed at the ground floor entries to amend this problem somewhat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dickson_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dickson-6">[6]</a></sup>  Several windows cracked, and at least one large pane popped out on upper stories, at least in part due to the effects of wind, eventually requiring all the windows to be replaced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dickson_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Dickson-6">[6]</a></sup>  A few years later, a similar-appearing problem was repeated in Boston's <a href="/wiki/John_Hancock_Tower" title="John Hancock Tower">John Hancock Tower</a> located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Back_Bay" title="Back Bay">Back Bay</a> across the river, a 60-story skyscraper which happened to be designed by the same architectural firm.
</p><p>It is incorrectly rumored that <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Calder" title="Alexander Calder">Alexander Calder</a>'s monumental sculpture <i>The Big Sail</i>, was situated in front of the building to deflect the high winds.  The sculpture is located too far from the building entryway to have much effect on wind velocities there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hacks">Hacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Building_(MIT)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of its height and visibility from the Boston <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Back_Bay" title="Back Bay">Back Bay</a> neighborhood across the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River</a> Basin, plus its rectangular grid of large 6-by-8-foot (1.8 m × 2.4 m) upright rectangular single-pane windows forming a crude 9 × 18 <a href="/wiki/Dot-matrix_display" title="Dot-matrix display">dot-matrix display</a>, the Green Building has been the site of many <a href="/wiki/Hacks_at_the_Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Hacks at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology">hacks</a> or pranks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreenBldgHacks_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-GreenBldgHacks-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1993, one widely viewed hack repurposed the nine top-floor windows as an enormous digital <a href="/wiki/VU_meter" title="VU meter">VU meter</a> for the traditional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourth_of_July" title="Fourth of July">Fourth of July</a> concert of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Pops" title="Boston Pops">Boston Pops</a> orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VUMeterHack_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-VUMeterHack-9">[9]</a></sup> Several other simpler hacks have used the entire window array for stationary displays; this practice is sufficiently commonplace to have acquired the term <b>greenspeak</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NightworkGreenspeak_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NightworkGreenspeak-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Greenspeak_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Greenspeak-11">[11]</a></sup> (which should not be confused with the famously obscure <a href="/wiki/Pythia" title="Pythia">Delphic</a> pronouncements<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GreenspeakUVa_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-GreenspeakUVa-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FRBDallas_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FRBDallas-13">[13]</a></sup> of former <a href="/wiki/Federal_Reserve_Bank" title="Federal Reserve Bank">Federal Reserve Bank</a> chairman <a href="/wiki/Alan_Greenspan" title="Alan Greenspan">Alan Greenspan</a>).
</p><p>In September 2011, hackers installed 153 (= 9 × 17) custom-made wirelessly controlled color-changing high-power <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LED" title="LED">LED</a> lights into every window above the first floor. They displayed a waving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_flag" title="American flag">American flag</a> throughout the evening of September 11, 2011 in remembrance of the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> of 2001. For a short time in the early morning of September 12, the lights displayed a <a href="/wiki/Tetris" title="Tetris">Tetris</a> game, thus realizing a long-standing hack proposal, the "<a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a>" of hacks. The display hardware had occasional glitches, and was removed as of September 13. The hardware and software designs were further developed and refined for better reliability. On April 20, 2012, MIT hackers successfully turned the Green Building into a huge, playable Tetris game, operated from a wireless control podium at a comfortable viewing distance in front of the building. Visitors to Campus Preview Weekend (a gathering for admitted prospective freshman students) were invited to play the game on the colossal 80-by-250-foot (24 m × 76 m) display grid, which was claimed to be the second-largest full-color video display in the US.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pourian_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pourian-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parker_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Parker-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HackGalleryTetris_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-HackGalleryTetris-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Instead of a one-shot temporary installation, the hackers have designed and built a permanent facility that can be re-used repeatedly by the MIT community. An understanding has been reached with the Department of Earth, Atmospheric and Planetary Sciences (EAPS), which is headquartered in the Green Building, to allow the light display hardware to remain installed in each window.  To avoid annoying the occupants and to allow late-working staff to "opt out", each light display is equipped with a <a href="/wiki/Manual_override" title="Manual override">manual override</a> button, which will disable the <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixel</a> lighting for that window for several hours after it is pressed. In addition, the hackers have released <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_Source" title="Open Source">Open Source</a> software tools used to develop new display patterns, so that others can design and deploy new stationary or animated images, in cooperation with the hacker engineers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pourian_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pourian-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>On the night after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Marathon_bombings" title="Boston Marathon bombings">bombings of the Boston marathon</a> of April 15, 2013, the Green Building lighting displayed an American flag pattern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marathon_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marathon-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Awesome_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Awesome-18">[18]</a></sup> After the shooting death of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT_Campus_Patrol" title="MIT Campus Patrol">MIT Campus Patrolman</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sean_Collier" title="Sean Collier">Sean Collier</a> by the alleged bombers a few days later, a 250 foot (76 m) black ribbon pattern was displayed in his memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marathon_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marathon-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>As a prototype feasibility demonstration, the <a href="/wiki/Tech_Model_Railroad_Club" title="Tech Model Railroad Club">Tech Model Railroad Club</a> (located in Building N52) had years earlier added a scale model of the Green Building to its <a href="/wiki/HO_scale" title="HO scale">HO scale</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Model_railroad" title="Model railroad">model railroad</a> layout. Passersby inside Building N52 can view the model building and railway layout through a large window and play a monochromatic version of Tetris via remote control, accompanied by authentic-sounding music, even when the facility is closed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other hacks utilize the height of the building, such as a 1974 failed attempt to operate a giant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yoyo" title="Yoyo">yoyo</a> from the roof of the tower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TechGreenYoyo_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-TechGreenYoyo-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TechArmyYoyo_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-TechArmyYoyo-21">[21]</a></sup> Launching of projectiles from the roof is strongly discouraged, due to the unpredictable high wind gusts, posing a serious danger to passersby, and residents of nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_MIT_undergraduate_dormitories#East_Campus_Alumni_Memorial_Housing" title="List of MIT undergraduate dormitories">East Campus</a> dormitory.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pumpkin_Drop">Pumpkin Drop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Building_(MIT)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pumpkin Drop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At midnight on the last Saturday of October, 1West (the smallest hall in the East Campus dorm) drops a large number of pumpkins (up to the low hundreds) off the roof of the Green Building. Students come to watch, but the area around the foot of the building is quarantined off, to prevent anyone from being hit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Building_(MIT)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green Building faces McDermott Court (also known as <b>the dot</b>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> This grassy area is flanked by the 33-ton metal sculpture <i>La Grande Voile</i> (<i>The Big Sail</i>), one of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Calder" title="Alexander Calder">Alexander Calder</a>'s "stabile" artworks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LVACVoile_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-LVACVoile-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2011, a temporary artwork was installed in the arched "breezeway" at the base of the Green Building, to take advantage of its legendary wind gusts.  Designed by Meejin Yoon, an Associate Professor of Architecture, <i>Wind Screen</i> was an array of wind-driven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micro-turbine" title="Micro-turbine">micro-turbine</a> generators that would light up whenever there was enough air movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WindScreen_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-WindScreen-29">[29]</a></sup>  This installation was featured in the FAST (Festival of Art, Science, and Technology) celebration, part of the <i>MIT 150</i> commemoration of MIT's 150th anniversary.
</p><p>On May 18, 2013, a night-time projection on the <a href="/wiki/Radome" title="Radome">radome</a> on the rooftop of the Green Building by artist <a class="external text" href="http://davidyannrobert.com" rel="nofollow">David Yann Robert</a> beamed the image of Bengali polymath and biophysicist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Jagadish_Chandra_Bose" title="Sir Jagadish Chandra Bose">Sir Jagadish Chandra Bose</a> during a <a class="external text" href="http://www.davidyannrobert.com/green-building/" rel="nofollow">lecture-performance</a> on plant signaling and behavior.
</p>
