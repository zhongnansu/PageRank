<p><b>Green libertarianism</b> (also known as <b>eco-libertarianism</b>) is a hybrid <a href="/wiki/Political_philosophy" title="Political philosophy">political philosophy</a> that has developed in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Based upon a mixture of political <a href="/wiki/Third_party_(politics)" title="Third party (politics)">third party</a> values, such as the environmental and economic platform from the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">civil liberties</a> platform of the <a href="/wiki/Libertarian_Party_(United_States)" title="Libertarian Party (United States)">Libertarian Party</a>, the green libertarian philosophy attempts to consolidate <a href="/wiki/Progressivism" title="Progressivism">progressive</a> or <a href="/wiki/Agrarianism" title="Agrarianism">agrarian</a> values with <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarianism</a>. While green libertarians have tended to associate with the Green Party, the movement has grown to encompass <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">economic liberals</a> who advocate <a href="/wiki/Free_market" title="Free market">free markets</a> and commonly identify with contemporary <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism_in_the_United_States" title="Libertarianism in the United States">American libertarianism</a>.
</p>

<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Green_libertarianism&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span> <small><i>(November 2014)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><a href="/wiki/Left-libertarianism" title="Left-libertarianism">Left-libertarian</a> political philosophy, like that of the greens, is historically rooted in the <a href="/wiki/Individualist_anarchism" title="Individualist anarchism">individualist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_anarchism" title="Social anarchism">social schools</a> of anarchism. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anarchist_communism" title="Anarchist communism">Anarcho-communist</a> theorist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kropotkin" title="Peter Kropotkin">Peter Kropotkin</a>, a Russian prince and leading opponent of <i><a href="/wiki/Laissez-faire" title="Laissez-faire">laissez-faire</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Social_Darwinism" title="Social Darwinism">social Darwinism</a>, developed a theory of how "mutual aid" is the real basis for social organization in his <i>Mutual Aid: A Factor in Evolution</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (see also <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Joseph_Proudhon" title="Pierre-Joseph Proudhon">Proudhonian</a> <a href="/wiki/Mutualism_(movement)" title="Mutualism (movement)">mutualism</a>). <a href="/wiki/Murray_Bookchin" title="Murray Bookchin">Murray Bookchin</a> and the Institute for Social Ecology sought to further elaborate these ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Bookchin was one of the main influences behind the formation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Green_Party" title="German Green Party">German Green Party</a>, the first green party to win seats in state and national parliaments.
</p><p>Some more moderate, green libertarians are both <a href="/wiki/Egalitarianism" title="Egalitarianism">egalitarian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grassroots_democracy" title="Grassroots democracy">democratic</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_England_Transcendentalism" title="New England Transcendentalism">New England Transcendentalism</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Henry_David_Thoreau" title="Henry David Thoreau">Henry David Thoreau</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronson_Alcott" title="Bronson Alcott">Bronson Alcott</a>) and <a href="/wiki/German_Romanticism" title="German Romanticism">German Romanticism</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Raphaelites" title="Pre-Raphaelites">Pre-Raphaelites</a> and other "<a href="/wiki/Anarcho-primitivism" title="Anarcho-primitivism">back to nature</a>" movements combined with anti-war, anti-industrialism, civil liberties and decentralization movements are all part of this tradition. The modern Green Party of the United States seeks to apply these ideas to a more pragmatic system of democratic governance as opposed to contemporary <a href="/wiki/Anarcho-capitalism" title="Anarcho-capitalism">right</a> or <a href="/wiki/Left_anarchism" title="Left anarchism">left anarchism</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_capitalism">Natural capitalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_libertarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Natural capitalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">Sustainability</a> advocates <a href="/wiki/Paul_Hawken" title="Paul Hawken">Paul Hawken</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amory_Lovins" title="Amory Lovins">Amory Lovins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hunter_Lovins" title="Hunter Lovins">Hunter Lovins</a> posited in their book <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_Capitalism:_Creating_the_Next_Industrial_Revolution" title="Natural Capitalism: Creating the Next Industrial Revolution">Natural Capitalism: Creating the Next Industrial Revolution</a></i> (2000) that elements of libertarianism and green politics could be coalesced to produce economic as well as environmental benefits.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  The 2006 book <i><a href="/wiki/Green_to_Gold_(book)" title="Green to Gold (book)">Green to Gold</a></i>, written by environmental scholars <a href="/wiki/Daniel_C._Esty" title="Daniel C. Esty">Daniel C. Esty</a> and Andrew S. Winston, provided ideas on how companies can apply the principles of green libertarianism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alter_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-alter-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The work of <a href="/wiki/Austrian_School" title="Austrian School">Austrian School</a> economist <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Hayek" title="Friedrich Hayek">Friedrich Hayek</a> is especially important to understanding the organic view<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> of society and how most human institutions, including law and the economy, are "the result of human action but not of human design".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In his last major work, <i><a href="/wiki/Law,_Legislation_and_Liberty" title="Law, Legislation and Liberty">Law, Legislation and Liberty</a></i>, Hayek differentiates between endogenous orders or self-organizing systems and exogenous orders imposed from without.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Hayek argues<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> that free and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sustainable" title="Sustainable">sustainable</a> societies and economies which support them should follow general guidelines, such as "<a href="/wiki/Seven_generation_sustainability" title="Seven generation sustainability">7th Generation Principle</a>" rather than specific economic laws and regulations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Balance_of_ecology_and_economics">Balance of ecology and economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_libertarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Balance of ecology and economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Original_research" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="40" data-file-width="40" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section  <b>possibly contains <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">original research</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on <a href="/wiki/Talk:Green_libertarianism#Balance_of_ecology_and_economics" title="Talk:Green libertarianism">Talk:Green libertarianism</a>. Statements consisting only of original research should be removed.</span> <small><i>(October 2013)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>A fundamental concern among green libertarians is the health of global <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> in view of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" title="Adaptation to global warming">climate adaptation</a>. The green libertarian philosophy recognizes that ecology and <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> are inseparable. It seeks a system of effective <a href="/wiki/Environmental_law" title="Environmental law">environmental law</a> that is compatible with civil liberties and <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">market economy</a>.
</p><p>Green libertarians believe there should be a clear distinction between <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">science</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_ideology" title="Political ideology">political ideology</a>. For example, a green libertarian might be concerned by the phrases such as "wealth redistribution" and "reducing poverty" in the <a href="/wiki/Stern_Review" title="Stern Review">Stern Review</a> and in some <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> documents and statements.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Among green libertarians, the preservation of civil and economic <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individual freedom</a> may take precedence over long-term climate concerns because ultimately humans are part of nature. They believe that natural ecologies, like the free markets, are dynamic and self-adjusting systems.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limited_government">Limited government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_libertarianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Limited government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As part of the libertarian tradition, green libertarians maintain that the government itself is responsible for most environmental degradation, either directly or by encouraging and protecting politically powerful corporations and other organized interests which degrade, pollute and deplete the natural environment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The government should be thus held accountable to all the same environmental regulations they place on businesses. One problem is that while private corporations or individuals can be sued under the common law for damaging the environment, the government protects itself from the same suits, therefore green libertarians call for the abolition of <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_immunity" title="Sovereign immunity">sovereign immunity</a>. Increasingly, federal and state law is being amended by lobbyists for those who pollute or extract resources from public lands or waterways so that such actions can no longer be challenged in the courts.
</p><p>The green libertarian philosophy supports <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_liberalism" title="Constitutional liberalism">constitutionally</a> <a href="/wiki/Limited_government" title="Limited government">limited government</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grassroots_democracy" title="Grassroots democracy">grassroots democracy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Decentralization" title="Decentralization">decentralized</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minarchism" title="Minarchism">minarchism</a>. Although many in the movement oppose <a href="/wiki/Business#Government_regulation" title="Business">government regulation</a> of business, believing it to be generally counterproductive, they contend that different legal and economic principles such as <a href="/wiki/Environmental_full-cost_accounting" title="Environmental full-cost accounting">full-cost accounting</a> or "internalizing externalities" – rather than government regulations – would be more effective at remedying problems such as <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>. A central tenet of a <a href="/wiki/Free-market_environmentalism" title="Free-market environmentalism">libertarian environmentalist</a> stance is that corporate <a href="/wiki/Externality" title="Externality">externalities</a> are not priced into the market correctly, creating <a href="/wiki/Market_distortion" title="Market distortion">market distortions</a> in the valuation and price of goods, healthy living and the value of the environment. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" title="Greenhouse gases">Greenhouse gases</a> should be taxed directly according to a formula which calculates the negative costs to the global environment of burning more non-renewable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>. This approach, it is argued, also has the advantage of providing the correct <a href="/wiki/Price_signal" title="Price signal">price signals</a> to utilities and other energy consumers so that they can rapidly convert to more environmentally friendly technologies. The <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Institute" title="Rocky Mountain Institute">Rocky Mountain Institute</a> advocates this kind of <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">market-based</a> environmental protection strategy.
</p>
