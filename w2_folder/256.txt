<p><i><b>Evergreen, Volume 2</b></i> is the second album from the <a href="/wiki/Stone_Poneys" title="Stone Poneys">Stone Poneys</a>, released five months after <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stone_Poneys_(album)" title="The Stone Poneys (album)">The Stone Poneys</a></i>. It was the most commercially successful of the Stone Poneys' three studio albums.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_data">Release data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen,_Volume_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Release data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album was released in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LP_album" title="LP album">LP</a> format on <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol</a> in June 1967 in both <a href="/wiki/Monaural" title="Monaural">monaural</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereophonic" title="Stereophonic">stereophonic</a> editions (catalogue numbers T 2763 and ST 2763, respectively), and subsequently, on <a href="/wiki/8-track_tape" title="8-track tape">8-track tape</a> (catalogue number 8XT 2763) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_cassette" title="Compact cassette">cassette</a> (catalogue number C4-80129). In 1995, Capitol reissued the album on <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">CD</a> (catalogue number CDP-80129).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Raven_Records" title="Raven Records">Raven</a> issued a 27-track "two-fer" CD In 2008, featuring all tracks from this and the band's first album (under its 1975 reissue name, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Stone_Poneys_Featuring_Linda_Ronstadt" title="The Stone Poneys Featuring Linda Ronstadt">The Stone Poneys Featuring Linda Ronstadt</a></i>), plus four tracks from their third album, <i><a href="/wiki/Linda_Ronstadt,_Stone_Poneys_and_Friends,_Vol._III" title="Linda Ronstadt, Stone Poneys and Friends, Vol. III">Linda Ronstadt, Stone Poneys and Friends, Vol. III</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes_on_the_tracks">Notes on the tracks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen,_Volume_2&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notes on the tracks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a departure from the first album, <a href="/wiki/Linda_Ronstadt" title="Linda Ronstadt">Linda Ronstadt</a> was the lead vocalist on almost all songs, with only occasional harmony vocals. The exception is the title song, "Evergreen" (also released on the B-side of the album's first single, "One for One"). Kenny Edwards sang lead on "Part One", while "Part Two" is an instrumental. Both parts have a <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_rock" title="Psychedelic rock">psychedelic rock</a> feel and feature <a href="/wiki/Sitar" title="Sitar">sitar</a> playing (also by Edwards).
</p><p>The album contains the band's biggest hit, "<a href="/wiki/Different_Drum" title="Different Drum">Different Drum</a>", written by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mike_Nesmith" title="Mike Nesmith">Mike Nesmith</a> prior to his joining <a href="/wiki/The_Monkees" title="The Monkees">The Monkees</a>. The Stone Poneys' version went to No. 12 on <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billboard_Magazine" title="Billboard Magazine">Billboard</a>'s</i> Hot 100 chart (with 'featuring Linda Ronstadt' on the single label; she was the only band member on the track). As Edwards recalled, the band based their original recording of the song on a version by <a href="/wiki/The_Greenbriar_Boys" title="The Greenbriar Boys">The Greenbriar Boys</a> from their 1966 album <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Better_Late_than_Never!" title="Better Late than Never!">Better Late than Never!</a></i>: "We cut a version very much like that, with mandolin, kind of a <a href="/wiki/Jug_band" title="Jug band">jug bandy</a>, bluegrass-lite version."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richie_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Richie-2">[2]</a></sup> Record producer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nik_Venet" title="Nik Venet">Nik Venet</a>, sensing that the song could be a hit, had Ronstadt re-record it with other musicians. However, "Different Drum" did not chart until November 1967, after the band's four-month tour to support the album; Edwards had already left the Stone Poneys by then.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The first single from the album, "One for One," did not chart. It was co-written by Austin DeLone, later a member of seminal <a href="/wiki/Country_rock" title="Country rock">country rock</a> band <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eggs_Over_Easy" title="Eggs Over Easy">Eggs Over Easy</a>, a group credited with launching the <a href="/wiki/Pub_rock_(United_Kingdom)" title="Pub rock (United Kingdom)">pub rock</a> movement in Great Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Five of the songs were co-written by band members <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Kimmel" title="Bobby Kimmel">Bobby Kimmel</a> and Edwards. Kimmel also co-wrote "New Hard Times" – with the unusual theme of examining the downside of '60s affluence – with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mayne_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mayne Smith (page does not exist)">Mayne Smith</a>, a member of the San Francisco Bay Area's first bluegrass band, the Redwood Canyon Ramblers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many of the other songwriters featured on the album, like the Stone Poneys themselves, were struggling <a href="/wiki/Singer-songwriter" title="Singer-songwriter">singer-songwriters</a> on the Los Angeles folk scene. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steve_Gillette" title="Steve Gillette">Steve Gillette</a> contributed "Song about the Rain" and "Back on the Street Again", and sang harmony vocals with Ronstadt on the latter. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunshine_Company" title="Sunshine Company">Sunshine Company</a> had their biggest hit with "Back on the Street Again" (reaching No. 36 in Billboard); and Gillette included it on his eponymous debut album;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> both versions were released in 1967. More than 30 years later, West Coast <a href="/wiki/Bluegrass_music" title="Bluegrass music">bluegrass</a> band Laurel Canyon Ramblers (led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herb_Pederson" title="Herb Pederson">Herb Pederson</a>) released the song as the title track of their third CD, in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>"December Dream," the album's opening track, was written by <a href="/wiki/John_Braheny" title="John Braheny">John Braheny</a>, who had a brief career as a singer-songwriter before moving on to other areas of the music business. <a href="/wiki/Fred_Neil" title="Fred Neil">Fred Neil</a> recorded the song in the same general time period, although it remained unreleased until the 1998 double-CD <a href="/wiki/Compilation_album" title="Compilation album">compilation album</a> <i>The Many Sides of Fred Neil</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Braheny also included it on his eccentric 1970 LP, <i>Some Kind of Change</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
