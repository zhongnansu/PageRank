<p>In <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>, a <b>red–green alliance</b> or <b>red–green coalition</b> is an alliance of "red" (often <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social-democratic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Democratic_socialism" title="Democratic socialism">democratic socialist</a>) parties with "green" (often <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green</a> and/or occasionally <a href="/wiki/Agrarianism" title="Agrarianism">agrarian</a>) parties. The alliance is often based on common left political views, especially a shared distrust of <a href="/wiki/Corporation" title="Corporation">corporate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Institution" title="Institution">institutions</a>. While the "red" social-democratic parties tend to focus on the effects of capitalism on the <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a>, the "green" environmentalist parties tend to focus on the environmental effects of capitalism.
</p><p>The term was coined by conservative <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Sweden" title="Prime Minister of Sweden">Prime Minister of Sweden</a> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Bildt" title="Carl Bildt">Carl Bildt</a> in a debate against the Social Democrat opposition leader <a href="/wiki/Ingvar_Carlsson" title="Ingvar Carlsson">Ingvar Carlsson</a> 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="Red.E2.80.93green_coalition_governments"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Red–green_coalition_governments">Red–green coalition governments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red%E2%80%93green_alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Red–green coalition governments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There have been a number of red–green governments in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> since the 1990s.
</p>
<ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Germany" title="Politics of Germany">Germany</a>, a red–green coalition of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democratic Party of Germany</a> (SPD) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Alliance '90/The Greens</a> led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Chancellor_of_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Federal Chancellor of the Federal Republic of Germany">Chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Schr%C3%B6der" title="Gerhard Schröder">Gerhard Schröder</a> formed the federal government from September 1998 to September 2005.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_France" title="Politics of France">France</a>, the '<a href="/wiki/Plural_Left" title="Plural Left">Plural Left</a>' coalition of the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(France)" title="Socialist Party (France)">Socialist Party</a> (PS), <a href="/wiki/The_Greens_(France)" title="The Greens (France)">The Greens</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Communist_Party" title="French Communist Party">French Communist Party</a> and allies governed from 1997 until 2002. The <a href="/wiki/Ayrault_government" title="Ayrault government">Ayrault government</a> which governed from May 2012 until March 2014 had ministers affiliated with the PS, <a href="/wiki/Radical_Party_of_the_Left" title="Radical Party of the Left">Radical Party of the Left</a> (PRG) and <a href="/wiki/Europe_Ecology_%E2%80%93_The_Greens" title="Europe Ecology – The Greens">Europe Ecology – The Greens</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Second_Valls_government" title="Second Valls government">second Valls government</a> (August 2014 to December 2016) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cazeneuve_Government" title="Cazeneuve Government">Cazeneuve Government</a> (December 2016 to May 2017) were both formed of ministers from the PS, PRG and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecologist_Party_(France)" title="Ecologist Party (France)">Ecologist Party</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Finland" title="Politics of Finland">Finland</a>, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Paavo_Lipponen" title="Paavo Lipponen">Paavo Lipponen</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paavo_Lipponen%27s_first_cabinet" title="Paavo Lipponen's first cabinet">first</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paavo_Lipponen%27s_second_Cabinet" title="Paavo Lipponen's second Cabinet">second cabinets</a> contained ministers from the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Finland" title="Social Democratic Party of Finland">Social Democratic Party of Finland</a> (including Lipponen himself) and <a href="/wiki/Green_League" title="Green League">Green League</a>. The Green League participated in government from April 1995 until May 2002.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Norway" title="Politics of Norway">Norway</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Red%E2%80%93green_coalition_(Norway)" title="Red–green coalition (Norway)">Red-Green Coalition</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(Norway)" title="Labour Party (Norway)">Labour Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Left_Party_(Norway)" title="Socialist Left Party (Norway)">Socialist Left Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Centre_Party_(Norway)" title="Centre Party (Norway)">Centre Party</a> governed Norway as a majority government from the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_parliamentary_election,_2005" title="Norwegian parliamentary election, 2005">2005 general election</a> until <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_parliamentary_election,_2013" title="Norwegian parliamentary election, 2013">2013</a>. The 'green' element was the Centre Party, an <a href="/wiki/Nordic_agrarian_parties" title="Nordic agrarian parties">agrarian party</a> with green as its official color. Though <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Norway)" title="Green Party (Norway)">Greens</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(Norway)" title="Liberal Party (Norway)">Liberals</a> are also green color, they are not considered as green parties.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Iceland" title="Politics of Iceland">Iceland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/First_cabinet_of_J%C3%B3hanna_Sigur%C3%B0ard%C3%B3ttir" title="First cabinet of Jóhanna Sigurðardóttir">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_cabinet_of_J%C3%B3hanna_Sigur%C3%B0ard%C3%B3ttir" title="Second cabinet of Jóhanna Sigurðardóttir">Second</a> Cabinets of Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3hanna_Sigur%C3%B0ard%C3%B3ttir" title="Jóhanna Sigurðardóttir">Jóhanna Sigurðardóttir</a> were formed from a coalition of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Alliance" title="Social Democratic Alliance">Social Democratic Alliance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Left-Green_Movement" title="Left-Green Movement">Left-Green Movement</a>, governing from February 2009 to May 2013.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Italy" title="Politics of Italy">Italy</a>, from 1996-2001 and 2006-2008, the <a href="/wiki/Prodi_I_Cabinet" title="Prodi I Cabinet">Prodi I</a>, <a href="/wiki/D%27Alema_I_Cabinet" title="D'Alema I Cabinet">D'Alema I</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=D%27Alema_II_Cabinet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="D'Alema II Cabinet (page does not exist)">D'Alema II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amato_II_Cabinet" title="Amato II Cabinet">Amato II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prodi_II_Cabinet" title="Prodi II Cabinet">Prodi II</a> Cabinets included the social-democratic <a href="/wiki/Democrats_of_the_Left" title="Democrats of the Left">Democrats of the Left</a> (later to become the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(Italy)" title="Democratic Party (Italy)">Democratic Party</a>) as the largest party, with the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_the_Greens" title="Federation of the Greens">Federation of the Greens</a> receiving at least one ministry. However, unlike a straightforward red–green alliance, these centre-left cabinets involved a broad range of political parties that were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a>-<a href="/wiki/Christian_left" title="Christian left">Christian left</a> inspired, <a href="/wiki/Centrism" title="Centrism">centrist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">social-liberal</a> and even with <a href="/wiki/Italian_Communist_Party" title="Italian Communist Party">communist</a> backgrounds.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Denmark" title="Politics of Denmark">Denmark</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Thorning-Schmidt_I_Cabinet" title="Thorning-Schmidt I Cabinet">Thorning-Schmidt government</a>, which governed from October 2011 to February 2014, contained the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Social Democrats (Denmark)">Social Democrats</a> as the largest party in coalition with the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Social_Liberal_Party" title="Danish Social Liberal Party">Social Liberals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socialist_People%27s_Party_(Denmark)" title="Socialist People's Party (Denmark)">Socialist People's Party</a>, the latter being a green party and member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Party" title="European Green Party">European Green Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Global_Greens" title="Global Greens">Global Greens</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Sweden" title="Politics of Sweden">Sweden</a>, the <a href="/wiki/L%C3%B6fven_Cabinet" title="Löfven Cabinet">Löfven Cabinet</a> established on 3 October 2014 is a <a href="/wiki/Minority_government" title="Minority government">minority government</a> coalition of the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Social_Democratic_Party" title="Swedish Social Democratic Party">Social Democrats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Sweden)" title="Green Party (Sweden)">Greens</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Portugal" title="Politics of Portugal">Portugal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/XXI_Constitutional_Government_of_Portugal" title="XXI Constitutional Government of Portugal">António Costa Cabinet</a> established on 26 November 2015 is a <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(Portugal)" title="Socialist Party (Portugal)">Socialist Party</a> <a href="/wiki/Minority_government" title="Minority government">minority government</a> with external support of the <a href="/wiki/Left_Bloc" title="Left Bloc">Left Bloc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Communist_Party" title="Portuguese Communist Party">Portuguese Communist Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecologist_Party_%22The_Greens%22" title='Ecologist Party "The Greens"'>Ecologist Party The Greens</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span id="Radical_red.E2.80.93green_alliances"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Radical_red–green_alliances">Radical red–green alliances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red%E2%80%93green_alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Radical red–green alliances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_left" title="Green left">Green left</a></div>
<p>Political parties or <a href="/wiki/Electoral_alliance" title="Electoral alliance">joint electoral lists</a> have been formed over the years, most often between <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialists</a> and left-oriented <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">greens</a>. Example include:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GreenLeft" title="GreenLeft">GreenLeft</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>: a political party that began in 1989 as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_coalition" title="Political coalition">political alliance</a> comprising the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Netherlands" title="Communist Party of the Netherlands">Communist Party of the Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pacifist_Socialist_Party" title="Pacifist Socialist Party">Pacifist Socialist Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Christian_left" title="Christian left">Christian left</a> parties <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_People%27s_Party_(Netherlands)" title="Evangelical People's Party (Netherlands)">Evangelical People's Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Political_Party_of_Radicals" title="Political Party of Radicals">Political Party of Radicals</a>. The alliance had been known as <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_(Netherlands)" title="Rainbow (Netherlands)">Rainbow</a> for the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1989_(Netherlands)" title="European Parliament election, 1989 (Netherlands)">1989 European elections</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Red%E2%80%93Green_Alliance_(Denmark)" title="Red–Green Alliance (Denmark)">Unity List – The Red–Greens</a> of Denmark:  a political party, originally a political alliance, formed in 1989 by the <a href="/wiki/Left_Socialists" title="Left Socialists">Left Socialists</a> (VS), <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Denmark" title="Communist Party of Denmark">Communist Party of Denmark</a> (DKP) and <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Workers_Party_(Denmark)" title="Socialist Workers Party (Denmark)">Socialist Workers Party</a> (SAP).</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Nordic_Green_Left_Alliance" title="Nordic Green Left Alliance">Nordic Green Left Alliance</a> was a European political alliance formed by the <a href="/wiki/Left_Alliance_(Finland)" title="Left Alliance (Finland)">Left Alliance</a> (<a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Left-Green_Movement" title="Left-Green Movement">Left-Green Movement</a> (<a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Left_Party_(Sweden)" title="Left Party (Sweden)">Left Party</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Left_Party_(Norway)" title="Socialist Left Party (Norway)">Socialist Left Party</a> (<a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_People%27s_Party_(Denmark)" title="Socialist People's Party (Denmark)">Socialist People's Party</a> (<a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">MEPs</a> of the NGLA sat in the <a href="/wiki/European_United_Left%E2%80%93Nordic_Green_Left" title="European United Left–Nordic Green Left">European United Left–Nordic Green Left</a> (GUE/NGL) group in the European Parliament, although the MEPs of the Socialist People's Party sat in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Greens%E2%80%93European_Free_Alliance" title="The Greens–European Free Alliance">The Greens–European Free Alliance</a> (G/EFA) group and later joined the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Party" title="European Green Party">European Green Party</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Left_Ecology_Freedom" title="Left Ecology Freedom">Left Ecology Freedom</a>: a former political party in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> that was initially formed as a political alliance comprising socialists, greens and social democrats. The political alliance was itself a partial successor to the short-lived <a href="/wiki/The_Left_%E2%80%93_The_Rainbow" title="The Left – The Rainbow">The Left – The Rainbow</a> electoral alliance which had existed in Italy from December 2007 until May 2008 comprising the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_the_Greens" title="Federation of the Greens">Federation of the Greens</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Refoundation_Party" title="Communist Refoundation Party">Communist Refoundation Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Party_of_Italian_Communists" title="Party of Italian Communists">Party of Italian Communists</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Left_(Italy)" title="Democratic Left (Italy)">Democratic Left</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Syriza" title="Syriza">Coalition of the Radical Left</a> (SYRIZA), a Greek party formed by the merger of a broad set of left-wing and eco-socialist parties, many of which were themselves formerly red–green alliances, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coalition_of_Left,_of_Movements_and_Ecology" title="Coalition of Left, of Movements and Ecology">Coalition of Left, of Movements and Ecology</a> (Synaspismos), <a href="/wiki/Renewing_Communist_Ecological_Left" title="Renewing Communist Ecological Left">Renewing Communist Ecological Left</a> (AKOA), and <a href="/wiki/Ecosocialists_of_Greece" title="Ecosocialists of Greece">Ecosocialists of Greece</a>. The SYRIZA-led <a href="/wiki/Second_Cabinet_of_Alexis_Tsipras" title="Second Cabinet of Alexis Tsipras">cabinet of Alexis Tsipras</a> contains ones junior minister, <a href="/wiki/Giannis_Tsironis" title="Giannis Tsironis">Giannis Tsironis</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/Ecologist_Greens" title="Ecologist Greens">Ecologist Greens</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span id="Red.E2.80.93green_alliances_with_centre-left_parties"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Red–green_alliances_with_centre-left_parties">Red–green alliances with centre-left parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red%E2%80%93green_alliance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Red–green alliances with centre-left parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are also red/green political alliances and/or electoral agreements between social-democratic or <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberal</a> parties with green parties
</p>
<ul><li>In Canada, the term <i>red–green alliance</i> has been used to describe the limited co-operation between the centrist <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal Party of Canada</a> which uses red as its colour, and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">Green Party of Canada</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which is centre-left but not seen as being as radical as many of its overseas sister parties.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Red-Greens_(Sweden)" title="Red-Greens (Sweden)">Red-Greens</a> (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <i lang="sv">De rödgröna</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red-green_political_alliance" title="Red-green political alliance">red-green political alliance</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, established on 7 December 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It consisted of the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Social_Democratic_Party" title="Swedish Social Democratic Party">Social Democrats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Left_Party_(Sweden)" title="Left Party (Sweden)">Left Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Sweden)" title="Green Party (Sweden)">Greens</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Riksdag" title="Riksdag">Riksdag</a>, sitting in opposition to the centre-right <a href="/wiki/Alliance_(Sweden)" title="Alliance (Sweden)">Alliance for Sweden</a> coalition government. The Red-Greens aimed to achieve a majority at the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_general_election,_2010" title="Swedish general election, 2010">2010 Swedish general election</a> held on 19 September 2010 and form its own <a href="/wiki/Coalition_government" title="Coalition government">coalition government</a>. However, the Red-Greens failed to unseat the incumbent centre-right Alliance government, and the pact was dissolved on 26 October 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> After that, the Social Democrats and Greens remain co-operations and formed a minority government coalition after the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_general_election,_2014" title="Swedish general election, 2014">2014 general election</a>.</li>
<li>A red–green alliance of sorts occurred during the campaign leading up to the <a href="/wiki/London_mayoral_election,_2008" title="London mayoral election, 2008">London mayoral election, 2008</a>. Incumbent mayor <a href="/wiki/Ken_Livingstone" title="Ken Livingstone">Ken Livingstone</a>, candidate for the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>, formed an electoral pact with the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party</a> mayoral candidate <a href="/wiki/Si%C3%A2n_Berry" title="Siân Berry">Siân Berry</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Contingent_vote#Supplementary_Vote" title="Contingent vote">supplementary voting system</a>, in which Labour voters were encouraged to place the Green candidate as their second preference, and vice versa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>In Italy, <a href="/wiki/The_Olive_Tree_(Italy)" title="The Olive Tree (Italy)">The Olive Tree</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Union_(Italy)" title="The Union (Italy)">The Union</a> coalitions comprised the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_the_Greens" title="Federation of the Greens">Federation of the Greens</a> along with social-democratic, <a href="/wiki/Christian_left" title="Christian left">social Christian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centrism" title="Centrism">centrist</a> and other parties in a broad heterogenous centre-left alliance. The successor party to the Olive Tree, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(Italy)" title="Democratic Party (Italy)">Democratic Party</a>, maintains an internal faction of greens called the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Ecologists" title="Democratic Ecologists">Democratic Ecologists</a>.</li>
<li>In the run up to <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2017" title="New Zealand general election, 2017">New Zealand's 2017 general election</a>, the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party" title="New Zealand Labour Party">New Zealand Labour Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand" title="Green Party of Aotearoa New Zealand">Green Party of Aotearoa</a> signed a <a href="/wiki/Memorandum_of_understanding" title="Memorandum of understanding">memorandum of understanding</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> This formed a loose relationship between the two parties with the goal of working together when possible to unseat the incumbent <a href="/wiki/Fifth_National_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Fifth National Government of New Zealand">National Government</a>. Later, the two parties also agreed to a set of budget responsibility rules, committing both parties to sustainable surpluses and capping debt, amongst other rules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Following the election, a Labour-led coalition government was formed, supported by the Greens through a confidence and supply arrangement.</li>
<li>In Australia, the term <i>red–green alliance</i> has been used to describe the limited co-operation between the centre-left <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party" title="Australian Labor Party">Australian Labor Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Greens" title="Australian Greens">Australian Greens</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li></ul>
