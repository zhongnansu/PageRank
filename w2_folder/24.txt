<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Greenhouse</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_(disambiguation)" title="Greenhouse (disambiguation)">Greenhouse (disambiguation)</a>, <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Green_House_(disambiguation)" title="Green House (disambiguation)">Green House (disambiguation)</a>, <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Greene_House_(disambiguation)" title="Greene House (disambiguation)">Greene House (disambiguation)</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Hothouse_(disambiguation)" title="Hothouse (disambiguation)">Hothouse (disambiguation)</a>.</div>

<p>A <b>greenhouse</b> (also called a <b>glasshouse</b>, or, if with sufficient heating, a <b>hothouse</b>) is a structure with walls and roof made chiefly of transparent material, such as glass, in which <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a> requiring regulated climatic conditions are grown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> These structures range in size from small sheds to industrial-sized buildings. A miniature greenhouse is known as a <a href="/wiki/Cold_frame" title="Cold frame">cold frame</a>. The interior of a greenhouse exposed to sunlight becomes significantly warmer than the external ambient temperature, protecting its contents in cold weather.
</p><p>Many commercial glass greenhouses or hothouses are <a href="/wiki/High_tech" title="High tech">high tech</a> production facilities for vegetables or flowers. The glass greenhouses are filled with equipment including screening installations, heating, cooling, lighting, and may be controlled by a computer to optimize conditions for plant growth. Different techniques are then used to evaluate optimality-degrees and comfort ratio of greenhouse micro-climate (i.e., air temperature, relative humidity and vapor pressure deficit) in order to reduce production risk prior to cultivation of a specific crop.
</p>

<p>The idea of growing plants in environmentally controlled areas has existed since <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> times. The Roman emperor <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a> ate a <a href="/wiki/Armenian_cucumber" title="Armenian cucumber">cucumber-like</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> vegetable daily.  The Roman gardeners used artificial methods (similar to the greenhouse system) of growing to have it available for his table every day of the year. Cucumbers were planted in wheeled carts which were put in the sun daily, then taken inside to keep them warm at night. The cucumbers were stored under frames or in cucumber houses glazed with either oiled cloth known as <i>specularia</i> or with sheets of <a href="/wiki/Selenite_(mineral)" title="Selenite (mineral)">selenite</a> (a.k.a. <i>lapis specularis</i>), according to the description by <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">Pliny the Elder</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first description of a heated greenhouse is from the <i><a href="/wiki/Sanga_Yorok" title="Sanga Yorok">Sanga Yorok</a></i>, a treatise on husbandry compiled by a royal physician of the <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a> dynasty of Korea during the 1450s, in its chapter on cultivating vegetables during winter. The treatise contains detailed instructions on constructing a greenhouse that is capable of cultivating vegetables, forcing flowers, and ripening fruit within an artificially heated environment, by utilizing <i><a href="/wiki/Ondol" title="Ondol">ondol</a></i>, the traditional Korean underfloor heating system, to maintain heat and humidity; cob walls to insulate heat; and semi-transparent oiled <i><a href="/wiki/Korean_paper" title="Korean paper">hanji</a></i> windows to permit light penetration for plant growth and provide protection from the outside environment. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annals_of_the_Joseon_Dynasty" title="Annals of the Joseon Dynasty">Annals of the Joseon Dynasty</a> confirm that greenhouse-like structures incorporating <i>ondol</i> were constructed to provide heat for <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_orange" title="Mandarin orange">mandarin orange</a> trees during the winter of 1438.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept of greenhouses also appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and then <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in the 17th century, along with the plants. Some of these early attempts required enormous amounts of work to close up at night or to winterize. There were serious problems with providing adequate and balanced heat in these early greenhouses. Today, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> has many of the largest greenhouses in the world, some of them so vast that they are able to produce millions of vegetables every year.
</p><p>The French botanist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lucien_Bonaparte" title="Charles Lucien Bonaparte">Charles Lucien Bonaparte</a> is often credited with building the first practical modern greenhouse in <a href="/wiki/Leiden" title="Leiden">Leiden</a>, Holland, during the 1800s to grow medicinal tropical plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
Originally only on the estates of the rich, the growth of the science of <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">botany</a> caused greenhouses to spread to the universities. The French called their first greenhouses <i>orangeries</i>, since they were used to protect orange trees from freezing. As pineapples became popular, <i>pineries</i>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pineapple_Pit" title="Pineapple Pit">pineapple pits</a>, were built.
</p><p>Experimentation with the design of greenhouses continued during the 17th century in Europe, as technology produced better glass and construction techniques improved. The greenhouse at the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Versailles" title="Palace of Versailles">Palace of Versailles</a> was an example of their size and elaborateness; it was more than 150 metres (490 ft) long, 13 metres (43 ft) wide, and 14 metres (46 ft) high.
</p><p>The golden era of the greenhouse was in England during the Victorian era, where the largest glasshouses yet conceived were constructed, as the wealthy upper class and aspiring botanists competed to build the most elaborate buildings. A good example of this trend is the pioneering <a href="/wiki/Kew_Gardens" title="Kew Gardens">Kew Gardens</a>. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Paxton" title="Joseph Paxton">Joseph Paxton</a>, who had experimented with glass and iron in the creation of large greenhouses as the head gardener at <a href="/wiki/Chatsworth_House" title="Chatsworth House">Chatsworth</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>, working for the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Devonshire" title="Duke of Devonshire">Duke of Devonshire</a>, designed and built <a href="/wiki/The_Crystal_Palace" title="The Crystal Palace">The Crystal Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, (although the latter was constructed for both horticultural and non-horticultural exhibition).
</p><p>Other large greenhouses built in the 19th century included the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Crystal_Palace" title="New York Crystal Palace">New York Crystal Palace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Glaspalast_(Munich)" title="Glaspalast (Munich)">Glaspalast</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Greenhouses_of_Laeken" title="Royal Greenhouses of Laeken">Royal Greenhouses of Laeken</a> (1874–1895) for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Leopold_II_of_Belgium" title="King Leopold II of Belgium">King Leopold II of Belgium</a>.
</p><p>In Japan, the first greenhouse was built in 1880 by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Cocking" title="Samuel Cocking">Samuel Cocking</a>, a British merchant who exported <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herbs</a>.
</p><p>In the 20th century, the <a href="/wiki/Geodesic_dome" title="Geodesic dome">geodesic dome</a> was added to the many types of greenhouses. Notable examples are the <a href="/wiki/Eden_Project" title="Eden Project">Eden Project</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Rodale_Institute" title="The Rodale Institute">The Rodale Institute</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  in Pennsylvania, the <a href="/wiki/Climatron" title="Climatron">Climatron</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_Botanical_Garden" title="Missouri Botanical Garden">Missouri Botanical Garden</a> in St. Louis, Missouri, and <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Motor_Manufacturing_Kentucky" title="Toyota Motor Manufacturing Kentucky">Toyota Motor Manufacturing Kentucky</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Greenhouse structures adapted in the 1960s when wider sheets of polyethylene film became widely available. Hoop houses were made by several companies and were also frequently made by the growers themselves. Constructed of aluminum extrusions, special galvanized steel tubing, or even just lengths of steel or PVC water pipe, construction costs were greatly reduced. This resulted in many more greenhouses being constructed on smaller farms and garden centers. Polyethylene film durability increased greatly when more effective UV-inhibitors were developed and added in the 1970s; these extended the usable life of the film from one or two years up to 3 and eventually 4 or more years.
</p><p>Gutter-connected greenhouses became more prevalent in the 1980s and 1990s. These greenhouses have two or more bays connected by a common wall, or row of support posts. Heating inputs were reduced as the ratio of floor area to exterior wall area was increased substantially. Gutter-connected greenhouses are now commonly used both in production and in situations where plants are grown and sold to the public as well. Gutter-connected greenhouses are commonly covered with structured polycarbonate materials, or a double layer of polyethylene film with air blown between to provide increased heating efficiencies.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory_of_operation">Theory of operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theory of operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The warmer temperature in a greenhouse occurs because incident <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> passes through the transparent roof and walls and is absorbed by the floor, earth, and contents, which become warmer. As the structure is not open to the atmosphere, the warmed air cannot escape via <a href="/wiki/Convective_heat_transfer" title="Convective heat transfer">convection</a>, so the temperature inside the greenhouse rises. This differs from the earth-oriented theory known as the "<a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect#Real_greenhouses" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Quantitative studies suggest that the effect of infrared radiative cooling is not negligibly small, and may have economic implications in a heated greenhouse. Analysis of issues of near-infrared radiation in a greenhouse with screens of a high coefficient of reflection concluded that installation of such screens reduced heat demand by about 8%, and application of dyes to transparent surfaces was suggested. Composite less-reflective glass, or less effective but cheaper anti-reflective coated simple glass, also produced savings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ventilation">Ventilation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ventilation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ventilation_(architecture)" title="Ventilation (architecture)">Ventilation</a> is one of the most important components in a successful greenhouse. If there is no proper ventilation, greenhouses and their growing plants can become prone to problems. The main purposes of ventilation are to regulate the temperature and humidity to the optimal level, and to ensure movement of air and thus prevent build-up of plant pathogens (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Botrytis_cinerea" title="Botrytis cinerea">Botrytis cinerea</a></i>) that prefer still air conditions. Ventilation also ensures a supply of fresh air for photosynthesis and plant <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">respiration</a>, and may enable important <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pollinators" title="Pollinators">pollinators</a> to access the greenhouse crop.
</p><p>Ventilation can be achieved via use of vents - often controlled automatically via a computer - and recirculation fans.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heating_and_cooling">Heating and cooling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Heating and cooling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heating" title="Heating">Heating</a> or <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> is one of the most considerable costs in the operation of greenhouses across the globe, especially in colder climates. The main problem with heating a greenhouse as opposed to a building that has solid <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opaque</a> walls is the amount of heat lost through the greenhouse covering. Since the coverings need to allow light to filter into the structure, they conversely cannot insulate very well. With traditional plastic greenhouse coverings having an <a href="/wiki/R-value_(insulation)" title="R-value (insulation)">R-value</a> of around 2, a great amount of money is therefore spent to continually replace the heat lost. Most greenhouses, when supplemental heat is needed use natural gas or <a href="/wiki/Electric_heating" title="Electric heating">electric furnaces</a>.
</p><p>Passive heating methods exist which seek heat using low energy input. <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_collector" title="Solar thermal collector">Solar energy can be captured</a> from periods of relative abundance (day time/summer), and released to boost the temperature during cooler periods (night time/winter). <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">Waste heat</a> from livestock can also be used to heat greenhouses, e.g., placing a chicken coop inside a greenhouse recovers the heat generated by the chickens, which would otherwise be wasted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some greenhouses also rely on <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heating" title="Geothermal heating">geothermal heating</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cooling is typically done by opening windows in the greenhouse when it gets too warm for the plants inside it. This can be done manually, or in an automated manner. Window actuators can open windows due to temperature difference<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> or can be opened by <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronic</a> controllers. Electronic controllers are often used to monitor the temperature and adjusts the furnace operation to the conditions. This can be as simple as a basic thermostat, but can be more complicated in larger greenhouse operations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lighting">Lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the day, light enters the greenhouse via the windows and is used by the plants. Some greenhouses are also equipped with <a href="/wiki/Grow_light" title="Grow light">grow lights</a> (often LED lights) which are switched on at night to increase the amount of light the plants get, hereby increasing the yield with certain crops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_enrichment">Carbon dioxide enrichment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide enrichment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The possibility of using <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> enrichment in greenhouse cultivation to enhance plant growth has been known for nearly 100 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> After the development of equipment for the controlled serial enrichment of carbon dioxide, the technique was established on a broad scale in the Netherlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Secondary metabolites, e.g., cardiac glycosides in <a href="/wiki/Digitalis_lanata" title="Digitalis lanata">Digitalis lanata</a>, are produced in higher amounts by greenhouse cultivation at enhanced temperature and at enhanced carbon dioxide concentration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Carbon dioxide enrichment can also reduce greenhouse water usage by a significant fraction by mitigating the total air-flow needed to supply adequate carbon for plant growth and thereby reducing the quantity of water lost to evaporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Commercial greenhouses are now frequently located near appropriate industrial facilities for mutual benefit. For example, Cornerways Nursery in the UK is strategically placed near a major sugar refinery,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> consuming both waste heat and CO<sub>2</sub> from the refinery which would otherwise be vented to atmosphere. The refinery reduces its carbon emissions, whilst the nursery enjoys boosted tomato yields and does not need to provide its own greenhouse heating.
</p><p>Enrichment only becomes effective where, by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liebig%27s_law" title="Liebig's law">Liebig's law</a>, carbon dioxide has become the <a href="/wiki/Limiting_factor" title="Limiting factor">limiting factor</a>. In a controlled greenhouse, <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> may be trivial, and soils may be <a href="/wiki/Soil_fertility" title="Soil fertility">fertile</a> by default. In less-controlled gardens and open fields, <a href="/wiki/Keeling_Curve" title="Keeling Curve">rising CO<sub>2</sub> levels</a> only increase <a href="/wiki/Primary_production" title="Primary production">primary production</a> to the point of soil depletion (assuming no droughts,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> flooding,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> or both<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>), as demonstrated <i>prima facie</i> by CO<sub>2</sub> levels continuing to rise.  In addition, laboratory experiments, free air carbon enrichment (FACE) test plots,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and field measurements provide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Replicability" title="Replicability">replicability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:!!!_Palazzo_Scicluna,_now_Parisio_06.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1936" data-file-width="2592" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/%21%21%21_Palazzo_Scicluna%2C_now_Parisio_06.jpg/220px-%21%21%21_Palazzo_Scicluna%2C_now_Parisio_06.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/%21%21%21_Palazzo_Scicluna%2C_now_Parisio_06.jpg/330px-%21%21%21_Palazzo_Scicluna%2C_now_Parisio_06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/%21%21%21_Palazzo_Scicluna%2C_now_Parisio_06.jpg/440px-%21%21%21_Palazzo_Scicluna%2C_now_Parisio_06.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:!!!_Palazzo_Scicluna,_now_Parisio_06.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Recreational Greenhouse at <a href="/wiki/Palazzo_Parisio_(Naxxar)" title="Palazzo Parisio (Naxxar)">Palazzo Parisio</a>, Malta.</div></div></div>
<p>In domestic greenhouses, the glass used is typically 3mm (or ⅛″) 'horticultural glass' grade, which is good quality glass that should not contain air bubbles (which can produce scorching on leaves by acting like lenses).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Plastics mostly used are <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> film and multiwall sheets of <a href="/wiki/Polycarbonate" title="Polycarbonate">polycarbonate</a> material, or PMMA <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acrylic_glass" title="Acrylic glass">acrylic glass</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Commercial glass greenhouses are often high-tech production facilities for vegetables or flowers. The glass greenhouses are filled with equipment such as screening installations, heating, cooling and lighting, and may be automatically controlled by a computer.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch_Light">Dutch Light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dutch Light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the UK and other Northern European countries a pane of horticultural glass referred to as "Dutch Light" was historically used as a standard unit of construction, having dimensions of 28¾″ x 56″ (approx. 730mm x 1422 mm). This size gives a larger glazed area when compared with using smaller panes such as the 600mm width typically used in modern domestic designs which then require more supporting framework for a given overall greenhouse size. A style of greenhouse having sloped sides (resulting in a wider base than at eaves height) and using these panes uncut is also often referred to as of "Dutch Light design", and a <a href="/wiki/Cold_frame" title="Cold frame">cold frame</a> using a full- or half-pane as being of "Dutch" or "half-Dutch" size.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greenhouses allow for greater control over the growing environment of plants. Depending upon the technical specification of a greenhouse, key factors which may be controlled include temperature, levels of light and shade, <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> application, and atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>. Greenhouses may be used to overcome shortcomings in the growing qualities of a piece of land, such as a short growing season or poor light levels, and they can thereby improve food production in marginal environments.  Greenhouses in hot, dry climates used specifically to provide shade are sometimes called "shadehouses".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>As they may enable certain crops to be grown throughout the year, greenhouses are increasingly important in the food supply of high-latitude countries. One of the largest complexes in the world is in <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Almer%C3%ADa" title="Province of Almería">Almería</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andaluc%C3%ADa" title="Andalucía">Andalucía</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, where greenhouses cover almost 200 km<sup>2</sup> (49,000 acres).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plastic_sea,_Almer%C3%ADa_Spain.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2008" data-file-width="3032" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Plastic_sea%2C_Almer%C3%ADa_Spain.jpg/220px-Plastic_sea%2C_Almer%C3%ADa_Spain.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Plastic_sea%2C_Almer%C3%ADa_Spain.jpg/330px-Plastic_sea%2C_Almer%C3%ADa_Spain.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Plastic_sea%2C_Almer%C3%ADa_Spain.jpg/440px-Plastic_sea%2C_Almer%C3%ADa_Spain.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plastic_sea,_Almer%C3%ADa_Spain.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Greenhouses in <a href="/wiki/Almer%C3%ADa" title="Almería">Almería</a> as seen from space. Credit: <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a></div></div></div>
<p>Greenhouses are often used for growing <a href="/wiki/Floriculture" title="Floriculture">flowers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Transplanting" title="Transplanting">transplants</a>. Special greenhouse varieties of certain crops, such as tomatoes, are generally used for commercial production. Many vegetables and flowers can be grown in greenhouses in late winter and early spring, and then transplanted outside as the weather warms. <a href="/wiki/Bumblebee" title="Bumblebee">Bumblebees</a> are the <a href="/wiki/Pollinator" title="Pollinator">pollinators</a> of choice for most <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">pollination</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> although other types of <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bees</a> have been used, as well as artificial pollination. <a href="/wiki/Hydroponics" title="Hydroponics">Hydroponics</a> (especially hydroponic <a href="/wiki/A-frame" title="A-frame">A-frames</a>) can be used to make the most use of the interior space.
</p><p>The relatively closed environment of a greenhouse has its own unique management requirements, compared with outdoor production. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pest_(animal)" title="Pest (animal)">Pests</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">diseases</a>, and extremes of heat and humidity, have to be controlled, and irrigation is necessary to provide water. Most greenhouses use sprinklers or drip lines. Significant inputs of heat and light may be required, particularly with winter production of warm-weather vegetables.
</p><p>Greenhouses also have applications outside of the <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> industry. <a href="/wiki/GlassPoint_Solar" title="GlassPoint Solar">GlassPoint Solar</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Fremont,_California" title="Fremont, California">Fremont, California</a>, encloses solar fields in greenhouses to produce steam for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Enhanced_Oil_Recovery" title="Solar Enhanced Oil Recovery">solar-enhanced oil recovery</a>. For example, in November 2017 GlassPoint announced that it is developing a <a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Solar thermal enhanced oil recovery">solar enhanced oil recovery</a> facility near Bakersfield, CA that uses greenhouses to enclose its <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_trough" title="Parabolic trough">parabolic troughs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="Alpine_house"></span>
An "alpine house" is a specialized greenhouse used for growing <a href="/wiki/Alpine_plant" title="Alpine plant">alpine plants</a>. The purpose of an alpine house is to mimic the conditions in which alpine plants grow; particularly to provide protection from wet conditions in winter. Alpine houses are often unheated, since the plants grown there are hardy, or require at most protection from hard frost in the winter. They are designed to have excellent ventilation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grif85_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grif85-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adoption">Adoption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Adoption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Westland_kassen.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="488" data-file-width="1576" height="68" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Westland_kassen.jpg/220px-Westland_kassen.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Westland_kassen.jpg/330px-Westland_kassen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Westland_kassen.jpg/440px-Westland_kassen.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Westland_kassen.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Greenhouses in the <a href="/wiki/Westland_(region),_Netherlands" title="Westland (region), Netherlands">Westland region</a>, Netherlands</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TomateJungpflanzenAnzuchtNiederlande.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/TomateJungpflanzenAnzuchtNiederlande.jpg/220px-TomateJungpflanzenAnzuchtNiederlande.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/TomateJungpflanzenAnzuchtNiederlande.jpg/330px-TomateJungpflanzenAnzuchtNiederlande.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/TomateJungpflanzenAnzuchtNiederlande.jpg/440px-TomateJungpflanzenAnzuchtNiederlande.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TomateJungpflanzenAnzuchtNiederlande.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Young tomatoes in an industrial-sized greenhouse in the Netherlands</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> has some of the largest greenhouses in the world. Such is the scale of food production in the country that in 2000, greenhouses occupied 10,526 hectares, or 0.25% of the total land area.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Greenhouses began to be built in the <a href="/wiki/Westland_(region),_Netherlands" title="Westland (region), Netherlands">Westland region</a> of the Netherlands in the mid-19th century. The addition of sand to bogs and clay soil created fertile soil for agriculture, and around 1850, grapes were grown in the first greenhouses, simple glass constructions with one of the sides consisting of a solid wall. By the early 20th century, greenhouses began to be constructed with all sides built using glass, and they began to be heated. This also allowed for the production of fruits and vegetables that did not ordinarily grow in the area. Today, the Westland and the area around <a href="/wiki/Aalsmeer" title="Aalsmeer">Aalsmeer</a> have the highest concentration of greenhouse agriculture in the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Westland produces mostly vegetables, besides plants and flowers; Murno Gladst is noted mainly for the production of flowers and potted plants. Since the 20th century, the area around <a href="/wiki/Venlo" title="Venlo">Venlo</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Drenthe" title="Drenthe">Drenthe</a> have also become important regions for greenhouse agriculture.
</p><p>Since 2000, technical innovations include the "closed greenhouse", a completely closed system allowing the grower complete control over the growing process while using less energy. Floating greenhouses<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> are used in watery areas of the country.
</p><p>The Netherlands has around 4,000 greenhouse enterprises that operate over 9,000 hectares<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> of greenhouses and employ some 150,000 workers, producing €7.2 billion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> worth of vegetables, fruit, plants, and flowers, some 80% of which is exported.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Worldwide, there's an estimated 9 million acres of greenhouses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
