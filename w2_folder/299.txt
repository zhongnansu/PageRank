<p>The <b>Green Party</b> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">Comhaontas Glas</i>, literally "Green Alliance") is a <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nordsieck_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nordsieck-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="List of political parties in the Republic of Ireland">political party</a> that operates in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>—both the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. It was founded as the <b>Ecology Party of Ireland</b> in 1981 by <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> teacher <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Fettes" title="Christopher Fettes">Christopher Fettes</a>. The party became the <b>Green Alliance</b> in 1983; it adopted its current English name in 1987, while the Irish name was kept unchanged. Its leader is <a href="/wiki/Eamon_Ryan" title="Eamon Ryan">Eamon Ryan</a>, its deputy leader is <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Martin_(politician)" title="Catherine Martin (politician)">Catherine Martin</a> and its chairman is <a href="/wiki/Roderic_O%27Gorman" title="Roderic O'Gorman">Roderic O'Gorman</a>. Green Party candidates have been elected to most levels of representation: <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Local government in the Republic of Ireland">local</a> (in the Republic), <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="Dáil Éireann">Dáil Éireann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>.
</p><p>The Green Party first entered the Dáil in 1989. It has served in the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Irish government</a> once, from 2007 to 2011 as junior partner in a coalition with <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna Fáil">Fianna Fáil</a>. The party suffered a wipeout in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_2011" title="Irish general election, 2011">February 2011 election</a>, losing all six of its TDs. In the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_2016" title="Irish general election, 2016">February 2016 election</a>, it returned to the Dáil with two seats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Following this, <a href="/wiki/Grace_O%27Sullivan" title="Grace O'Sullivan">Grace O'Sullivan</a> was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Seanad Éireann">Seanad</a> on 26 April that year.
</p><p>The party gained its first representation in the Northern Ireland Assembly in 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Green Party in Northern Ireland">Green Party in Northern Ireland</a> having become a regional branch of the party the previous year. It currently has two representatives in the Assembly.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Ireland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Ireland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The party's first electoral outing was at the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_November_1982" title="Irish general election, November 1982">November 1982 general election</a> when seven candidates contested under the <i>Ecology Party</i> banner, winning 0.2% of the vote. Following a name change, it contested the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1984_(Ireland)" title="European Parliament election, 1984 (Ireland)">1984 European elections</a>, with party founder <a href="/wiki/Roger_Garland" title="Roger Garland">Roger Garland</a> winning 1.9% in the Dublin constituency. The following year, it won its first election when Marcus Counihan was elected to <a href="/wiki/Killarney" title="Killarney">Killarney Urban District Council</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Irish_local_elections,_1985" title="Irish local elections, 1985">1985 local elections</a>. The party nationally ran 34 candidates and won 0.6% of the vote.
</p><p>The party continued to struggle until the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1989" title="Irish general election, 1989">1989 general election</a> when the Green Party (as it was now named) won its first <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="Dáil Éireann">Dáil</a> seat, when Roger Garland was elected in <a href="/wiki/Dublin_South_(D%C3%A1il_constituency)" title="Dublin South (Dáil constituency)">Dublin South</a>. Garland lost his seat at the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1992" title="Irish general election, 1992">1992 general election</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Sargent" title="Trevor Sargent">Trevor Sargent</a> gained a seat in <a href="/wiki/Dublin_North_(D%C3%A1il_constituency)" title="Dublin North (Dáil constituency)">Dublin North</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1994_(Ireland)" title="European Parliament election, 1994 (Ireland)">1994 European election</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patricia_McKenna" title="Patricia McKenna">Patricia McKenna</a> topped the poll in the Dublin constituency and <a href="/wiki/Nuala_Ahern" title="Nuala Ahern">Nuala Ahern</a> won a seat in Leinster. They retained their <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> seats in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1999_(Ireland)" title="European Parliament election, 1999 (Ireland)">1999 European election</a>, although the party lost five councillors in local elections held that year despite an increase in its vote. At the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_1997" title="Irish general election, 1997">1997 general election</a>, the party gained a seat when <a href="/wiki/John_Gormley" title="John Gormley">John Gormley</a> won a Dáil seat in <a href="/wiki/Dublin_South-East_(D%C3%A1il_constituency)" title="Dublin South-East (Dáil constituency)">Dublin South-East</a>.
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_2002" title="Irish general election, 2002">2002 general election</a> the party made a breakthrough, getting six <a href="/wiki/Teachta_D%C3%A1la" title="Teachta Dála">Teachtaí Dála</a> (TDs) elected to the Dáil with 4% of the national vote. However, in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2004_(Ireland)" title="European Parliament election, 2004 (Ireland)">2004 European election</a>, the party lost both of its European Parliament seats. In the <a href="/wiki/Irish_local_elections,_2004" title="Irish local elections, 2004">2004 local elections</a>, it increased its number of councillors at county level from 8 to 18 (out of 883) and at town council level from 5 to 14 (out of 744).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Term_in_government">Term in government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Ireland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Term in government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_30th_D%C3%A1il" title="Government of the 30th Dáil">Government of the 30th Dáil</a></div>
<p>The Green Party entered government for the first time after the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_2007" title="Irish general election, 2007">2007 general election</a>, held on 24 May. Although its share of first preference votes increased at the election, the party failed to increase the number of TDs returned. <a href="/wiki/Mary_White_(Green_Party_politician)" title="Mary White (Green Party politician)">Mary White</a> won a seat for the first time in <a href="/wiki/Carlow%E2%80%93Kilkenny_(D%C3%A1il_constituency)" title="Carlow–Kilkenny (Dáil constituency)">Carlow–Kilkenny</a>; however, <a href="/wiki/Dan_Boyle_(politician)" title="Dan Boyle (politician)">Dan Boyle</a> lost his seat in <a href="/wiki/Cork_South-Central_(D%C3%A1il_constituency)" title="Cork South-Central (Dáil constituency)">Cork South-Central</a>.
</p><p>The party had approached the 2007 general election on an independent platform, ruling out no coalition partners while expressing its preference for an alternative to the outgoing coalition of <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna Fáil">Fianna Fáil</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Democrats" title="Progressive Democrats">Progressive Democrats</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Neither the outgoing government nor an alternative of <a href="/wiki/Fine_Gael" title="Fine Gael">Fine Gael</a>, <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(Ireland)" title="Labour Party (Ireland)">Labour</a> and the Green Party had sufficient seats to form a majority. Fine Gael ruled out a coalition arrangement with <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> opening the way for Green Party negotiations with Fianna Fáil.
</p><p>Before the negotiations began, <a href="/wiki/Ciar%C3%A1n_Cuffe" title="Ciarán Cuffe">Ciarán Cuffe</a> TD wrote on his blog that "a deal with Fianna Fáil would be a deal with the devil… and [the Green Party would be] decimated as a Party".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The negotiations were undertaken by Donall Geoghegan (the party's general secretary), Dan Boyle and the then party Chair <a href="/wiki/John_Gormley" title="John Gormley">John Gormley</a>. The Green Party walked out after six days; this, Geoghegan later said, was owing to there not being "enough in [the deal] to allow [the Green Party] to continue".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The negotiations restarted on 11 June with a draft programme for government being agreed one day later, which under party rules needed 66% of members to endorse it at a special convention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
On 13 June 2007, Green members in the <a href="/wiki/Mansion_House,_Dublin" title="Mansion House, Dublin">Mansion House</a>, Dublin, voted 86% in favour (441 to 67; with 2 spoilt votes) of entering coalition with Fianna Fáil. The following day, the six Green Party TDs voted for the re-election of <a href="/wiki/Bertie_Ahern" title="Bertie Ahern">Bertie Ahern</a> as <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>New party leader John Gormley was appointed as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_for_Housing,_Planning,_Community_and_Local_Government" title="Minister for Housing, Planning, Community and Local Government">Minister for the Environment, Heritage and Local Government</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eamon_Ryan" title="Eamon Ryan">Eamon Ryan</a> was appointed as <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Communications,_Climate_Action_and_Environment" title="Minister for Communications, Climate Action and Environment">Minister for Communications, Energy and Natural Resources</a>. Trevor Sargent was named <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_State_for_Food,_Forestry_and_Horticulture" title="Minister of State for Food, Forestry and Horticulture">Minister of State for Food and Horticulture</a> (he resigned this position in 2010).
</p><p>Before their entry into government, the Green Party had been vocal supporters of the <a href="/wiki/Shell_to_Sea" title="Shell to Sea">Shell to Sea</a> movement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> the campaign to reroute the M3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motorway" title="Motorway">motorway</a> away from <a href="/wiki/Hill_of_Tara" title="Hill of Tara">Tara</a> and (to a lesser extent) the campaign to end <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_military" title="United States military">United States military</a> use of <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Airport" title="Shannon Airport">Shannon Airport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> After the Green Party entered government there were no substantive changes in government policy on these issues, which meant that Eamon Ryan oversaw the <a href="/wiki/Corrib_gas_project" title="Corrib gas project">Corrib gas project</a> while he was in office. The Green Party had, at its last annual conference, made an inquiry into the irregularities surrounding the project (see <a href="/wiki/Corrib_gas_controversy" title="Corrib gas controversy">Corrib gas controversy</a>) a precondition of entering government<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> but changed its stance during post-election negotiations with Fianna Fáil.
</p><p>The 2008 budget, announced on 6 December 2007, did not include a carbon levy on fuels such as petrol, diesel and home heating oil, which the Green Party had sought before the election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indep-green-budget_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-indep-green-budget-14">[14]</a></sup> A carbon levy was however introduced in the 2010 Budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The 2008 budget did include a separate carbon budget announced by Gormley,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> which introduced new energy efficiency tax credit,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indep-green-budget_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-indep-green-budget-14">[14]</a></sup> a ban on <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent bulbs</a> from January 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> a tax scheme incentivising commuters' purchases of bicycles<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and a new scale of <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_registration_tax_(Ireland)" title="Vehicle registration tax (Ireland)">vehicle registration tax</a> based on carbon emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>At a special convention on whether to support the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lisbon" title="Treaty of Lisbon">Treaty of Lisbon</a> on 19 January 2008, the party voted 63.5% in favour of supporting the Treaty; this fell short of the party's two-third majority requirement for policy issues. As a result, the Green Party did not have an official campaign in the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-eighth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_Bill_2008" title="Twenty-eighth Amendment of the Constitution Bill 2008">first Lisbon Treaty referendum</a>, although individual members were involved on different sides<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The referendum did not pass in 2008, and following the Irish government's negotiation with EU member states of additional legal guarantees and assurances, the Green Party held another special convention meeting in Dublin on 18 July 2009 to decide its position on the <a href="/wiki/Twenty-eighth_Amendment_of_the_Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Twenty-eighth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland">second Lisbon referendum</a>. Precisely two-thirds of party members present voted to campaign for a Yes in the referendum. This was the first time in the party's history that it campaigned in favour of a European treaty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenparty1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenparty1-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The government's response to the <a href="/wiki/Post-2008_Irish_banking_crisis" title="Post-2008 Irish banking crisis">post-2008 banking crisis</a> significantly affected the party's support, and it suffered at the <a href="/wiki/Irish_local_elections,_2009" title="Irish local elections, 2009">2009 local elections</a>, returning with only three County Council seats in total and losing its entire traditional Dublin base with the exception of a Town Council Seat in Balbriggan.
</p><p>In 2010, <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9irdre_de_B%C3%BArca" title="Déirdre de Búrca">Déirdre de Búrca</a>, one of two Green Senators <a href="/wiki/Nominated_members_of_Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Nominated members of Seanad Éireann">nominated by Taoiseach</a> Bertie Ahern in 2007, resigned from the party and her seat, in part owing to the party's inability to secure her a job in the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> On 23 February 2010, Trevor Sargent resigned as Minister of State for Food and Horticulture owing to allegations over contacting <a href="/wiki/Garda_S%C3%ADoch%C3%A1na" title="Garda Síochána">Gardaí</a> about a criminal case involving a constituent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> On 23 March 2010, Ciarán Cuffe was appointed as Minister for Horticulture, Sustainable Travel, Planning and Heritage while the party gained a junior ministerial position with Mary White appointed as Minister for Equality, Human Rights and Integration.
</p><p>The party supported the passage legislation for <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">EC</a>–<a href="/wiki/European_Central_Bank" title="European Central Bank">ECB</a>–<a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">IMF</a> financial support for the Ireland's bank bailout. On 19 January, the Greens derailed Taoiseach <a href="/wiki/Brian_Cowen" title="Brian Cowen">Brian Cowen</a>'s plans to reshuffle his cabinet when it refused to endorse Cowen's intended replacement ministers, forcing Cowen to redistribute the vacant portfolios among incumbent ministers. The Greens were angered at not having been consulted about this effort, and went as far as to threaten to pull out of the coalition unless Cohen set a firm date for an election due that spring. Cowen ultimately set the date for 11 March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-worstweek_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-worstweek-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>
On 23 January 2011, the Green Party met with Cowen following his resignation as leader of senior coalition partner Fianna Fáil the previous afternoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The Green Party then announced it was breaking off the coalition and going into opposition with immediate effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-withdrawal_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-withdrawal-26">[26]</a></sup> Green Party leader <a href="/wiki/John_Gormley" title="John Gormley">John Gormley</a> said at a press conference announcing the withdrawal: </p><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>For a very long time we in the Green Party have stood back in the hope that Fianna Fáil could resolve persistent doubts about their party leadership. A definitive resolution of this has not yet been possible. And our patience has reached an end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote><p> The government ministerial posts of Gormley and Ryan were reassigned to Fianna Fáil ministers <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_%C3%93_Cu%C3%ADv" title="Éamon Ó Cuív">Éamon Ó Cuív</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pat_Carey" title="Pat Carey">Pat Carey</a> respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_Cuiv_and_Carey_get_vacant_ministerial_posts_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-O_Cuiv_and_Carey_get_vacant_ministerial_posts-29">[29]</a></sup> Green <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_State_(Ireland)" title="Minister of State (Ireland)">Ministers of State</a> Ciarán Cuffe and Mary White also resigned from their roles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O_Cuiv_and_Carey_get_vacant_ministerial_posts_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-O_Cuiv_and_Carey_get_vacant_ministerial_posts-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>In almost four years in Government, from 2007 to 2011, the Green Party contributed to the passage of <a href="/wiki/Civil_Partnership_and_Certain_Rights_and_Obligations_of_Cohabitants_Act_2010" title="Civil Partnership and Certain Rights and Obligations of Cohabitants Act 2010">civil partnership for same-sex couples</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> the introduction of major planning reform,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> a major increase in renewable energy output,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> progressive budgets,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and a nationwide scheme of home insulation retrofitting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wipeout_and_recovery">Wipeout and recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Ireland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wipeout and recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The party suffered a wipeout at the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_2011" title="Irish general election, 2011">2011 general election</a>, with all of its six TDs losing their seats, including those of former Ministers John Gormley and Eamon Ryan. Three of their six incumbent TDs lost their deposits. The party's share of the vote fell below 2%, meaning that they could not reclaim election expenses, and their lack of parliamentary representation led to the ending of state funding for the party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p><p>The party candidates in the <a href="/wiki/Members_of_the_24th_Seanad" title="Members of the 24th Seanad">2011 election to the Seanad</a> were Dan Boyle and Niall Ó Brolcháin; neither was elected, and as a result, for the first time since 1989 the Green Party had no representatives in the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a>.
</p><p>Eamon Ryan was elected as party leader on 27 May 2011, succeeding John Gormley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leader_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-leader-36">[36]</a></sup> Catherine Martin, a former <a href="/wiki/Carrickmacross" title="Carrickmacross">Carrickmacross</a> town councillor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> was later appointed deputy leader, while Ciarán Cuffe and <a href="/wiki/Mark_Dearey" title="Mark Dearey">Mark Dearey</a> were also placed on the party's front bench.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2014_(Ireland)" title="European Parliament election, 2014 (Ireland)">2014 European election</a> the party received 4.9% of the vote nationally (an increase of 3% on the 2009 result), failing to return a candidate to the European Parliament. In the <a href="/wiki/Irish_local_elections,_2014" title="Irish local elections, 2014">2014 local elections</a> the party received 1.6% of the vote nationally. 12 candidates were elected to County Councils, an increase of 9.
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_2016" title="Irish general election, 2016">2016 general election</a> the Green Party gained two seats, becoming the first Irish political party to lose all seats at an election and win seats at the subsequent election. In the election to <a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Seanad Éireann">Seanad Éireann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grace_O%27Sullivan" title="Grace O'Sullivan">Grace O'Sullivan</a> became the first elected Green Party Senator, winning a seat of the <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_Panel" title="Agricultural Panel">Agricultural Panel</a>. She established the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Engagement_group" title="Civil Engagement group">Civil Engagement group</a> with five Independent Senators. On 30 May 2016, the Green Party joined the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democrats_(Ireland)" title="Social Democrats (Ireland)">Social Democrats</a> to form a <a href="/wiki/Technical_group" title="Technical group">technical group</a> in the Dáil.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideology_and_policies">Ideology and policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Ireland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ideology and policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While strongly associated with <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalist</a> policies, the party also has policies covering all other key areas. These include: protection of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish language</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> lowering the <a href="/wiki/Voting_age" title="Voting age">voting age in Ireland to 16</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> a directly elected <a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Seanad Éireann">Seanad</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> support for <a href="/wiki/Universal_health_care" title="Universal health care">universal healthcare</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> and a constitutional amendment which guarantees that the water of Ireland will never be privatised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>The party also supports terminally ill people to have the right to legally choose assisted dying, on which subject it believes, "provisions should apply only to those with a terminal illness which is likely to result in death within six months". It also states that "such a right would only apply where the person has a clear and settled intention to end their own life which is proved by making, and signing, a written declaration to that effect. Such a declaration must be countersigned by two qualified doctors".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation">Organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Ireland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The National Executive Committee is the organising committee of the party. It comprises the party leader Eamon Ryan, deputy leader Catherine Martin, Chair Roderic O'Gorman, Young Greens representative, Treasurer and ten members elected annually at the party convention.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership">Leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Ireland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The party did not have a national leader until 2001.  At a special "Leadership Convention" in Kilkenny on 6 October 2001, <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Sargent" title="Trevor Sargent">Trevor Sargent</a> was elected the first official leader of the Green Party. He was re-elected to this position in 2003 and again in 2005. The party's constitution requires that a leadership election be held within six months of a general election.
</p><p>Sargent resigned the leadership in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_general_election,_2007" title="Irish general election, 2007">2007 general election</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/30th_D%C3%A1il" title="30th Dáil">30th Dáil</a>. During the campaign, Sargent had promised that he would not lead the party into Government with <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna Fáil">Fianna Fáil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> In the election outcome the party retained 6 Dáil seats, making them the most likely partner for Fianna Fáil. Sargent and the party negotiated a coalition government and at the 12 June 2007 membership meeting to approve the agreement, he announced his resignation as leader.
</p><p>In the subsequent leadership election, <a href="/wiki/John_Gormley" title="John Gormley">John Gormley</a> became the new leader on 17 July 2007, defeating <a href="/wiki/Patricia_McKenna" title="Patricia McKenna">Patricia McKenna</a> by 478 votes to 263. <a href="/wiki/Mary_White_(Green_Party_politician)" title="Mary White (Green Party politician)">Mary White</a> was subsequently elected as the deputy Leader. <a href="/wiki/John_Gormley" title="John Gormley">John Gormley</a> served as Minister for the Environment, Heritage and Local Government from July 2007 until the Green Party's decision to exit Government in December 2010.
</p><p>Following the election defeat of 2011, John Gormley announced his intention not to seek another term as Green Party leader. <a href="/wiki/Eamon_Ryan" title="Eamon Ryan">Eamon Ryan</a> was elected as the new party leader, over party colleagues Phil Kearney and Cllr Malcolm Noonan in a postal ballot election of party members in May 2011. Monaghan based former councillor Catherine Martin defeated Down based <a href="/wiki/John_Barry_(politician)" title="John Barry (politician)">Dr John Barry</a> and former Senator <a href="/wiki/Mark_Dearey" title="Mark Dearey">Mark Dearey</a> to the post of Deputy Leader on 11 June 2011 during the party's annual convention. <a href="/wiki/Roderic_O%27Gorman" title="Roderic O'Gorman">Roderic O'Gorman</a> was elected party chairperson.
</p><p>The Green Party had six seats in the Irish parliament but lost them all in the 2011 general election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> Party Chairman <a href="/wiki/Dan_Boyle_(politician)" title="Dan Boyle (politician)">Dan Boyle</a> and <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9irdre_de_B%C3%BArca" title="Déirdre de Búrca">Déirdre de Búrca</a> were <a href="/wiki/Nominated_members_of_Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Nominated members of Seanad Éireann">nominated by the Taoiseach</a> to <a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Seanad Éireann">Seanad Éireann</a> after the formation of the Fianna Fáil–<a href="/wiki/Progressive_Democrats" title="Progressive Democrats">PD</a>–Green Party government in 2007 and <a href="/wiki/Niall_%C3%93_Brolch%C3%A1in" title="Niall Ó Brolcháin">Niall Ó Brolcháin</a> elected in December 2009. De Búrca resigned in February 2010, and was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Dearey" title="Mark Dearey">Mark Dearey</a>. Neither Dan Boyle or Niall O'Brolchain were re-elected to Seanad Éireann in the Seanad election of 2011, leaving the Green Party without Oireachtas representation until the 2016, where they regained 2 Dáil seats.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish_and_European_politics">Irish and European politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Ireland)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Irish and European politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Green Party is organised throughout the island of Ireland, with regional structures in both the Republic of Ireland and Northern Ireland. The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Green Party in Northern Ireland">Green Party in Northern Ireland</a> voted to become a regional partner of the Green Party in Ireland in 2005 at its annual convention, and again in a postal ballot in March 2006.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>) <a href="/wiki/Brian_Wilson_(Northern_Ireland_politician)" title="Brian Wilson (Northern Ireland politician)">Brian Wilson</a>, formerly a councillor for the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Party_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Alliance Party of Northern Ireland">Alliance Party</a>, won the Green Party's first seat in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election,_2007" title="Northern Ireland Assembly election, 2007">2007 election</a>. <a href="/wiki/Steven_Agnew" title="Steven Agnew">Steven Agnew</a> held that seat in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly_election,_2011" title="Northern Ireland Assembly election, 2011">2011 election</a>.
</p>
