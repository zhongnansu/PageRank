<p><b>Green liberalism</b>, or <b>liberal environmentalism</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-one-1">[1]</a></sup> is <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberalism</a> that includes <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green politics</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Ideology" title="Ideology">ideology</a>. Green liberals are usually <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">liberal</a> on social issues and "green" on economic issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-one-1">[1]</a></sup> The term "green liberalism" was coined by political philosopher <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Wissenburg" title="Marcel Wissenburg">Marcel Wissenburg</a> in his 1998 book <i>Green Liberalism: The Free and The Green Society</i>. He argues that liberalism must reject the idea of absolute property rights and accept restraints that limit the freedom to abuse nature and natural resources. However, he rejects the control of population growth and any control over the distribution of resources as incompatible with individual liberty, instead favoring supply-side control: more efficient production and curbs on overproduction and over-exploitation. This view tends to dominate the movement, although critics say it actually puts individual liberties above sustainability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">Philosophy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_liberalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Philosophy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Green liberalism values the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> very highly, emphasizing the importance of the planet being passed down to the next generation unharmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  Green liberalism accepts that the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural world</a> is in a state of <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">flux</a> and does not seek to conserve the natural world as it is. However, it does seek to minimize the damage done by the <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> species on the natural world and to aid the regeneration of damaged areas. Green liberalism seeks to combine liberal democratic institutions and tenets such as equality and freedom of the individual with environmental protections that seek to reduce major threats to the environment like <a href="/wiki/Overconsumption" title="Overconsumption">overconsumption</a> and air pollution.
</p><p>On economic issues, green liberals take a position somewhere between <a href="/wiki/Classical_liberalism" title="Classical liberalism">classical liberalism</a> (on the center/center-right) and <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">social liberalism</a> (on the center/center-left): green liberals may favor slightly less government involvement than social liberals, but far more than classical liberals. Some green liberals practice <a href="/wiki/Free-market_environmentalism" title="Free-market environmentalism">free-market environmentalism</a> and thus share some values with rightist classical liberalism or <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarianism</a>. This is one of a few reasons why a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue-Green_Alliance" title="Blue-Green Alliance">blue-green alliance</a> is possible in politics.
</p><p>The historian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conrad_Russell" title="Conrad Russell">Conrad Russell</a>, a <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrat</a> member of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, dedicated a chapter of his book <i>The Intelligent Person's Guide to Liberalism</i> to the subject of green liberalism. In a literary sense, the term "Green Liberalism" was coined, however, by political philosopher Marcel Wissenburg in his 1998 book <i>Green Liberalism: The Free and The Green Society</i>., among others.
</p><p><b>Green Liberal Democrats</b>
</p><p>The existence of a Green liberal group predates Wissenburg`s book by at least ten years in the UK when a pressure group was formed within the newly merged Liberal Democrats at a meeting in Nottingham addressed (as the keynote speaker) by Simon Hughes MP <a href="/wiki/Simon_Hughes" title="Simon Hughes">Simon Hughes</a>. The Green Liberal Democrats emerged from this inaugural meeting which had been organised by the Chair of the pre-existing Liberal Ecology Group (LEG) which had itself been set up eleven years previously in 1977. Keith Melton, one of the earliest members of LEG (its long title was the Liberal Ecology Group for Economic and Social Reform) was, at the time of the merger between the Liberal Party and the SDP in 1988, a senior lecturer in International Marketing at Nottingham Trent University, so it made sense to call that meeting in Nottingham. Most of the delegates at that meeting were LEG members, although there was a modest contingent from the SDP "Green Group" 
</p><p>The Liberal Ecology Group had been campaigning within the Liberal Party for years, pushing for a different, zero growth strategy for economics, following the philosophy elucidated in the Club of Rome`s report "Limits to Growth". They also campaigned on air pollution issues, calling for the banning of lead in petrol for example and the banning of HFCs which were known to damage the ozone layer.
</p><p>The Green Liberal Democrats has been a very active pressure group within the Liberal Democrats over the years and in 2018 celebrated 30 years of existence with a conference, also held in Nottingham, also organised by Keith Melton and also with (now <b>Sir</b>) Simon Hughes as the initial speaker. The significance of the group and its influence on the party was marked by other key speakers at the 2018 conference, including one quarter of the current Liberal Democrats` parliamentary representatives in the House of Commons. <a href="/wiki/Ed_Davey" title="Ed Davey">Sir Ed Davey MP</a> related the environmental impact of the Liberal Democrats in the coalition years, establishing a Green Investment Bank (subsequently sold off by the Tory government)
</p><p>Wera Hobhouse <a href="/wiki/Wera_Hobhouse" title="Wera Hobhouse">Wera Hobhouse MP</a> updated the Green approach to Air pollution and Vince Cable MP <a href="/wiki/Vince_Cable" title="Vince Cable">Sir Vince Cable MP</a>, leader of the Liberal Democrats, and Honorary Professor of Economics at Nottingham University, the venue for the 2018 GLD conference, had the task of reviewing how the concept of Sustainable Development withstood the ravages of time. Cable was one of the co-authors of the Brundtland Commission report in 1987 which first introduced the sustainable development concept, championed by Gro Harlem Brundtland, the Commission`s Chair (and three-time Prime Minister of Norway).
</p><p>One of the key early successes of the Green Liberal Democrats, and its new Chair, Keith Melton, was to ensure that the preamble to the Liberal Democrat constitution had a key reference to Green issues at the heart of the party with the following sentence appearing immediately after the first paragraph defining Liberal Philosophy - "<b>We believe that each generation is responsible for the fate of our planet and, by safeguarding the balance of nature and the environment, for the long term continuity of life in all its forms.</b>"
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Canada" title="Liberal Party of Canada">Liberal Party of Canada</a> under <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A9phane_Dion" title="Stéphane Dion">Stéphane Dion</a> placed the environment at the front of its political agenda, proposing an <a href="/wiki/Ecotax" title="Ecotax">ecotax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tax_shift" title="Tax shift">tax shift</a> called the Green Shift. Similarly, the British Liberal Democrats have drawn on the same concept to propose a "Green Tax Switch".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_neoliberalism">Green neoliberalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_liberalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Green neoliberalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_liberalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One kind of green liberalism is called <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green</a> <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberalism</a>, which became significant and increasingly prominent in world institutions beginning in the 1980s. In this decade, the two main institutions of global development, the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a> and the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>, began to face increasing global outrage as a result of their <a href="/wiki/Structural_adjustment" title="Structural adjustment">structural adjustment plans</a>, which were loans with severe conditions on debt-ridden countries. The conditions focused on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austerity_measure" title="Austerity measure">austerity measures</a>, e.g. reducing government control of the market and provision of social services and liberalizing trade, thus enabling corporations from the Global North to enter into developing countries and out-compete local markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This caused social unrest to increase on many fronts: farmers were losing their livelihoods to large corporations importing artificially cheap, subsidized stable crops from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_North" title="Global North">Global North</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialized_agriculture" title="Industrialized agriculture">Industrialized agriculture</a> and agronomy became the status quo in both research institutions and practice, bringing with it many environmental and social costs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>. Companies could move their operations to countries where labor was cheaper much more easily, meaning some people lost their jobs while others accepted very low wages. People, social movements and non-governmental organizations began to openly criticize and blame the World Bank, International Monetary Fund and <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a> for being the root cause of the food crises, job losses and environmental degradation. This was picked up by media in the Global North as well, adding to the shift of the view of the <a href="/wiki/Global_South" title="Global South">Global South</a> as "happy recipients of Bank Aid"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> to very real and vivid images of bread riots, <a href="/wiki/1999_Seattle_WTO_protests" title="1999 Seattle WTO protests">protests</a>, mass marches and fasts. The pressure was heightened by the increasingly prominent environmental movement, which brought attention to the way that business as usual economics did not account for environmental costs, the largest of which, which is in turn connected to many other problems, is human-caused <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.
</p><p>In response to this criticism, the World Bank first responded with denial. When this did not prove effective, it completely turned around and decided to make significant changes in its organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Led by actors within the Bank who were intent on reforming it in response to global criticism, the Bank now made the environment one of its primary focuses. Whereas in 1985 the World Bank had only five staff working on environmental issues, with a budget of less than $15 million, by 1995 it had more than three hundred environment-related staff, with almost one billion dollars to work with.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup> An entirely new Environment Department was created, which had to approve the environmental <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> of large-scale projects before they were implemented.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_and_context">Characteristics and context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_liberalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Characteristics and context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thus began a new era of green neoliberalism, in which the World Bank and its fellow institutions did not let go of their neoliberal ideology, i.e. their commitment to free-market economics, low regulation, free trade, and so on, but at the same time adopted the mainstream rhetoric of sustainability and environmental consciousness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-9">[9]</a></sup> This kind of green liberalism is mainly economic and it is supported by a range of people, both socially liberal and socially conservative. It is related to, if not synonymous, with <a href="/wiki/Eco-capitalism" title="Eco-capitalism">eco-capitalism</a>.
</p><p>In the larger context of the history of development, this transition follows a trajectory that began with <a href="/wiki/Modernization_theory" title="Modernization theory">modernization theory</a> and a project to modernize developing countries, followed by the globalization project, where free-market and free-trade was meant to help countries develop, which was then succeeded by the sustainability project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The green neoliberal view of sustainability is one of <a href="/wiki/Weak_and_strong_sustainability" title="Weak and strong sustainability">weak sustainability</a>, which contrasts with many ecologists' view of <a href="/wiki/Weak_and_strong_sustainability" title="Weak and strong sustainability">strong sustainability</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
