<p><b>Keurig Dr Pepper</b>, formerly <b>Keurig Green Mountain</b> (2014–2018) and originally <b>Green Mountain Coffee Roasters</b> (1981–2014), is a publicly traded American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beverage" title="Beverage">beverage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coffeemaker" title="Coffeemaker">beverage-maker</a> conglomerate with dual headquarters in <a href="/wiki/Burlington,_Massachusetts" title="Burlington, Massachusetts">Burlington, Massachusetts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plano,_Texas" title="Plano, Texas">Plano, Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Its east-coast division sells coffee and other beverages, and <a href="/wiki/Keurig" title="Keurig">Keurig</a> brewers. As of July 2018, the newly merged conglomerate also sells sodas, juices, and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_drinks" title="Soft drinks">soft drinks</a> via its <a href="/wiki/Dr_Pepper_Snapple_Group" title="Dr Pepper Snapple Group">Dr Pepper Snapple</a> division based in Texas.
</p><p>In its east-coast division, originally founded in 1981, the company sources, produces, and sells coffee, hot cocoa, teas, and other beverages under various brands in portion packs for its <a href="/wiki/Keurig" title="Keurig">Keurig</a> brewing systems; and sells coffee beans and ground coffee in bags and fractional packs. It sells many of these beverage varieties in Keurig <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K-Cup" title="K-Cup">K-Cup</a> single-serve pods, and as of 2017 offers Vue, K-Carafe, and K-Mug pods as well. Through its own and its partnership licensed brands, the east-coast division offers over 400 different varieties of coffee and other beverage selections. These include coffees that are <a href="/wiki/Organic_certification" title="Organic certification">certified organic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fairtrade_certification" title="Fairtrade certification">Fair Trade Certified</a>, specialty blends, and flavored coffees and beverages.
</p><p>Begun as a small <a href="/wiki/Specialty_coffee" title="Specialty coffee">specialty coffee</a> roaster and store in Vermont in 1981, after regional and national expansion in the late 1980s and an IPO in 1993, Green Mountain Coffee Roasters completed its acquisition of the brewing-machine manufacturer Keurig, Inc. in 2006, enabling rapid growth through the high-margin sales of its many varieties of single-serve K-Cup pods for home and office use. GMCR changed its name to Keurig Green Mountain in March 2014. Its Canadian business unit subsidiary operates as Keurig Canada Inc.
</p><p>A publicly traded company from 1993 through 2015, Keurig Green Mountain was acquired by a group of investors led by <a href="/wiki/JAB_Holding_Company" title="JAB Holding Company">JAB Holding Company</a> in March 2016 for $13.9 billion in cash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egan_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-egan-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vbcompletes_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-vbcompletes-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bwcompletes_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bwcompletes-9">[9]</a></sup> Keurig Green Mountain became a privately held company, and was an independent entity run by its pre-existing management team and a new CEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-udland_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-udland-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaplan_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-kaplan-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamgort_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-gamgort-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 9, 2018, Keurig Green Mountain acquired the <a href="/wiki/Dr_Pepper_Snapple_Group" title="Dr Pepper Snapple Group">Dr Pepper Snapple Group</a> in an $18.7 billion deal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The combined company was renamed Keurig Dr Pepper, and began trading publicly again on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a> under the ticker "KDP". Shareholders of Dr Pepper Snapple Group own 13% of the combined company, with Keurig shareholder <a href="/wiki/Mondelez_International" title="Mondelez International">Mondelez International</a> owning 13% to 14% of that fraction. JAB Holdings owns the remaining, majority, stake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings">Beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Green Mountain Coffee Roasters had its inception when entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Bob_Stiller" title="Bob Stiller">Bob Stiller</a> discovered a cup of coffee so good near a Vermont ski resort that he sought out the source of the coffee. In 1981 he and a partner bought a two-thirds stake in the small specialty coffee roasting company in <a href="/wiki/Waitsfield,_Vermont" title="Waitsfield, Vermont">Waitsfield, Vermont</a> that produced the roasted beans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-java_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-java-15">[15]</a></sup> The store and cafe sold beans, grounds, and coffee to the public and a few restaurants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDCH2000_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-IDCH2000-16">[16]</a></sup> At a time when premium, specialty, or gourmet coffee was still rare, Stiller dedicated himself to coffee-roasting, using exclusively <a href="/wiki/Coffea_arabica" title="Coffea arabica">arabica</a> coffee beans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carpenter_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-carpenter-17">[17]</a></sup> By 1982 the company had around 30 employees, and moved its production facilities to <a href="/wiki/Waterbury,_Vermont" title="Waterbury, Vermont">Waterbury, Vermont</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brew_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-brew-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Stiller bought out his two partners for $100,000 and became sole proprietor of the company within two years of his original purchase,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-java_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-java-15">[15]</a></sup> but it took four years to turn a profit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-java_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-java-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDCH2000_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-IDCH2000-16">[16]</a></sup> To grow the business Stiller sold the coffee to high-end restaurants and gas stations alike, and since he couldn't afford advertising, he gave out free samples. In 1986, he launched a mail-order business which he advertised in gourmet magazines, and acquired his first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supermarket_chain" title="Supermarket chain">supermarket-chain</a> customer, <a href="/wiki/Kings_Food_Markets" title="Kings Food Markets">Kings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brew_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-brew-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Stiller embraced technology very early on – to track customers' orders; to regulate roasting-heat levels appropriate to each bag; and to track distribution, manufacturing, sales, and personnel (adopting <a href="/wiki/PeopleSoft" title="PeopleSoft">PeopleSoft</a> in 1997).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-java_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-java-15">[15]</a></sup> The company also began to embrace an <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socially_responsible" title="Socially responsible">socially responsible</a> ethos very quickly; by 1983 employees composted <a href="/wiki/Used_coffee_grounds" title="Used coffee grounds">used coffee grounds</a> at its retail stores, and by 1986 Green Mountain introduced its first <a href="/wiki/Organic_food" title="Organic food">organic</a> coffee in a retail market test.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brew_18-2"><a href="#cite_note-brew-18">[18]</a></sup> Environmentalism, sustainability, <a href="/wiki/Corporate_social_responsibility" title="Corporate social responsibility">corporate social responsibility</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade" title="Fair trade">fair trade</a> soon were overarching policies Green Mountain became well known for. It is one of the largest suppliers of double-certified fair-trade and organic coffee in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As American tastes in coffee changed, sales of Green Mountain roasted coffee beans grew.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carpenter_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-carpenter-17">[17]</a></sup> In 1991 GMCR had seven retail outlets, 1,000 wholesale clients, $11 million in sales, and  $200,000 in profits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDCH2000_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-IDCH2000-16">[16]</a></sup> By 1993, the company had 2,400 wholesale accounts and sales of about $10 million,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDCH2000_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-IDCH2000-16">[16]</a></sup> and Green Mountain Coffee Roasters, Inc. started trading publicly, under the ticker "GMCR". The company expanded its retail locations, food-service distribution, mail-order business, and  wholesale business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDCH2000_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-IDCH2000-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1994, Green Mountain began exporting to <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDCH2000_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-IDCH2000-16">[16]</a></sup> In the late 1990s it broadened its national supermarket chain distribution, gas-station and convenience-store distribution throughout the northeast, and it sold its product in airlines and <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a>, specialty coffee stores, and venues such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LL_Bean" title="LL Bean">LL Bean</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weight_Watchers" title="Weight Watchers">Weight Watchers International</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Staples_Inc." title="Staples Inc.">Staples</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDCH2000_16-6"><a href="#cite_note-IDCH2000-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brew_18-3"><a href="#cite_note-brew-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Keurig_and_the_launch_of_K-Cups_with_Green_Mountain_Coffee">Keurig and the launch of K-Cups with Green Mountain Coffee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Keurig and the launch of K-Cups with Green Mountain Coffee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1993, three engineering entrepreneurs from a Massachusetts start-up called <a href="/wiki/Keurig" title="Keurig">Keurig</a> approached GMCR about developing a single-cup coffee brewing system, and Green Mountain first invested in the nascent company at that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brew_18-4"><a href="#cite_note-brew-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-booming_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-booming-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1996 GMCR invested further in Keurig, buying a 35% interest in the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-java_15-4"><a href="#cite_note-java-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-completed_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-completed-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1997, Green Mountain Coffee Roasters became the first roaster to offer coffee in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K-Cup" title="K-Cup">K-Cup</a> pod for the Keurig Single-Cup Brewing System,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brew_18-5"><a href="#cite_note-brew-18">[18]</a></sup> and in 1998 Keurig delivered its first brewing system, designed for office use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canada_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-canada-23">[23]</a></sup> The launch of the first K-Cups with Green Mountain Coffee helped GMCR begin to further compete with the ubiquitous presence of <a href="/wiki/Starbucks" title="Starbucks">Starbucks</a>, by allowing people to brew their own single servings of premium coffee.
</p><p>Also in 1997, a deal with <a href="/wiki/Poland_Spring" title="Poland Spring">Poland Spring</a> opened the office-worker market by distributing Green Mountain Coffee to thousands of offices in the Northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDCH2000_16-7"><a href="#cite_note-IDCH2000-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1998 GMCR closed its 12 retail shops in favor of the burgeoning direct-mail and online market, its growing distributions to business offices and other national venues, and its wholesale market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-java_15-5"><a href="#cite_note-java-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDCH2000_16-8"><a href="#cite_note-IDCH2000-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pendergrast2004_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-pendergrast2004-24">[24]</a></sup> That year it signed an exclusive deal with <a href="/wiki/American_Skiing_Company" title="American Skiing Company">American Skiing Company</a>, offered its first corporate gifts catalogue, sold its certified organic coffee in ExxonMobil's national and international <a href="/wiki/On_the_Run_(convenience_store)" title="On the Run (convenience store)">On the Run</a> convenience stores, and expanded its supermarket distribution to 500 stores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDCH2000_16-9"><a href="#cite_note-IDCH2000-16">[16]</a></sup> In 1999 it expanded its export market, including to Great Britain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IDCH2000_16-10"><a href="#cite_note-IDCH2000-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2000, Green Mountain reached an agreement to promote and sell <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade_coffee" title="Fair trade coffee">fair trade coffee</a>, committing to making  at least 3% of its sales <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade" title="Fair trade">fair trade</a>–certified by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TransFair_USA" title="TransFair USA">TransFair USA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pendergrast2004_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-pendergrast2004-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In 2001, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/Frontier_Natural_Products_Co-op" title="Frontier Natural Products Co-op">Frontier Organic Coffee</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and in 2002, it signed an agreement to sell fair trade coffee under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newman%27s_Own_Organics" title="Newman's Own Organics">Newman's Own Organics</a> label.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> In late 2005 GMCR reached a deal to sell its Newman's Own Organics Blend coffee in more than 600 <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald's">McDonald's</a> restaurants in New England and upstate New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Acquisition_of_Keurig.2C_Inc."></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_Keurig,_Inc.">Acquisition of Keurig, Inc.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Acquisition of Keurig, Inc.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2006, Green Mountain – which had successively invested in and acquired increasing percentage ownership of Keurig, Inc. in 1993, 1996, and 2003, by which time it had a 43% ownership – completed its full acquisition of the single-cup brewing systems manufacturer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-completed_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-completed-22">[22]</a></sup> The successive acquisition allowed Green Mountain to adopt a multi-brand portfolio, and multichannel distribution of brands in a variety of
settings, with numerous opportunities for consumers to try individual brands by the cup.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fred_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-fred-29">[29]</a></sup> It also fueled enormous revenue growth, and allowed GMCR to transition fully from deriving 95% of its revenue from its low-margin wholesale coffee business in the late 1990s (approximately $65 million), to deriving 95% of its revenue from high-margin sales of K-Cups as of 2014 (more than $4.3 billion).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Green Mountain also acquired the four additional Keurig licensees, <a href="/wiki/Tully%27s_Coffee" title="Tully's Coffee">Tully's Coffee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timothy%27s_World_Coffee" title="Timothy's World Coffee">Timothy's World Coffee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diedrich_Coffee" title="Diedrich Coffee">Diedrich Coffee</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Van_Houtte" title="Van Houtte">Van Houtte</a>, in 2009 and 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In March 2009 it purchased Seattle-based Tully's  brand and its wholesale coffee business for $40.3 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fred_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-fred-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> In November 2009, Green Mountain acquired the wholesale division of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> coffee provider Timothy's for $157 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> It purchased the California-based Diedrich Coffee in December 2009 for $290 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In 2010, Green Mountain Coffee bought the Canadian distributor, Quebec-based coffee services company Van Houtte, for $915 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> GMCR Canada was founded in 2010, and officially became the Canadian Business Unit of Green Mountain Coffee Roasters, Inc. in 2011; it has been known as Keurig Canada Inc. since March 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canada_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-canada-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 28, 2010, the company's stock rose to what was then an all-time high, but the company disclosed after the markets closed that the Securities and Exchange Commission had requested documents and data related to an inquiry into how it accounted for revenue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ap20100928_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-ap20100928-38">[38]</a></sup>  The company announced that U.S. regulators had inquired into some of Green Mountain's accounting practices, including <a href="/wiki/Revenue_recognition" title="Revenue recognition">revenue recognition</a>, and the large inventory with a single vendor, M. Block and Sons, Inc. Most analysts felt that the company practices were sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> The SEC ended the probe in October 2014, and brought no enforcement action against Green Mountain or its employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2011 Green Mountain announced an agreement with <a href="/wiki/Dunkin%27_Donuts" title="Dunkin' Donuts">Dunkin' Donuts</a> to make Dunkin’ Donuts coffee available in single-serve K-Cup pods for use with Keurig Single-Cup Brewers. In addition, participating Dunkin’ Donuts restaurants on occasion offer Keurig Single-Cup Brewers for sale.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gmcrpr2011_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-gmcrpr2011-42">[42]</a></sup> In March 2011, Green Mountain Coffee and <a href="/wiki/Starbucks" title="Starbucks">Starbucks</a> announced a similar deal whereby Starbucks would sell its coffee and tea in Keurig single-serve pods, and would in return sell Keurig machines in their stores as part of the deal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="New_brewers.2C_and_company_name_change"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="New_brewers,_and_company_name_change">New brewers, and company name change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: New brewers, and company name change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Green Mountain Coffee Roasters introduced the Keurig Vue brewer, paired with new Vue packs, in February 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VuePR_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-VuePR-44">[44]</a></sup> seven months before patents on the K-Cup expired in September 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> The Vue system was announced as having customizable features so consumers had control over the strength, size, and temperature of their beverages, and the Vue pack is made of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recyclable" title="Recyclable">recyclable</a> #5 plastic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VuePR_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-VuePR-44">[44]</a></sup> In November 2012, GMCR released its espresso, cappucino, and latte brewer, the Rivo, co-developed with the Italian coffee company <a href="/wiki/Lavazza" title="Lavazza">Lavazza</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In the fall of 2013, the company released a full-pot brewer, the Keurig Bolt, for use mainly in offices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2014, <a href="/wiki/The_Coca-Cola_Company" title="The Coca-Cola Company">The Coca-Cola Company</a> purchased a 10% stake in the company, valued at $1.25 billion, with an option to increase their stake to 16%, which was exercised in May 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K-Coke_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-K-Coke-48">[48]</a></sup> The partnership was part of Coca-Cola's support of a cold beverage system to be developed by Keurig that allows customers to make Coca-Cola and other brand soft drinks at home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-K-Coke_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-K-Coke-48">[48]</a></sup>  In January 2015, the company made a similar deal with <a href="/wiki/Dr_Pepper_Snapple_Group" title="Dr Pepper Snapple Group">Dr Pepper Snapple Group</a>, but without a stockholder stake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gasparro2015_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-gasparro2015-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>In early March 2014, Green Mountain Coffee Roasters shareholders voted to change its name to <b>Keurig Green Mountain</b> to reflect its business of selling Keurig coffee makers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Its stock-market symbol remained "GMCR".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the fall of 2014, Keurig Green Mountain introduced the Keurig 2.0 brewer, with technology to prevent old or unlicensed pods being used in the brewer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The digital lock-out sparked hacking attempts and anti-trust lawsuits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> The 2.0 brewer also has the capacity to brew full carafes in addition to single servings,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> via the use of the new K-Carafe portion pack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2015, it launched the K-Mug pod, a recyclable pod which brews large travel mug–sized portions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> In mid 2015 Keurig debuted the K200, a smaller Keurig 2.0 model that can brew single cups or four-cup carafes and comes in a variety of colors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> announced that its new Café French Door refrigerator, due out in late 2015, would have a Keurig coffee machine built into the door.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2015, Keurig launched a line of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Campbell%27s_Soup" title="Campbell's Soup">Campbell's Soup</a> available in K-Cups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowerman_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-bowerman-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hotsoup_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-hotsoup-68">[68]</a></sup> The kits come with a packet of noodles and a K-Cup pod of soup, and the varieties include Chicken Noodle and Southwest Style Chicken Noodle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowerman_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-bowerman-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hotsoup_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-hotsoup-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also in September 2015, Keurig launched Keurig Kold, a brewer which created a variety of cold beverages including soft drinks, functional beverages, and sparkling waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kold_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-kold-69">[69]</a></sup> The machine brewed beverages from The Coca-Cola Company and the Dr Pepper Snapple Group, in addition to Keurig's own line of flavored sparkling and non-sparkling waters and teas, sports drinks, and soda-fountain drinks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kold_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-kold-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> The company's primary competitor in this market area was <a href="/wiki/SodaStream" title="SodaStream">SodaStream</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gasparro2015_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-gasparro2015-49">[49]</a></sup> In June 2016 Keurig announced it was discontinuing the machine and offered refunds to purchasers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_by_JAB_and_other_investors">Acquisition by JAB and other investors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Acquisition by JAB and other investors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On December 7, 2015, an investor group led by <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private-equity</a> firm <a href="/wiki/JAB_Holding_Company" title="JAB Holding Company">JAB Holding Company</a> announced its intent to acquire Keurig Green Mountain for $13.9 billion. JAB is an investment firm dealing in high-end consumer goods; its holdings include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peet%27s_Coffee_%26_Tea" title="Peet's Coffee &amp; Tea">Peet's Coffee &amp; Tea</a>. The minority investors in the KGM purchase include shareholders in the global coffee and tea company <a href="/wiki/Jacobs_Douwe_Egberts" title="Jacobs Douwe Egberts">Jacobs Douwe Egberts</a>, which owns <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_House" title="Maxwell House">Maxwell House</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tassimo" title="Tassimo">Tassimo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lat-keurigacquired_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-lat-keurigacquired-72">[72]</a></sup> The agreement was unanimously approved by Keurig Green Mountain's board of directors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-udland_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-udland-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The selling price, at $92 per share, represented a 77.9% premium over the closing price of Keurig Green Mountain (stock ticker "GMCR") on December 5, 2015. The Coca-Cola Company, Keurig Green Mountain's largest shareholder at 17.4%, announced its support for the JAB takeover since the sale of its stock holding would provide Coca-Cola  with a substantial financial benefit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-egan_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-egan-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaplan_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-kaplan-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The acquisition closed in March 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vbcompletes_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-vbcompletes-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bwcompletes_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-bwcompletes-9">[9]</a></sup> Keurig Green Mountain became a privately held company, and remained an independent entity run by its existing management team, retaining its head office in Waterbury, Vermont.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-udland_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-udland-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaplan_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-kaplan-11">[11]</a></sup> In a statement, JAB's chairman Bart Becht said that "Keurig Green Mountain will operate as an independent entity .... The company’s management team ... will continue to run Keurig."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaplan_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-kaplan-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_Dr_Pepper_Snapple_Group_and_company_name_change">Acquisition of Dr Pepper Snapple Group and company name change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Acquisition of Dr Pepper Snapple Group and company name change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In July 2018, Keurig Green Mountain acquired <a href="/wiki/Dr_Pepper_Snapple_Group" title="Dr Pepper Snapple Group">Dr Pepper Snapple Group</a> in a deal worth $18.7 billion, and the merged company changed its name to <b>Keurig Dr Pepper</b>, creating the third largest beverage company in North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> On July 10th, shares in Keurig Dr Pepper (KDP) began trading on the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">New York Stock Exchange</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Dr Pepper Snapple Group continues to operate as a business unit under the Keurig Dr Pepper parent company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_affairs">Corporate affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Corporate affairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">Sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>GMCR began to embrace an environmental ethos within two years of its founding, and environmentalism and sustainability were overarching policies for which Green Mountain became well known. Among other initiatives within Green Mountain's first decade, in 1983 employees began composting used coffee grounds at its retail stores; in 1986 the company introduced its first organic coffee in a retail market test;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brew_18-6"><a href="#cite_note-brew-18">[18]</a></sup> in 1989 it formed an Employee Environmental Committee, and began a recycling program;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brew_18-7"><a href="#cite_note-brew-18">[18]</a></sup> and in 1990 it introduced Rain Forest Nut coffee to sponsor rainforest preservation, donating 10% of the product's profits to <a href="/wiki/Conservation_International" title="Conservation International">Conservation International</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rainforest_Alliance" title="Rainforest Alliance">Rainforest Alliance</a>, and it introduced the first earth-friendly, oxygen-whitened, dioxin-free coffee filters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brew_18-8"><a href="#cite_note-brew-18">[18]</a></sup> In 1992 it formed a Stewardship Program to promote sustainability and sound environmental practices;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brew_18-9"><a href="#cite_note-brew-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> in 1997 it pioneered the first biodegradable bag for bulk coffee purchases;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brew_18-10"><a href="#cite_note-brew-18">[18]</a></sup> and in 2006 it introduced the ecotainer, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/To-go" title="To-go">to-go</a> cup for hot beverages made entirely out of renewable materials.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-start_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-start-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, Green Mountain was the first coffee company to support the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>' <a href="/wiki/Global_Reporting_Initiative" title="Global Reporting Initiative">Global Reporting Initiative</a> mission to develop globally accepted sustainability reporting guidelines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cases_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-cases-83">[83]</a></sup> In 2008 GMCR's board of directors added a social and environmental responsibility committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cases_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-cases-83">[83]</a></sup> During that time period GMCR also established a vice president for environmental affairs reporting directly to the CEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cases_83-2"><a href="#cite_note-cases-83">[83]</a></sup> As of 2015, Keurig Green Mountain has a Chief Sustainability Officer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>The company offsets 100% of its direct greenhouse gases,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> and prioritizes waste reduction and responsible energy use, and sustainability over the life-cycle of its products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-start_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-start-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-addressing_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-addressing-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-creating_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-creating-87">[87]</a></sup> In addition to other awards and recognition for sustainable practices,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awards_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-awards-88">[88]</a></sup> GMCR was on <i>Sustainable Business</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s "World's Top 20 Sustainable Business Stocks" annually from 2002–2007,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> and as of 2015 it is on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EPA" title="EPA">EPA</a>'s National Top 100 of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_power" title="Green power">green power</a> users in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> In 2014, Keurig Green Mountain announced a multi-faceted effort to address the long-term challenges of the global <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_crisis" title="Water crisis">water crisis</a>, and instituted an initial commitment of $11 million to support nonprofits working to promote water security.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p><p>Environmental advocates and journalists have criticized the company for the billions of non-recyclable and non-biodegradable K-Cups consumers purchase and dispose of every year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> and for the dichotomy between the company's historic environmentally conscious image and the impact of K-Cups on the environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup> GMCR's vice president of sustainability stated in 2013 that "The system has a lot of pretty demanding technical requirements in terms of being able to withstand certain amount of temperature and to have a certain kind of rigidity, and provide the right kinds of moisture barriers and oxygen barriers and the like. So it isn't the simplest challenge."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> In 2015, the company's chief sustainability officer stated that every new K-Cup spin-off product introduced since 2006 – including the Vue, Bolt, and K-Carafe cups – is recyclable if disassembled into paper, plastic, and metal components.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> In its 2014 Sustainability Report, released in February 2015, Keurig Green Mountain re-affirmed that a priority for the company is ensuring that 100% of K-Cup pods are recyclable by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-addressing_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-addressing-86">[86]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-creating_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-creating-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_social_responsibility">Corporate social responsibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Corporate social responsibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From its inception, and in tandem with its environmentally conscious outlook, Green Mountain has had a culture grounded in social responsibility, community mindedness, philanthropy, and respect for employees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-branded_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-branded-106">[106]</a></sup> Among other recognition and awards for its corporate social responsibility (CSR),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awards_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-awards-88">[88]</a></sup> GMCR was in the top ten of the "100 Best Corporate Citizens" each year from 2003–2007, and was ranked #1 in 2006 and 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> GMCR was not under consideration for the ranking in 2008, because the focus switched to exclusively <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Large-cap" title="Large-cap">large-cap</a> companies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup> It re-entered the "100 Best Corporate Citizens" list in 2010<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-110"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> and 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> In 2005, Green Mountain released its first Corporate Social Responsibility Report.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csrr2007_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-csrr2007-112">[112]</a></sup> In 2008 GMCR's board of directors added a social and environmental responsibility committee overseeing the company’s social responsibilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cases_83-3"><a href="#cite_note-cases-83">[83]</a></sup> It also established a vice president for corporate social responsibility who reports directly to the CEO.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cases_83-4"><a href="#cite_note-cases-83">[83]</a></sup> In addition to its environmental oversight, the company's Sustainability Committee focuses on areas including the financial and environmental viability, health, and resiliency of its coffee-growing and manufacturing supply chains; community outreach; and corporate and employee social responsibility and awareness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cases_83-5"><a href="#cite_note-cases-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup>
</p><p>GMCR was a pioneer in the Fair Trade movement in 2000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_98-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-congress7610_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-congress7610-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ground_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-ground-117">[117]</a></sup> guaranteeing farmers a steady minimum price far above market value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-congress7610_116-1"><a href="#cite_note-congress7610-116">[116]</a></sup> Since 2010 Green Mountain has been the largest purchaser of Fair Trade coffee in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_98-2"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-98">[98]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-congress7610_116-2"><a href="#cite_note-congress7610-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> In addition, the company was from its beginnings known for its long-term relationships and fair dealings with coffee-growing suppliers,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-branded_106-1"><a href="#cite_note-branded-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ground_117-1"><a href="#cite_note-ground-117">[117]</a></sup> and for its large percentage of farmer-direct coffee purchases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-congress7610_116-3"><a href="#cite_note-congress7610-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> Since the early 1990s, the company has annually sent groups of employees on trips to coffee farms in Latin America, to gain first-hand experience in the lives of coffee growers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-branded_106-2"><a href="#cite_note-branded-106">[106]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-congress7610_116-4"><a href="#cite_note-congress7610-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> GMCR has prioritized initiatives to alleviate poverty and hunger in coffee-growing communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> These include Coffee Kids, an international non-profit which improves the lives of children and families in remote coffee-growing villages;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-congress7610_116-5"><a href="#cite_note-congress7610-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dlabay_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-dlabay-122">[122]</a></sup> and the FomCafe cooperative’s quality-control training program, which helps farmers earn higher profits for coffee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-congress7610_116-6"><a href="#cite_note-congress7610-116">[116]</a></sup> In 2002 GMCR was the first corporate investor of the non-profit micro-loan organization Root Capital,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> and through it Green Mountain has provided millions of dollars in loans to cash-strapped coffee farmers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-start_81-2"><a href="#cite_note-start-81">[81]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csrr2007_112-1"><a href="#cite_note-csrr2007-112">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> In 2002 Green Mountain also formed a joint alliance with the U.S. Agency for International Development, to improve the livelihoods of those in impoverished coffee-growing regions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-congress7610_116-7"><a href="#cite_note-congress7610-116">[116]</a></sup>
</p><p>Internally, GMCR has cultivated and maintained a flat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporate_culture" title="Corporate culture">corporate culture</a>, where employee buy-in, enthusiasm and vision, collaboration, and input is important.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup> The company offers employees continuous training and development opportunities;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> tuition for outside education; profit-sharing; financial education; and continuous career-advancement support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fred_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-fred-29">[29]</a></sup> Employees are paid for up to 52 hours of volunteer work in their community per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dlabay_122-1"><a href="#cite_note-dlabay-122">[122]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-you_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-you-127">[127]</a></sup> GMCR has been on <i><a href="/wiki/Forbes" title="Forbes">Forbes</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span> list of Best Small Companies five times, and has been recognized as a "Best Place to Work" in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HR_Magazine" title="HR Magazine">HR Magazine</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-you_127-1"><a href="#cite_note-you-127">[127]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Business_segments_and_brands">Business segments and brands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Business segments and brands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Keurig Green Mountain operates in two business segments: domestic and Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AR2014_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-AR2014-128">[128]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-129">[129]</a></sup> The domestic segment produces and sells coffee, hot cocoa, teas and other beverages, to be prepared hot or cold, in Keurig pods; it also sells coffee in traditional packaging, including whole beans and ground coffee in bags, and ground coffee in fractional packs.  It also sells patented Keurig single-cup brewing systems for use both at home and away from home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AR2014_128-1"><a href="#cite_note-AR2014-128">[128]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters_129-1"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-129">[129]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Canadian business unit – Keurig Canada Inc. – sells Keurig brewers, and produces and sells coffees, teas, and other beverages in a variety of packaging formats, including Keurig pods, as well as coffee in traditional packaging such as bags, cans, and fractional packs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AR2014_128-2"><a href="#cite_note-AR2014-128">[128]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reuters_129-2"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-129">[129]</a></sup> It sells under a variety of brands, including Van Houtte, Brulerie St. Denis, Brulerie Mont-Royal, and Orient Express, and its licensed Bigelow and Wolfgang Puck brands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup>
</p><p>Through its owned brands and through its partnerships and licensing, Keurig Green Mountain's K-Cup pods offer more than 400 varieties of coffee, tea, and other beverages from 60 brands, including the top ten best-selling coffee brands in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_governance">Corporate governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keurig_Dr_Pepper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Corporate governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Founder Bob Stiller was president and CEO of the company from its inception in 1981 until 2007, when he stepped down but remained chairman until May 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blanford_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-blanford-133">[133]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup> Lawrence J. Blanford became Green Mountain's president and CEO in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blanford_133-1"><a href="#cite_note-blanford-133">[133]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-135"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> Brian Kelley, previously chief product supply officer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coca-Cola_Refreshments" title="Coca-Cola Refreshments">Coca-Cola Refreshments</a>, became the company's president and CEO in December 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2016, JAB Holding Company and other investors acquired Keurig Green Mountain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaplan_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-kaplan-11">[11]</a></sup> Robert Gamgort, previously CEO of <a href="/wiki/Pinnacle_Foods" title="Pinnacle Foods">Pinnacle Foods</a>, took over as KGM's new CEO in May 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gamgort_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-gamgort-3">[3]</a></sup> The pre-existing management team, with Gamgort as its new CEO, continued to run Keurig Green Mountain as an independent entity, following its acquisition by JAB Holding Company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kaplan_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-kaplan-11">[11]</a></sup> Following the 2018 merger with Dr Pepper Snapple, Gamgort became CEO of Keurig Dr Pepper, and Larry Young, who had been President and CEO of Dr Pepper Snapple, retired from those positions and joined the new company's board of directors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup>
</p>
