Belgium">Princess Stéphanie of Belgium</a> in the Winter Garden.</div></div></div><p>The <b>Royal Greenhouses of Laeken</b> (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">Koninklijke Serres van Laken</i>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Serres Royales de Laeken</i>) are a vast complex of monumental heated <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouses</a> in the park of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Castle_of_Laeken" title="Royal Castle of Laeken">Royal Castle of Laeken</a> in the north of <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>. The historic complex contains tropical, sub tropical and cold greenhouses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> These greenhouses are world famous, but not a tourist attraction. The greenhouses are part of the Royal Park, and the royal private gardens and usually not open for visitors.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Greenhouses_of_Laeken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The gardens date back to the 18th century, but <a href="/wiki/Leopold_II_of_Belgium" title="Leopold II of Belgium">King Leopold II</a> changed its garden-architecture. A brand new complex was commissioned by King Leopold II and designed by <a href="/wiki/Alphonse_Balat" title="Alphonse Balat">Alphonse Balat</a>. Built between 1874 and 1895, the complex was finished with the completion of the so-called <i>Iron Church</i>, a domed greenhouse, which would originally serve as the <a href="/wiki/Royal_chapel" title="Royal chapel">royal chapel</a>. The total floor surface of this immense complex is 2.5 hectares (270,000 square feet). 800,000 liters (over 200,000 US gallons) of fuel oil are needed each year to heat the buildings.
After the death of the king, the greenhouses were kept, but the <i>Iron Church</i> was converted into a private royal bathing house.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_botanic_collection">Royal botanic collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Greenhouses_of_Laeken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Royal botanic collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Famous is the royal botanic collection, with old plants from <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> and various species of flowers which are cultivated inside the royal greenhouses for use at <a href="/wiki/Court" title="Court">court</a>.
Though the current collection has lost many <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> since the death of King Leopold II, the collection is still famous.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1909, there were 314 species of <a href="/wiki/Camellia" title="Camellia">camellias</a> in the royal collection, with more than 1000 plants. Today, only 305 remain. The camellias are the world's largest and oldest collection in a greenhouse.
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orange_tree" title="Orange tree">Orange tree</a> collection of Leopold II was renowned with 130 trees aged 200 to 300 years; one even 400 years. In the 1970s, only 45 trees were still alive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Visit">Visit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Royal_Greenhouses_of_Laeken&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Visit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The royal complex can only be visited each year during a two-week period in April–May, when most flowers are in full bloom. Other times, the greenhouses are visited by heads of state during official visits.
</p>
