<p>The <b>Green Party</b>,  also known as the <b>Green Party UK</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">Green</a> <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.
</p><p>Prior to 1985 it was called the <a href="/wiki/Ecology_Party" title="Ecology Party">Ecology Party</a>, and before that <a href="/wiki/PEOPLE_Party" title="PEOPLE Party">PEOPLE</a>. In 1990, it separated into three political parties:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party of England and Wales</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party" title="Scottish Green Party">Scottish Green Party</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Green Party in Northern Ireland">Green Party in Northern Ireland</a></li></ul>
<p>Despite the UK Green Party no longer existing as an entity, <b>"Green Party"</b> (singular) is still used in most media<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2017)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> to refer collectively to the three separate parties; for example, when they report opinion polls and election results.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="PEOPLE.2C_1973.E2.80.931975"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="PEOPLE,_1973–1975">PEOPLE, 1973–1975</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: PEOPLE, 1973–1975">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/PEOPLE_Party" title="PEOPLE Party">PEOPLE Party</a></div>
<p>An interview with overpopulation expert <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul R. Ehrlich</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a></i> magazine inspired<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> a small group of professional and business people to form the 'Club of Thirteen', so named because it first met on 13 October 1972 in <a href="/wiki/Daventry" title="Daventry">Daventry</a>. This included surveyors and property agents Freda Sanders and Michael Benfield, Jitendrakumar Patel (1950-2016) and husband and wife solicitors Lesley and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Whittaker" title="Tony Whittaker">Tony Whittaker</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> (a former <a href="/wiki/Kenilworth" title="Kenilworth">Kenilworth</a> councillor for the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a>), all with practices in Coventry. Many in this 'club' were wary of forming a political party so, after a few weeks, in November 1972 these five agreed to form 'PEOPLE' as a new political party to challenge the UK political establishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup>  Its policy concerns published in 1973 included economics, employment, defence, energy and fuel supplies, land tenure, pollution and social security, all set within an <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> perspective. "Zero growth" (or "steady state") economics were a strong feature in the party's philosophical basis.
</p><p>Later recognised as perhaps the world's earliest Green party, the party created the first edition of the Manifesto for a Sustainable Society as a background statement of policies. The manifesto was inspired by <i><a href="/wiki/A_Blueprint_for_Survival" title="A Blueprint for Survival">A Blueprint for Survival</a></i> published by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ecologist" title="The Ecologist">The Ecologist</a></i> magazine. The editor of <i>The Ecologist</i>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Goldsmith" title="Edward Goldsmith">Edward 'Teddy' Goldsmith</a>, merged his 'Movement for Survival' with PEOPLE. Goldsmith became one of the leading members of the new party during the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>With "Steady State" economics featured in the party's philosophical basis, the all-UK party became a persistent and growing presence in general elections and European elections, often fielding enough candidates to qualify for television and radio election broadcasts.
</p><p>Derek Wall, in his history of the Green Party, contends that the new political movement focused initially on the theme of survival, which shaped the "bleak evolution" of the nascent ecological party during the 1970s. In Wall's eyes, the party suffered from a lack of media attention and "opposition from many environmentalists", which contrasted with the experience of other emerging Green parties, such as Germany's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Die Grünen</a>. Nonetheless, PEOPLE invested many of its resources in engaging with the indifferent environmental movement, which Wall calls a "tactical mistake".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Membership rose and the party contested both 1974 General Elections. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_February_1974" title="United Kingdom general election, February 1974">February 1974 General Election</a>, PEOPLE received 4,576 votes in 7 seats. Following the election, an influx of left-wing activists took PEOPLE in a more left-wing direction, causing something of a split. This affected preparations for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_October_1974" title="United Kingdom general election, October 1974">October 1974 General Election</a>, where PEOPLE's average vote fell to just 0.7%.  After much internal debate the party's 1975 Conference adopted a proposal to change its name to 'The Ecology Party' in order to gain more recognition as the party of environmental concern.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Party co-founder Tony Whittaker noted in an interview with Derek Wall "… voters did not connect PEOPLE with ecology. What I wanted was something that the media could look up in their files so that, when they wanted a spokesman of the issue of ecology, they could find the Ecology Party and pick up the phone. It was as brutal and basic as that. PEOPLE didn’t communicate what we had hoped it would communicate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="The_Ecology_Party.2C_1975.E2.80.931985"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Ecology_Party,_1975–1985">The Ecology Party, 1975–1985</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Ecology Party, 1975–1985">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Ecology_Party" title="The Ecology Party">The Ecology Party</a></div>
<p>The party won its first representation in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_local_elections,_1976" title="United Kingdom local elections, 1976">1976</a>, when John Luck took a seat on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rother_District_Council" title="Rother District Council">Rother District Council</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Sussex" title="East Sussex">East Sussex</a>, and party Campaign Secretary John Davenport won a <a href="/wiki/Parish_councils_in_England" title="Parish councils in England">parish council</a> seat in <a href="/wiki/Kempsey,_Worcestershire" title="Kempsey, Worcestershire">Kempsey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-:4-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Tyler" title="Jonathan Tyler">Jonathan Tyler</a> was elected Chairman of the party in 1976,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-:4-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Jonathon_Porritt" title="Jonathon Porritt">Jonathon Porritt</a> became a prominent member. At the 1977 Party Conference in Birmingham, the party's first constitution was ratified and <a href="/wiki/Jonathon_Porritt" title="Jonathon Porritt">Jonathon Porritt</a> was elected to the Ecology Party National Executive Committee (NEC). Porritt would become the party's most significant public figure, working, with <a href="/wiki/David_Fleming_(writer)" title="David Fleming (writer)">David Fleming</a>, "to provide the Party with an attractive image and effective organisation".
</p><p>With Porritt gaining increasing prominence and an election manifesto called <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Real_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Real Alternative (page does not exist)">The Real Alternative</a></i>, the Ecology Party fielded 53 candidates in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1979" title="United Kingdom general election, 1979">1979 General Election</a>, entitling them to radio and television election broadcasts. Though many<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2017)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> considered this a gamble, the plan, encouraged by Porritt, worked, as the party received 39,918 votes (an average of 1.5%) and membership jumped tenfold from around 500 to 5,000 or more. This, Derek Wall notes, meant that the Ecology Party "became the fourth party in UK politics, ahead of the National Front and Socialist Unity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following this electoral success, the party introduced Annual Spring Conferences to accompany Autumn Conferences, and a process of building up a large compendium of policies began, culminated in today's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Policies_for_a_Sustainable_Society" title="Policies for a Sustainable Society">Policies for a Sustainable Society</a></i> (which encompasses around 124 520 words).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YGSite_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-YGSite-7">[7]</a></sup> At the same time, according to Wall,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> "the Post-1968 generation" began to join the party, advocating non-violent direct action as an important element of the Ecology Party vision outside of electoral politics. This manifested itself in an apparent "decentralist faction" who gained ground within the party, leading to the Party Conference stripping the Executive of powers and rejecting the election of a single leader. The new generation was in evidence in the first 'Summer Green Gathering' in July 1980, the action of the Ecology Party CND (later Green CND), and the <a href="/wiki/RAF_Greenham_Common" title="RAF Greenham Common">Greenham Common</a> camp. The party also became increasingly <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminist</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the recession causing the marginalisation of Green issues, <a href="/wiki/Roy_Jenkins" title="Roy Jenkins">Roy Jenkins</a> leaving the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> to form the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(UK)" title="Social Democratic Party (UK)">Social Democratic Party</a> (SDP), and the inability of the party to absorb the rapid increase in membership, the early 1980s were extremely tough for the Ecology Party.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Nonetheless, the party prepared for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1983" title="United Kingdom general election, 1983">1983 General Election</a>, spurred on by the success of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Die Grünen</a> in Germany. In the 1983 election, the Ecology Party stood over 100 candidates and gained 54,299 votes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Name_change_and_internal_strife.2C_1985-86"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_change_and_internal_strife,_1985-86">Name change and internal strife, 1985-86</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Name change and internal strife, 1985-86">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:First_Green_Party_Poster_13_June_1985.pdf"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1753" data-file-width="1240" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/First_Green_Party_Poster_13_June_1985.pdf/page1-220px-First_Green_Party_Poster_13_June_1985.pdf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/First_Green_Party_Poster_13_June_1985.pdf/page1-330px-First_Green_Party_Poster_13_June_1985.pdf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/First_Green_Party_Poster_13_June_1985.pdf/page1-440px-First_Green_Party_Poster_13_June_1985.pdf.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:First_Green_Party_Poster_13_June_1985.pdf" title="Enlarge"></a></div>First British 'Green Party' public meeting, Hackney 13 June 1985</div></div></div><p> 1985 was a time of political change in the UK. After the formation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(UK,_1990)" title="Social Democratic Party (UK, 1990)">Social Democratic Party</a> (SDP), there were noises being made that the UK needed a "green" party. In response to the rumours that a group of <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal Party</a> activists were about to launch a UK 'Green Party',<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> HELP (the <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Hackney" title="London Borough of Hackney">Hackney</a> Local Ecology Party) registered the name <i>The Green Party,</i> with a green circle, designed by Steve O’Brien, as its logo. The first public meeting, chaired by David Fitzpatrick (then an Ecology Party speaker), was 13 June 1985 in Hackney Town Hall. <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ekins" title="Paul Ekins">Paul Ekins</a> (then co-chair of the Ecology Party) spoke on the subject of <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">Green politics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Inner_city" title="Inner city">inner city</a>. Hackney Green Party put a formal proposal to the Ecology Party Autumn Conference in <a href="/wiki/Dover" title="Dover">Dover</a> that year to change to the Green Party, which was supported by the majority of attendees, including <a href="/wiki/John_Abineri" title="John Abineri">John Abineri</a>, formerly an actor in the BBC series <i><a href="/wiki/Survivors_(1975_TV_series)" title="Survivors (1975 TV series)">Survivors</a></i> who supported adding <i>Green</i> to the name to fall in line with other environmental parties in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The next year, an internal dispute arose within the party. A faction calling itself the <i>Party Organisation Working Group (POWG)</i> proposed <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_amendment" title="Constitutional amendment">constitutional amendments</a> designed to create a streamlined, two-tier structure to govern the internal workings of the party. Decentralists voted these proposals down. <a href="/wiki/Paul_Ekins" title="Paul Ekins">Paul Ekins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Tyler" title="Jonathan Tyler">Jonathan Tyler</a>, prominent party activists and leading members of POWG, then formed a semi-covert group called <i>Maingreen,</i> whose private comments, upon becoming public knowledge, suggested to many that they wished to take control of the party. Tyler and Ekins resigned and left the party but Derek Wall describes how the "wounds" left by the 'Maingreen Affair' lingered on in the heated internal debates of the late 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="General_election.2C_1987"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="General_election,_1987">General election, 1987</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: General election, 1987">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Meanwhile, the party gained ground electorally. The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1987" title="United Kingdom general election, 1987">1987 General Election</a> saw the 133 Greens standing for office take 89,753 votes (1.3% on average), an improvement on 1983. The next two years would see growing membership and increasing media attention. This coincided with greater concern over the environment following the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl disaster</a> in 1986 and concern over <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">CFCs</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Campaign_success.2C_1989"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaign_success,_1989">Campaign success, 1989</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Campaign success, 1989">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The party enjoyed further success. Its Campaign for Real Democracy' launched by the party allowed it to play a part in the Anti-<a href="/wiki/Poll_tax_(Great_Britain)" title="Poll tax (Great Britain)">Poll Tax</a> Campaign. The party's biggest success came at the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1989_(United_Kingdom)" title="European Parliament election, 1989 (United Kingdom)">1989 European Elections</a>, where the Green Party won 2,292,695 votes and received 15% of the overall vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> European Elections in Great Britain were then run on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plurality_electoral_system" title="Plurality electoral system">first-past-the-post</a> basis, whilst the three seats in Northern Ireland were elected by <a href="/wiki/Single_transferable_vote" title="Single transferable vote">single transferable vote</a>, and the party failed to gain any seats.
</p><p>According to Derek Wall,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the party would have gained 12 seats if they had been running in other European countries who employed <a href="/wiki/Proportional_representation" title="Proportional representation">Proportional Representation</a>. Wall explains this "breakthrough" as a combination of the declining popularity of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>, the reaction to the <a href="/wiki/Poll_tax_(Great_Britain)" title="Poll tax (Great Britain)">Poll Tax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> opposition to the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, ineffective <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrat</a> campaigns and a well-prepared Green Party campaign. That environmental issues were very prominent in UK politics at the time should also be added to this list. At no time before or since have Green issues been so high on the minds of UK voters as a voting issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MORI_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-MORI-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>As a result of this success, <a href="/wiki/Sara_Parkin" title="Sara Parkin">Sara Parkin</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Icke" title="David Icke">David Icke</a> rose to prominence in the UK media, soon becoming two of the four Principal Speakers, a position created in lieu of a leader. Parkin especially was in demand as a Green spokesperson. However, the new media attention was not always handled well by the party as a whole.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In the run up to the 1989 party conference, it attracted criticism for advocating policies aiming to reduce the total population,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> proposals which were subsequently rejected. Further controversies included Derek Wall's intervention as a maverick 'Green fundamentalist'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and rejection of possible alliances to establish PR.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Icke too attracted criticism soon after writing his second book in 1989, an outline of his views on the environment, The Observer<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> called him "the Greens' Tony Blair."
</p><p>Mainstream political parties were, however, alarmed by the Greens' electoral performance and adopted some 'Green policies' in an attempt to counter the threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup> In this period, the Green Party had representation in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> in the person of  <a href="/wiki/George_MacLeod" title="George MacLeod">George MacLeod, Baron MacLeod of Fuinary</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-:3-4">[4]</a></sup> who died in 1991. He was the first British Green parliamentarian.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="The_breakup_of_the_party.2C_1990"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_breakup_of_the_party,_1990">The breakup of the party, 1990</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(UK)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The breakup of the party, 1990">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1990, the Scottish and Northern Ireland wings of the Green Party in the United Kingdom decided to separate amicably from the party in England and Wales, to form the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Green_Party" title="Scottish Green Party">Scottish Green Party</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-2">[2]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Green Party in Northern Ireland">Green Party in Northern Ireland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">[1]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Wales_Green_Party" title="Wales Green Party">Wales Green Party</a> became an autonomous regional party and remained within the new <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party of England and Wales</a>.
</p>
