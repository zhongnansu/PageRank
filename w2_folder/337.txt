<p>Throughout its history, the policies and objectives of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-governmental" title="Non-governmental">non-governmental</a> environmental protection and conservation organization <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> have been criticized by a number of groups, including national governments, members of industry, former Greenpeace members, scientists, political groups, and other environmentalists. The organization's methods, such as the use of <a href="/wiki/Direct_action" title="Direct action">direct action</a>, have also led to controversy and legal action.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prominent_critics">Prominent critics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Criticism_of_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prominent critics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="388" data-file-width="304" height="281" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Patrick_Moore_%28environmentalist%29.jpg/220px-Patrick_Moore_%28environmentalist%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3e/Patrick_Moore_%28environmentalist%29.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center><a href="/wiki/Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)" title="Patrick Moore (environmentalist)">Patrick Moore's</a> position on forests, nuclear energy, and more always went against the majority Greenpeace staff<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></center></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phil_Radford.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="2305" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Phil_Radford.jpg/220px-Phil_Radford.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Phil_Radford.jpg/330px-Phil_Radford.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Phil_Radford.jpg/440px-Phil_Radford.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phil_Radford.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center><a href="/wiki/Phil_Radford" title="Phil Radford">Phil Radford</a> disputes Moore by claiming that the U.S. does not need nuclear energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenpeace.org_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-greenpeace.org-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blogs.wsj.com_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-blogs.wsj.com-3">[3]</a></sup></center></div></div></div>
<p>Canadian ecologist <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)" title="Patrick Moore (environmentalist)">Patrick Moore</a>, a former Greenpeace founding member, is a critic of the organization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Moore_(environmentalist)" title="Patrick Moore (environmentalist)">Patrick Moore</a> had once spoken out against <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> in 1976,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> but today he supports it, along with <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In Australian newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Age" title="The Age">The Age</a></i>, he writes "Greenpeace is wrong — we must consider nuclear power".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> He argues that any realistic plan to reduce reliance on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>  or <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions need increased use of nuclear energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wapo_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-wapo-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Phil_Radford" title="Phil Radford">Phil Radford</a>, Executive Director of <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> US responded that nuclear energy is too risky and takes too long to build to address <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>. Radford asserts that the U.S. can shift to nearly 100% <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> while phasing out nuclear power by 2050.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenpeace.org_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-greenpeace.org-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blogs.wsj.com_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-blogs.wsj.com-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, 107 Nobel laureates signed a letter urging Greenpeace to end its opposition to genetically modified organisms (GMOs).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup> The letter stated: "We urge Greenpeace and its supporters to re-examine the experience of farmers and consumers worldwide with crops and foods improved through biotechnology, recognize the findings of authoritative scientific bodies and regulatory agencies, and abandon their campaign against "GMOs" in general and <a href="/wiki/Golden_rice" title="Golden rice">Golden rice</a> in particular. Scientific and regulatory agencies around the world have repeatedly and consistently found crops and foods improved through biotechnology to be as safe as, if not safer than those derived from any other method of production. There has never been a single confirmed case of a negative health outcome for humans or animals from their consumption. Their environmental impacts have been shown repeatedly to be less damaging to the environment, and a boon to global biodiversity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Criticism_of_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_party_structure">Internal party structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Criticism_of_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Internal party structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Wyn_Grant" title="Wyn Grant">Wyn Grant</a>, Greenpeace is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hierarchical" title="Hierarchical">hierarchical</a> and undemocratic organisation which allows very little control of its members over the campaigns the organisation embarks upon. For example, the criticisms Grant has given include: Greenpeace has a strictly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bureaucratic" title="Bureaucratic">bureaucratic</a> and borderline <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authoritarian" title="Authoritarian">authoritarian</a> internal structure; a small group of individuals have control over the organisation in both international and local levels; local action groups are totally dependent on the central body; and the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rank_and_file" title="wikt:rank and file">rank and file</a> are excluded from most decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fairclough-Lynch2013_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fairclough-Lynch2013-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shell_oil_storage_buoy">Shell oil storage buoy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Criticism_of_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Shell oil storage buoy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1995, Greenpeace mounted a successful campaign to force <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Royal Dutch Shell</a>, co-owner of the <a href="/wiki/Brent_Spar" title="Brent Spar">Brent Spar</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oil_storage_buoy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oil storage buoy (page does not exist)">oil storage buoy</a>, to dismantle the platform on land rather than <a href="/wiki/Scuttling" title="Scuttling">scuttling</a> it at sea, which involved the platform's occupation by Greenpeace members. A moratorium on the dumping of offshore installations was adopted in Europe soon after the affair, and three years later the Environment Ministers of countries bordering the northeast Atlantic sided with Greenpeace, <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20070404053913/http://archive.greenpeace.org/odumping/radioactive/reports/odhistory.pdf" rel="nofollow">(PDF)</a> adopting a permanent ban on the dumping of offshore installations at sea.
</p><p>After the affair, it came to light that Greenpeace had miscalculated the amount of toxic waste present aboard the Brent Spar. Greenpeace admitted that its claims that the Spar contained 5000 tons of oil were inaccurate, apologizing to Shell on September 5.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, Greenpeace dismissed the importance of the amount of oil on board, pointing to wider industrial responsibility as the main issue at hand, as the Brent Spar was to be the first offshore installation to be dumped in the northeast Atlantic ocean; Greenpeace claimed that it would likely have been followed by the scuttling of dozens or hundreds more platforms, setting what they consider to be a dangerous precedent. The organization went on to point out that Shell's decision to scrap the platform had been taken before Greenpeace announced the existence of an incorrect amount of toxic waste, and that their mistake therefore did not influence Shell's decision.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anti-DDT_campaign">Anti-DDT campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Criticism_of_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Anti-DDT campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Greenpeace supports the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stockholm_Convention" title="Stockholm Convention">Stockholm Convention</a> on Persistent Organic Pollutants, a legally binding international agreement which aims to phase out substances such as <a href="/wiki/DDT" title="DDT">DDT</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Both the Stockholm Convention and Greenpeace allow DDT to be used for malaria control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> However, according to <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bate" title="Roger Bate">Roger Bate</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">libertarian</a> critic of Greenpeace, the organization's campaign to shut down the last major DDT factory in the world located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cochin" title="Cochin">Cochin</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, would make the eradication of malaria more difficult for poorer countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  Robert Gwadz of the US <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a> said in 2007, "The ban on DDT may have killed 20 million children."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greener_Electronics_campaign">Greener Electronics campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Criticism_of_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Greener Electronics campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2006, Greenpeace released its first "Guide to Greener Electronics," which ranked leading mobile phone, PC, TV, and game console manufacturers on their global policies and practice on eliminating harmful chemicals and on taking responsibility for their products once they are discarded by consumers. Greenpeace encouraged manufacturers to clean up their products by eliminating hazardous substances and to take back and recycle their products responsibly once they become obsolete.
</p><p>The Version 1 Guide to Greener Electronics<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> stated "the ranking is important because the amounts of toxic e-waste is growing everyday and it often ends up dumped in the developing world. Reducing the toxic chemicals in products reduces pollution from old products and makes recycling safer, easier and cheaper." It ranked <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dell" title="Dell">Dell</a> near the top, but essentially gave failing grades across the industry, ranking <a href="/wiki/Toshiba" title="Toshiba">Toshiba</a> thirteenth, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_Computer" title="Apple Computer">Apple Computer</a> in eleventh place out of the fourteen brands. The report singled out Apple for its low rank, saying: "Already, many of the companies are in a race to reach the head of the class - that is, except for Apple, who seems determined to remain behind rather than be the teacher's pet we'd hoped for." This caught the attention of tech media news sites, and was widely reported. Greenpeace gave Nintendo a score of 0.3 / 10 based on the fact that Greenpeace has almost no information on the company, which, by Greenpeace's grading system, automatically results in a zero for the affected categories.
</p><p>Daniel Eran of Apple news blog <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=RoughlyDrafted&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="RoughlyDrafted (page does not exist)">RoughlyDrafted</a></i> criticized the guide in an article,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> saying the Greenpeace guide's "ranking puts far more weight upon what companies publicly say rather than what they actually do. It is also clear that Greenpeace intended the report more as an attention getting stunt than a serious rating of corporations' actual responsibility." In response, Greenpeace attacked <i>RoughlyDrafted'</i>s credibility, pointing out that it has in the past been called "the lunatic fringe of Mac fandom" by other bloggers after comparing the cost of <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Windows" title="Microsoft Windows">Microsoft Windows</a> and Apples' <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X" title="Mac OS X">Mac OS X</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-techn-not-good_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-techn-not-good-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was alleged that Greenpeace had no factual evidence, instead relying on unsubstantiated official company information for the report in order to garner publicity, as well as political and monetary support. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>'s 2007 report <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Product_Environmental_Assessment_Tool" title="Electronic Product Environmental Assessment Tool">Electronic Product Environmental Assessment Tool</a> (EPEAT) showed Apple leading the ranks in all categories. The <a href="/wiki/Ars_Technica" title="Ars Technica">Ars Technica</a> website said the report "should make Greenpeace red-faced",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arstec-eco_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-arstec-eco-23">[23]</a></sup> after factual substantiation was questioned.
</p><p>Greenpeace responded to the criticisms in a rebuttal also published by <i>RoughlyDrafted</i>. Along with the Greenpeace rebuttal, the article<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> further presented the results of a second Greenpeace report, called "<i>Toxic Chemicals in Your Laptop Exposed</i>," which <i>RoughlyDrafted</i> called an 'apology' for the initial claims Greenpeace made in the Greener Guide rankings. While Greenpeace itself has never used the word "apology", they did restate several of their initial claims in a response to Keith Ripley, another reviewer of the report.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> For example, the data reported findings of minimal traces of <a href="/wiki/Tetrabromobisphenol_A" title="Tetrabromobisphenol A">Tetrabromobisphenol A</a> (TBBPA), an unregulated fire retardant in the Apple computer; the Greenpeace press release said Apple "appears to be using far more of this toxic chemical than its competitors".  This is despite the fact that the EU Scientific Committee on Health and Environmental Risks concluded in March 2005 that TBBPA "presents no risk to human health"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and "the World Health organisation conducted a scientific assessment of TBBPA and found that the risk for the general population is considered to be insignificant."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>More criticism of the statement in the Greenpeace press release followed in <i>Greenpeace Lies About Apple</i> on RoughlyDrafted:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-roughdr-lies-_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-roughdr-lies--28">[28]</a></sup> "The most recent report, 'Toxics in Your Laptop Exposed,' did credible scientific tests, but then threw out the data to instead present a lathered up, misleading and deceptive press release that was simply a lie. No amount of credible science is worth anything if you ignore the findings and simply present the message you wanted the data to support."
</p><p>Greenpeace published an article on its website, addressing the criticism so far, with a special focus on scientific issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Guide to Greener Electronics has been continually updated with new rankings of the electronics manufacturers, and as of May 2010, 15 editions have been produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenland_indigenous_complaints">Greenland indigenous complaints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Criticism_of_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Greenland indigenous complaints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, when <a href="/wiki/Cairn_Energy" title="Cairn Energy">Cairn Energy</a> found initial traces of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> in one of its test wells that indicate the possibility of much larger hydrocarbon deposits, Greenpeace sent its ship, the <i>Esperanza</i>, into a stand-off with the Danish navy near Cairn's oil platforms. Many members of the community were angry with Greenpeace telling youngsters not to eat whale or seal, which the <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> have eaten for centuries. As a result, Cairn Energy has gotten a warmer welcome than many environmentalists have hoped for. One person said this was "because it is for the greater good." A reference to the potential revenue energy exploration could bring to offset the $500 million annual grant from Denmark which could transform the economy and lead to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenland_independence" title="Greenland independence">Greenland independence</a>.
</p><p>However, Aqqaluk Lynge, from the Inuit Circumpolar Council, said an influx of foreign companies and workers could mean the natives "risk being a minority in our own country." He added that "One thing is for sure, yes Greenland has a chance to be rich, it's something that is essential for the people of Greenland to discuss and then decide if we want forced industrialisation." Greenpeace also stopped the trade of sealskin, something Greenlanders have never forgotten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neo-luddism">Neo-luddism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Criticism_of_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Neo-luddism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several publications have accused Greenpeace of being <a href="/wiki/Technophobia" title="Technophobia">anti-technology</a>.  In an editorial in the Register,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Andrew Orlowski cites Greenpeace's opposition to research on nuclear fusion.  Orlowski points out the incoherent and contradictory argument by Greenpeace that nuclear fusion is non-viable but then warns of the dangers that commercial nuclear fusion posed on the environment despite their claims that nuclear fusion is an impractical, technological deadend.  Other publications also criticized Greenpeace's stand against <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_crops" title="Genetically modified crops">genetically modified crops</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> and the unlawful destruction of those crops by its members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_to_golden_rice">Opposition to golden rice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Criticism_of_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Opposition to golden rice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 2013, several prominent scientists published a letter condemning Greenpeace and other NGOs for their opposition to <a href="/wiki/Golden_rice" title="Golden rice">golden rice</a>, a type of rice that would be used in poorer countries. It is modified so that it has more vitamin A than normal rice. In the letter they state, "If ever there was a clear-cut cause for outrage, it is the concerted campaign by Greenpeace and other nongovernmental organizations, as well as by individuals, against Golden Rice."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mismanagement_of_funds">Mismanagement of funds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Criticism_of_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mismanagement of funds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In June 2014, media outlets reported that one employee lost 3.8 millon euros by betting on fixed rate concurrency exchange when the euro was gaining against foreign currency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Internal leaked communications by Kumi Naidoo, executive director of Greenpeace, indicates a "huge problem" and that the staff have "good reason" to be upset.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> At the same time Pascal Husting, one of the top executives of the organization, was shown to commute several times a month during a two year period between Luxembourg, his home residence,  and Amsterdam, the Head Quarters of Greenpeace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> This is against the company's view that short flights add to the CO2 emissions and internal policies regarding short flights. Pascal later apologized publicly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Damage_to_Nazca_Lines">Damage to Nazca Lines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Criticism_of_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Damage to Nazca Lines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In December 2014, Greenpeace came under criticism following a publicity stunt within the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazca_lines" title="Nazca lines">Nazca lines</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_heritage_site" title="World heritage site">UN World Heritage Site</a> inside <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>. Demonstrators entered the restricted area surrounding the Hummingbird lines and laid down banners that spelled out "Time for Change! / The Future is Renewable / Greenpeace". In doing so, they tracked multiple footprints and damaged both the line itself and the area surrounding it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Peru's deputy minister for culture criticized the actions, calling them "thoughtless, insensitive, illegal, irresponsible and absolutely pre-meditated."
</p><p>Greenpeace responded with apologies, claiming that demonstrators took care to avoid damage, but this is contradicted by video and photographs showing the activists wearing conventional shoes (not special protective shoes) while walking on the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The organization stated they were surprised that this resulted "in some kind of moral offense." Conversely, they stood by "...history of more than 40 years of peaceful activism [which] clearly shows that we have always been most respectful with people around the world and their diverse cultural legacies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Greenpeace members were allowed to leave Peru without being charged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
Despite Greenpeace offering to take "total responsibility", the president of the <a href="/wiki/Maria_Reiche" title="Maria Reiche">Maria Reiche</a> Association Anne Maria Cogorno stated that the damage was "irreparable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenpeace_and_Indian_government_controversy">Greenpeace and Indian government controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Criticism_of_Greenpeace&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Greenpeace and Indian government controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Greenpeace(NGO) India Society has been accused by the Indian Ministry of Home Affairs of a violation of the Foreign Contribution Regulation Act. As per the FCRA act,  no NGO can use more than 50% of received funds for administrative purposes, and Greenpeace India is alleged to have used 60% of these funds for administrative purposes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Greenpeace India is challenging these allegations, and specifically the inclusion of campaign staff salaries as admin expenses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Intelligence Bureau of India allegedly leaked a report accusing Greenpeace of anti-development activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The Delhi High Court overturned the governments decision to offload an Indian citizen from her travel to London - saying you cannot muzzle dissent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
