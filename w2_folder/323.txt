<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Green cheese</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Queso_fresco.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Queso_fresco.JPG/220px-Queso_fresco.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Queso_fresco.JPG/330px-Queso_fresco.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Queso_fresco.JPG/440px-Queso_fresco.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Queso_fresco.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queso_fresco" title="Queso fresco">Queso fresco</a> ("fresh cheese"), a green cheese, with typical white, round appearance</div></div></div>
<p><b>Green cheese</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fresh_cheese" title="Fresh cheese">fresh cheese</a> that has not thoroughly dried nor aged, which is white in colour and usually round in shape. The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> gives a reference from the year 1542 of the <a href="/wiki/Types_of_cheese" title="Types of cheese">four sorts of cheese</a>. The first sort is green cheese, which is not green by reason of colour but for its newness or under-ripened state,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> for the whey is not half pressed out of it yet. The phrase is not commonly used to describe the colour of a cheese, though there are some cheeses with a greenish tint, usually from mold or added herbs. There are other instances in which the word "green" is used to mean "new", such as in the term "<a href="/wiki/Cowboy#Symbolism" title="Cowboy">greenhorn</a>", which refers to an inexperienced person.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The phrase <a href="/wiki/The_Moon_is_made_of_green_cheese" title="The Moon is made of green cheese">the Moon is made of green cheese</a> refers to the similarity in appearance of a typical round, green cheese and the full Moon. It is commonly misinterpreted to mean that the Moon is green in colour, which isn't the case. (The surface of the Moon is quite dark and could be described as having the color of old, weathered black asphalt.)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">John Maynard Keynes</a> made a reference comparing a green cheese to the moon in his 1936 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_General_Theory_of_Employment,_Interest_and_Money" title="The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money">The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money</a></i>. The exact meaning of the sentence is debated, but the equating of the moon with a green cheese is clear.:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Unemployment develops, that is to say, because people want the <b>moon</b>; -- men cannot be employed when the object of desire (<i>i.e.</i> money) is something which cannot be produced and the demand for which cannot be readily choked off. There is no remedy but to persuade the public that <b>green cheese</b> is practically the same thing and to have a <b>green cheese</b> factory (<i>i.e.</i> a central bank) under public control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>There is a popular saying in Scotland, "You can't see green cheese go past you", This means that you must have whatever someone else has just for the sake of having it. For example, a child's friend may get a bike and although the child is unable to ride a bike, they will pester their parents to buy one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The following <a href="/wiki/West_Frisian_language#Folklore_about_relation_to_English_and_Dutch" title="West Frisian language">West Frisian language shibboleth</a> makes reference to it: "Bûter en brea en griene tsiis, hwa't dat net sizze kin is gjin oprjuchte Frys." meaning "Butter and bread and green cheese, who can't say that is no true Frisian."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Actual_green_cheeses">Actual green cheeses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Actual green cheeses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The veins of most <a href="/wiki/Blue_cheese" title="Blue cheese">blue cheese</a> are in fact a dark bluish-green. There are several varieties of cheese which are actually green or pale green in colour. Green cheese varieties include:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cherni_Vit_(cheese)" title="Cherni Vit (cheese)">Cherni Vit</a> - Green cheese from Bulgaria</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sage_Derby_cheese" title="Sage Derby cheese">Sage Derby cheese</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Schabziger" title="Schabziger">Schabziger</a> - Swiss green cheese</li></ul>
<p>Other cheeses exist which are wholly or partly green in colour due to the addition of herbs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
