<p>The <b>Green Party of the United States</b> (<b>GPUS</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Green_party" title="Green party">green</a> <a href="/wiki/Political_parties_in_the_United_States" title="Political parties in the United States">federation of political parties</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The party promotes <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green politics</a>, specifically <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Participatory_democracy" title="Participatory democracy">participatory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grassroots_democracy" title="Grassroots democracy">grassroots democracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a>, <a href="/wiki/LGBT_rights_in_the_United_States" title="LGBT rights in the United States">LGBT rights</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-war" title="Anti-war">anti-war</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anti-racism" title="Anti-racism">anti-racism</a>. On the <a href="/wiki/Political_spectrum" title="Political spectrum">political spectrum</a>, the party is generally seen as <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">left-wing</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gp.org_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-gp.org-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The GPUS was founded in 2001 as the evolution of the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_State_Green_Parties" title="Association of State Green Parties">Association of State Green Parties</a> (ASGP), which was formed in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> After its founding, the GPUS soon became the primary national green organization in the country, eclipsing the <a href="/wiki/Greens/Green_Party_USA" title="Greens/Green Party USA">Greens/Green Party USA</a> (G/GPUSA), which formed in 1991 out of the Green Committees of Correspondence (CoC), a collection of local <a href="/wiki/Green_party" title="Green party">green groups</a> active since 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The ASGP had increasingly distanced itself from the G/GPUSA in the late 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Greens gained widespread public attention during the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2000" title="United States presidential election, 2000">2000 presidential election</a>, when the ticket composed of <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winona_LaDuke" title="Winona LaDuke">Winona LaDuke</a> won 2.7% of the popular vote. Nader was vilified by many <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrats</a> and even some Greens, who accused him of <a href='/wiki/Ralph_Nader#The_"spoiler"_controversy' title="Ralph Nader">spoiling</a> the election for <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a>, the Democratic candidate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The degree of Nader's impact on the 2000 election remains controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The GPUS has had several members elected into state legislatures, including in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>. In September 2017, independent <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Chapman_(politician)" title="Ralph Chapman (politician)">Ralph Chapman</a>, member of the <a href="/wiki/Maine_House_of_Representatives" title="Maine House of Representatives">Maine House of Representatives</a>, switched his affiliation to the Green Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bangordailynews.com_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-bangordailynews.com-14">[14]</a></sup> A number of Greens around the United States hold positions on the municipal level, including on school boards, city councils and as mayors.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="History of the Green Party of the United States">History of the Green Party of the United States</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The political movement that began in 1985 as the decentralized Committees of Correspondence<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> evolved into a more centralized structure by 1990, opening a national clearinghouse and forming governing bodies, bylaws and a platform as the Green Committees of Correspondence (GCoC) and by 1990 simply The Greens. The organization conducted grassroots organizing efforts, educational activities and electoral campaigns.
</p><p>Internal divisions arose between members who saw electoral politics as ultimately corrupting and supported the notion of an "anti-party party" formed by <a href="/wiki/Petra_Kelly" title="Petra Kelly">Petra Kelly</a> and other leaders of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Greens</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> vs. those who saw electoral strategies as a crucial engine of social change. A struggle for the direction of the organization culminated a "compromise agreement", ratified in 1990 at the Greens National Congress in <a href="/wiki/Elkins,_West_Virginia" title="Elkins, West Virginia">Elkins, West Virginia</a> and in which both strategies would be accommodated within the same <a href="/wiki/527_organization" title="527 organization">527 political organization</a> renamed the <a href="/wiki/Greens/Green_Party_USA" title="Greens/Green Party USA">Greens/Green Party USA</a> (G/GPUSA). It was recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Election_Commission" title="Federal Election Commission">FEC</a> as a national political party in 1991.
</p><p>The compromise agreement subsequently collapsed and two <a href="/wiki/Green_party" title="Green party">Green party</a> organizations have co-existed in the United States since. The Green Politics Network was organized in 1990 and the National Association of Statewide Green Parties formed by 1994. Divisions between those pressing to break onto the national political stage and those aiming to grow roots at the local level continued to widen during the 1990s. The <a href="/wiki/Association_of_State_Green_Parties" title="Association of State Green Parties">Association of State Green Parties</a> (ASGP) encouraged and backed Nader's presidential runs in 1996 and 2000. By 2001, the push to separate electoral activity from the G/GPUSA issue-based organizing led to the Boston Proposal and subsequent rise of the Green Party of the United States.  The G/GPUSA lost most of its affiliates in the following months and dropped its FEC national party status in 2005.
</p><p>In 2016, Mark Salazar set a new record for a Green Party nominee for Congress. Running in the Arizona 8th district against incumbent Republican Congressman <a href="/wiki/Trent_Franks" title="Trent Franks">Trent Franks</a>, Salazar received 93,954 votes or 31.43%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The GPUS follows the ideals of <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green politics</a>, which are based on the Four Pillars, namely <a href="/wiki/Ecosophy" title="Ecosophy">ecological wisdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grassroots_democracy" title="Grassroots democracy">grassroots democracy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolence</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Ten Key Values, which expand upon the Four Pillars, are as follows:
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Grassroots_democracy" title="Grassroots democracy">Grassroots democracy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">Social justice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Equal_opportunity" title="Equal opportunity">equal opportunity</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ecosophy" title="Ecosophy">Ecological wisdom</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">Nonviolence</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Decentralization" title="Decentralization">Decentralization</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Community-based_economics" title="Community-based economics">Community-based economics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_justice" title="Economic justice">economic justice</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">Feminism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equity</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Multiculturalism" title="Multiculturalism">Respect for diversity</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Social_responsibility" title="Social responsibility">Personal and global responsibility</a></li>
<li>Future focus and <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a></li></ol>
<p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Camejo" title="Peter Camejo">Peter Camejo</a> was quoted in 2002 as claiming that he was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watermelon_(politics)" title="Watermelon (politics)">watermelon</a>—green on the outside, but red on the inside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In January 2004, he initiated the Avocado Declaration, which compares Greens to avocados. "An avocado is Green on the outside and Green on the inside".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The Declaration goes on to explain that Greens have a vital role in bringing democracy to the otherwise undemocratic <a href="/wiki/Two-party_system" title="Two-party system">two party system</a> of the United States; that the Greens have a unique and independent identity as a third party, which cannot be subsumed into the Republican or Democratic parties; and that they cannot be dismissed by Republican or Democratic critics by implying that they are merely socialists or communists.
</p><p>The Green Party does not accept donations from corporations, <a href="/wiki/Political_action_committee" title="Political action committee">political action committees</a> (PACs), 527(c) organizations or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_money" title="Soft money">soft money</a>. The party's <a href="/wiki/Party_platform" title="Party platform">platforms</a> and rhetoric harshly criticize corporate influence and control over government, media and society at large.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_Positions">Political Positions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Political Positions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_issues">Economic issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economic issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Healthcare">Healthcare</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Healthcare">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The party supports the implementation of a <a href="/wiki/Single-payer_healthcare" title="Single-payer healthcare">single-payer healthcare</a> system. They have also called for contraception and abortion procedures to be available on demand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Green Party calls for proving tuition-free college at public universities and vocational schools, increasing funding for after-school and daycare programs, cancelling all student loan debt, and repealing the No Child Left Behind Act. They are strongly against the dissolution of public schools and the privatization of education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_issues">Social issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Social issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_justice">Criminal justice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Criminal justice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The party favors the abolition of the death penalty, repeal of three-strikes laws, banning of private prisons, legalization of marijuana, and decriminalization of other drugs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Racial_justice">Racial justice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Racial justice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Green Party advocates for "complete and full" reparations to the African American community, as well the removal of the Confederate flag from all government buildings. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="LGBT.2B_rights"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="LGBT+_rights">LGBT+ rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: LGBT+ rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The party supports same-sex marriage, the right of access to medical and surgical treatment for sex reassignment, and withdrawing foreign aid to countries with poor LGBT+ rights records.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_policy">Foreign policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Foreign policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Green Party calls on the United States to join the <a href="/wiki/International_Criminal_Court" title="International Criminal Court">International Criminal Court</a>, and sign the <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_Nuclear-Test-Ban_Treaty" title="Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty">Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Treaty_on_the_Non-Proliferation_of_Nuclear_Weapons" title="Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons">Non-Proliferation Treaty</a>. Additionally, it supports cutting the defense budget in half as well as prohibiting all arms sales to foreign countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Greens support the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Comprehensive_Plan_of_Action" title="Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action">Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action</a>.
</p>
<h4><span id="Israel.2FPalestine"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel/Palestine">Israel/Palestine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Israel/Palestine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Green Party advocates for the right of return, cutting all U.S. aid to Israel, and a <a href="/wiki/One-state_solution" title="One-state solution">one-state solution</a>. It has also expressed support for the international <a href="/wiki/Boycott,_Divestment_and_Sanctions" title="Boycott, Divestment and Sanctions">Boycott, Divestment and Sanctions</a> (BDS) movement.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_composition">Structure and composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Structure and composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Committees">Committees</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Committees">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Green Party has two national committees recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Election_Commission" title="Federal Election Commission">Federal Election Commission</a> (FEC):
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Green_National_Committee" title="Green National Committee">Green National Committee</a> (GNC)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Senatorial_Campaign_Committee" title="Green Senatorial Campaign Committee">Green Senatorial Campaign Committee</a> (GSCC)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_National_Committee">Green National Committee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Green National Committee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_National_Committee" title="Green National Committee">Green National Committee</a></div>
<p>The GNC is composed of delegates elected by affiliated state parties. The state parties also appoint delegates to serve on the various standing committees of the GNC. The National Committee elects a steering committee of seven co-chairs, a secretary and a treasurer to oversee daily operations. The National Committee performs most of its business online, but it also holds an annual national meeting to conduct business in person.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caucuses">Caucuses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Caucuses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Five Identity Caucuses have achieved representation on the GNC:
</p>
<ul><li>Black Caucus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></li>
<li>Latinx Caucus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>Lavender Greens Caucus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LGBTIQ" title="LGBTIQ">LGBTIQ</a>)</li>
<li>National Women's Caucus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></li>
<li>Young Greens<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Other caucuses have worked toward formal recognition by the GNC:
</p>
<ul><li>Disability Caucus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></li>
<li>Labor Caucus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></li>
<li>Indigenous Caucus<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">Geographic distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green Party has its strongest popular support on the Pacific Coast, Upper Great Lakes, and Northeast, as reflected in the geographical distribution of Green candidates elected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> As of  June 2007<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, Californians have elected 55 of the 226 office-holding Greens nationwide. Other states with high numbers of Green elected officials include <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> (31), <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a> (23), <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> (18) and <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a> (17). Maine has the highest per capita number of Green elected officials in the country and the largest Green registration percentage with more than 29,273 Greens comprising 2.95% of the electorate as of  November 2006<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Madison,_Wisconsin" title="Madison, Wisconsin">Madison, Wisconsin</a> is the city with the most Green elected officials (8), followed by <a href="/wiki/Portland,_Maine" title="Portland, Maine">Portland, Maine</a> (7).
</p><p>The 2016 <a href="/wiki/Jill_Stein_presidential_campaign,_2016" title="Jill Stein presidential campaign, 2016">presidential campaign</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jill_Stein" title="Jill Stein">Jill Stein</a> got substantive support from counties and precincts with a high percentage of <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> population. For instance, in <a href="/wiki/Sioux_County,_North_Dakota" title="Sioux County, North Dakota">Sioux County</a> (North Dakota, 84,1% Native American), Stein gained her best county-wide result: 10.4% of the votes. In <a href="/wiki/Rolette_County,_North_Dakota" title="Rolette County, North Dakota">Rolette County</a> (also North Dakota, 77% Native American), she got 4.7% of the votes. Other majority Native American counties where Stein did above state average are <a href="/wiki/Menominee_County,_Wisconsin" title="Menominee County, Wisconsin">Menominee</a> (WI), <a href="/wiki/Roosevelt_County,_Montana" title="Roosevelt County, Montana">Roosevelt</a> (MN) and several precincts in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, the Green Party had 305,000 registered members in states allowing party registration and tens of thousands of members and contributors in the rest of the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> One challenge that the Green Party (as well as other <a href="/wiki/Third_party_(United_States)" title="Third party (United States)">third parties</a>) faces is the difficulty of overcoming <a href="/wiki/Ballot_access" title="Ballot access">ballot access</a> laws in many states.
</p>

<p>As of  October 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, 143 officeholders in the United States were affiliated with the Green Party, the majority of them in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, several in <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, with five or fewer in ten other states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPUSofficeholders_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-GPUSofficeholders-44">[44]</a></sup> These included one mayor and one deputy mayor and fourteen county or city commissioners (or equivalent). The remainder were members of school boards, clerks and other local administrative bodies and positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GPUSofficeholders_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-GPUSofficeholders-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Several Green Party members have been elected to state-level office, though not always as affiliates of the party. <a href="/wiki/John_Eder" title="John Eder">John Eder</a> was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Maine_House_of_Representatives" title="Maine House of Representatives">Maine House of Representatives</a>, re-elected in 2004, but defeated in 2006. <a href="/wiki/Audie_Bock" title="Audie Bock">Audie Bock</a> was elected to the <a href="/wiki/California_State_Assembly" title="California State Assembly">California State Assembly</a> in 1999, but switched her registration to independent seven months later<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> running as such in the 2000 election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Carroll_(politician)" title="Richard Carroll (politician)">Richard Carroll</a> was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_House_of_Representatives" title="Arkansas House of Representatives">Arkansas House of Representatives</a> in 2008, but switched parties to become a Democrat five months after his election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-San_Francisco_Chronicle_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-San_Francisco_Chronicle-47">[47]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fred_Smith_(Arkansas_politician)" title="Fred Smith (Arkansas politician)">Fred Smith</a> was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_House_of_Representatives" title="Arkansas House of Representatives">Arkansas House of Representatives</a> in 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> but re-registered as a Democrat in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reelection_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-reelection-49">[49]</a></sup> In 2010, former Green Party leader <a href="/wiki/Ben_Chipman" title="Ben Chipman">Ben Chipman</a> was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Maine_House_of_Representatives" title="Maine House of Representatives">Maine House of Representatives</a> as an <a href="/wiki/Independent_politician" title="Independent politician">unenrolled</a> candidate and was re-elected in 2012 and 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Gayle_McLaughlin" title="Gayle McLaughlin">Gayle McLaughlin</a> was twice elected mayor of <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_California" title="Richmond, California">Richmond, California</a>, defeating two Democrats in 2006<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> and then reelected in 2010; and elected to City Council in 2014 after completing her second term as mayor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> With a population of over 100,000 people, it was the largest American city with a Green mayor. <a href="/wiki/Fairfax,_California" title="Fairfax, California">Fairfax, California</a>; <a href="/wiki/Arcata,_California" title="Arcata, California">Arcata, California</a>; <a href="/wiki/Sebastopol,_California" title="Sebastopol, California">Sebastopol, California</a>; and <a href="/wiki/New_Paltz,_New_York" title="New Paltz, New York">New Paltz, New York</a> are the only towns in the United States to have had a Green Party majority in their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Town_councils" title="Town councils">town councils</a>. Twin Ridges Elementary in <a href="/wiki/Nevada_County,_California" title="Nevada County, California">Nevada County, California</a> held the first Green Party majority school board in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 21, 2017, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Chapman_(politician)" title="Ralph Chapman (politician)">Ralph Chapman</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Maine_House_of_Representatives" title="Maine House of Representatives">Maine House of Representatives</a>, switched his party registration from unaffiliated to Green, providing the Green Party with their first state-level representative since 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bangordailynews.com_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-bangordailynews.com-14">[14]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Henry_John_Bear" title="Henry John Bear">Henry John Bear</a> became a member of the Green Party in the same year as Chapman, giving the Maine Green Independent Party and GPUS its second currently-serving state representative, though Bear is a nonvoting tribal member of the Maine House of Representatives.
</p><p>No nominee of the Green Party has been elected to office in the federal government.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fundraising_and_position_on_Super_PACs">Fundraising and position on Super PACs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Fundraising and position on Super PACs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early decades of Green organizing in the United States, the prevailing American system of money-dominated elections was universally rejected by Greens, so that some Greens were reluctant to have Greens participate in the election system at all because they deemed the campaign finance system inherently corrupt. Other Greens felt strongly that the Green Party should develop in the electoral arena and many of these Greens felt that adopting an alternative model of campaign finance, emphasizing self-imposed contribution limits, would present a wholesome and attractive contrast to the odious campaign finance practices of the money-dominated major parties.
</p><p>Over the years, some state Green parties have come to place less emphasis on the principle of self-imposed limits than they did in the past. Nevertheless, it is safe to say that Green Party fundraising (for candidates' campaigns and for the party itself) still tends to rely on relatively small contributions and that Greens generally decry not only the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Political_action_committee" title="Political action committee">Super PACs</a>, but also the big-money system, which some Greens criticize as <a href="/wiki/Plutocracy" title="Plutocracy">plutocracy</a>.
</p><p>Some Greens feel that the Green Party's position should be simply to follow the laws and regulations of campaign finance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> Other Greens argue that it would injure the Green Party not to practice a principled stand against the anti-democratic influence of money in the political process. Candidates for office, like <a href="/wiki/Jill_Stein" title="Jill Stein">Jill Stein</a>, the 2012 and 2016 Green Party nominee for the President of the United States, typically rely on smaller donations to fund their campaigns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
