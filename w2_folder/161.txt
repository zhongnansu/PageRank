<p><b>Green Templeton College</b> (<b>GTC</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Colleges_of_the_University_of_Oxford" title="Colleges of the University of Oxford">constituent college</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> in the United Kingdom. The college is located on the previous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_College,_Oxford" title="Green College, Oxford">Green College</a> site on Woodstock Road next to <a href="/wiki/Somerville_College,_Oxford" title="Somerville College, Oxford">Somerville College</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Oxford" title="North Oxford">North Oxford</a> and is centred on the architecturally important <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_Observatory" title="Radcliffe Observatory">Radcliffe Observatory</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> an 18th-century building, modelled on the ancient <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_the_Winds" title="Tower of the Winds">Tower of the Winds</a> at <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>. It is the university's newest graduate college, having been founded by the historic merger of Green College and <a href="/wiki/Templeton_College,_Oxford" title="Templeton College, Oxford">Templeton College</a> in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The College has a distinctive academic profile, specialising in subjects relating to human welfare and social, economic and environmental well-being, including medical and health sciences, management and business, and most social sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Green Templeton's sister college at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> is <a href="/wiki/St_Edmund%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="St Edmund's College, Cambridge">St Edmund's College</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Templeton_College,_Oxford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The merger between Green College and <a href="/wiki/Templeton_College,_Oxford" title="Templeton College, Oxford">Templeton College</a> was the first of its kind in the University's modern history. It was announced formally in July 2007 following its approval by the University Council and the Governing Bodies of both colleges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Green Templeton College has always accepted both female and male students, as did both of its predecessors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although both Green College and Templeton College were young colleges by Oxford standards, they each had their own individual history and established culture.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_College">Green College</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Templeton_College,_Oxford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Green College">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Green College was founded in 1979 to bring together graduate students of medicine and related disciplines, and especially to encourage academic programmes in industry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It was named after its main benefactors: <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Howard_Green" title="Cecil Howard Green">Cecil H. Green</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/Texas_Instruments" title="Texas Instruments">Texas Instruments</a>, and his wife, Ida Green. It was one of three colleges established due to his generosity, the others being <a href="/wiki/Green_College,_University_of_British_Columbia" title="Green College, University of British Columbia">Green College, University of British Columbia</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_University_of_Texas_at_Dallas" title="The University of Texas at Dallas">The University of Texas at Dallas</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Short_History_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Short_History-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Of its student population, around 30% studied in the field of medicine, around 20% were engaged in postgraduate medical research, and other focuses included social work, environmental change and education studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Short_History_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Short_History-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Templeton_College">Templeton College</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Templeton_College,_Oxford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Templeton College">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Templeton College was founded in 1965 as the Oxford Centre for Management Studies under the chairmanship of <a href="/wiki/Norman_Chester" title="Norman Chester">Sir Norman Chester</a>, Warden of <a href="/wiki/Nuffield_College,_Oxford" title="Nuffield College, Oxford">Nuffield College, Oxford</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_John_Templeton" title="Sir John Templeton">Sir John Templeton</a> provided an endowment to the centre in 1983 to raise professional standards in British management. This was one of the largest endowments ever made to a British educational establishment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> The centre was later renamed Templeton College in his memory.
</p><p>The College emphasised a commitment to lifelong individual development and aimed to bring together leaders in various fields to explore key issues in management and related policy areas. It was previously based at Egrove Park, in nearby <a href="/wiki/Kennington,_Oxfordshire" title="Kennington, Oxfordshire">Kennington village</a>. Its buildings were opened in 1969 and granted <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed status</a> in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> It was granted a <a href="/wiki/Royal_charter" title="Royal charter">royal charter</a> and full college status in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_of_arms">Coat of arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Templeton_College,_Oxford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Coat of arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Green Templeton College's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armorial_bearings" title="Armorial bearings">armorial bearings</a> combine elements from the original coats of arms of both Green College and Templeton College, capturing the spirit of the history and character of each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Its <a href="/wiki/Escutcheon_(heraldry)" title="Escutcheon (heraldry)">shield</a> comprises two primary symbols: the <a href="/wiki/Rod_of_Asclepius" title="Rod of Asclepius">rod of Aesculapius</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nautilus" title="Nautilus">Nautilus</a> shell. The former was the principal <a href="/wiki/Charge_(heraldry)" title="Charge (heraldry)">charge</a> of Green College's coat of arms. (In Greek mythology, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aesculapius" title="Aesculapius">Aesculapius</a>, the son of <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a>, was a medical practitioner. The serpent coiled around his staff symbolises the healing arts.) The Nautilus shell was chosen by Sir John Templeton, as symbolising evolution and renewal, and was adopted by Templeton College in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Green Templeton College's <a href="/wiki/Crest_(heraldry)" title="Crest (heraldry)">crest</a> depicts a heraldic representation of the Sun behind the astronomical device for <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> (♀), acknowledging the historic <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">transit of Venus</a> across the Sun in 1761, which astronomical event prompted the foundation of the Radcliffe Observatory. The crest is blazoned:
</p>
<dl><dd>(<i>On a Helm with a Wreath Or and Vert</i>) <i>In front of a Sun in splendour the rays voided Or the Astrological Symbol for Venus Vert</i>.</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings_and_grounds">Buildings and grounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Templeton_College,_Oxford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Buildings and grounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Radcliffe_Observatory">The Radcliffe Observatory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Templeton_College,_Oxford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Radcliffe Observatory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_Observatory" title="Radcliffe Observatory">Radcliffe Observatory</a></div>
<p>The College is located on the three-acre (1.2 ha) site on Woodstock Road in North Oxford that previously housed Green College. It is centred on the architecturally important <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_Observatory" title="Radcliffe Observatory">Radcliffe Observatory</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> an 18th-century, Grade I <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a> building, modelled on the ancient <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_the_Winds" title="Tower of the Winds">Tower of the Winds</a> in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>.
</p><p>The observatory was built at the suggestion of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hornsby" title="Thomas Hornsby">Thomas Hornsby</a>, the Savilian Professor of Astronomy at the university, after he had used his room in the Bodleian Tower to observe the transit of Venus across the Sun's disc in 1769. The transit was a notable event which helped to produce greatly improved measurements for nautical navigation. The observatory was built with funds from the <a href="/wiki/Trust_law" title="Trust law">trust</a> of <a href="/wiki/John_Radcliffe_(physician)" title="John Radcliffe (physician)">John Radcliffe</a>, whose considerable estate had already financed a new quadrangle for his old college (<a href="/wiki/University_College,_Oxford" title="University College, Oxford">University College, Oxford</a>) as well as the Radcliffe Library (now the <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_Camera" title="Radcliffe Camera">Radcliffe Camera</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_Infirmary" title="Radcliffe Infirmary">Radcliffe Infirmary</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Building began in 1772 to plans by the architect <a href="/wiki/Henry_Keene" title="Henry Keene">Henry Keene</a>, but only Observer's House is his design. Upon Keene's death in 1776, the observatory was completed to a different design by <a href="/wiki/James_Wyatt" title="James Wyatt">James Wyatt</a>. Wyatt based his design on an illustration of the Tower of the Winds in Athens that had appeared in Stuart and Revett's Antiquities of Athens, published in 1762.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Atop the observatory rests the Tower of Winds. Beneath the tower are three levels, with rooms on each level. The ground floor is today used as the college dining room. The first floor was originally the library, but is now the Graduate Common Room. The third floor is an octagonal observation room, which is now empty except for some of the original furniture.
</p><p>The observatory was a functioning observatory from 1773 until its owners, the Radcliffe Trustees, sold it in 1934 to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Nuffield" title="Lord Nuffield">Lord Nuffield</a>, who then presented it to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radcliffe_Hospital" title="Radcliffe Hospital">Radcliffe Hospital</a>. In 1936, Lord Nuffield established the Nuffield Institute for Medical Research there. In 1979, the Nuffield Institute relocated to the <a href="/wiki/John_Radcliffe_Hospital" title="John Radcliffe Hospital">John Radcliffe Hospital</a> and the observatory was taken over by Green College.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Norham_Gardens_Site">The Norham Gardens Site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Templeton_College,_Oxford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Norham Gardens Site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:135px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Osler.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="160" data-file-width="133" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/84/Osler.jpg" width="133"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Osler.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sir William Osler</div></div></div>
<p>A short walk from Green Templeton's main buildings is 13 Norham Gardens. In 1905 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_William_Osler" title="Sir William Osler">Sir William Osler</a> was appointed to the Regius Professorship of Medicine at Oxford. In 1907, Sir William acquired 13 Norham Gardens, one of the finest houses in Oxford at the time. During the fourteen years of his Regius Professorship, Osler made Norham Gardens a meeting place for academics from all over the world. It became a favourite of medical students, physicians, and scientists, even receiving the label of 'The Open Arms' for the warmth it exuded.
</p><p>After Osler, 13 Norham Gardens was occupied by another Regius Professor, <a href="/wiki/Sir" title="Sir">Sir</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_White_Pickering" title="George White Pickering">George White Pickering</a>, and after him the Regius Professor <a href="/wiki/Richard_Doll" title="Richard Doll">Sir Richard Doll</a>, who was the last Regius Professor to live there. The property of 13 Norham Gardens was then acquired by Green College.
</p><p>Today, Norham Gardens houses the Osler-McGovern Centre. The Centre promotes the art and science of medicine through its workshops, conferences, visiting scholars and post-doctoral Fellows. It follows in Sir William Osler's footsteps by uniting scholars, lecturers and academics. It is also home to the <a href="/wiki/Reuters_Institute_for_the_Study_of_Journalism" title="Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism">Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism</a> (RISJ), established in 2006.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Housing">Housing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Templeton_College,_Oxford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Housing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Green Templeton College has housing on the main site and various annexes. On-site housing includes the Doll Building (built in 1981) with 30 student rooms, Walton Building with 3 student rooms, Observer's House with 13 student rooms and New Block with 4 student rooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-On-site_accommodation_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-On-site_accommodation-14">[14]</a></sup> Furthermore, the college has various student rooms in the Lord Napier House (Observatory Street), 2- and 3-bedroomed terraced houses in Observatory Street, various student rooms on St Margaret's Road, 1- and 2-bedroom flats in Rewley Abbey Court and 1- and 2-bedroom flats in Norham Gardens.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Templeton_College,_Oxford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Green Templeton also provides laundry facilities, a lecture theatre (the Abraham Lecture Theatre), a seminar/presentation room (the Barclay Room), and a computing room with 6 Windows computers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Facilities_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Facilities-15">[15]</a></sup> There are two 24-hour-access libraries on-campus: a management library (Management Studies Library) and a medical/social science library (Medical Library).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Facilities_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Facilities-15">[15]</a></sup> The Management Studies Library is adjacent to the Observer's House, and the Medical Library is adjacent to the Radcliffe Observatory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Facilities_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Facilities-15">[15]</a></sup> Since August 2014, Green Templeton has a on-site 171 square meter gym with rowing machines, spinning bikes, threadmills and weights located between the main site and Observatory street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gym_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gym-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_developments">Future developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Templeton_College,_Oxford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Future developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the next phase of development, the area to the north-east of the College is to be developed, allowing for the extension of the College's academic departments and residential accommodation. Furthermore, the University of Oxford is redeveloping the adjoining old <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_Infirmary" title="Radcliffe Infirmary">Radcliffe Infirmary</a> site as the <a href="/wiki/Radcliffe_Observatory_Quarter" title="Radcliffe Observatory Quarter">Radcliffe Observatory Quarter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Templeton_College,_Oxford&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a graduate college, it has a single common room, known as the Graduate Common Room ("GCR") – equivalent to the Middle Common Room ("MCR") in other colleges – to encourage interaction between students and fellows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Green Templeton offers a wide variety of activities to its students. The various Green Templeton College clubs and societies include the <a href="/wiki/Green_Templeton_Boat_Club" title="Green Templeton Boat Club">Boat Club</a>, the Book Club, Choir, Golf Society, LGBT Society, Medical Anthropology Society, Richard Doll Society, and Music Society. College events include the annual college Garden Party, the Summer Ball, the Human Welfare Conference, "Welfare and Wine", formals, and themed "bops" (discos or college parties), held throughout the year. Lecture series are routinely held for those interested, including the Green Templeton Lectures and those held by the Reuter's Institute.
</p><p>The college is also active in various sports, especially rowing. It co-owns a boathouse on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/River_Isis" title="River Isis">River Isis</a> with a sizeable fleet, and <a href="/wiki/Green_Templeton_Boat_Club" title="Green Templeton Boat Club">Green Templeton Boat Club</a> has been competing successfully since its establishment in 2008. Other sports at Green Templeton College include badminton, basketball, cricket, croquet, football, golf, netball, rugby, running, squash and tennis. The College also has on-site tennis and squash courts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sports_Facilities_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sports_Facilities-19">[19]</a></sup> In addition to this, all students of the college are entitled to free membership at the Iffley Road Sports Centre.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sports_Facilities_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sports_Facilities-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The college bar, known as the Stables Bar, is open for drinks in the evenings, and serves as a meeting place during the day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Social_Events_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Social_Events-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Green Templeton is actively involved in charity work, supporting a local and an overseas charity every year. Both the Graduate Common Room and the College Charity Committee organise numerous events throughout the year, both at Green Templeton College and in Oxford.
</p><p>Green Templeton College's strong ties with the clinical medicine community are fostered through its affiliation with Osler House. Osler House is Oxford University's club for medical students and is open to students and Fellows involved in research in a range of topics related to human health and welfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The friendly and comfortable ambience of Osler House is focused around a games room which has <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billiards" title="Billiards">pool</a> and table football facilities.
</p><p>The college publishes a newsletter every term, called <i>In Transit</i>, as well as an annual Green Templeton College alumni magazine, called <i>The GTC Magazine</i> (formerly, albeit for Templeton College only, <i>Templeton Views</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and the college Graduate Common Room circulates a weekly electronic newsletter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
