<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Greenway (landscape)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>greenway</b> is "a strip of undeveloped land near an urban area, set aside for recreational use or environmental protection".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  However, the term can in fact include "a scenic road"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and though many are in urban areas, there are some rural greenways, as for example the <a href="/wiki/Monadnock-Sunapee_Greenway" title="Monadnock-Sunapee Greenway">Monadnock-Sunapee Greenway</a>, a hiking trail in southern New Hampshire.
</p><p>A greenway is a <a href="/wiki/Trail" title="Trail">trail</a> (and sometimes a <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_corridor" title="Wildlife corridor">wildlife corridor</a>) found in both urban and rural settings that is frequently created out of a disused railway, canal towpath, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Utility_company" title="Utility company">utility</a> or similar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_of_way_(transportation)" title="Right of way (transportation)">right of way</a>, or derelict industrial land. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rail_trails" title="Rail trails">Rail trails</a> are one of the most common forms of greenway, and they also resemble <a href="/wiki/Linear_park" title="Linear park">linear parks</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CrossVermontTrail-NewburyVT-2010-10-22.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="448" data-file-width="598" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/CrossVermontTrail-NewburyVT-2010-10-22.jpg/220px-CrossVermontTrail-NewburyVT-2010-10-22.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/CrossVermontTrail-NewburyVT-2010-10-22.jpg/330px-CrossVermontTrail-NewburyVT-2010-10-22.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/CrossVermontTrail-NewburyVT-2010-10-22.jpg/440px-CrossVermontTrail-NewburyVT-2010-10-22.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CrossVermontTrail-NewburyVT-2010-10-22.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="external text" href="http://www.traillink.com/trail/cross-vermont-trail.aspx" rel="nofollow">The Cross Vermont Trail, a greenway in New England, US</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Southern_England" title="Southern England">Southern England</a>, the term also refers to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_trackway" title="Ancient trackway">ancient trackways</a> or <a href="/wiki/Green_lane_(road)" title="Green lane (road)">green lanes</a>, especially those found on chalk <a href="/wiki/Downland" title="Downland">downlands</a>, like <a href="/wiki/The_Ridgeway" title="The Ridgeway">the Ridgeway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenway_(landscape)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:USA-Charles_River_Esplanade6.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="5184" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/USA-Charles_River_Esplanade6.JPG/220px-USA-Charles_River_Esplanade6.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/USA-Charles_River_Esplanade6.JPG/330px-USA-Charles_River_Esplanade6.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/USA-Charles_River_Esplanade6.JPG/440px-USA-Charles_River_Esplanade6.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:USA-Charles_River_Esplanade6.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Charles_River_Esplanade" title="Charles River Esplanade">Charles River Esplanade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, US</div></div></div>
<p>The American author Charles Little in his 1990 book, <i>Greenways for America,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> defines a greenway as: 
</p>
<ul><li>a linear open space established along either a natural corridor, such as a riverfront, stream valley or ridgeline, or overland along a railroad right-of-way converted to recreational use, a <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canal</a>, scenic road or other route. It is a natural or landscaped course for pedestrian or bicycle passage; an open-space connector linking parks, nature reserves, cultural features, or historic sites with each other and with populated areas; locally certain strip or <a href="/wiki/Linear_park" title="Linear park">linear parks</a> designated as parkway or <a href="/wiki/Green_belt" title="Green belt">greenbelt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The term <i>greenway</i> comes from the <i>green</i> in <a href="/wiki/Green_belt" title="Green belt">green belt</a> and the <i>way</i> in <a href="/wiki/Parkway" title="Parkway">parkway</a>, implying a recreational or pedestrian use rather than a typical street corridor, as well as an emphasis on introducing or maintaining vegetation, in a location where such vegetation is otherwise lacking. Some greenways include community gardens as well as typical park-style landscaping of trees and shrubs. They also tend to have a mostly contiguous pathway. Greenways resemble <a href="/wiki/Linear_park" title="Linear park">linear parks</a>, but the latter are only found in an urban and suburban environment.
</p><p>Though wildlife corridors are also greenways, because they have conservation as their primary purpose, they are not necessarily managed as parks for recreational use, and may not include facilities such as public trails.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tom_Turner" title="Tom Turner">Tom Turner</a> analyzed greenways in London looking for common patterns among successful examples. He was inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Pattern_language" title="Pattern language">pattern language</a> technique of architect <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Alexander" title="Christopher Alexander">Christopher Alexander</a>. Turner concluded there are seven types, or 'patterns', of greenway which he named: parkway, blueway, paveway, glazeway, skyway, ecoway and cycleway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Greenways_Association" title="European Greenways Association">European Greenways Association</a> defines it as "communication routes reserved exclusively for non-motorised journeys, developed in an integrated manner which enhances both the environment and quality of life of the surrounding area. These routes should meet satisfactory standards of width, gradient and surface condition to ensure that they are both user-friendly and low-risk for users of all abilities." (Lille Declaration, European Greenways Association, 12 September 2000).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenway_(landscape)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Railway_Platforms_on_Parkland_Walk.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Railway_Platforms_on_Parkland_Walk.JPG/220px-Railway_Platforms_on_Parkland_Walk.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Railway_Platforms_on_Parkland_Walk.JPG/330px-Railway_Platforms_on_Parkland_Walk.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Railway_Platforms_on_Parkland_Walk.JPG/440px-Railway_Platforms_on_Parkland_Walk.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Railway_Platforms_on_Parkland_Walk.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Railway Platforms on <a href="/wiki/Parkland_Walk" title="Parkland Walk">Parkland Walk</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_London" title="North London">North London</a>, England</div></div></div>
<p>Charles Little describes five general types of greenways:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Urban riverside (or other water body) greenways, usually created as part of (or instead of) a redevelopment program along neglected, often run-down, city waterfronts.</li>
<li>Recreational greenways, featuring paths and trails of various kinds, often relatively long distance, based on natural corridors as well as canals, abandoned rail beds, and public rights-of-way.</li>
<li>Ecologically significant natural corridors, usually along rivers and streams and less often ridgelines, to provide for wildlife migration and species interchange, nature study and hiking.</li>
<li>Scenic and Historic routes, usually along a road, highway or waterway, the most representative of them making an effort to provide pedestrian access along the route or at least places to alight from the car.</li>
<li>Comprehensive greenway systems or networks, usually based on natural landforms such as valleys or ridges but sometimes simply an opportunistic assemblage of greenways and open spaces of various kinds to create an alternative municipal or regional green infrastructure.</li></ul>
<p>Greenways are vegetated, linear, and multi-purpose. They incorporate a <a href="/wiki/Footpath" title="Footpath">footpath</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bikeway" title="Bikeway">bikeway</a> within a <a href="/wiki/Linear_park" title="Linear park">linear park</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Urban_design" title="Urban design">urban design</a>, they are a component of planning for <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_commuting" title="Bicycle commuting">bicycle commuting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walkability" title="Walkability">walkability</a>.
</p><p>Greenways are found in rural areas as well as urban. Corridors redeveloped as greenways often travel through both city and country, connecting them together. Even in rural areas greenways serve the purpose of providing residents access to open land managed as parks, as contrasted with land that is vegetated but inappropriate for public use, such as agricultural land. Where the historic rural road network has been enlarged and redesigned to favor highspeed automobile travel, greenways provide an alternative for people who are elderly, young, less mobile or seeking a reflective pace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Greenways are found almost globally. However, most examples are known to be in Europe and North America.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreshoreway">Foreshoreway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenway_(landscape)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Foreshoreway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lake_front_bike2.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1615" data-file-width="2537" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Lake_front_bike2.JPG/220px-Lake_front_bike2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Lake_front_bike2.JPG/330px-Lake_front_bike2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Lake_front_bike2.JPG/440px-Lake_front_bike2.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lake_front_bike2.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Lakefront_Trail" title="Chicago Lakefront Trail">Chicago Lakefront Trail</a></div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, a foreshoreway is a greenway that provides a public <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right-of-way" title="Right-of-way">right-of-way</a> along <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreshore" title="Foreshore">the edge of the sea</a>, open to both walkers and cyclists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Foreshoreways include <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanway" title="Oceanway">oceanways</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and resemble <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Promenade" title="Promenade">promenades</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boardwalk" title="Boardwalk">boardwalks</a>.
</p><p>Foreshoreways are usually concerned with the idea of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_transport" title="Sustainable transport">sustainable transport</a> and the term is used to avoid the suggestion that the route favours either pedestrians (footpath) or cyclists (bikeway).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> A foreshoreway is accessible to both pedestrians and cyclists and gives them the opportunity to move unimpeded along the seashore. Dead end paths that offer public access only to the ocean are not part of a foreshoreway.
</p><p>A foreshoreway corridor often includes a number of <a href="/wiki/Traffic" title="Traffic">traffic</a> routes that provide access along an oceanfront,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> including:
</p>
<ul><li>walking along the <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beach</a></li>
<li>edge of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreshore" title="Foreshore">foreshore</a> off-road greenway</li>
<li>edge of road off-road greenway</li>
<li>on road <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bikeway" title="Bikeway">bikeway</a></li>
<li>on road private vehicles routes</li>
<li>on road <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a> corridor</li></ul>
<p>A major example is The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_Coast_Oceanway" title="Gold Coast Oceanway">Gold Coast Oceanway</a> along beaches in <a href="/wiki/Gold_Coast,_Queensland" title="Gold Coast, Queensland">Gold Coast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, a shared use <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian" title="Pedestrian">pedestrian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyclist" title="Cyclist">cyclist</a> pathway on the Gold Coast, connecting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_Danger,_NSW/Queensland" title="Point Danger, NSW/Queensland">Point Danger</a> <a href="/wiki/Lighthouse" title="Lighthouse">lighthouse</a> on the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> and Queensland border to the <a href="/wiki/Gold_Coast_Seaway" title="Gold Coast Seaway">Gold Coast Seaway</a>. The network includes 36 kilometres (22 mi) of poor, medium and high quality pathways. Others include: The <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Lakefront_Trail" title="Chicago Lakefront Trail">Chicago Lakefront Trail</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dubai_Marina" title="Dubai Marina">Dubai Marina</a>, the <a href="/wiki/East_River_Greenway" title="East River Greenway">East River Greenway</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Plymouth_Coastal_Walkway" title="New Plymouth Coastal Walkway">New Plymouth Coastal Walkway</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Waterfront_Greenway" title="Manhattan Waterfront Greenway">Manhattan Waterfront Greenway</a>.
</p><p>Public rights of way frequently exist on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreshore" title="Foreshore">foreshore</a> of beaches throughout the world. In legal discussions the foreshore is often referred to as the wet-sand area (see <a href="/wiki/Right_of_way" title="Right of way">Right of way</a> for a fuller discussion).
</p>
