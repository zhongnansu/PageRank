<p>The <b>General Services Administration</b> (<b>GSA</b>), an <a href="/wiki/Independent_agencies_of_the_United_States_government" title="Independent agencies of the United States government">independent agency of the United States government</a>, was established in 1949 to help manage and support the basic functioning of federal agencies. GSA supplies products and communications for U.S. government offices, provides transportation and office space to federal employees, and develops government-wide cost-minimizing policies and other management tasks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>GSA employs about 12,000 federal workers and has an annual operating budget of roughly $20.9 billion. GSA oversees $66 billion of procurement annually. It contributes to the management of about $500 billion in U.S. federal property, divided chiefly among 8,700 owned and leased buildings and a 215,000 vehicle <a href="/wiki/Fleet_vehicle" title="Fleet vehicle">motor pool</a>. Among the real estate assets managed by GSA are the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan_Building" title="Ronald Reagan Building">Ronald Reagan Building and International Trade Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> – the largest U.S. federal building after the <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">Pentagon</a> – and the <a href="/wiki/Hart-Dole-Inouye_Federal_Center" title="Hart-Dole-Inouye Federal Center">Hart-Dole-Inouye Federal Center</a> (which had previously been the <a href="/wiki/Battle_Creek_Sanitarium" title="Battle Creek Sanitarium">Battle Creek Sanitarium</a> run by <a href="/wiki/John_Harvey_Kellogg" title="John Harvey Kellogg">John Harvey Kellogg</a>).
</p><p>GSA's business lines include the Federal Acquisition Service (FAS) and the Public Buildings Service (PBS), as well as several Staff Offices including the Office of Government-wide Policy, the Office of Small Business Utilization, and the Office of Mission Assurance. As part of FAS, GSA's Technology Transformation Services (TTS) helps federal agencies improve delivery of information and services to the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Key initiatives include <a href="/wiki/FedRAMP" title="FedRAMP">FedRAMP</a>, Cloud.gov, the USAGov platform (<i>e.g.</i>, <a href="/wiki/USA.gov" title="USA.gov">USA.gov</a>, <a href="/wiki/GobiernoUSA.gov" title="GobiernoUSA.gov">GobiernoUSA.gov</a>, and Kids.gov), <a href="/wiki/Data.gov" title="Data.gov">Data.gov</a>, Performance.gov, and Challenge.gov.
</p><p>GSA is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Procurement_G6" title="Procurement G6">Procurement G6</a>, an informal group leading the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Framework_agreements" title="Framework agreements">framework agreements</a> and <a href="/wiki/E-procurement" title="E-procurement">e-procurement</a> instruments in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_procurement" title="Public procurement">public procurement</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1947 President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harry_Truman" title="Harry Truman">Harry Truman</a> asked former President <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">Herbert Hoover</a> to lead what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Hoover_Commission" title="Hoover Commission">Hoover Commission</a> to make recommendations to reorganize the operations of the federal government. One of the recommendations of the commission was the establishment of an "Office of the General Services." This proposed office would combine the responsibilities of the following organizations:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Treasury_Department" title="U.S. Treasury Department">U.S. Treasury Department</a>'s Bureau of Federal Supply</li>
<li>U.S. Treasury Department's Office of Contract Settlement</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/National_Archives_and_Records_Administration" title="National Archives and Records Administration">National Archives Establishment</a></li>
<li>All functions of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Works_Agency" title="Federal Works Agency">Federal Works Agency</a>, including the Public Buildings Administration and the Public Roads Administration</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/War_Assets_Administration" title="War Assets Administration">War Assets Administration</a></li></ul>
<p>GSA became an independent agency on July 1, 1949, after the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Property_and_Administrative_Services_Act_of_1949" title="Federal Property and Administrative Services Act of 1949">Federal Property and Administrative Services Act</a>. General <a href="/wiki/Jess_Larson" title="Jess Larson">Jess Larson</a>, Administrator of the War Assets Administration, was named GSA's first Administrator.
</p><p>The first job awaiting Administrator Larson and the newly formed GSA was a complete renovation of the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a>. The structure had fallen into such a state of disrepair by 1949 that one inspector of the time said the historic structure was standing "purely from habit." Larson later explained the nature of the total renovation in depth by saying, "In order to make the White House structurally sound, it was necessary to completely dismantle, and I mean completely dismantle, everything from the White House except the four walls, which were constructed of stone. Everything, except the four walls without a roof, was finally stripped down, and that's where the work started." GSA worked closely with President Truman and First Lady <a href="/wiki/Bess_Truman" title="Bess Truman">Bess Truman</a> to ensure that the new agency's first major project would be a success.  GSA completed the renovation in 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1986 GSA headquarters, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._General_Services_Administration_Building" title="U.S. General Services Administration Building">U.S. General Services Administration Building</a>, located at Eighteenth and F Streets, NW, was listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>, at the time serving as Interior Department offices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nris_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nris-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1960 GSA created the Federal Telecommunications System, a government-wide intercity telephone system. In 1962 the Ad Hoc Committee on Federal Office Space created a new building program to address obsolete office buildings in Washington, D.C., resulting in the construction of many of the offices that now line <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independence_Avenue_(Washington_D.C.)" title="Independence Avenue (Washington D.C.)">Independence Avenue</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsa.gov_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-gsa.gov-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1970 the Nixon administration created the Consumer Product Information Coordinating Center, now part of <a href="/wiki/USAGov" title="USAGov">USAGov</a>. In 1974 the Federal Buildings Fund was initiated, allowing GSA to issue rent bills to federal agencies. In 1972 GSA established the Automated Data and Telecommunications Service, which later became the Office of Information Resources Management. In 1973 GSA created the Office of Federal Management Policy. GSA's Office of Acquisition Policy centralized <a href="/wiki/Procurement" title="Procurement">procurement</a> policy in 1978. GSA was initially responsible for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergency_preparedness" title="Emergency preparedness">emergency preparedness</a> and stockpiling strategic materials to be used in wartime until these functions were transferred to the newly-created <a href="/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency" title="Federal Emergency Management Agency">Federal Emergency Management Agency</a> in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsa.gov_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-gsa.gov-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1984 GSA introduced the federal government to the use of <a href="/wiki/Charge_card" title="Charge card">charge cards</a>, known as the GMA SmartPay system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_and_Records_Administration" title="National Archives and Records Administration">National Archives and Records Administration</a> was spun off into an independent agency in 1985. The same year, GSA began to provide governmentwide policy oversight and guidance for federal real property management as a result of an Executive Order signed by President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsa.gov_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-gsa.gov-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003 the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Protective_Service_(United_States)" title="Federal Protective Service (United States)">Federal Protective Service</a> was moved to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="Department of Homeland Security">Department of Homeland Security</a>. In 2005 GSA reorganized to merge the Federal Supply Service (FSS) and Federal Technology Service (FTS) business lines into the Federal Acquisition Service (FAS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSA_Releases_Draft_FTS/FSS_Reorganization_Plan_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-GSA_Releases_Draft_FTS/FSS_Reorganization_Plan-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>On April 3, 2009, President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> nominated <a href="/wiki/Martha_N._Johnson" title="Martha N. Johnson">Martha N. Johnson</a> to serve as GSA Administrator. After a nine-month delay, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> confirmed her nomination on February 4, 2010. On April 2, 2012, Johnson resigned in the wake of a management-deficiency report that detailed improper payments for a 2010 "Western Regions" training conference put on by the Public Buildings Service in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Las Vegas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brockell2010_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brockell2010-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ted_Weiss_Federal_Building_controversy">Ted Weiss Federal Building controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ted Weiss Federal Building controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In July 1991 GSA contractors began the excavation of what is now the <a href="/wiki/Ted_Weiss_Federal_Building" title="Ted Weiss Federal Building">Ted Weiss Federal Building</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. The planning for that building did not take into account the possibility of encountering the historic cemetery for colonial-era African New Yorkers located beneath the footprint of the $276-million-dollar office building.  When initial excavation disturbed burials, destroying skeletons and artifacts, GSA sent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeologist" title="Archaeologist">archaeologists</a> to excavate—but hid their findings from the public. Revelation of the discoveries led to 18 months of activism by African-descendant community members, public officials, academics, and concerned citizens. Ultimately, GSA made public amends by funding extensive scientific research under the auspices of Michael Blakey; creating a new subagency, the Office of Public Education and Interpretation; truncating the building plan; and funding public reports on the story of the African Burial Ground. The efforts led to the creation of a new unit of the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a>, The <a href="/wiki/African_Burial_Ground_National_Monument" title="African Burial Ground National Monument">African Burial Ground National Monument</a>, at the facility. GSA fully funded that portion of the National Park Service<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> until 2010, when GSA's formal involvement with the African Burial Ground ceased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lurita_Doan_controversy">Lurita Doan controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lurita Doan controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In an earlier scandal, President George W. Bush forced a previous GSA Administrator, <a href="/wiki/Lurita_Doan" title="Lurita Doan">Lurita Doan</a>, to resign after GSA had awarded a sole source contract for $20,000 to her friend. Doan appeared to have violated the <a href="/wiki/Hatch_Act_of_1939" title="Hatch Act of 1939">Hatch Act</a> and was criticized for political activity while on the job.  The investigating team recommended she be punished to the fullest extent, and she resigned soon after.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_Regions_Training_Conference_controversy">Western Regions Training Conference controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Western Regions Training Conference controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2012 U.S. Representative <a href="/wiki/John_Mica" title="John Mica">John Mica</a>, chairman of the House Transportation and Infrastructure Committee, called for a congressional investigation into the misuse of federal money by GSA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brockell2010_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brockell2010-13">[13]</a></sup> Lawmakers accused GSA of "lavish spending" following the 2010 Western Regions Training Conference at the <a href="/wiki/M_Resort" title="M Resort">M Resort</a> in <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Las Vegas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>GSA spent $823,000 in taxpayer money toward the October 2010 convention, including $100,405.37 spent on employee travel costs for a total of eight pre-planning meetings, scouting trips, and a "dry run." The report also found excessive spending for event planners, gifts for participants, and lavish meals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliott_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The conference had been the most recent in a series of similar lavish conferences organized by regions of GSA's Public Buildings Service. In May 2010 GSA treated 120 interns to a five-day conference at a <a href="/wiki/Palm_Springs,_California" title="Palm Springs, California">Palm Springs, California</a>, resort. An additional investigation led by Inspector General Brian D. Miller found 115 missing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple_iPods" title="Apple iPods">Apple iPods</a> meant for an employee rewards program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>GSA Administrator Martha N. Johnson resigned in the wake of the controversy. Before turning in her own resignation, Johnson fired two other GSA senior executives, Public Buildings Service head Robert Peck and senior advisor Stephen Leeds.  Four PBS Regional Commissioners, who had been responsible for planning the conference, were placed on administrative leave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Procurement_and_the_GSA_Schedule">Procurement and the GSA Schedule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Procurement and the GSA Schedule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Federal Acquisition Service (FAS) provides comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solutions</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for products and services across the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> GSA assists with procurement work for other government agencies. As part of this effort, it maintains the large GSA Schedules, which other agencies can use to buy goods and services. The GSA Schedule can be thought of as a collection of pre-negotiated contracts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Procurement managers from government agencies can view these agreements and make purchases from the GSA Schedule knowing that all legal obligations have been taken care of by GSA.
</p><p>The GSA Schedule is awarded as a prime contract entered into by the federal government and a vendor that has submitted an acceptable proposal. At the core of the GSA Schedule contract lie two key concepts: 1) Basis of Award customer or group of customers and 2) Price Reduction Clause. The two concepts are applied in concert to achieve the government's pricing objectives for the GSA Schedule program. Namely, the government wants to ensure that when the vendor experiences competitive pressures to reduce its pricing, then the government can benefit from these and be extended reduced pricing as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Basis of Award customer or group of customers represents the customer or group of customers whose sales are affected on the same terms and conditions as those with GSA, and whose pricing is used: 1) as the baseline during negotiations to establish discounts offered to GSA, and 2) as a <a href="/wiki/Price_floor" title="Price floor">price floor</a> that, when breached, constitutes additional discounting that triggers the Price Reduction Clause.
</p><p>The Price Reduction Clause ensures that vendor discounting practices and GSA Schedule prices maintain a fixed relationship. The vendor specifies in its GSA proposal, and during negotiations of GSA Schedule contract prices, the discounts to be given to Basis of Award customer(s). If the vendor then provides a larger discount to a Basis of Award customer than what was agreed upon in the GSA Schedule contract (i.e., if the price floor is breached), then the vendor's GSA price will be reduced proportionately and retroactively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Effective Price Reduction Clause compliance procedures will protect vendors if their discounting practices are fully and accurately disclosed in their original proposals to GSA, and then are used as a basis for compliance over the term of the contract. Although not ideal, a compliance system implemented after a contract has been awarded can bring a contract into compliance, although sometimes at the expense of profits. If implementing a system in the middle of a contract period, inaccuracies that turn up should be corrected immediately, and the GSA contracting officer should be made aware of them. Price Reduction Clause compliance systems and procedures can range from simple to complex. A simple, manual system would be appropriate for a service contractor with standard labor rates that are not discounted. A complex system would be required for a <a href="/wiki/Reseller" title="Reseller">reseller</a> with thousands of products and discounting policies that differ among product groups.
</p><p>In response to increased mandates and standards required by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Government" title="Federal Government">Federal Government</a> of its agencies and in a push to plan for federal sustainability, GSA offers online tools to aid in the building and management of government offices that are subject to these requirements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>GSA has delegated authority to the Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) to procure medical supplies under the VA Federal Supply Schedules Program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_property_and_buildings">Federal property and buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Federal property and buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1930s_Courthouse_window_replacement.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="227" data-file-width="304" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/1930s_Courthouse_window_replacement.jpg/220px-1930s_Courthouse_window_replacement.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e8/1930s_Courthouse_window_replacement.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1930s_Courthouse_window_replacement.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Thoughtful analysis to balance GSA preservation, cost, and performance goals supported historic window retention with replacement of non-historic windows at this 1930 courthouse</div></div></div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Federal_Building_(disambiguation)" title="Federal Building (disambiguation)">Federal Building (disambiguation)</a></div>
<p>The Public Buildings Service (PBS) acquires and manages thousands of federal properties. In accordance with <a href="/wiki/Title_40_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 40 of the United States Code">Title 40 of the United States Code</a>, GSA is charged with promulgating regulations governing the acquisition, use, and disposal of real property (real estate and land) and personal property (essentially all other property).  This activity is centered in GSA's Office of Governmentwide Policy.  Policies promulgated by GSA are developed in collaboration with federal agencies, and are typically published for public comment in the Federal Register prior to publication as a Final Rule.
</p><p>The Public Buildings Service provides workplaces for federal customer agencies and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_courthouse" title="United States courthouse">United States courthouses</a> at good economies to the American taxpayer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-32">[32]</a></sup>  PBS is funded primarily through the Federal Buildings Fund, which is supported by rent from federal customer agencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Disposal of surplus <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">real property</a> is managed by the Office of Property Disposal within the Public Buildings Service.  The Office is responsible for property which includes land, office buildings, warehouses, former post offices, farms, family residences, commercial facilities, or airfields located in the United States, Puerto Rico, the U.S. Virgin Islands, or the U.S. Pacific Territories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-note-0_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-note-0-33">[33]</a></sup> Surplus property is made available to both government and private bidders and, in some cases, land sold for public purposes (such as parks or welfare) may be made available for a discount of up to 100% of the fair-market value.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-note-0_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-note-0-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>GSA has earned a <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a> rating for 24 <a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">green buildings</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Some of green offerings at new buildings includes <a href="/wiki/Green_roof" title="Green roof">green roofs</a> (planted roofs that can substantially reduce rainwater run-off during storms and provide significant insulation for the buildings),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Underfloor_air_distribution" title="Underfloor air distribution">underfloor air distribution</a> (that delivers cooling and heating air at floor level instead of from the ceiling),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> purchasing and using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewable_power" title="Renewable power">renewable power</a> from utility companies, and light shelves (located outside of the building that reduce the amount of heat radiating into the building from the sun while increasing the amount of natural light and high ceilings that help direct daylight deep into the work environment).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gsa.gov_2_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-gsa.gov_2-37">[37]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act_of_2009" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009">American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009</a> made available not less than $4.5 billion for measures necessary to convert GSA facilities to High-Performance Green Buildings, as defined in the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Independence_and_Security_Act_of_2007" title="Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007">Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Law_110-140" title="Public Law 110-140">Public Law 110-140</a>).
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">Department of Energy's</a> <a href="/wiki/Federal_Energy_Management_Program" title="Federal Energy Management Program">Federal Energy Management Program</a> facilitates to GSA the implementation, through <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Project_transaction&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Project transaction (page does not exist)">project transaction</a> services, applied technology services and decision support services, to deploy renewable energy technologies and cultivate change to embrace energy efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, GSA was presented with the <a href="/wiki/Honor_Award" title="Honor Award">Honor Award</a> from the <a href="/wiki/National_Building_Museum" title="National Building Museum">National Building Museum</a> for "success in creating and maintaining innovative environments for the federal community as well as providing a positive federal presence for the public."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_vehicle_fleet_management">Federal vehicle fleet management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Federal vehicle fleet management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Ambox current red.svg" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="360" height="34" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" width="42"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article's <b><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">factual accuracy</a> may be compromised due to out-of-date information</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span> <small><i>(November 2010)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_official_vehicles_of_the_President_of_the_United_States" title="List of official vehicles of the President of the United States">List of official vehicles of the President of the United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fleet_vehicle" title="Fleet vehicle">fleet vehicle</a></div>
<p>GSA contributes to the management of U.S. Federal property, including a 215,000 vehicle <a href="/wiki/Fleet_vehicle" title="Fleet vehicle">motor pool</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act_of_2009" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009">American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009</a> (stimulus bill) included $300 million to acquire energy-efficient motor vehicles for the federal fleet.  President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> announced that GSA was to support the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._auto_industry" title="U.S. auto industry">U.S. auto industry</a> with orders for about 17,600 new <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_automobiles" title="Fuel economy in automobiles">fuel-efficient vehicles</a> by June 1, 2009, on an accelerated schedule, with money from the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act. GSA was to pay $285 million to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Motors_Corporation" title="General Motors Corporation">General Motors Corporation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chrysler_LLC" title="Chrysler LLC">Chrysler LLC</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a>. It was to include 2,500 <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">hybrid sedans</a>—the largest one–time purchase yet of hybrid vehicles for the federal government—and each new vehicle was claimed to yield at least a 10% fuel economy improvement over its predecessor. GSA was to spend $15 million more that year on a pilot fleet of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advanced-technology_vehicle" title="Advanced-technology vehicle">advanced-technology vehicles</a>, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All-electric_vehicle" title="All-electric vehicle">all-electric vehicles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_bus" title="Hybrid electric bus">hybrid buses</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hybrids accounted for about 10 percent of the 145,473 vehicles the U.S. General Services Administration bought during the fiscal years 2009 and 2010, after making up less than 1 percent of government vehicle purchases in 2008. As for specific models, Obama took a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buy-American_Restrictions" title="Buy-American Restrictions">buy-American</a> stance. The U.S. government bought about two-thirds of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Malibu_Hybrid" title="Chevrolet Malibu Hybrid">Chevrolet Malibu Hybrids</a> sold during the past two years, and almost a third of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fusion_Hybrid" title="Ford Fusion Hybrid">Ford Fusion Hybrids</a>, but only 17 <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Prius" title="Toyota Prius">Toyota Prius</a> hybrids and five <a href="/wiki/Honda_Civic_Hybrid" title="Honda Civic Hybrid">Honda Civic Hybrids</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ground_Force_One" title="Ground Force One">Ground Force One</a>, so designated when transporting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/POTUS" title="POTUS">POTUS</a>, is one of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armored_bus" title="Armored bus">armored buses</a> procured in 2010 for the transportation of dignitaries under protection of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secret_Service" title="United States Secret Service">Secret Service</a>, at a cost of $1.1 million each. The coaches were assembled in <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> on frames made in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interagency_Resources_Management_Conference">Interagency Resources Management Conference</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Interagency Resources Management Conference">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Advert" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="40" data-file-width="40" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article <b>contains content that is written like <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:What_Wikipedia_is_not#Wikipedia_is_not_a_soapbox_or_means_of_promotion" title="Wikipedia:What Wikipedia is not">an advertisement</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit">improve it</a> by removing <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Spam" title="Wikipedia:Spam">promotional content</a> and inappropriate <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:External_links" title="Wikipedia:External links">external links</a>, and by adding encyclopedic content written from a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">neutral point of view</a>.</span> <small><i>(March 2013)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The Interagency Resources Management Conference (IRMCO) was a federal executive conference of the General Services Administration, hosting about 300 federal and industry leaders each year. The Interagency Resources Management Conference began in 1961 as the ADPCO conference.  In 1979, the Department of Commerce, GSA and the Office of Management and Budget (OMB) jointly sponsored a conference for Senior Executive Service (SES) officials at Gettysburg, Pennsylvania.  At the same time, the National Archives hosted a small records management conference for senior executives, also located in Gettysburg. These two conferences merged with ADPCO and became The Interagency Resources Management Conference. Over the years, the conference has evolved its focus from highly specialized to integrated.  In 1996, when U.S. Congress mandated the role of Chief Information Officer (CIO), these new federal executives were invited to attend The Interagency Resources Management Conference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Interagency Resources Management Conference was the government's primary senior executive conference when it was held as an offsite retreat for leaders from across government. Originally, industry participation was managed by a division of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i>, Post-Newsweek Tech Media, and from 1999 to 2008, Post-Newsweek, with the assistance of a small, woman-owned business, Hosky Communications Inc., developed a strong following from the SES community for the event, on average generating 3-400 attendees with a 3:1 government to industry ratio. GSA leadership crafted conference content that directly addressed the Administration's goals throughout those years. Industry showcase demonstrated the roles that partners play in executing cost-cutting technology <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solutions</a> for government.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In 2008, Hosky was awarded a competitive contract to continue to manage and develop the forum.  From 2008 to 2010, IRMCO drew attendees from about 65 federal agencies and diverse disciplines including information technology, human resources, acquisition, management and finance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>Once travel restrictions and budget concerns surfaced in late 2010, IRMCO was moved to a local venue under the management services of A-S-K Associates, where primary attendance by industry was established as a means to inform commercial firms on GSA policies. IRMCO 2011 was held in Washington, D.C., at the Kellogg Conference Center and Hotel on the campus of <a href="/wiki/Gallaudet_University" title="Gallaudet University">Gallaudet University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Shortly after IRMCO 2011, GSA's Associate Administrator for Governmentwide Policy, Kathleen Turco, announced to media that she and other GSA officials felt that IRMCO had lost its spark and retired the event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_Transformation_Services">Technology Transformation Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Technology Transformation Services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article appears to <b>contain a large number of <a href="/wiki/Buzzword" title="Buzzword">buzzwords</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> There might be a discussion about this on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:General_Services_Administration" title="Talk:General Services Administration">talk page</a>. Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> if you can.</span> <small><i>(August 2018)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Beginning with the Federal Citizen Information Center in 1970, GSA has had a long history of connecting the public to government information and services. In 2009, a new Office of Citizen Services and Innovative Technologies was created to expand the effort to serve the public through technology. GSA began managing the Presidential Innovation Fellows program the same year it launched 18F, a first of its kind startup within the agency, launched in 2014 with a team of 15 designers, engineers, and product specialists focused on improving the federal government’s digital services.
</p><p>Now all of those offices have joined forces under the Technology Transformation Services sub-unit of the Federal Acquisition Service. Its mission is to improve the public’s experience with the government by helping federal and state agencies build, buy, and share technology that allows them to better serve the public. Joanne Collins Smee is the director of the Technology Transformation Services and a deputy commissioner to Alan Thomas of the Federal Acquisition Service. The Centers of Excellence, introduced in October 2017, are working to accelerate the modernization of IT infrastructure and reduce legacy IT spending across government.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Section_1122_Program">Section 1122 Program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Services_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Section 1122 Program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2009, Section 1122 of the FY 1994 National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 2009 was added. This expanded the program from buying counter-drug materials and equipment at a discount to <a href="/wiki/Homeland_security" title="Homeland security">homeland security</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emergency_response" title="Emergency response">emergency response</a> operations. Besides GSA, the Department of Defense (DOD) and the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Logistics_Agency" title="Defense Logistics Agency">Defense Logistics Agency</a> (DLA) provide equipment, as in the <a href="/wiki/1033_program" title="1033 program">1033 program</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1122pr_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-1122pr-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
