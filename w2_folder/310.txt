<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lawful permanent residents (United States)</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:2017-us-green-card-specimen.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="602" data-file-width="430" height="308" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/2017-us-green-card-specimen.png/220px-2017-us-green-card-specimen.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/2017-us-green-card-specimen.png/330px-2017-us-green-card-specimen.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2c/2017-us-green-card-specimen.png 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:2017-us-green-card-specimen.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sample of a <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> "permanent resident card" (2017)</div></div></div>
<p><b>Lawful permanent residents</b>, also known as <b>legal permanent residents</b>, and informally known as <b>green card holders</b>, are a special <a href="/wiki/Class_(knowledge_representation)" title="Class (knowledge representation)">class</a> of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/immigrant#English" title="wikt:immigrant">immigrants</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_and_Nationality_Act" title="Immigration and Nationality Act">Immigration and Nationality Act</a> (INA),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPR_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-LPR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-section_1101(a)(20)_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-section_1101(a)(20)-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matter_of_J-H-J-_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Matter_of_J-H-J--3">[3]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_right#United_States" title="Constitutional right">rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Administration_of_federal_assistance_in_the_United_States" title="Administration of federal assistance in the United States">benefits</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Social_privilege" title="Social privilege">privileges</a> to <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/reside#English" title="wikt:reside">reside</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> permanently or until they are <a href="/wiki/Deportation" title="Deportation">deported</a> (removed) from the country pursuant to applicable provisions of the INA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-removable_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-removable-4">[4]</a></sup> There are an estimated 13.2 million lawful permanent residents of whom 8.9 million are eligible for <a href="/wiki/Citizenship_of_the_United_States" title="Citizenship of the United States">citizenship of the United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mejia_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mejia-6">[6]</a></sup> Approximately 65,000 of them serve in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">U.S. armed forces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowd_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dowd-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lawful permanent residents are entitled to U.S. citizenship after showing by a <a href="/wiki/Burden_of_proof_(law)#Preponderance_of_the_evidence" title="Burden of proof (law)">preponderance of the evidence</a> that they, <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_Latin_phrases_(I)#inter_alia" title="List of Latin phrases (I)">inter alia</a></i>, have continuously resided in the United States for at least five years and are persons of <a href="/wiki/Good_moral_character" title="Good moral character">good moral character</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-section_1427_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-section_1427-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-national_of_the_United_States_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-national_of_the_United_States-9">[9]</a></sup> Those who are under the age of 18 years automatically derive <a href="/wiki/United_States_nationality_law" title="United States nationality law">U.S. nationality</a> and/or citizenship through at least one of their <a href="/wiki/Americans" title="Americans">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Parent" title="Parent">parents</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-national_and_citizen_of_the_United_States_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-national_and_citizen_of_the_United_States-10">[10]</a></sup> Like "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nationals_but_not_citizens_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nationals but not citizens of the United States (page does not exist)">nationals but not citizens of the United States</a>," the immigrants who were originally "admitted to the United States" as <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugees</a> are statutorily <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/exempt#English" title="wikt:exempt">exempt</a> from <a href="/wiki/Non-refoulement" title="Non-refoulement">deportation</a> for <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lifetime#English" title="wikt:lifetime">lifetime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matter_of_J-H-J-_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Matter_of_J-H-J--3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nguyen_v._Chertoff_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nguyen_v._Chertoff-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deprivation_Of_Rights_Under_Color_Of_Law_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Deprivation_Of_Rights_Under_Color_Of_Law-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sections_241-249_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-sections_241-249-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAT_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-CAT-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CQ_Press_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-CQ_Press-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_v._Mendoza-Martinez_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy_v._Mendoza-Martinez-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60_FR_7885_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-60_FR_7885-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Every lawful permanent resident (LPR) is issued by the U.S. government a "permanent resident card," which is commonly known as a "green card" because of its historical <a href="/wiki/Shades_of_green" title="Shades of green">greenish</a> color.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> It was formerly called "alien registration card" or "alien registration receipt card."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campos_v._United_States_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Campos_v._United_States-20">[20]</a></sup> The permanent resident card serves as proof that its holder is a legal immigrant, with constitutional rights to work and reside in the United States similar to that of all other <a href="/wiki/Americans" title="Americans">Americans</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deprivation_Of_Rights_Under_Color_Of_Law_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Deprivation_Of_Rights_Under_Color_Of_Law-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sections_241-249_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-sections_241-249-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60_FR_7885_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-60_FR_7885-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAT_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-CAT-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CQ_Press_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-CQ_Press-15">[15]</a></sup> It may be used to obtain a <a href="/wiki/Real_ID_Act#Analysis_of_the_law" title="Real ID Act">State ID card and/or a driver's license</a>. Absent <a href="/wiki/Exceptional_circumstances#United_States" title="Exceptional circumstances">exceptional circumstances</a>, immigrants who are 18 years of age or older could spend up to 30 days in jail for not carrying their green cards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campos_v._United_States_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Campos_v._United_States-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Applications for permanent resident cards are decided by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Citizenship_and_Immigration_Services" title="United States Citizenship and Immigration Services">United States Citizenship and Immigration Services</a> (USCIS), but in some cases an <a href="/wiki/Immigration_Judge_(United_States)" title="Immigration Judge (United States)">immigration judge</a> or a member of the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Immigration_Appeals" title="Board of Immigration Appeals">Board of Immigration Appeals</a> (BIA), acting on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney_General" title="United States Attorney General">U.S. Attorney General</a>, may grant permanent residency in the course of <a href="/wiki/Removal_proceedings" title="Removal proceedings">removal proceedings</a>. A <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_judge" title="United States federal judge">federal judge</a> may also do the same in particular case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-injunctive_relief_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-injunctive_relief-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OriginalGreenCard.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="585" data-file-width="438" height="294" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/OriginalGreenCard.jpg/220px-OriginalGreenCard.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/OriginalGreenCard.jpg/330px-OriginalGreenCard.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4d/OriginalGreenCard.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OriginalGreenCard.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1949 "alien registration receipt card" of a female <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/immigrant#English" title="wikt:immigrant">immigrant</a>, which was issued by the now-abolished <a href="/wiki/Immigration_and_Naturalization_Service" title="Immigration and Naturalization Service">Immigration and Naturalization Service</a> (INS) under the <a href="/wiki/Nationality_Act_of_1940" title="Nationality Act of 1940">Nationality Act of 1940</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lennon%27s_Green_Card.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2550" data-file-width="3699" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Lennon%27s_Green_Card.jpg/220px-Lennon%27s_Green_Card.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Lennon%27s_Green_Card.jpg/330px-Lennon%27s_Green_Card.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Lennon%27s_Green_Card.jpg/440px-Lennon%27s_Green_Card.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lennon%27s_Green_Card.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1976 card issued by the INS to <a href="/wiki/John_Lennon" title="John Lennon">John Lennon</a>, stating the following: "This is to certify that [Lennon] has been duly registered according to the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_and_Nationality_Act" title="Immigration and Nationality Act">law</a> and was admitted to the United States as an immigrant."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-section_1101(a)(20)_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-section_1101(a)(20)-2">[2]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The INA, which was enacted by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">U.S. Congress</a> in 1952, states that "[t]he term '<a href="/wiki/Alien_(law)#United_States" title="Alien (law)">alien</a>' means any person not a <a href="/wiki/Citizenship_of_the_United_States" title="Citizenship of the United States">citizen</a> <b>or</b> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_of_the_United_States" title="National of the United States">national of the United States</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alien_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-alien-24">[24]</a></sup> An "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/immigrant#English" title="wikt:immigrant">immigrant</a>" can either be an "alien" or a "national of the United States," which requires a case-by-case analysis and depends mainly on the number of continuous years he or she has spent in the United States as an LPR (green card holder).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-national_of_the_United_States_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-national_of_the_United_States-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPR_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-LPR-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahmadi_v._Att'y_Gen._25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ahmadi_v._Att'y_Gen.-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Supreme_Court_cases,_volume_459" title="List of United States Supreme Court cases, volume 459">Landon v. Plasencia</a></i>, <a class="external text" href="https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/459/21/" rel="nofollow">459 U.S. 21</a>, 32 (1982), the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">U.S. Supreme Court</a> reminded the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney_General" title="United States Attorney General">U.S. Attorney General</a> that "once an alien gains admission to our country and begins to develop the ties that go with permanent residence, his <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">constitutional</a> status changes accordingly." That opinion was issued after Congress and the <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Ronald_Reagan" title="Presidency of Ronald Reagan">Reagan administration</a> brought into the United States large number of <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugee</a> families from countries experiencing wars and <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocides</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Deportation_of_Afghan_refugees_from_the_United_States" title="Deportation of Afghan refugees from the United States">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deportation_of_Cambodian_refugees_from_the_United_States" title="Deportation of Cambodian refugees from the United States">Cambodia</a>, El Salvadore, Liberia, Palestine, Vietnam, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refugee_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-refugee-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matter_of_J-H-J-_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Matter_of_J-H-J--3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ahmadi_v._Att'y_Gen._25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ahmadi_v._Att'y_Gen.-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nguyen_v._Chertoff_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nguyen_v._Chertoff-11">[11]</a></sup> As legal immigrants,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60_FR_7885_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-60_FR_7885-17">[17]</a></sup> these refugee families lost their former nationalities and gradually became nationals and citizens of the United States (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Americans" title="Americans">Americans</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alien_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-alien-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-section_1427_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-section_1427-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Expansion_of_the_definition_of_.22nationals_but_not_citizens_of_the_United_States.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='Expansion_of_the_definition_of_"nationals_but_not_citizens_of_the_United_States"'>Expansion of the definition of "nationals but not citizens of the United States"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title='Edit section: Expansion of the definition of "nationals but not citizens of the United States"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Americans" title="Americans">Americans</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_nationality_law" title="United States nationality law">United States nationality law</a></div><p>
In 1986, less than a year before the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_against_Torture" title="United Nations Convention against Torture">United Nations Convention against Torture</a> (CAT) became effective, Congress expressly and intentionally expanded the definition of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_of_the_United_States" title="National of the United States">national of the United States</a>" by adding paragraph (4) to 8 U.S.C. § 1408, which reads as follows:</p><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Unless otherwise provided in section <a class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/8/1401" rel="nofollow">1401</a> of this title, the following <i>shall</i> be <b>nationals, but not citizens, of the United States at birth</b>: .... (4) <b>A person</b> born outside the United States <b>and</b> its <a href="/wiki/Territories_of_the_United_States" title="Territories of the United States">outlying possessions</a> of parents one of whom is an <a href="/wiki/Alien_(law)#United_States" title="Alien (law)">alien</a>, and the other <b>a national, but not a citizen, of the United States who</b>, prior to the birth of such person, <b>was physically present in the United States or its outlying possessions</b> for a period or periods <b>totaling not less than seven years</b> <u>in any continuous period of ten years</u>—(A) <b>during which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_of_the_United_States" title="National of the United States">national</a> <a href="/wiki/Parent" title="Parent">parent</a></b> was not outside the United States or its outlying possessions for a continuous period of more than one year, <b>and (B) at least five years of which were after attaining the age of fourteen</b> years. (emphasis added).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alabama_v._Bozeman_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alabama_v._Bozeman-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote><p>
The natural reading of § 1408(4) demonstrates that it was not exclusively written for the 55,000 <a href="/wiki/American_Samoa" title="American Samoa">American Samoans</a> but for all people who statutorily and manifestly qualify as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nationals_but_not_citizens_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nationals but not citizens of the United States (page does not exist)">nationals but not citizens of the United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alabama_v._Bozeman_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Alabama_v._Bozeman-30">[30]</a></sup> An LPR who was admitted to the United States as a refugee, especially if he or she was a child under the age of 18, has identical <a href="/wiki/Rights" title="Rights">rights</a> as a <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_but_a_citizen_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National but a citizen of the United States (page does not exist)">national but a citizen of the United States</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refugee_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-refugee-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-national_of_the_United_States_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-national_of_the_United_States-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deprivation_Of_Rights_Under_Color_Of_Law_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Deprivation_Of_Rights_Under_Color_Of_Law-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sections_241-249_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-sections_241-249-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAT_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-CAT-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CQ_Press_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-CQ_Press-15">[15]</a></sup> Such a person can proudly say to everyone that he or she "is not an alien at all but is actually a national of the United States."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> "Deprivation of <a href="/wiki/Nationality" title="Nationality">[nationality]</a>—particularly <a href="/wiki/United_States_nationality_law" title="United States nationality law">American [nationality]</a>, which is one of the most valuable rights in the world today—has grave practical consequences."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_v._Mendoza-Martinez_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy_v._Mendoza-Martinez-16">[16]</a></sup> Congress has long warned every government employee by stating the following:</p><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Whoever, <a href="/wiki/Color_(law)" title="Color (law)">under color of any law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Local_ordinance" title="Local ordinance">ordinance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulation</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Custom_(law)" title="Custom (law)">custom</a>, willfully subjects <b>any person</b> in any State, Territory, or District to the deprivation of any <a href="/wiki/Rights" title="Rights">rights</a>, privileges, or immunities secured or protected by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">Constitution</a> or <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_United_States" title="Law of the United States">laws</a> of the United States, or to different <a href="/wiki/Punishment" title="Punishment">punishments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suffering" title="Suffering">pains</a>, or penalties, <b>on account of such person being an <a href="/wiki/Alien_(law)#United_States" title="Alien (law)">alien</a></b>, or by reason of his <a href="/wiki/Race_and_ethnicity_in_the_United_States" title="Race and ethnicity in the United States">color</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Race_and_ethnicity_in_the_United_States" title="Race and ethnicity in the United States">race</a>, than are prescribed for the punishment of citizens, shall be subject to specified criminal penalties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alabama_v._Bozeman_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-Alabama_v._Bozeman-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote><p>
In February 1995, U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> issued a directive in which he stated the following: </p><link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Our efforts to combat <a href="/wiki/Illegal_immigration_to_the_United_States" title="Illegal immigration to the United States">illegal immigration</a> must not violate the <a href="/wiki/Civil_and_political_rights" title="Civil and political rights">privacy and civil rights</a> of <b>legal immigrants</b> and U.S. citizens. Therefore, I direct the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney_General" title="United States Attorney General">Attorney General</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Secretary of Health and Human Services">Secretary of Health and Human Services</a>, the Chair of the <a href="/wiki/Equal_Employment_Opportunity_Commission" title="Equal Employment Opportunity Commission">Equal Employment Opportunity Commission</a>, and other relevant <a href="/wiki/List_of_courts_of_the_United_States" title="List of courts of the United States">Administration officials</a> to vigorously protect our citizens and <b>legal immigrants</b> from immigration-related instances of <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harassment" title="Harassment">harassment</a>. All illegal immigration enforcement measures <i>shall</i> be taken with due regard for the basic <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> of individuals and in accordance with our obligations under applicable <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">international agreements</a>. (emphasis added).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60_FR_7885_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-60_FR_7885-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alabama_v._Bozeman_30-3"><a href="#cite_note-Alabama_v._Bozeman-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:US_Permanent_Resident_Card_2010-05-11.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="362" data-file-width="275" height="290" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/US_Permanent_Resident_Card_2010-05-11.JPG/220px-US_Permanent_Resident_Card_2010-05-11.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3a/US_Permanent_Resident_Card_2010-05-11.JPG 1.5x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:US_Permanent_Resident_Card_2010-05-11.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Permanent resident card (2010)</div></div></div>
<p>Today, there are approximately 13.2 million legal immigrants of whom 8.9 million are "eligible to naturalize."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mejia_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mejia-6">[6]</a></sup> An LPR can secure many types of federal jobs just like a citizen can. For example, about 65,000 members of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">U.S. armed forces</a> are LPRs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dowd_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dowd-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>An LPR can lose the right to become a U.S. citizen and can even get <a href="/wiki/Deportation" title="Deportation">deported</a> from the country upon getting convicted of an <a href="/wiki/Aggravated_felony" title="Aggravated felony">aggravated felony</a>, which is generally recognized as a <a href="/wiki/Felony" title="Felony">particularly serious crime</a>. A conviction for some lessor offenses may also trigger deportation for LPRs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-removable_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-removable-4">[4]</a></sup> except those that were admitted to the United States as <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugees</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matter_of_J-H-J-_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Matter_of_J-H-J--3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-refugee_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-refugee-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nguyen_v._Chertoff_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nguyen_v._Chertoff-11">[11]</a></sup> These specific immigrants have already experienced <a href="/wiki/Genocide" title="Genocide">genocide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Persecution" title="Persecution">persecution</a> in the past, and they have no country of <a href="/wiki/Permanent_residency" title="Permanent residency">permanent residence</a> other than the United States. Deporting them from the United States amounts to a very serious international <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Deprivation_Of_Rights_Under_Color_Of_Law_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Deprivation_Of_Rights_Under_Color_Of_Law-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sections_241-249_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-sections_241-249-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAT_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-CAT-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CQ_Press_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-CQ_Press-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kennedy_v._Mendoza-Martinez_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy_v._Mendoza-Martinez-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60_FR_7885_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-60_FR_7885-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>LPRs are also subject to similar obligations as U.S. citizens. Male LPRs between the ages of 18 and 25 are subject to registering in the <a href="/wiki/Selective_Service_System" title="Selective Service System">Selective Service System</a>. LPRs living in the United States must pay <a href="/wiki/Taxation_in_the_United_States" title="Taxation in the United States">taxes</a> on their worldwide income (this includes filing annual U.S. income tax returns), like U.S. citizens. LPRs are not allowed to vote and they cannot be elected to federal or state offices.
</p><p>Like U.S. citizens, LPRs can petition (sponsor) certain family members to immigrate to the United States, but the number of family members of LPRs who can immigrate is limited by an annual cap, and there is a years-long backlog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>An LPR can file <a href="/wiki/Form_N-400" title="Form N-400">Form N-400</a> ("application for naturalization") after five years of continuous residency in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> This period may be shortened to three years if married to a U.S. citizen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> An LPR may submit his or her applications for naturalization as early as 90 days before meeting the residency requirement. In addition to continuous residency, the applicants must demonstrate good moral character, pass an English test and a civics test, and demonstrate attachment to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_States" title="Constitution of the United States">U.S. Constitution</a>. In the summer of 2018, a new program was initiated to help LPRs prepare themselves for naturalization.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_immigration">Types of immigration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types of immigration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_the_United_States" title="Immigration to the United States">Immigration to the United States</a></div>
<p>The INA stipulates that a person may obtain permanent resident status primarily through the course of the following proceedings:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>immigration through a family member</li>
<li>immigration through employment</li>
<li>immigration through investment (from 0.5 to 1 million US dollars)</li>
<li>immigration through the <a href="/wiki/Diversity_Immigrant_Visa" title="Diversity Immigrant Visa">Diversity Lottery</a></li>
<li>immigration through refugee or asylum status</li>
<li>immigration through "The Registry" provisions of the Immigration and Nationality Act</li>
<li>immigration approved by the Director of Central Intelligence.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_process">Application process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Application process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Form_I-140" title="Form I-140">Form I-140</a></div>
<p>Applications for permanent resident cards (green cards) were decided by the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_and_Naturalization_Service" title="Immigration and Naturalization Service">Immigration and Naturalization Service</a> (INS) until 2003 when the INS was abolished and replaced by the current <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Homeland_Security" title="United States Department of Homeland Security">Department of Homeland Security</a> (DHS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The whole process may take several years, depending on the type of immigrant category and the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_state_of_chargeability" title="Foreign state of chargeability">country of chargeability</a>. An immigrant usually has to go through a three-step process to get permanent residency:
</p>
<ol><li><b>Immigrant petition</b> (<a href="/wiki/Form_I-140" title="Form I-140">Form I-140</a> or Form I-130) – in the first step, USCIS approves the immigrant petition by a qualifying relative, an employer, or in rare cases, such as with an investor visa, the applicant himself. If a sibling is applying, she or he must have the same parents as the applicant.</li>
<li><b>Immigrant visa availability</b> – in the second step, unless the applicant is an "immediate relative", an immigrant visa number through the National Visa Center (NVC)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">United States Department of State</a> (DOS) must be available. A visa number might not be immediately available even if the USCIS approves the petition, because the number of immigrant visa numbers is limited every year by quotas set in the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_and_Nationality_Act_of_1965" title="Immigration and Nationality Act of 1965">Immigration and Nationality Act</a> (INA). There are also certain additional limitations by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Country_of_chargeability" title="Country of chargeability">country of chargeability</a>.  Thus, most immigrants will be placed on lengthy waiting lists. Those immigrants who are immediate relatives of a U.S. citizen (spouses and children under 21 years of age, and parents of a U.S. citizen who is 21 years of age or older) are not subject to these quotas and may proceed to the next step immediately (since they qualify for the IR immigrant category).</li>
<li><b>Immigrant visa adjudication</b> – in the third step, when an immigrant visa number becomes available, the applicant must either apply with USCIS to adjust their current status to permanent resident status or apply with the DOS for an immigrant visa at the nearest U.S. consulate before being allowed to come to the United States.
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Adjustment_of_status" title="Adjustment of status">Adjustment of status</a> (AOS)</b> – Adjustment of status is for when the immigrant is in the United States and entered the U.S. legally. Except for immediate relatives of U.S. citizens, the immigrant must also be in legal status at the time of applying for adjustment of status. For immediate relatives and other relative categories whose visa numbers are current, adjustment of status can be filed for at the same time with the petition (step 1 above). Adjustment of status is submitted to USCIS via form I-485, <i>Application to Register Permanent Residence or Adjust Status</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The USCIS conducts a series of background checks (including fingerprinting for FBI criminal background check and name checks) and makes a decision on the application. Once the adjustment of status application is accepted, the alien is allowed to stay in the United States even if the original period of authorized stay on the <a href="/wiki/Form_I-94" title="Form I-94">Form I-94</a> is expired, but he/she is generally not allowed to leave the country until the application is approved, or the application will be abandoned. If the alien has to leave the United States during this time, he/she can apply for travel documents at the USCIS with form I-131, also called <i>Advance parole</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> If there is a potential risk that the applicant's work permit (visa) will expire or become invalid (laid off by the employer and visa sponsor) or the applicant wants to start working in the United States, while he/she is waiting for the decision about his/her application to change status, he/she can file form I-765, to get <i>Employment Authorization Documents</i> (also called <i>EAD</i>) and be able to continue or start working legally in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> In some cases, the applicant will be interviewed at a USCIS office, especially if it is a marriage-based adjustment from a <a href="/wiki/K-1_visa" title="K-1 visa">K-1 visa</a>, in which case both spouses (the US citizen and the applicant) will be interviewed by the USCIS. If the application is approved, the alien becomes an LPR, and the actual green card is mailed to the alien's last known mailing address.</li>
<li><b>Consular processing</b> – This is the process if the immigrant is outside the United States, or is ineligible for AOS. It still requires the immigrant visa petition to be first completed and approved. The applicant may make an appointment at the U.S. embassy or consulate in his/her home country, where a consular officer adjudicates the case. If the case is approved, an immigrant visa is issued by the U.S. embassy or consulate. The visa entitles the holder to travel to the United States as an immigrant. At the port of entry, the immigrant visa holder immediately becomes a permanent resident, and is processed for a permanent resident card and receives an I-551 stamp in his/her passport. The permanent resident card is mailed to his/her U.S. address within several weeks.</li></ul></li></ol>
<p>An applicant (alien) in the United States can obtain two permits while the case is pending after a certain stage is passed in green card processing (filing of I-485). 
</p>
<ul><li>The first is a temporary work permit known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Employment_Authorization_Document" title="Employment Authorization Document">Employment Authorization Document</a> (EAD), which allows the alien to take employment in the United States.</li>
<li>The second is a temporary travel document, <a href="/wiki/Advance_parole" title="Advance parole">advance parole</a>, which allows the alien to re-enter the United States. Both permits confer benefits that are independent of any existing status granted to the alien. For example, the alien might already have permission to work in the United States under an <a href="/wiki/H-1B_visa" title="H-1B visa">H-1B visa</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_process_for_family-sponsored_visa_for_both_parents_and_for_children">Application process for family-sponsored visa for both parents and for children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Application process for family-sponsored visa for both parents and for children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>U.S. citizens may sponsor for permanent residence in the United States the following relatives:
</p>
<ol><li>Spouses, and unmarried children under the age of 21;</li>
<li>Parents (once the U.S. citizen is at least 21 years old);</li>
<li>Unmarried children over the age of 21 (called "sons and daughters");</li>
<li>Married sons and daughters;</li>
<li>Brothers and sisters (once the U.S. citizen is at least 21 years old).</li></ol>
<p>U.S. permanent residents may sponsor for permanent residence in the United States the following relatives:
</p>
<ol><li>Spouses, and unmarried children under the age of 21;</li>
<li>Unmarried children over the age of 21 (called "sons and daughters");</li></ol>
<p>The Department of State's "Visa Bulletin," issued every month, gives the priority date for those petition beneficiaries currently entitled to apply for immigrant status through immigrant visas or adjustment of status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> There is no annual quota for the spouses, unmarried children, and parents of U.S. citizens, so there is no waiting period for these applicants—just the required processing time.  However, all other family-based categories have significant backlogs, even with a U.S. citizen petitioner.
</p><p>Regardless of whether the family member being sponsored is located in the United States (and therefore likely to be applying for adjustment of status) or outside the United States (in which case the immigrant visa is the likely option), the process begins with the filing of an I-130 Petition for Alien Relative. The form and instructions can be found on the U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services website.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Required later in the process will be additional biographic data regarding the beneficiary (the person being sponsored) and a medical examination.  Additional documents, such as police certificates, may be required depending on whether immigrant visa (consular processing) or adjustment of status is being utilized.  In the case of consular processing outside the United States one should ensure one is up-to-date with the particular practices of the relevant US embassy or consulate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> All petitioners must supply the I-864 Affidavit of Support.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Green-card_holders_and_families">Green-card holders and families</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Green-card holders and families">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg"><img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>Green-card holders married to non-U.S. citizens are able to legally bring their spouses and minor children to join them in the USA,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> but must wait for their priority date to become current. The foreign spouse of a green-card holder must wait for approval of an "immigrant visa" from the State Department before entering the United States. Due to numerical limitation on the number of these visas, the wait time for approval may be months or years. In the interim, the spouse cannot be legally present in the United States, unless he or she secures a visa by some other means. Green-card holders may opt to wait to become U.S. citizens, and only then sponsor their spouses and children, as the process is much faster for U.S. citizens. However, many green-card holders can choose to apply for the spouse or children and update their application after becoming a U.S. citizen.
</p><p>The issue of U.S. green-card holders separated from their families for years is not a new problem. A mechanism to unite families of green-card holders was created by the LIFE Act by the introduction of a "<a href="/wiki/V_visa" title="V visa">V visa</a>", signed into law by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">President Clinton</a>. The law expired on December 31, 2000, and V visas are no longer available. From time to time, bills are introduced in Congress to reinstate V visas, but so far none have been successful.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Improving_the_application_process_in_obtaining_a_green_card">Improving the application process in obtaining a green card</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Improving the application process in obtaining a green card">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The most common challenges that USCIS faces in providing services in the green card process are: (1) the length of the application and approval process, and (2) the quotas of green cards granted. USCIS tries to shorten the time qualified applicants wait to receive permanent residence.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges_with_processing_time_of_application">Challenges with processing time of application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Challenges with processing time of application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Under the current system, immediate family members (spouse, child, and dependent mother and father), have priority status for green cards and generally wait 6 months to a year to have their green card application approved. For non-immediate family members, the process may take up to 10 years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Paperwork is processed on a first-come, first-served basis, so new applications may go untouched for several months. To address the issue of slow processing times, USCIS has made a policy allowing applicants to submit the I-130 and I-485 forms at the same time. This has reduced the processing time. Another delay in the process comes when applications have mistakes. In these cases papers are sent back to the applicant, further delaying the process. Currently the largest issue creating long wait times is not processing time, but rather immigrant visa quotas set by Congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Quota_system_challenges">Quota system challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Quota system challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Long wait times are a symptom of another issue—quotas preventing immigrants from receiving immigrant visas. Georgia's <i>Augusta Chronicle</i> in 2006 stated that an estimated two million people are on waiting lists in anticipation to become legal and permanent residents of the United States. Immigrants need visas to get off of these waiting lists, and Congress would need to change immigration law in order to accommodate them with legal status.
</p><p>The number of green cards that can be granted to family-based applicants depends on what preference category they fall under. An unlimited number of immediate relatives can receive green cards because there is no quota for that category. Family members who fall under the other various preference categories have fixed quotas, however the number of visas issued from each category may vary because unused visas from one category may rollover into another category.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_process_for_employment-based_visa">Application process for employment-based visa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Application process for employment-based visa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many immigrants opt for this route, which typically requires an employer to "sponsor" (i.e. to petition before USCIS) the immigrant (known as the <i>alien beneficiary</i>) through a presumed future job (in some special categories, the applicant may apply on his/her behalf without a sponsor). The three-step process outlined above is described here in more detail for employment-based immigration applications. After the process is complete, the alien is expected to take the certified job offered by the employer to substantiate his or her immigrant status, since the application ultimately rests on the alien's employment with that company in that particular position.
</p>
<ol><li><b>Immigrant petition</b> – the first step includes the pre-requisite <a href="/wiki/Labor_certification" title="Labor certification">labor certification</a> upon which the actual petition will reside.
<ul><li><b>Labor certification</b> – the employer must legally prove that it has a need to hire an alien for a specific position and that there is no minimally qualified U.S. citizen or LPR available to fill that position, hence the reason for hiring the alien. Some of the requirements to prove this situation include: proof of advertising for the specific position; skill requirements particular to the job; verification of the prevailing wage for a position; and the employer's ability to pay. This is currently done through an electronic system known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PERM,_labor_certification" title="PERM, labor certification">PERM</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>  The date when the labor certification application is filed becomes the applicant's <i><a href="/wiki/Priority_date" title="Priority date">priority date</a></i>. In some cases, for highly skilled foreign nationals (EB1 and EB2 <i>National Interest Waiver</i>, e.g. researchers, athletes, artists or business executives) and "Schedule A" labor<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> (nurses and physical therapists), this step is waived. This step is processed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Labor" title="United States Department of Labor">United States Department of Labor</a> (DOL).  The labor certification is valid for 6 months from the time it is approved.</li>
<li><b>Immigrant petition</b> – the employer applies on the alien's behalf to obtain a visa number.  The application is form I-140, <i>Immigrant Petition for Alien Worker</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> and it is processed by the USCIS.  There are several EB (<i>employment-based</i>) immigrant categories (i.e. EB1-EA, EB2-NIW, EB5)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> under which the alien may apply, with progressively stricter requirements, but often shorter waiting times.  Many of the applications are processed under the EB3 category.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> Currently, this process takes up to 6 months. Many of the EB categories allow expedited processing of this stage, known as "premium processing".</li></ul></li>
<li><b>Immigrant visa availability</b>. When the immigrant petition is approved by the USCIS, the petition is forwarded to the NVC for visa allocation. Currently this step centers around the <a href="/wiki/Priority_date" title="Priority date">priority date</a> concept.
<ul><li><b>Priority date</b> – the visa becomes available when the applicant's <i><a href="/wiki/Priority_date" title="Priority date">priority date</a></i> is earlier than the <i>cutoff date</i> announced on the DOS's Visa Bulletin<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> or when the immigrant visa category the applicant is assigned to is announced as "current".  A "current" designation indicates that visa numbers are available to all applicants in the corresponding immigrant category. Petitions with priority dates earlier than the cutoff date are expected to have visas available, therefore those applicants are eligible for final adjudication. When the NVC determines that a visa number could be available for a particular immigrant petition, a visa is tentatively allocated to the applicant. The NVC will send a letter stating that the applicant may be eligible for adjustment of status, and requiring the applicant to choose either to adjust status with the USCIS directly, or apply at the U.S. consulate abroad. This waiting process determines when the applicant can expect the immigration case to be adjudicated. Due to quotas imposed on EB visa categories, there are more approved immigrant petitions than visas available under INA. High demand for visas has created a backlog of approved but unadjudicated cases. In addition, due to processing inefficiencies throughout DOS and USCIS systems, not all visas available under the quota system in a given year were allocated to applicants by the DOS. Since there is no quota carry-over to the next fiscal year, for several years visa quotas have not been fully used, thus adding to the visa backlog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li><b>Immigrant visa adjudication</b>. When the NVC determines that an immigrant visa is available, the case can be adjudicated. If the alien is already in the USA, that alien has a choice to finalize the green card process via adjustment of status in the USA, or via consular processing abroad. If the alien is outside of the USA he/she can only apply for an immigrant visa at the U.S. consulate. The USCIS does not allow an alien to pursue consular processing and AOS simultaneously. Prior to filing the form I-485 (Adjustment of Status) it is required that the applicant have a <i>medical examination</i> performed by a USCIS-approved civil surgeon.  The examination includes a blood test and specific immunizations, unless the applicant provides proof that the required immunizations were already done elsewhere. The civil surgeon hands the applicant a sealed envelope containing a completed form I-693, which must be included unopened with the I-485 application.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> (The cited reference also states that the February 25, 2010 edition of the Form I-693 reflects that an individual should no longer be tested for HIV infection.)
<ul><li><b>Adjustment of status (AOS)</b> – after the alien has a labor certification and has been provisionally allocated a visa number, the final step is to change his or her status to permanent residency. Adjustment of status is submitted to USCIS via form I-485, <i>Application to Register Permanent Residence or Adjust Status</i>. If an immigrant visa number is available, the USCIS will allow <i>"concurrent filing"</i>: it will accept forms I-140 and I-485 submitted in the same package or will accept form I-485 even before the approval of the I-140.</li>
<li><b>Consular processing</b> – this is an alternative to AOS, but still requires the immigrant visa petition to be completed. In the past (pre-2005), this process was somewhat faster than applying for AOS, so was sometimes used to circumvent long backlogs (of over two years in some cases). However, due to recent efficiency improvements by the USCIS, it is not clear whether applying via consular processing is faster than the regular AOS process. Consular processing is also thought to be riskier since there is no or very little recourse for appeal if the officer denies the application.</li></ul></li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_card_lottery">Green card lottery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Green card lottery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Diversity_Immigrant_Visa" title="Diversity Immigrant Visa">Diversity Immigrant Visa</a></div>
<p>Each year, around 50,000 immigrant visas are made available through the Diversity Visa (DV) program, also known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_Card_Lottery" title="Green Card Lottery">Green Card Lottery</a> to people who were born in countries with low rates of <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_the_United_States" title="Immigration to the United States">immigration to the United States</a> (fewer than 50,000 immigrants in the past five years). Applicants can only qualify by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Country_of_chargeability" title="Country of chargeability">country of chargeability</a>, not by citizenship. Anyone who is selected under this lottery will be given the opportunity to apply for permanent residence. They can also file for their spouse and any unmarried children under the age of 21.
</p><p>If permanent residence is granted, the winner (and his/her family, if applicable) receives an immigrant visa in their passport(s) that has to be "activated" within six months of issuance at any port of entry to the United States. If already in the U.S. adjustment of status may be pursued. The new immigrant receives a stamp on the visa as proof of lawful admittance to the United States, and the individual is now authorized to live and work permanently in the United States. Finally, the actual "green card" typically arrives by mail within a few months.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime:_green_card_lottery_scam">Crime: green card lottery scam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Crime: green card lottery scam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There is a growing number of fraudulent green card lottery <a href="/wiki/Confidence_trick" title="Confidence trick">scams</a>, in which false agents take money from applicants by promising to submit application forms for them. Most agents are not working for the distribution service. Some claim that they can increase the chance of winning the lottery. This is not true; in fact, they may delay or not submit the application. Likewise, some claim to provide to winners free airline tickets or other benefits, such as submissions in future years or cash funds. There is no way to guarantee their claims, and there are numerous nefarious reasons for them not to fulfill their promises. Applicants are advised to use only official U.S. government websites, in which the <a href="/wiki/URL" title="URL">URL</a> ends in <a href="/wiki/.gov" title=".gov">.gov</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_card_lottery_e-mail_fraud">Green card lottery e-mail fraud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Green card lottery e-mail fraud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Other fraud perpetrators will e-mail potential victims posing as State Department or other government officials with requests to wire or transfer money online as part of a "processing fee." These fraudulent e-mails are designed to steal money from unsuspecting victims. The senders often use phony e-mail addresses and logos designed to make them look more like official government correspondence. One easy way to tell that an email is a fraud is that it does not end with a ".gov". One particularly common fraud email asks potential victims to wire money via <a href="/wiki/Western_Union" title="Western Union">Western Union</a> to an individual (the name varies) at the following address in the United Kingdom: 24 Grosvenor Square, London. These emails come from a variety of email addresses designed to impersonate the U.S. State Department. The USCIS blog has published information on this email scam and how to report fraudulent emails to the authorities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup> The U.S. government has issued warnings about this type of fraud or similar business practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Registry">Registry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Registry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The "registry" is a provision of the INA which allows an alien who has previously entered the United States illegally to obtain legal permanent residence simply on the basis of having <i>de facto</i> resided in the country over a very long time. To avail himself of the benefit of this provision, the alien has to prove that he has continuously resided since before the stipulated "registry date".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bruno2001_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-bruno2001-72">[72]</a></sup> The concept of "registry" was first added to the INA in 1929, with the registry date set to June 3, 1921. Since then, the registry date has been adjusted several times, being set to July 1, 1924; June 28, 1940; and June 30, 1948. The most recent adjustment to the registry date came with the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_Reform_and_Control_Act_of_1986" title="Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986">Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986</a>, when it was set to January 1, 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holtzman2015_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-holtzman2015-73">[73]</a></sup> A number of bills have been introduced in Congress since then to further alter the registry date, but they have not been passed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bruno2001_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-bruno2001-72">[72]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holtzman2015_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-holtzman2015-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditional_permanent_residents">Conditional permanent residents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Conditional permanent residents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As part of immigration reform under the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_Reform_and_Control_Act_of_1986" title="Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986">Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986</a> (IRCA), as well as further reform enacted in the <a href="/wiki/Illegal_Immigration_Reform_and_Immigrant_Responsibility_Act_of_1996" title="Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act of 1996">Illegal Immigration Reform and Immigrant Responsibility Act of 1996</a> (IIRIRA), eligible persons who properly apply for permanent residency based on either a recent marriage to a U.S. citizen or as an investor are granted such privilege only on a <b>conditional basis</b>, for two years. An exception to this rule is the case of a U.S. citizen legally sponsoring a spouse in which the marriage at the time of the adjustment of status (I-485) is more than two years old. In this case, the conditional status is waived and a 10-year "permanent resident card" is issued after the USCIS approves the case. A permanent resident under the conditional clause may receive an I-551 stamp as well as a permanent resident card. The expiration date of the conditional period is two years from the approval date. The immigrant visa category is CR (conditional resident).
</p><p>When this two-year conditional period is over, the permanent residence automatically expires and the applicant is subject to deportation and removal unless, up to 90 days before the conditional residence expires, the applicant must file form I-751 <i>Petition to Remove Conditions on Residence</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> (if conditional permanent residence was obtained through marriage) or form I-829 <i>Petition by Entrepreneur to Remove Conditions</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> (if conditional permanent residence was obtained through investment) with USCIS to have the conditions removed.  Once the application is received, permanent residence is extended in 1-year intervals until the request to remove conditions is approved or denied. For conditional permanent residence obtained through marriage, both spouses must sign the form I-751; if the spouses are divorced, it is possible to get a waiver of the other spouse's signing requirement, if it can be proved that the marriage was bona fide.
</p><p>The USCIS requires that the application for the removal of conditions provide both general and specific supporting evidence that the basis on which the applicant obtained conditional permanent residence was not fraudulent. For an application based on marriage, birth certificates of children, joint financial statements, and letters from employers, friends and relatives are some types of evidence that may be accepted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> That is to ensure that the marriage was in good faith and not a fraudulent <a href="/wiki/Marriage_of_convenience" title="Marriage of convenience">marriage of convenience</a> with a sole intention of obtaining a green card. A follow-up interview with an immigration officer is sometimes required but may be waived if the submitted evidence is sufficient. Both the spouses must usually attend the interview.
</p><p>The applicant receives an I-551 stamp in their foreign passport upon approval of their case. The applicant is then free from the conditional requirement once the application is approved. The applicant's new permanent resident card arrives <i>via</i> mail to their house several weeks to several months later and replaces the old two-year conditional residence card. The new card must be renewed after 10 years, but permanent resident status is now granted for an indefinite term if residence conditions are satisfied at all times. The USCIS may request to renew the card earlier because of security enhancements of the card or as a part of a revalidation campaign to exclude counterfeit green cards from circulation.
</p><p>It is important to note that the two-year conditional residence period counts toward satisfying a residency requirement for U.S. <a href="/wiki/Naturalization" title="Naturalization">naturalization</a>, and other purposes. Application for the removal of conditions must be adjudicated before a separate naturalization application could be reviewed by the USCIS on its own merits.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_between_permanent_residents_and_conditional_permanent_residents">Differences between permanent residents and conditional permanent residents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Differences between permanent residents and conditional permanent residents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conditional permanent residents have all of the equal "rights, privileges, responsibilities and duties which apply to all other lawful permanent residents."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> The only difference is the requirement to satisfy the conditions (such as showing marriage status or satisfying entrepreneur requirements) before the two-year period ends.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Abandonment_or_loss_of_permanent_residence_status">Abandonment or loss of permanent residence status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Abandonment or loss of permanent residence status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A green-card holder may abandon permanent residence by filing form I-407, with the green card, at a U.S. Embassy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p><p>Under certain conditions, permanent residence status can be lost involuntarily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> This includes committing a criminal act that makes a person removable from the United States. A person might also be found to have abandoned his/her status if he or she moves to another country to live there permanently, stays outside the USA for more than 365 days (without getting a <a href="/wiki/U.S._Re-entry_Permit" title="U.S. Re-entry Permit">re-entry permit</a> before leaving),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup> or does not file an <a href="/wiki/Income_tax_in_the_United_States" title="Income tax in the United States">income tax</a> return on their worldwide income.  Permanent resident status can also be lost if it is found that the application or grounds for obtaining permanent residence was fraudulent. The failure to renew the permanent resident card does not result in the loss of status, except in the case of conditional permanent residents as noted above.  Nevertheless, it is still a good idea to renew the green card on time because it also acts as a <a href="/wiki/Work_permit" title="Work permit">work permit</a> and travel permit (<a href="/wiki/Advance_parole" title="Advance parole">advance parole</a>), but if the green card is renewed late, there is no penalty or extra fee to pay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>A person who loses permanent residence status is immediately removable from the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and must leave the country as soon as possible or face deportation and removal. In some cases the person may be banned from entering the country for three or seven years, or even <a href="/wiki/Moral_turpitude" title="Moral turpitude">permanently</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tax_costs_of_green_card_relinquishment">Tax costs of green card relinquishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Tax costs of green card relinquishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the <a href="/wiki/Expatriation_tax" title="Expatriation tax">Heart Act</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> 
foreign workers who have owned a green card in eight of the last 15 years and choose to relinquish it will be subject to the <a href="/wiki/Expatriation_tax" title="Expatriation tax">expatriation tax</a>, which taxes unrealized gains above $600,000, anywhere in the world. However this will only apply to those people who have a federal tax liability greater than $139,000 a year or have a worth of more than $2 million or have failed to certify to the IRS that they have been in compliance with U.S. federal tax obligations for the past five years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p>If the green card is not relinquished then the holder is subject to <a href="/wiki/Double_taxation#U.S._Citizens_and_Resident_Aliens_Abroad" title="Double taxation">double taxation</a> when living or working outside of the United States, whether or not within their home nation, although double taxation may be mitigated by <a href="/wiki/Foreign_tax_credit" title="Foreign tax credit">foreign tax credits</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reading_a_permanent_resident_card">Reading a permanent resident card</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lawful_permanent_residents_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Reading a permanent resident card">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:GC_Altered.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="419" data-file-width="652" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/GC_Altered.JPG/260px-GC_Altered.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/GC_Altered.JPG/390px-GC_Altered.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/GC_Altered.JPG/520px-GC_Altered.JPG 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:GC_Altered.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Permanent resident card (2008)</div></div></div>
<p>While most of the information on the card is self-evident, the computer- and human-readable signature at the bottom is not.  The format follows the <a href="/wiki/Machine-readable_passport#Official_travel_documents" title="Machine-readable passport">machine-readable travel document</a> TD1 format:
</p>
<ul><li>First line:</li></ul>
<dl><dd>1–2: C1 or C2. C1 = resident within the United States, C2 = permanent resident commuter (living in Canada or Mexico)</dd>
<dd>3–5: USA (issuing country, United States)</dd>
<dd>6–14: 9-digit number (A#, alien number)</dd>
<dd>15: check digit over digits 6–14</dd>
<dd>16–30: 13-character USCIS receipt number,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> padded with "&lt;" as a filler character<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icao_9303-4_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-icao_9303-4-86">[86]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li>Second line:</li></ul>
<dl><dd>1–6: birth date (in YYMMDD format)</dd>
<dd>7: check digit over digits 1–6</dd>
<dd>8: gender</dd>
<dd>9–14: expiration date (in YYMMDD format)</dd>
<dd>15: check digit over digits 9–14</dd>
<dd>16–29: country of birth</dd>
<dd>30: cumulative check digit (over digits 6–30 (upper line), 1–7, 9–15, 19–29 (lower line))</dd></dl>
<ul><li>Third line:</li></ul>
<dl><dd>surname, given name, middle name, first initial of father, first initial of mother (this line is spaced with "&lt;&lt;" between the surname and given name).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="parents' initials not shown in examples; nor are they specified in ICAO Doc 9303–5 (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Depending on the length of the name, the father's and mother's initials may be omitted.</dd></dl>
<p>A full list of category codes (i.e. IR1, E21, etc.) can be found in the <i>Federal Register</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> or Foreign Affairs Manual.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since May 11, 2010, new green cards contain an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RFID" title="RFID">RFID</a> chip<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> and can be electronically accessed at a distance.  They are shipped with a protective sleeve intended to protect the card from remote access, but it is reported to be inadequate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup>
</p>

It is not necessary that the crime be motivated by animus toward the race, color, religion, sex, handicap, familial status or national origin of the victim.
