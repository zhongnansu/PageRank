<p><i><b>The Twilight Zone</b></i> is the second of three <a href="/wiki/Revival_(television)" title="Revival (television)">revivals</a> of <a href="/wiki/Rod_Serling" title="Rod Serling">Rod Serling</a>'s original <a href="/wiki/The_Twilight_Zone_(1959_TV_series)" title="The Twilight Zone (1959 TV series)">1959–64 television series</a>. It aired for one season on the <a href="/wiki/UPN" title="UPN">UPN</a> network, with actor <a href="/wiki/Forest_Whitaker" title="Forest Whitaker">Forest Whitaker</a> assuming Serling's role as narrator and on-screen host.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It premiered on September 18, 2002, and aired its final episode on May 21, 2003.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Series_history">Series history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Twilight_Zone_(2002_TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Series history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Broadcast in an hour format with two half-hour stories, it was canceled after one season. Reruns continue to air in syndication, and have aired on <a href="/wiki/MyNetworkTV" title="MyNetworkTV">MyNetworkTV</a> since summer 2008. The opening theme music was provided by <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Davis" title="Jonathan Davis">Jonathan Davis</a> (singer of the band <a href="/wiki/Korn" title="Korn">Korn</a>).
</p><p>The series tended to address contemporary issues head-on; i.e. terrorism, racism, gender roles, sexuality, and <a href="/wiki/Stalking" title="Stalking">stalking</a>. Noteworthy episodes featured <a href="/wiki/Jason_Alexander" title="Jason Alexander">Jason Alexander</a> as <a href="/wiki/Death_(personification)" title="Death (personification)">Death</a> wanting to retire from harvesting souls, <a href="/wiki/Lou_Diamond_Phillips" title="Lou Diamond Phillips">Lou Diamond Phillips</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Swimming_pool_service_technician" title="Swimming pool service technician">swimming pool cleaner</a> being shot repeatedly in his dreams, <a href="/wiki/Susanna_Thompson" title="Susanna Thompson">Susanna Thompson</a> as a woman whose stated wish results in an "upgrading" of her family, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Usher_(entertainer)" title="Usher (entertainer)">Usher</a> as a police officer being bothered by telephone calls from beyond the grave, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Austin_Green" title="Brian Austin Green">Brian Austin Green</a> as a businessman who encounters items from his past that somehow reappear, <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Combs" title="Jeffrey Combs">Jeffrey Combs</a> as a hypochondriac whose diseases become reality, and <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Heigl" title="Katherine Heigl">Katherine Heigl</a> playing a woman who went back in time on a suicide mission to kill the infant <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>.
</p><p>The series also includes remakes and updates of stories presented in the original <i>Twilight Zone</i> television series, including the famous "<a href="/wiki/The_Eye_of_the_Beholder" title="The Eye of the Beholder">The Eye of the Beholder</a>" starring <a href="/wiki/Molly_Sims" title="Molly Sims">Molly Sims</a>. One of the updates, "The Monsters Are on Maple Street", is a modernized version of the classic episode similarly called "<a href="/wiki/The_Monsters_Are_Due_on_Maple_Street" title="The Monsters Are Due on Maple Street">The Monsters Are Due on Maple Street</a>". The original show was about the paranoia surrounding a neighborhood-wide blackout. In the course of the episode, somebody suggests an alien invasion being the cause of the blackouts, and that one of the neighbors may be an alien. The anti-alien hysteria is an allegory for the anti-communist paranoia of the time, and the 2003 remake, starring <a href="/wiki/Andrew_McCarthy" title="Andrew McCarthy">Andrew McCarthy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Titus_Welliver" title="Titus Welliver">Titus Welliver</a>, replaces aliens with terrorists. The show also contains "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_Still_a_Good_Life" title="It's Still a Good Life">It's Still a Good Life</a>", a sequel to the events of "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_a_Good_Life_(The_Twilight_Zone)" title="It's a Good Life (The Twilight Zone)">It's a Good Life</a>", an episode of the original series produced 41 years earlier. <a href="/wiki/Bill_Mumy" title="Bill Mumy">Bill Mumy</a> returned to play the adult version of Anthony, the demonic child he had played in the original story, with Mumy's daughter, <a href="/wiki/Liliana_Mumy" title="Liliana Mumy">Liliana</a>, appearing as Anthony's daughter, a more benevolent but even more powerful child. <a href="/wiki/Cloris_Leachman" title="Cloris Leachman">Cloris Leachman</a> also returned as Anthony's mother. Mumy went on to serve as screenwriter for other episodes in the revival.
