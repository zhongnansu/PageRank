<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">East River Greenway</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:East_River_at_Stuyvesant_Cove_Park_(3356237699).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/East_River_at_Stuyvesant_Cove_Park_%283356237699%29.jpg/250px-East_River_at_Stuyvesant_Cove_Park_%283356237699%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/East_River_at_Stuyvesant_Cove_Park_%283356237699%29.jpg/375px-East_River_at_Stuyvesant_Cove_Park_%283356237699%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/East_River_at_Stuyvesant_Cove_Park_%283356237699%29.jpg/500px-East_River_at_Stuyvesant_Cove_Park_%283356237699%29.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:East_River_at_Stuyvesant_Cove_Park_(3356237699).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The narrow portion of the Greenway, at 18th Street, at night in 2009</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>East River Greenway</b> (also called the <b>East River Esplanade</b>) is an approximately 9.44-mile-long (15.19 km) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreshoreway" title="Foreshoreway">foreshoreway</a> for <a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">walking</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cycling" title="Cycling">cycling</a> on the east side of the island of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> on the <a href="/wiki/East_River" title="East River">East River</a>. It is part of the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Waterfront_Greenway" title="Manhattan Waterfront Greenway">Manhattan Waterfront Greenway</a>. The largest portions are operated by the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Department_of_Parks_and_Recreation" title="New York City Department of Parks and Recreation">New York City Department of Parks and Recreation</a>.  It is separated from motor traffic, and many sections also separate <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian" title="Pedestrian">pedestrians</a> from cyclists. The greenway is parallel to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt_East_River_Drive" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt East River Drive">Franklin D. Roosevelt East River Drive</a> for a majority of its length.
</p><p>Parts of the greenway were built at different times. Most of the greenway was built in the 1930 to 1950s in conjunction with the nearby FDR Drive, with exceptions:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Waterside_Plaza" title="Waterside Plaza">Waterside Plaza</a>: 1973</li>
<li>East River Waterfront: Late 1990s</li>
<li>Stuyvesant Cove: 2002</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Headquarters_of_the_United_Nations" title="Headquarters of the United Nations">United Nations</a> portion (under construction): 2015–24</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Route">Route</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Route">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pier35Waterfall.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1387" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Pier35Waterfall.jpg/220px-Pier35Waterfall.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Pier35Waterfall.jpg/330px-Pier35Waterfall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Pier35Waterfall.jpg/440px-Pier35Waterfall.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pier35Waterfall.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Waterfalls" title="New York City Waterfalls">New York City Waterfalls</a></i> along the East River Greenway at Pier 35</div></div></div>
<p>The greenway runs along the East Side, from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_Park" title="Battery Park">Battery Park</a> and past <a href="/wiki/South_Street_Seaport" title="South Street Seaport">South Street Seaport</a> to a dead end at 125th Street, <a href="/wiki/East_Harlem" title="East Harlem">East Harlem</a> with a 1.3-mile (2.1 km) gap from <a href="/wiki/34th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="34th Street (Manhattan)">34th</a> to 60th streets in <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Manhattan" title="Midtown Manhattan">Midtown</a> where pedestrians use busy <a href="/wiki/First_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="First Avenue (Manhattan)">First</a> and <a href="/wiki/Second_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Second Avenue (Manhattan)">Second</a> Avenues to get around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Headquarters" title="United Nations Headquarters">United Nations Headquarters</a> between the <a href="/wiki/Upper_East_Side" title="Upper East Side">Upper East Side</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kips_Bay" title="Kips Bay">Kips Bay</a> portions of the Greenway.
</p><p>Some places are narrow due to sinkholes being blocked off by protective fencing, and one part squeezes between the highway and the dock of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Con_Edison" title="Con Edison">Con Edison</a>'s East River Station, requiring slower speeds.  Other parts are shared space with motor access to <a href="/wiki/Waterside_Plaza" title="Waterside Plaza">Waterside Plaza</a> or a filling station. Approximately one mile (1.6 km) near the southwest end is in the shadow of the elevated <a href="/wiki/FDR_Drive" title="FDR Drive">FDR Drive</a>. This part is to be improved by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_River_Esplanade" title="East River Esplanade">East River Esplanade</a> project.
</p><p>In the summer of 2008 the East River Greenway, along with the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Heights_Promenade" title="Brooklyn Heights Promenade">Brooklyn Heights Promenade</a>, provided viewing locations to see the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Waterfalls" title="New York City Waterfalls">New York City Waterfalls</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components">Components</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Components">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Harlem">East Harlem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: East Harlem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ERGW_sinkhole_jeh.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1828" data-file-width="2656" height="151" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/ERGW_sinkhole_jeh.JPG/220px-ERGW_sinkhole_jeh.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/ERGW_sinkhole_jeh.JPG/330px-ERGW_sinkhole_jeh.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/ERGW_sinkhole_jeh.JPG/440px-ERGW_sinkhole_jeh.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ERGW_sinkhole_jeh.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A section in East Harlem with a pothole</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/East_Harlem" title="East Harlem">East Harlem</a> section runs from the <a href="/wiki/Triborough_Bridge" title="Triborough Bridge">Triborough Bridge</a> ramp at 124th Street to the <a href="/wiki/Gracie_Mansion" title="Gracie Mansion">Gracie Mansion</a> site at 90th Street, where the Greenway rises to a walkway above the double-decker FDR Drive that resembles the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Heights_Promenade" title="Brooklyn Heights Promenade">Brooklyn Heights Promenade</a>. It contains four <a href="/wiki/Footbridge" title="Footbridge">footbridges</a> across the FDR Drive as well as a footbridge, the <a href="/wiki/Wards_Island_Bridge" title="Wards Island Bridge">Wards Island Bridge</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Randalls_and_Wards_Islands" title="Randalls and Wards Islands">Randalls and Wards Islands</a> at 103rd Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the park is in East Harlem, where all residents have access to a <a href="/wiki/Pocket_park" title="Pocket park">pocket park</a>, neighborhood park, or major park within one-quarter mile (0.4 km), amenities are limited in this section of the greenway. This decrepit portion of the East River Greenway has little open space and is poorly maintained; it also has no recreation facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carl_Schurz_Park">Carl Schurz Park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Carl Schurz Park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John_Finley_Walk_snow_jeh.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2688" data-file-width="3312" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/John_Finley_Walk_snow_jeh.jpg/220px-John_Finley_Walk_snow_jeh.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/John_Finley_Walk_snow_jeh.jpg/330px-John_Finley_Walk_snow_jeh.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/John_Finley_Walk_snow_jeh.jpg/440px-John_Finley_Walk_snow_jeh.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:John_Finley_Walk_snow_jeh.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>John Finley Walk in the park, a promenade named after <a href="/wiki/John_Huston_Finley" title="John Huston Finley">John Huston Finley</a>, is also for bicycles</div></div></div>
<p>The East River Greenway then passes along <a href="/wiki/Carl_Schurz_Park" title="Carl Schurz Park">Carl Schurz Park</a> at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=East_River_Greenway&amp;params=40_46_31_N_73_56_37_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">40°46′31″N</span> <span class="longitude">73°56′37″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">40.77528°N 73.94361°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">40.77528; -73.94361</span></span></span></a></span>, near the location of Gracie Mansion, and overlooks the waters of <a href="/wiki/Hell_Gate" title="Hell Gate">Hell Gate</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wards_Island" title="Wards Island">Wards Island</a> in the East River. There are tours of the restored building every Wednesday. The park's waterfront promenade is a deck built over the FDR Drive, enclosing the roadway except on the side facing the <a href="/wiki/East_River" title="East River">East River</a>.  The park is bordered on the west by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_End_Avenue" title="East End Avenue">East End Avenue</a> and on the south by Gracie Square, the extension of East 84th Street to the river. 
</p><p>The area originally was named by an early owner, Siebert Classen, "Hoorn's Hook", for his native <a href="/wiki/Hoorn" title="Hoorn">Hoorn</a> on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zuider_Zee" title="Zuider Zee">Zuider Zee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Later, the area south of 86th Street was set aside as "East River Park", and was used as a picnic ground when the northern section was acquired by the City of New York in 1891.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The easternmost block of 86th Street was acquired subsequently, and the street de-mapped. The park was designed by <a href="/wiki/Calvert_Vaux" title="Calvert Vaux">Calvert Vaux</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Parsons" title="Samuel Parsons">Samuel Parsons</a> 1902, and it was reconstructed in 1935 by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Moses" title="Robert Moses">Robert Moses</a>, due to the creation of the FDR Drive,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> with revised landscaping by Maud Sargent. The park's restoration from a neglected state in the early 1970s was due to the energies of a neighborhood group, the not-for-profit Carl Schurz Park Association (incorporated 1974), formed originally to clean up the park's single playground.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Today, it is part of the greenway.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper_East_Side">Upper East Side</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Upper East Side">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:81st_St_Ped_Bridge_a_jeh.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/81st_St_Ped_Bridge_a_jeh.jpg/220px-81st_St_Ped_Bridge_a_jeh.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/81st_St_Ped_Bridge_a_jeh.jpg/330px-81st_St_Ped_Bridge_a_jeh.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/81st_St_Ped_Bridge_a_jeh.jpg/440px-81st_St_Ped_Bridge_a_jeh.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:81st_St_Ped_Bridge_a_jeh.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>81st Street ramp replaced stairway in 2017</div></div></div>
<p>This section continues at 81st Street and goes down to the <a href="/wiki/Queensboro_Bridge" title="Queensboro Bridge">Queensboro Bridge</a>, with footbridges at 78th, 71st, and 63rd Streets.
</p><p>The part of the greenway nearest the Queensboro Bridge was the topic of 197-a plans released by <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Community_Board_8" title="Manhattan Community Board 8">Community Board 8</a> in 2002, and again in 2006. Proposals include reusing a heliport <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Department_of_Sanitation" title="New York City Department of Sanitation">New York City Department of Sanitation</a> building at 59th Street, renovating existing parks nearby (including the <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Haswell_Green" title="Andrew Haswell Green">Andrew Haswell Green</a> Park between 60th and 62nd Streets), and using a disused vehicular ramp to connect the greenway to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/York_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="York Avenue (Manhattan)">York Avenue</a> at 60th Street. This renovation was funded by a development project at 73rd Street by the <a href="/wiki/City_University_of_New_York" title="City University of New York">City University of New York</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memorial_Sloan-Kettering_Cancer_Center" title="Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center">Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations">United Nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United Nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Robert_Moses_Playground" title="Robert Moses Playground">Robert Moses Playground</a></div>
<p>South of 60th Street, to 38th Street, the greenway enters its only undeveloped section, save for a small section accessed by a footbridge at 50th Street. In October 2011, the city and state reached an agreement to use the western portion of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Moses_Playground" title="Robert Moses Playground">Robert Moses Playground</a> at 41st Street for an expansion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Nations_Headquarters" title="United Nations Headquarters">United Nations Headquarters</a> campus. In exchange, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Development_Corporation" title="United Nations Development Corporation">United Nations Development Corporation</a> would pay $73 million to fund the development of the gap in the Greenway between 38th and 60th streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Designs for this part of the greenway were revealed in November 2013. The new design, which incorporates an amphitheater and a floating pier, is proposed to open in three phases. The first was to open in 2015 and the last by 2024.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The three parts are between 38th, 41st, 53rd, and 60th Streets, with three gathering nodes along the way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In April 2017, the city committed $100 million in funding toward building this part of the greenway<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> with construction to begin in 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Kips_Bay.2FWaterside"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Kips_Bay/Waterside">Kips Bay/Waterside</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Kips Bay/Waterside">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Waterside_Plaza" title="Waterside Plaza">Waterside Plaza</a></div>
<p>This approximately three-quarters-mile (1.2 km) portion of the greenway, accessed by a pedestrian crosswalk at 37th Street, contains both the <a href="/wiki/East_34th_Street_Ferry_Landing" title="East 34th Street Ferry Landing">East 34th Street Ferry Landing</a> and the <a href="/wiki/East_34th_Street_Heliport" title="East 34th Street Heliport">East 34th Street Heliport</a>, and formerly also was near the <a href="/wiki/East_34th_Street_Ferry_Landing" title="East 34th Street Ferry Landing">East 34th Street Ferry Landing</a>.
</p><p>The greenway portion and the apartment buildings that make up <a href="/wiki/Waterside_Plaza" title="Waterside Plaza">Waterside Plaza</a> (as well as the neighboring <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_International_School" title="United Nations International School">United Nations International School</a>) were constructed on top of platforms supported by over 2,000 <a href="/wiki/Deep_foundation" title="Deep foundation">concrete piles</a> sunk into the <a href="/wiki/East_River" title="East River">East River</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-waterside_plaza_history_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-waterside_plaza_history-13">[13]</a></sup> Developed by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Ravitch" title="Richard Ravitch">Richard Ravitch</a>, the first apartment buildings opened in 1973 and the complex and greenway section was completed the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stuyvesant_Cove_Park">Stuyvesant Cove Park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Stuyvesant Cove Park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stuyvesant_Cove_Park.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1794" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Stuyvesant_Cove_Park.jpg/220px-Stuyvesant_Cove_Park.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Stuyvesant_Cove_Park.jpg/330px-Stuyvesant_Cove_Park.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Stuyvesant_Cove_Park.jpg/440px-Stuyvesant_Cove_Park.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stuyvesant_Cove_Park.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Looking south from Stuyvesant Cove Park</div></div></div>
<p>The greenway enters Stuyvesant Cove Park (at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=East_River_Greenway&amp;params=40_43_59.5_N_73_58_26.5_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">40°43′59.5″N</span> <span class="longitude">73°58′26.5″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">40.733194°N 73.974028°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">40.733194; -73.974028</span></span></span></a></span>), a 1.9-acre (7,700 m<sup>2</sup>) public park that runs from  <a href="/wiki/23rd_Street_(Manhattan)" title="23rd Street (Manhattan)">23rd Street</a> to 18th Street, east of <a href="/wiki/Avenue_C_(Manhattan)" title="Avenue C (Manhattan)">Avenue C</a>. It is located to the south of Waterside Plaza and to the north of the East River Park, connecting to the Captain Patrick J. Brown Walk on the south end.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Outdoor_dance_rehearsal_NYC_3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1342" data-file-width="2124" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Outdoor_dance_rehearsal_NYC_3.jpg/220px-Outdoor_dance_rehearsal_NYC_3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Outdoor_dance_rehearsal_NYC_3.jpg/330px-Outdoor_dance_rehearsal_NYC_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Outdoor_dance_rehearsal_NYC_3.jpg/440px-Outdoor_dance_rehearsal_NYC_3.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Outdoor_dance_rehearsal_NYC_3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dance company rehearsing on the stage at Stuyvesant Cove Park</div></div></div>
<p>Located on the former <a href="/wiki/Brownfield_land" title="Brownfield land">brownfield</a> site of a cement plant and a parking lot, the park's plan, released in 1997<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> gave way to a park that was created after the failure of the proposed Riverwalk mixed-use development that would have included residential units, offices, a hotel and a marina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Surplus cement dumped from trucks into the East River has created a small beach in the middle of the park near the end of 20th Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>The park, which was completed in 2002, cost $8.3 million and was designed by Donna Walcavage Landscape Architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Solar_1" title="Solar 1">Solar 1</a>, an environmental learning center with a small outdoor stage for public performances, is located at the north end of the park.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Captain_Patrick_J._Brown_Walk">Captain Patrick J. Brown Walk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Captain Patrick J. Brown Walk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kipsbaydriz_crop.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="770" data-file-width="1046" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Kipsbaydriz_crop.JPG/220px-Kipsbaydriz_crop.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Kipsbaydriz_crop.JPG/330px-Kipsbaydriz_crop.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Kipsbaydriz_crop.JPG/440px-Kipsbaydriz_crop.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kipsbaydriz_crop.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Captain Patrick J. Brown Walk</div></div></div>
<p>The next part of the greenway is the Captain Patrick J. Brown Walk (at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=East_River_Greenway&amp;params=40_43_51_N_73_58_23_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">40°43′51″N</span> <span class="longitude">73°58′23″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">40.73083°N 73.97306°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">40.73083; -73.97306</span></span></span></a></span>), a brick-paved walkway that connects Stuyvesant Cove Park on the north to East River Park to the south. Designed for use by pedestrians and cyclists, it runs approximately between 15th and 18th streets, and Avenues <a href="/wiki/Avenue_C_(Manhattan)" title="Avenue C (Manhattan)">C</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avenue_D_(Manhattan)" title="Avenue D (Manhattan)">D</a>, and was named in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ERGW_traffic_jam_jeh.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2791" data-file-width="2737" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/ERGW_traffic_jam_jeh.JPG/170px-ERGW_traffic_jam_jeh.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/ERGW_traffic_jam_jeh.JPG/255px-ERGW_traffic_jam_jeh.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/ERGW_traffic_jam_jeh.JPG/340px-ERGW_traffic_jam_jeh.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ERGW_traffic_jam_jeh.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>15th Street bottleneck</div></div></div>
<p>It was named after Captain Patrick J. Brown, a firefighter who died in the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>, and who lived in nearby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stuyvesant_Town" title="Stuyvesant Town">Stuyvesant Town</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The East River Blueway plan between 38th Street and the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Bridge" title="Brooklyn Bridge">Brooklyn Bridge</a>, released on February 7, 2013,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> would elevate the walkway above the FDR Drive, with ramps rising to a gradual pedestrian bridge located where the 15th Street bottleneck is now.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Then-Manhattan Borough President <a href="/wiki/Scott_Stringer" title="Scott Stringer">Scott Stringer</a> pledged $3.5 million toward the whole plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_River_Park">East River Park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: East River Park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For the park in Brooklyn, see <a href="/wiki/East_River_State_Park" title="East River State Park">East River State Park</a>.</div>
<p>Then, the greenway goes into <a href="/wiki/East_River_Park" title="East River Park">East River Park</a> (at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=East_River_Greenway&amp;params=40_43_03_N_73_58_27_W_type:landmark_region:US-NY"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">40°43′03″N</span> <span class="longitude">73°58′27″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">﻿ / ﻿</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">40.71750°N 73.97417°W</span><span style="display:none">﻿ / <span class="geo">40.71750; -73.97417</span></span></span></a></span>), which is a 57.5-acre (20 ha) public park located on the <a href="/wiki/Lower_East_Side" title="Lower East Side">Lower East Side</a>. The park stretches from East 12th Street down to Montgomery Street on the east side of the FDR Drive. The southern entrance boasts good views of the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Bridge" title="Manhattan Bridge">Manhattan Bridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Bridge" title="Brooklyn Bridge">Brooklyn Bridge</a>.  The amphitheater, built in 1941 just south of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Grand Street (Manhattan)">Grand Street</a>, has been reconstructed and is often used for public performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nycparks_history_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-nycparks_history-29">[29]</a></sup>  The park includes football, baseball and soccer fields, tennis, basketball and handball courts, a running track, and bike paths. Fishing is another popular activity.  The park is bisected by the <a href="/wiki/Williamsburg_Bridge" title="Williamsburg Bridge">Williamsburg Bridge</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_River_Waterfront"><span id="East_River_Esplanade"></span>East River Waterfront</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: East River Waterfront">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:239px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Welding_under_FDR_pier_16_jeh.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="178" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Welding_under_FDR_pier_16_jeh.jpg/237px-Welding_under_FDR_pier_16_jeh.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Welding_under_FDR_pier_16_jeh.jpg/356px-Welding_under_FDR_pier_16_jeh.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Welding_under_FDR_pier_16_jeh.jpg/474px-Welding_under_FDR_pier_16_jeh.jpg 2x" width="237"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Welding_under_FDR_pier_16_jeh.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Construction site, 2013</div></div></div>
<p>The last part of the greenway is the East River Waterfront Project, also referred to as the "East River Esplanade", though the latter name includes other features along the waterfront. It consists of two miles (3.2 km) along the East River waterfront between Montgomery Street, along <a href="/wiki/South_Street_(Manhattan)" title="South Street (Manhattan)">South Street</a>, to the <a href="/wiki/Battery_Maritime_Building" title="Battery Maritime Building">Battery Maritime Building</a> east of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_Park" title="Battery Park">Battery Park</a>. A bike lane in Pike Street connects to the Manhattan Bridge. The project aims to rehabilitate the existing waterfront space and connect it to two existing waterfront parks, Battery Park and East River Park. Upon completion in 2015,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyc-gov_status_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyc-gov_status-30">[30]</a></sup> the East River Esplanade is slated to feature three rebuilt piers (14, 15, and 35) new glass pavilions underneath the elevated <a href="/wiki/FDR_Drive" title="FDR Drive">FDR Drive</a>, new <a href="/wiki/Bike_lane" title="Bike lane">bike lanes</a>, recreational facilities, and waterfront seating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lowermanhattan1_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-lowermanhattan1-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The East River waterfront in Lower Manhattan was known for heavy maritime activity, with over 40 piers in operation by the later 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-32">[32]</a></sup> The busy waterfront provided easy access to <a href="/wiki/New_York_Harbor" title="New York Harbor">New York Harbor</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> in the south, the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a> on the west, with a connection to the <a href="/wiki/Erie_Canal" title="Erie Canal">Erie Canal</a>. However, the rise of truck traffic and the transfer of port activity to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Newark-Elizabeth_Marine_Terminal" title="Port Newark-Elizabeth Marine Terminal">Port Newark-Elizabeth Marine Terminal</a> drastically reduced maritime traffic on the river after the middle 20th century. With many piers now defunct, ambitious plans have been made to reclaim and reuse the pier space. The north-south arterial highway, the FDR Drive, was moved to an elevated location to allow convenient access to the piers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> In the 1970s, the Water Street Access Plan was drafted to extend the confines of the traditional <a href="/wiki/Financial_District,_Manhattan" title="Financial District, Manhattan">Financial District</a> eastward and create a new business corridor along <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Water Street (Manhattan)">Water Street</a>, south of <a href="/wiki/Fulton_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Fulton Street (Manhattan)">Fulton Street</a>. Noting the success of the <a href="/wiki/Brookfield_Place_(New_York_City)" title="Brookfield Place (New York City)">World Financial Center</a>, the East Side Landing plan was created in the 1980s to add commercial and office buildings along the waterfront, again south of Fulton Street, similar to <a href="/wiki/Battery_Park_City" title="Battery Park City">Battery Park City</a>. This plan never materialized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1982, there was a plan to expand the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seaport_Museum_of_New_York" title="Seaport Museum of New York">Seaport Museum of New York</a> and attract tourist activity. Parts of the district were devoted to retail, including the main building of the <a href="/wiki/Fulton_Fish_Market" title="Fulton Fish Market">Fulton Fish Market</a>. A modern shopping mall was then built on Pier 17 and was opened in September 11, 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> Furthermore, the Fulton Fish Market formerly located around <a href="/wiki/South_Street_(Manhattan)" title="South Street (Manhattan)">South Street</a> and Fulton Street, was pressured to relocate in 2005 to <a href="/wiki/Hunts_Point,_Bronx" title="Hunts Point, Bronx">Hunts Point</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">the Bronx</a> due to plans for the redevelopment of the Manhattan waterfront.
</p><p>The pedestrian and bike path was first established in the late 1990s between Montgomery Street in the Lower East Side and Broad Street in the Financial District. Benches were also added along the partially restored waterfront. The pedestrian path/bikeway has been well received by community members. Drawbacks exist however:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The pedestrian path and bikeway are not segregated from each other; there is one lane in each direction which is shared by pedestrians and bikers.</li>
<li>The path curves around FDR Drive viaduct pillars.</li>
<li>The path occupies a narrow portion of the space below the viaduct; in most cases, the rest of the space is used as parking facilities and storage space.</li>
<li>Amenities are few.</li>
<li>Its legacy as a service and commercial zone offer limited pedestrian access.</li>
<li>City services, such as the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Department_of_Sanitation" title="New York City Department of Sanitation">Department of Sanitation</a>, use the waterfront for equipment.</li></ul>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bloomberg" title="Michael Bloomberg">Michael Bloomberg</a>'s tenure as mayor, he spearheaded the <a href="/wiki/PlaNYC" title="PlaNYC">PlaNYC</a> 2030 initiative, which highlights projects and plans to transform the city by the year 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> An important focus was put on the waterfront, including the East River waterfront. Bloomberg also stressed the importance of ferry transport, as an alternative to existing land transport options. The <a href="/wiki/Pier_11/Wall_Street" title="Pier 11/Wall Street">Pier 11/Wall Street</a> ferry terminal has long operated East River waterfront, with regular trips to New Jersey. In June 2011, a ferry service to piers on Brooklyn's waterfront started.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Renovation">Renovation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Renovation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>SHoP Architects led the design process in the creation of the East River Esplanade, consulting community members during the planning years between 2002 and 2006. A year-long study was undertaken in 2004, allowing designers to derive ways that would "enhance waterfront access". Other groups critical to the project's planning phase include the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Department_of_Transportation" title="New York City Department of Transportation">New York City Department of Transportation</a>, the Department of Parks and Recreation, the Economic Development Corporation, the Department of City Planning and other agencies and groups. $137 million was secured with the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Manhattan_Development_Corporation" title="Lower Manhattan Development Corporation">Lower Manhattan Development Corporation</a>, and work began on a "pilot section" in 2009 between John Street and Wall Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lowermanhattan1_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-lowermanhattan1-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the Department of City Planning, the overall plan is listed as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyc-gov_status_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyc-gov_status-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lowermanhattan1_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-lowermanhattan1-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The esplanade itself: The new esplanade will consist of a recreation zone along the water's edge with seating and plantings, pavilions under the FDR Drive to support outdoor activities, and a bikeway along South Street that ties into the Manhattan Greenway. Consistent paving, seating, railings, and lighting will be used throughout.</li>
<li>Pavilions and "get-downs": New, proposed pavilions will help reconnect the city to the river's edge, creating concentrated areas for recreation where appropriate. At Peck Slip and Rutgers Slip, the new esplanade design will mark historic locations by carving steps into the bulkhead line—creating means to "get down" to the water.</li>
<li>Archipelago: In order to provide a continuous greenway between Battery Park and the new East River Waterfront, the path between the <a href="/wiki/Battery_Maritime_Building" title="Battery Maritime Building">Battery Maritime Building</a> and Old Slip will be extended over the existing shoreline, creating a walkway out over the water with a of the city.</li>
<li>Pier 15: The proposed pier will lift its primary recreation area to a second tier, offering new and intimate views of the tall, historic ships that could be docked in this location.</li>
<li>Pier 35: This large pier will be opened to the public with a second tier, which will function as an amphitheater that faces west-southwest toward <a href="/wiki/Governors_Island" title="Governors Island">Governors Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_Park" title="Battery Park">Battery Park</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Harbor" title="New York Harbor">New York Harbor</a>. It is connected to the greenway by a bridge.</li>
<li>Sustainability: In keeping with the City's <a href="/wiki/PlaNYC" title="PlaNYC">PlaNYC</a> initiatives, the East River Waterfront project will seek <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a> certification for its pavilions and park uses. Plans aim to reduce energy demand and consumption, conserve natural resources, improve air quality, and catch and reuse rainwater water.</li></ul>
<p>The FDR Drive viaduct will be clad, and South Street will be narrowed for much of its length to make room for additional sidewalk space. The glass pavilions will be located underneath the viaduct, and the bike lanes would be moved to the side of the pavilion, underneath the FDR Drive. The piers would be reconstructed to encourage marine growth, by having <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reef_ball" title="Reef ball">reef balls</a> to attract fish populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Access to the Esplanade from the neighboring areas would be undertaken with the redevelopment of "slips" - wedge-shaped road shapes which previously allowed ships to berth. Some of these slips have been used as parking facilities, while others are the southern termini for critical north-south thoroughfares. Reconstructions of the existing slips in the Financial District, the Lower East Side and Chinatown will feature bikeways and landscaped medians. Five of these slip projects are at varying stages of construction. From east to west, they are Montgomery Slip, Rutgers Slip, Pike Slip, Peck Slip, and Burling Slip.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Renovation_phases">Renovation phases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Renovation phases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Ambox current red.svg" data-file-height="290" data-file-width="360" height="34" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" width="42"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section needs to be <b>updated</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span> <small><i>(October 2016)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The project is being undertaken in three phases, with first phase completed (including Pier 15).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Second phase construction started in summer 2011 (from Old Slip to the Battery Maritime Building).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Construction on the third phase (Pike Slip to Pier 35, Montgomery Slip) began in fall 2011. This phase will last until 2014 and will cover the rehabilitation underneath the FDR Drive from Maiden Lane to Pier 35.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Planning of Phase 4, between Catherine and Pike Slips, concluded in 2013, and construction is scheduled to start in 2014 and last for about a year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyc-gov_status_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-nyc-gov_status-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="U-shaped_barrier">U-shaped barrier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=East_River_Greenway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: U-shaped barrier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are plans for a new storm barrier along the southern third of the greenway, between West 57th and East 42nd Streets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbd_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-rbd-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nextcity_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-nextcity-51">[51]</a></sup> The final proposal, which is geographically U-shaped, will include many features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbd_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-rbd-49">[49]</a></sup> Under the elevated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt_East_River_Drive" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt East River Drive">FDR Drive</a> structure above <a href="/wiki/South_Street_(Manhattan)" title="South Street (Manhattan)">South Street</a> will be storm barriers hanging from the viaduct's ceiling, which will drop down in case of a storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbd_49-2"><a href="#cite_note-rbd-49">[49]</a></sup> A "Battery Berm" will be located at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battery_Park" title="Battery Park">Battery Park</a>, and a maritime museum will be opened on the site of a former Coast Guard building there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rbd_49-3"><a href="#cite_note-rbd-49">[49]</a></sup> The proposal, by Rebuild by Design, will also include components for storm barriers in <a href="/wiki/Hunts_Point,_Bronx" title="Hunts Point, Bronx">Hunts Point, Bronx</a> and on <a href="/wiki/Staten_Island" title="Staten Island">Staten Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nextcity_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-nextcity-51">[51]</a></sup> The first component, a 2.19-mile-long (4 km) barrier on the <a href="/wiki/Lower_East_Side" title="Lower East Side">Lower East Side</a> between Montgomery and East 13th Streets called "The Bridging Berm", will cost $335 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nextcity2_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-nextcity2-52">[52]</a></sup> In addition to storm protection, the berm—the first of three of the barrier's components—will also provide a pedestrian pathway and bikeway on top of berm, boating and fishing docks, a slope down to current sports fields, upgraded <a href="/wiki/Americans_with_Disabilities_Act_of_1990" title="Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990">ADA</a>-accessible ramps for bridges across the FDR Drive, and construction materials such as "slurry walls, concrete blocks, a compacted embankment, a clay cap, topsoil and salt-tolerant landscaping."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nextcity2_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-nextcity2-52">[52]</a></sup> The total cost of the project is over $3.5 billion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
