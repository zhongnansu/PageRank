<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Evergreen Game</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:246px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Evergreen_game_animation.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="248" data-file-width="244" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/54/Evergreen_game_animation.gif" width="244"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption">Game animation</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Evergreen Game</b> is a famous <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a> game, won by <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Anderssen" title="Adolf Anderssen">Adolf Anderssen</a> against <a href="/wiki/Jean_Dufresne" title="Jean Dufresne">Jean Dufresne</a> in 1852.
</p><p>This was probably an <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_chess#friendly_game" title="Glossary of chess"><span class="glossary-link" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of chess § friendly game">informal game</span></a></dfn>. At the time, there was no formal title of "World Champion", but the German mathematics professor Adolf Anderssen was widely considered to be the best player in the world after winning the <a href="/wiki/London_1851_chess_tournament" title="London 1851 chess tournament">first major international chess tournament</a> in London in 1851. Though not in the same class as Anderssen, Jean Dufresne, a popular author of chess books, was also a strong player. It is usually assumed that the game was played in Berlin, where Dufresne was resident and Anderssen was a frequent visitor, but no details of the circumstances of the game were provided in the original publication in the September and October 1852 issues of <i><a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Schachzeitung" title="Deutsche Schachzeitung">Deutsche Schachzeitung</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winter_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Winter-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SZ_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SZ-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Beginning with <a href="/wiki/Howard_Staunton" title="Howard Staunton">Howard Staunton</a> in 1853,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Staunton_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Staunton-3">[3]</a></sup> the game has been extensively analysed over the years, particularly the critical positions before and after White's remarkable 19th move, 19.Rad1. Although defensive resources for Black have since been found, Anderssen's <a href="/wiki/Combination_(chess)" title="Combination (chess)">combination</a> remains much admired.
</p><p>Following Anderssen's death in 1879, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Steinitz" title="Wilhelm Steinitz">Wilhelm Steinitz</a> published a tribute in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Field_(magazine)" title="The Field (magazine)">The Field</a></i> in which he annotated Anderssen's two most famous games, the Evergreen and the <a href="/wiki/Immortal_Game" title="Immortal Game">Immortal Game</a> against <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Kieseritzky" title="Lionel Kieseritzky">Lionel Kieseritzky</a>. Annotating the famous move 19.Rad1, Steinitz wrote "An evergreen in the laurel crown of the departed chess hero", thus giving this game its name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Winter_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Winter-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_game">The game</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Evergreen_Game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The game">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/White_and_Black_in_chess" title="White and Black in chess">White</a>: Anderssen <span style="padding-left:2;"> </span> Black: Dufresne <span style="padding-left:2;"> </span> <a href="/wiki/Chess_opening" title="Chess opening">Opening</a>: <a href="/wiki/Evans_Gambit" title="Evans Gambit">Evans Gambit</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Encyclopaedia_of_Chess_Openings" title="Encyclopaedia of Chess Openings">ECO</a></i> C52)
</p><p><b>1. e4 e5 2. Nf3 Nc6 3. Bc4 Bc5 4. b4</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>The Evans Gambit, a popular opening in the 19th century and still seen occasionally today.  White gives up <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_chess#material" title="Glossary of chess"><span class="glossary-link" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of chess § material">material</span></a></dfn> to gain an advantage in <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_chess#development" title="Glossary of chess"><span class="glossary-link" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of chess § development">development</span></a></dfn>.</dd></dl>

<dl><dd>Black attempts to slow down White's rapid development by depriving the <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_chess#queen's_knight" title="Glossary of chess"><span class="glossary-link" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of chess § queen's knight">queen's knight</span></a></dfn> of its preferred c3-square and forcing White to spend a <a href="/wiki/Tempo_(chess)" title="Tempo (chess)">tempo</a> capturing the <a href="/wiki/Pawn_(chess)" title="Pawn (chess)">pawn</a>. This move was favoured by Dufresne, but today it is considered inferior. Most popular today is 7...Nge7, in order to answer 8.cxd4 or 8.Ng5 with 8...d5. Other alternatives include 7...dxc3 (the risky "Compromised Defence") and 7...d6.</dd></dl>
<p><b>8. Qb3</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>Immediately attacking the f7-pawn. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FIDE_Master" title="FIDE Master">FIDE Master</a> <a href="/wiki/Graham_Burgess" title="Graham Burgess">Graham Burgess</a> suggests 8.Re1 instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgess_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Burgess-4">[4]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p><b>8... Qf6 9. e5 Qg6</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>White's e5-pawn cannot be taken: if 9...Nxe5, then 10.Re1 d6 11.Bg5, when 11...Qf5 (11...Qg6 12.Nxe5 dxe5 13.Rxe5+ wins the bishop) 12.Nxe5 wins the <a href="/wiki/Knight_(chess)" title="Knight (chess)">knight</a> (if the knight is recaptured with 12...dxe5, then 13.Qb5+ followed by 14.Rxe5+ wins).</dd></dl>

<p><b>10. Re1<a href="/wiki/Chess_annotation_symbols#!" title="Chess annotation symbols">!</a></b> (see diagram) <b>Nge7 11. Ba3 b5<a href="/wiki/Chess_annotation_symbols#?!" title="Chess annotation symbols">?!</a></b>
</p>
<dl><dd>Rather than defending his own position, Black offers a counter-<a href="/wiki/Sacrifice_(chess)" title="Sacrifice (chess)">sacrifice</a> to activate his a8-<a href="/wiki/Rook_(chess)" title="Rook (chess)">rook</a> with tempo. Burgess suggests 11...a6 instead, to allow the b-pawn to advance later with tempo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgess_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Burgess-4">[4]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p><b>12. Qxb5 Rb8 13. Qa4 Bb6</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>Black cannot <a href="/wiki/Castling" title="Castling">castle</a> here because 14.Bxe7 would win a piece, as the <a href="/wiki/Knight_(chess)" title="Knight (chess)">knight</a> on c6 cannot simultaneously protect the knight on e7 and the bishop on a5.</dd></dl>
<p><b>14. Nbd2 Bb7<a href="/wiki/Chess_annotation_symbols#?" title="Chess annotation symbols">?</a></b>
</p>
<dl><dd>Black must castle without delay.</dd></dl>
<p><b>15. Ne4 Qf5?</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>A poor move which loses a tempo. 15...0-0 16.Bxd3 also gives White a very dangerous attack (Neishtadt, 1961).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neishtadt_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Neishtadt-5">[5]</a></sup> Better was 15...d2! 16.Nexd2 0-0 (<a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Lasker" title="Emanuel Lasker">Lasker</a>), although White still has a clear advantage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harding_p45_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Harding_p45-6">[6]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p><b>16. Bxd3 Qh5 17. Nf6+<a href="/wiki/Chess_annotation_symbols#!?" title="Chess annotation symbols">!?</a></b>
</p>
<dl><dd>A dramatic sacrifice, although several commentators have pointed out that 17.Ng3 Qh6 18.Bc1 Qe6 19.Bc4 wins material in a simpler way.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgess_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Burgess-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipke_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lipke-7">[7]</a></sup> Or 18.Nf5 and White has a clear edge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harding_p45_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Harding_p45-6">[6]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Chessmaster" title="Chessmaster">Chessmaster</a></i> computer program annotation says "this [sacrifice] is not without danger, as Black now obtains an open g-file for counterplay."</dd></dl>

style="vertical-align:inherit;padding:0"></td></tr></tbody></table><div class="thumbcaption">Position after 19.Rad1!
</div></div></div>
<dl><dd>A somewhat controversial move, which has been both exulted and criticised over the years. It sets a deep <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_chess#trap" title="Glossary of chess"><span class="glossary-link" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of chess § trap">trap</span></a></dfn>, which Black walks into. In <i>Common Sense in Chess</i> (1895), the then-<a href="/wiki/World_Chess_Championship" title="World Chess Championship">world champion</a> <a href="/wiki/Emanuel_Lasker" title="Emanuel Lasker">Emanuel Lasker</a> praised it as "one of the most subtle and profound moves on record". However, probably influenced by the analysis of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Lipke" title="Paul Lipke">Paul Lipke</a> which revealed defensive possibilities for Black, he later criticised the move, saying that 19.Be4 would have won relatively easily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Lasker's analysis turned out to be faulty, however. Analysis by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Murey" title="Jacob Murey">Jacob Murey</a> and German Fridshtein published in the Soviet magazine <i><a href="/wiki/64_(magazine)" title="64 (magazine)">64</a></i> in 1975, found that after 19.Be4 Qh3! 20.g3 Rxg3+ 21.hxg3 Qxg3+ 22.Kh1 Bxf2 23.Bxe7! (Lasker's 23.Re2? is refuted by 23...Nd4!) 23...Qh3+! 24.Nh2 Bxe1 25.Rxe1 Qh4! 26.Qd1! Nxe7 27.Bxb7 Qxf6 it is unclear whether White is winning. Subsequent analysts such as Zaitsev and Kasparov have agreed with this assessment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Murey_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Murey-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasparov_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kasparov-10">[10]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p><b>19... Qxf3?</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>"Who would have played anything else here?!" (Lipke, 1898). White cannot play 20.gxf3 since the g2-pawn is <a href="/wiki/Pin_(chess)" title="Pin (chess)">pinned</a> by the rook on g8. Black now threatens to take either on f2 or g2, both major threats to the white king, but Anderssen has a shattering resource available.</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>Dissatisfied with the lack of analysis in the game's original publication, <a href="/wiki/Howard_Staunton" title="Howard Staunton">Howard Staunton</a> published a detailed analysis of several of Black's alternatives in the <i><a href="/wiki/Chess_Player%27s_Chronicle" title="Chess Player's Chronicle">Chess Player's Chronicle</a></i> in 1853. Staunton analysed 19...Ne5, 19...d6, 19...Bc5, 19...Rxg2+ and 19...Qh3, concluding that Black was lost in all lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Staunton_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Staunton-3">[3]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>This was the accepted view for many years, until the strong German master Paul Lipke published analysis in the May and June 1898 issues of the <i>Deutscher Schachzeitung</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipke_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lipke-7">[7]</a></sup> Lipke recommended 19...Rg4!? for Black, concluding that it offered Black excellent drawing chances with <dfn id=""><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_chess#optimal_play" title="Glossary of chess"><span class="glossary-link" style="color:inherit;" title="See entry at: Glossary of chess § optimal play">best play</span></a></dfn>. Lipke's main line went 19...Rg4 20.Bc4 Qf5! 21.Rxd7! Kxd7 22.Ne5+ Kc8 23.Nxg4 Nd5 24.Qd1 Nd8 25.Re5 Bxf2+ 26.Kh1 Nf4 27.h3 and now either 27...Qb1 or 27...Nxg2 will probably draw for Black.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipke_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lipke-7">[7]</a></sup> Analysis published in the early 1930s by O. Hoppe and H. Heckner found a win for White after 25.Bd3! (instead of 25.Re5); however, Black can also improve with 24...Nxf6 (Kasparov).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasparov_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kasparov-10">[10]</a></sup> Zaitsev's 21...Rxg2+ (rather than 21...Kxd7) also appears to be sufficient for a draw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasparov_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kasparov-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaitsev_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zaitsev-11">[11]</a></sup> Hoppe and Heckner also found a win for White after 20.c4 Rf4? (Lipke) 21.Bg6!!. Better is 20...Bd4 (<a href="/wiki/Igor_Zaitsev" title="Igor Zaitsev">Zaitsev</a>, <i>64</i>, 1976)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaitsev_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zaitsev-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hooper_p108_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hooper_p108-12">[12]</a></sup> or 20...Rxg2+! (Kasparov).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasparov_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kasparov-10">[10]</a></sup> 20.Re4 has also received renewed attention as an attempt for White to gain the advantage. The final assessment of 19...Rg4 remains unclear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasparov_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kasparov-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaitsev_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zaitsev-11">[11]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>Most analysts have followed Staunton in rejecting 19...Rxg2+?! on account of 20.Kxg2 Ne5 21.Qxd7+!!; however, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I.J._Good" title="I.J. Good">I.J. Good</a> contended that after 21...Kxd7 22.Bg6+ Ke6 23.Bxh5 Rg8+ 24.Kh3! N7g6 25.Bg4+ Kxf6 26.Nxe5 Nxe5 27.Be7+ Kxe7 28.Rxe5+ Kf6 White does not have a clear win in the <a href="/wiki/Chess_endgame" title="Chess endgame">endgame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>In 1958, analysis by readers of the <i>Schach-Echo</i> came to the conclusion that 19...Bd4 and 19...Qh3 are even better than 19...Rg4 and sufficient to force a draw.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> (19...Bd4 was also found independently by Zaitsev.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zaitsev_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-Zaitsev-11">[11]</a></sup>) This view is endorsed by Burgess, who quotes the lines (a) 19...Bd4 20.cxd4 Qxf3 21.Be4 Rxg2+ 22.Kh1 Rxh2+ 23.Kxh2 Qxf2+ and (b) 19...Qh3 20.Bf1 Qf5! (not analysed by Staunton) 21.Bd3 Qh3, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Draw_by_repetition" title="Draw by repetition">repeating moves</a> in each case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burgess_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Burgess-4">[4]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p><b>20. Rxe7+! Nxe7?</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>This loses instantly to a very attractive mate in four. 20...Kd8 would put up more resistance, but White should win after 21.Rxd7+ Kc8 22.Rd8+!! Kxd8 (if 22...Rxd8 23.gxf3; if 22...Nxd8 23.Qd7+ Kxd7 24.Bf5+ Ke8 25.Bd7<a href="/wiki/Algebraic_notation_(chess)##" title="Algebraic notation (chess)">#</a>) (Staunton, 1853)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Staunton_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Staunton-3">[3]</a></sup> 23.Bf5+ Qxd1+ (<a href="/wiki/Akiba_Rubinstein" title="Akiba Rubinstein">Rubinstein</a>, 1921).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hooper_p108_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hooper_p108-12">[12]</a></sup> Another way is 23.Be2+;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kasparov_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Kasparov-10">[10]</a></sup> however, White must play accurately in this case: after 23...Nd4! 24.Bxf3 Bxf3 25.Rxd4+? leads to a probable draw after 25...Bxd4 26.Qxd4+ Kc8 27.Qd3 Bxg2 28.f3 Bh3+ 29.Kf2 Rb6 30.Qxh7 Rg2+ 31.Ke3 Be6 (Levenfish, 1959).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> White must instead play 25.g3! (Neishtadt, 1961)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Neishtadt_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Neishtadt-5">[5]</a></sup> Bxd1 26.Qxd1 "with a boring but winning endgame" (Kasparov).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>It is unclear whether the following moves were actually played, or whether Anderssen simply "announced mate", a common practice at the time. The <i>Deutsche Schachzeitung</i> where the game was originally published simply said "Weiss giebt in 4 Zügen Matt" (White mates in 4 moves), without providing the actual moves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SZ_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SZ-2">[2]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p><b>21. Qxd7+<a href="/wiki/Chess_annotation_symbols#!!" title="Chess annotation symbols">!!</a> Kxd7 22. Bf5+</b>
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Double_check" title="Double check">Double checks</a> like 22.Bf5+ are powerful because they force the king to move. Here it is decisive.</dd></dl>
<p><b>22... Ke8</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>Or 22...Kc6 23.Bd7<a href="/wiki/Algebraic_notation_(chess)##" title="Algebraic notation (chess)">#</a>.</dd></dl>
<p><b>23. Bd7+ Kf8</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>Some sources give 23...Kd8 as Black's move, with the same reply, 24.Bxe7#.</dd></dl>
<p><b>24. Bxe7# <a href="/wiki/Algebraic_notation_(chess)#1–0" title="Algebraic notation (chess)">1–0</a></b>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Savielly_Tartakower" title="Savielly Tartakower">Savielly Tartakower</a> commented, "A combination second to none in the literature of the game." (<a href="#CITEREFTartakowerdu_Mont1975">Tartakower &amp; du Mont 1975</a>:35).
</p>
