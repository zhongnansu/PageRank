<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Green earth</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div> <div id="contentSub"></div>
<div id="jump-to-nav"></div> <a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Green earth</b>, also known as <b>terre verte</b> and <b>Verona green</b>, is an inorganic <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> derived from the minerals <a href="/wiki/Celadonite" title="Celadonite">celadonite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glauconite" title="Glauconite">glauconite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Its chemical formula is K[(Al,Fe<sup>III</sup>),(Fe<sup>II</sup>,Mg](AlSi<sub>3</sub>,Si<sub>4</sub>)O<sub>10</sub>(OH)<sub>2</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-a-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>First used by the ancient Romans, green earth has been identified on wall paintings at <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dura-Europos" title="Dura-Europos">Dura-Europos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-b-5">[5]</a></sup> The Renaissance painter and writer <a href="/wiki/Cennino_Cennini" title="Cennino Cennini">Cennino Cennini</a> claimed that “the ancients never <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gilded" title="Gilded">gilded</a>  except with this green” being used as a bole, or undercoating.  In the Middle Ages one of its best-known uses was in the underpainting of flesh tones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-a-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>High quality deposits can be found in England, France, Cyprus, Germany and at Monte Baldo near Verona in Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-b-5">[5]</a></sup>  The color ranges from neutral yellow green to pale greenish gray to dark matte olive green.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-a-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
