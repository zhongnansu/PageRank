<p>The <b>Yale School of Forestry &amp; Environmental Studies</b> (F&amp;ES) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Professional_school" title="Professional school">professional school</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>. It was founded to train <a href="/wiki/Forester" title="Forester">foresters</a>, and now trains environmental leaders through four 2-year degree programs (<a href="/wiki/Master_of_Environmental_Management" title="Master of Environmental Management">Master of Environmental Management</a>, Master of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Science" title="Environmental Science">Environmental Science</a>, Master of <a href="/wiki/Forestry" title="Forestry">Forestry</a>, and Master of Forest Science) and two 10-month mid-career programs. Yale F&amp;ES strives to create new knowledge that will sustain and restore the health of the biosphere and emphasizes the possibility of creating a regenerative coexistence between humans and non-human life and the rest of the natural world. Still offering forestry instruction, the school has the oldest graduate forestry program in the United States.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yale_School_of_Forestry_%26_Environmental_Studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Yale_School_of_Forestry_class_of_1904.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="336" data-file-width="500" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Yale_School_of_Forestry_class_of_1904.jpg/275px-Yale_School_of_Forestry_class_of_1904.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Yale_School_of_Forestry_class_of_1904.jpg/413px-Yale_School_of_Forestry_class_of_1904.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/Yale_School_of_Forestry_class_of_1904.jpg 2x" width="275"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Yale_School_of_Forestry_class_of_1904.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Yale School of Forestry class of 1904</div></div></div>
<p>The school was founded in 1900 as the <b>Yale Forest School</b>, to provide high-level forestry training suited to American conditions. At the urging of Yale alumnus <a href="/wiki/Gifford_Pinchot" title="Gifford Pinchot">Gifford Pinchot</a>, his parents endowed the two-year postgraduate program. At the time Pinchot was serving as <a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Fernow" title="Bernhard Fernow">Bernhard Fernow</a>'s successor as Chief of the Division of Forestry (predecessor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Forest_Service" title="U.S. Forest Service">U.S. Forest Service</a>, USFS). Pinchot released two foresters from the division to start the school: fellow Yale graduate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Solon_Graves" title="Henry Solon Graves">Henry Solon Graves</a> and James Toumey.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sparhawk_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sparhawk-1">[1]</a></sup> Graves became the School's first dean and Toumey its second.
</p><p>When the school opened, other places in the United States offered forestry training, but none had a post-graduate program. (Both Pinchot and Graves had gone to Europe to study forestry after graduating from Yale.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In the fall of 1900, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_College_of_Forestry_at_Cornell" title="New York State College of Forestry at Cornell">New York State College of Forestry at Cornell</a> had 24 students, <a href="/wiki/Biltmore_Forest_School" title="Biltmore Forest School">Biltmore Forest School</a> 9, and Yale 7.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sparhawk_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sparhawk-1">[1]</a></sup> Despite its small size, from its beginnings the school influenced American forestry. The first two chiefs of the USFS were Pinchot and Graves; the next three were graduates from the school's first decade. Wilderness and land conservation advocate <a href="/wiki/Aldo_Leopold" title="Aldo Leopold">Aldo Leopold</a> graduated in the class of 1908.
</p><p>In 1915, Yale School of Forestry's second dean, James Toumey, became one of the "charter members", along with <a href="/wiki/William_L._Bray" title="William L. Bray">William L. Bray</a> of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_New_York_State_College_of_Forestry" title="History of the New York State College of Forestry">New York State College of Forestry</a>, by then reestablished at <a href="/wiki/Syracuse_University" title="Syracuse University">Syracuse University</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Raphael_Zon" title="Raphael Zon">Raphael Zon</a>, of the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_Society_of_America" title="Ecological Society of America">Ecological Society of America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esa.org_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-esa.org-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1950, the "activist wing" of that society formed <a href="/wiki/The_Nature_Conservancy" title="The Nature Conservancy">The Nature Conservancy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esa.org_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-esa.org-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Besides the school's own forests, Yale has used a number of other sites in the eastern United States for field education over the years. From 1904 to 1926, the summer session leading to a master's degree in forestry was held at <a href="/wiki/Grey_Towers_National_Historic_Site" title="Grey Towers National Historic Site">Grey Towers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forester%27s_Hall" title="Forester's Hall">Forester's Hall</a> in <a href="/wiki/Milford,_Pennsylvania" title="Milford, Pennsylvania">Milford, Pennsylvania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arch_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-arch-4">[4]</a></sup> Beginning in 1912, Yale classes took occasional field trips to the land of the Crossett Lumber Company in <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>. For two decades from 1946 until 1966, the company provided the school a "camp," including cabins and a mess hall, used during spring coursework on forest management and wood products production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Yale students have also used a field camp at the <a class="external text" href="http://www.greatmountainforest.org" rel="nofollow">Great Mountain Forest</a> in northwestern Connecticut since 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Reflective of the expanding variety of environmental interests, the school changed its name to the <b>Yale School of Forestry &amp; Environmental Studies</b> in 1972. Today, F&amp;ES is a leader in global sustainability, hosting the bi-annual <a class="external text" href="http://yess.yale.edu" rel="nofollow">Yale Environmental Sustainability Summit</a> to assemble thought leaders from around the globe. The school's 16th and present dean is <a class="external text" href="https://environment.yale.edu/profile/burke/" rel="nofollow">Ingrid "Indy" Burke</a>, who replaced <a href="/wiki/Peter_Crane" title="Peter Crane">Sir Peter Crane</a> in October, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Indy_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Indy-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The school offers classes at Kroon Hall, Sage Hall, Greeley Labs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Othniel_C._Marsh_House" title="Othniel C. Marsh House">Marsh Hall</a>, the Environmental Science Center, the houses at 301 Prospect St. and 380 Edwards St., and teaches the Yale College undergraduate courses needed for the Environmental Studies major. Kroon Hall, the school's main building, is named for the philanthropist Richard Kroon (Yale Class of 1964). The building has 50,000 square feet (5,000 m<sup>2</sup>) of space. It is "a showcase of the latest developments in green building technology, a healthy and supportive environment for work and study, and a beautiful building that actively connects students, faculty, staff, and visitors with the natural world."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kroon_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kroon-8">[8]</a></sup> The building obtained Platinum Rating under the <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a> certification system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kroon_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kroon-8">[8]</a></sup> It is designed by <a href="/wiki/Hopkins_Architects" title="Hopkins Architects">Hopkins Architects</a> of London with Architect of Record Centerbrook Architects.  Goodfellow Inc from Delson, Quebec, supplied the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glulam" title="Glulam">glulam</a> roof structure for this project.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="School_forest">School forest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yale_School_of_Forestry_%26_Environmental_Studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: School forest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The school owns and manages 10,880 acres (44 km<sup>2</sup>) of forestland in <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Yale-Myers_Forest" title="Yale-Myers Forest">Yale-Myers Forest</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Union,_Connecticut" title="Union, Connecticut">Union, Connecticut</a>, donated to Yale in 1930 by alumnus <a href="/wiki/George_Hewitt_Myers" title="George Hewitt Myers">George Hewitt Myers</a>, is managed by the school as a multiple-use working forest. Yale-Toumey Forest, near <a href="/wiki/Keene,_New_Hampshire" title="Keene, New Hampshire">Keene, New Hampshire</a>, was set up by James W. Toumey (a former dean of the school) in 1913. Other Yale forestlands include Goss Woods, Crowell Forest, Cross Woods, <a href="/wiki/Bowen_Forest" title="Bowen Forest">Bowen Forest</a>, and Crowell Ravine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> A three-alarm fire burned several buildings within the Yale Myers Forest Camp on May 28th, 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  Rebuilding efforts are ongoing.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yale_School_of_Forestry_%26_Environmental_Studies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The school has an active tradition of student involvement in academic and extracurricular life. Many students take part in student interest groups, which organize events around environmental issues of interest to them. These groups range in interest from Conservation Finance and International Development, to the Built Environment and "Fresh &amp; Salty: The Society for Marine and Coastal systems". There are also social and recreational groups, such as the Forestry Club, which every Friday organizes themed "TGIF" ("Thank-God-I'm-a-Forester") happy hours and school parties; the Polar Bear club, which swims monthly in Long Island Sound under the full moon (year-round); Veggie Dinner, which is a weekly vegetarian dinner club; the Loggerrhythms, an a cappella singing group; and the student-run BYO Café in Kroon Hall opened in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  A notable F&amp;ES tradition is the extravagant environmentally inspired decoration of graduation caps in preparation for commencement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
