<p><b>Enterprise Community Partners</b>, formerly <b>The Enterprise Foundation</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_Maryland" title="Columbia, Maryland">Columbia, Maryland</a>-based nonprofit organization that works with partners nationwide to finance, build and advocate for affordable housing for low- and moderate-income families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Founded in 1982 by developer/philanthropist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_W._Rouse" title="James W. Rouse">James W. Rouse</a> and his wife Patty, Enterprise has worked with community-based nonprofit organizations to develop nearly 470,000 homes, investing $28.9 billion throughout the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Enterprise is a family of companies comprising Enterprise Community Partners (the parent nonprofit organization) and its related organizations: Enterprise Community Investment (a financial services company), Enterprise Community Asset Management (a multifamily asset management firm), Enterprise Community Loan Fund (a certified <a href="/wiki/Community_development_financial_institution" title="Community development financial institution">Community Development Financial Institution</a>), Enterprise Homes (a housing developer) and Bellwether-Enterprise Real Estate Capital (a multifamily and commercial mortgage originator). The organization has 10 regional offices across the country, including a team in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> that advocates for changes to federal affordable housing policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">Mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_Community_Partners&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="external text" href="http://www.enterprisecommunity.org/about" rel="nofollow">Enterprise's mission</a> is to create opportunity for low- and moderate income people through affordable housing in diverse, thriving communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_Community_Partners&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1972, three members of the Church of the Saviour, Terry Flood, Barbara Moore and Carolyn Banker wanted to create low-income housing in the <a href="/wiki/Adams_Morgan" title="Adams Morgan">Adams Morgan</a> neighborhood of D.C. With no development, financial or construction experience, they put down a non-refundable deposit to purchase the Ritz and Mozart apartment buildings. Their commitment won over <a href="/wiki/James_Rouse" title="James Rouse">James Rouse</a>, CEO of The Rouse Company and he helped them secure $625,000 to complete the transaction and $125,000 toward the cost of rehabilitation.
</p><p>In 1981 the experience inspired Jim Rouse to found <b>Robin Hood Inc.</b> based in the Rouse owned American City building in <a href="/wiki/Columbia,_Maryland" title="Columbia, Maryland">Columbia, Maryland</a>. The company was renamed to Jubilee Housing to help with fundraising efforts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Jubilee Housing provided the launchpad for Jim and Patty Rouse to start the Enterprise Foundation in 1982. In 2005, it was renamed Enterprise Community Partners. Today, Enterprise continues to work to uphold the standards set by Jim and Patty to see that all people have the opportunity for affordable housing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1984, Jim Rouse was soliciting business representing both Rouse Company as CEO and Enterprise Development as president. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rouse_Company" title="Rouse Company">Rouse Company</a> board of directors asked Jim Rouse to leave as CEO of the Rouse Company and his position in Enterprise Development which ended his involvement with the company he founded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Enterprise_Model">Enterprise Model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_Community_Partners&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Enterprise Model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Enterprise foundation was to be the central clearing house for a profitable privately operated social welfare system.
</p><p>The model included.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Securities - Providing investments from and income to the foundation</li>
<li>Projects qualifying for charitable investment - Loans and equity from the foundation, and income to the foundation.</li>
<li>Enterprise Development Company - A for-profit company that provides Joint Ventures with Local Developers and Non-Profits with cash flow return to the foundation. The firm developed the <a href="/wiki/Festival_marketplace" title="Festival marketplace">Festival marketplace</a> projects using public and private funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Community_development_corporation" title="Community development corporation">Community Housing Corporations</a> - Receives loans from foundation for community projects and returns income to foundation.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Enterprise_Green_Communities">Enterprise Green Communities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enterprise_Community_Partners&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Enterprise Green Communities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On October 8, 2009, Enterprise Community Partners announced a $4 billion commitment to the next generation of its Green Communities initiative. Enterprise said its efforts will result directly in the creation, preservation, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrofit" title="Retrofit">retrofit</a> of 75,000 green homes and community and commercial buildings over the next five years. The group will provide <a href="/wiki/Loan" title="Loan">loans</a> to owners of existing <a href="/wiki/Multi-family_residential" title="Multi-family residential">multi-family buildings</a> in key markets for capital purchases that will reduce energy and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_consumption" title="Water consumption">water consumption</a> or will lead to more healthy living environments. Enterprise relies on support from a number of banks and foundations, and it is currently drawing on $2.5 million in grants for fundraising.  Enterprise also issued a national call to action to make all affordable housing in the United States meet green criteria by 2020. To provide background, the organization released a study proving the overall return on investment of meeting its <a class="external text" href="http://www.enterprisecommunity.com/servlet/servlet.FileDownload?file=00Pa000000FxwvNEAR" rel="nofollow">Green Communities Criteria</a>. Based on years of research, the report, "Incremental Cost, Measurable Savings: Enterprise," finds that the estimated lifetime savings exceed the initial investment made to incorporate the Green Communities Criteria into affordable housing during construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
