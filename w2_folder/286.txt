<p><b>Green conservatism</b> is a combination of <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservatism</a> with <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>. Environmental concern has been voiced by both conservative politicians and philosophers throughout the history of modern conservatism with <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a> (the philosophical founder of modern conservatism) in his <i><a href="/wiki/Reflections_on_the_Revolution_in_France" title="Reflections on the Revolution in France">Reflections on the Revolution in France</a></i> quoted as saying 'the earth, the kind and equal mother of all ought not to be monopolised to foster the pride and luxury of any men'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Americas">Americas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Americas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_Network" title="Sustainability Network">Sustainability Network</a> party was founded by <a href="/wiki/Marina_Silva" title="Marina Silva">Marina Silva</a>, a prominent conservative environmentalist who was previously a member of various political parties including the Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(Brazil)" title="Green Party (Brazil)">Green Party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Patriota" title="Patriota">National Ecologic Party</a> also had ties to the <a href="/wiki/Assemblies_of_God" title="Assemblies of God">Assemblies of God</a>, the largest evangelical denomination present in Brazil, and upheld green conservatism but it has now changed its name to <a href="/wiki/Patriota" title="Patriota">Patriota</a> and renounced its green and pro-ecologist policies in favor of its conservative and nationalist policies; it has maintained and strengthened its religious opposition to <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a> and other left-wing policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Canada, the term 'green conservatism' was popularized in 2006 by <a href="/wiki/Preston_Manning" title="Preston Manning">Preston Manning</a>, former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_(Canada)" title="Leader of the Opposition (Canada)">federal opposition leader</a> and founder of the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Party_of_Canada" title="Reform Party of Canada">Reform Party of Canada</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Specifically Manning has argued that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Canadian" title="Western Canadian">Western Canadian</a> <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_Canada" title="Conservatism in Canada">Conservatism</a> with its strong <a href="/wiki/Agrarianism" title="Agrarianism">rural roots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Populism" title="Populism">populist</a> rhetoric will eventually have to reconcile the need for economic growth with protection of the environment. He has specifically talked about using <a href="/wiki/Water_pricing" title="Water pricing">water pricing</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athabasca_Oil_Sands" title="Athabasca Oil Sands">Athabasca Oil Sands</a> to prevent a "<a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_commons" title="Tragedy of the commons">tragedy of the commons</a>" scenario.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ecologist_Green_Party_of_Mexico" title="Ecologist Green Party of Mexico">Ecologist Green Party of Mexico</a> (PVE), founded in 1993, won 47 seats in the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_legislative_election,_2015" title="Mexican legislative election, 2015">2015 Mexican legislative election</a>.
</p><p>In 2008, the PVEM initiated an advertising campaign in favor of reintroducing the <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_Mexico" title="Capital punishment in Mexico">death penalty in Mexico</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This led to the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Party" title="European Green Party">European Green Party</a>'s withdrawal of recognition of the PVEM as a legitimate green party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>During an interview, PVE candidate <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gamaliel_Ramirez&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gamaliel Ramirez (page does not exist)">Gamaliel Ramirez</a> verbally attacked an openly gay candidate for Guadalajara mayor and called for criminal laws against homosexuality to be established. In the following days, Ramirez issued a written apology after the party expressed disappointment at his remarks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>One of the first uses of the term <i>green conservatism</i> was by former United States <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">House Speaker</a> <a href="/wiki/Newt_Gingrich" title="Newt Gingrich">Newt Gingrich</a>, in a debate on environmental issues with <a href="/wiki/John_Kerry" title="John Kerry">John Kerry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Around this time, the green conservative movement was sometimes referred to as the <i>crunchy con</i> movement, a term popularized by <i><a href="/wiki/National_Review" title="National Review">National Review</a></i> magazine and the writings of <a href="/wiki/Rod_Dreher" title="Rod Dreher">Rod Dreher</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DreherCC_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-DreherCC-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a> is generally considered as the <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">conservative party</a>. Green conservatism manifested itself as a movement in groups such as <a href="/wiki/ConservAmerica" title="ConservAmerica">ConservAmerica</a>, which seeks to strengthen the Republican Party's stance on environmental issues and support efforts to conserve natural resources and protect human and environmental health.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Independent_Greens_of_Virginia" title="Independent Greens of Virginia">Independent Greens of Virginia</a> (or Indy Greens) call themselves "common sense conservatives".  The party, over the last decade, has run many conservative greens for local, state, and federal office. In 2004, the party gave its ballot line to <a href="/wiki/Constitution_Party_(United_States)" title="Constitution Party (United States)">Constitution Party</a> nominee <a href="/wiki/Michael_Peroutka" title="Michael Peroutka">Michael Peroutka</a> for president, and in 2008, once again placed the Constitution Party nominee <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Baldwin" title="Chuck Baldwin">Chuck Baldwin</a> on the ballot as its presidential candidate. The Indy Greens call for balancing the federal budget and paying off the federal debt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Republican Party had long supported the protection of the environment at the first half of 20th Century. Republican President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> was a prominent conservationist whose policies eventually led to the creation of the modern <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Filler_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Filler-14">[14]</a></sup> Republican President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> was responsible for establishing the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> in 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nixon_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nixon-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>More recently, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> Republican Governor <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schwarzenegger" title="Arnold Schwarzenegger">Arnold Schwarzenegger</a>, with the support of 16 other states, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filing_(legal)" title="Filing (legal)">sued</a> the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> for the right to set vehicle emission standards higher than the <a href="/wiki/United_States_emission_standards" title="United States emission standards">federal standard</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarzenegger_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schwarzenegger-16">[16]</a></sup> a right to which California is entitled under the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a>.
</p><p>Democratic President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> did not send the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> to the U.S. Senate for ratification, as he thought it unfair to the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BushGW_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-BushGW-17">[17]</a></sup> President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> also publicly opposed ratification of the Kyoto Protocols on the grounds that they unfairly targeted Western industrialized nations such as the United States while favoring developing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North-South_divide" title="North-South divide">Global South</a> polluters such as China and India.
</p><p>In 2000, the Republican Party adopted as part of its platform support for the development of market-based solutions to environmental problems. According to the platform, "economic prosperity and environmental protection must advance together, environmental regulations should be based on science, the government’s role should be to provide market-based incentives to develop the technologies to meet environmental standards, we should ensure that environmental policy meets the needs of localities, and environmental policy should focus on achieving results processes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Encourage_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Encourage-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_George_W._Bush" title="Presidency of George W. Bush">George W. Bush administration</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FactSheet_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FactSheet-19">[19]</a></sup> along with several of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)_presidential_candidates,_2008" title="Republican Party (United States) presidential candidates, 2008">candidates that sought the Republican Presidential nomination in 2008</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kudlow_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kudlow-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IssueWatch_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-IssueWatch-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Candidates_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Candidates-22">[22]</a></sup> supported increased Federal investment into the development of clean alternative fuels, increased <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a>, as well as fuels such as <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>, as a way of helping the U.S. achieve <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_American_energy_independence" title="North American energy independence">energy independence</a>, as opposed to supporting less use of carbon dioxide-producing methods of generating energy. <a href="/wiki/John_McCain" title="John McCain">John McCain</a>, who ran unsuccessfully for President in <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2008" title="United States presidential election, 2008">2008</a>, supported the <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">cap-and-trade</a> policy, a policy that is quite popular among Democrats but much less so among other Republicans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Japan, the Environmental Green Political Assembly, or Midori no Kaigi, emerged from the conservative reformist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakigake_Party" title="Sakigake Party">Sakigake Party</a>. It combined a conservative ideology with an ecologist platform, forcing out a number of non-ecologist members to join the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(Japan,_1998)" title="Democratic Party (Japan, 1998)">Democratic Party</a>'s <i>Ryoun-kai</i> faction. It showed poor performance at the polls, and was dissolved in 2004, merged into the conservative <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democratic_Party_(Japan)" title="Liberal Democratic Party (Japan)">Liberal Democratic Party</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Austria">Austria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Austria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Austrian environmental movement (USB) was founded in 1973, out of this, the Electoral community for citizens' initiatives and environmental protection (WBU) has emerged. The WBU was thereby a grouping with bourgeois, liberal and conservative sides, which also did not shy away from the right-wing extremist spectrum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schandl_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schandl-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>The United Greens of Austria (VGÖ) was founded in 1982, this represented the bourgeois wing of the movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> By former members VGÖ later the Civil Green Austria (BGÖ) was founded.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Free_Party_Salzburg" title="Free Party Salzburg">Free Party Salzburg</a> (FPS) claims to be the only party that advocates that no genetically modified seed funds may be spread and has advocated against genetic engineering. It calls for a ban on glyphosate and it " deals with the green issues in Salzburg".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Denmark, the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_People%27s_Party_(Denmark)" title="Conservative People's Party (Denmark)">Conservative People's Party</a> advocates conservative policies, while being a part of the green bloc of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folketinget" title="Folketinget">Folketinget</a>. They opposed the Danish 2016 Agriculture Reform due to enivornmental concerns.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G%C3%A9n%C3%A9ration_%C3%89cologie" title="Génération Écologie">Génération Écologie</a> is an ecologically focused political party in France. It has been nicknamed 'The Blues' due to its association with a number of conservative political groups.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Antoine_Waechter" title="Antoine Waechter">Antoine Waechter</a>, a former presidential candidate of <a href="/wiki/The_Greens_(France)" title="The Greens (France)">Les Verts</a>, founded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mouvement_Ecologiste_Ind%C3%A9pendant" title="Mouvement Ecologiste Indépendant">Mouvement Ecologiste Indépendant</a> after Les Verts adopted left-wing positions. Mouvement Ecologiste Indépendant adheres to centrist politics and so it may include some members who lean towards Green-conservatism.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The governing <a href="/wiki/Alliance_90/The_Greens" title="Alliance 90/The Greens">Greens</a> in the state of <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a> under Minister-President <a href="/wiki/Winfried_Kretschmann" title="Winfried Kretschmann">Winfried Kretschmann</a> have been described as more conservative than their federal counterparts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Indeed, Kretschmann identifies as a green conservative<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and has been associated with <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalism" title="Economic liberalism">economically liberal</a> viewpoints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Green Action Future (GAZ), the predecessor party of the ÖDP, was perceived and criticized in opinion leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Der_Spiegel" title="Der Spiegel">Der Spiegel</a> as clearly conservative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>In Germany, the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_Democratic_Party" title="Ecological Democratic Party">Ecological Democratic Party</a> (ÖDP) was formed by more right-wing defectors from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Die Grünen</a> in 1982. It combined a focus on environmental protection with a promotion of the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_life" title="Right to life">right to life</a> (opposition to <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Euthanasia" title="Euthanasia">euthanasia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">capital punishment</a>); it differs from The Greens by being less supportive of immigration and restrictions on state powers in criminal justice issues, not focusing on gay and lesbian rights, and having a differing view on feminism.
</p><p>While having never gained seats in federal or state legislatures in Germany, it made a name for itself by its involvement in the opposition to a <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech</a> nuclear reactor in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temelin" title="Temelin">Temelin</a>, across the border from <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>. It led an initiative for a popular referendum to abolish the Bavarian Senate (that state's upper house) which was successful. The party won a seat in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2014" title="European Parliament election, 2014">2014 EU Parliament election</a> and remains active.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Greece">Greece</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Greece">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecologist_Greece" title="Ecologist Greece">Ecologist Greece</a> is a green conservative party.
</p><p>On 11 September 2009, <a href="/wiki/Georgios_Karatzaferis" title="Georgios Karatzaferis">Georgios Karatzaferis</a>, leader of the far-right nationalist <a href="/wiki/Popular_Orthodox_Rally" title="Popular Orthodox Rally">Popular Orthodox Rally</a> (Laos) party announced that Ecologists Greece leader Papanikolas would run on the statewide Laos ticket in the <a href="/wiki/Greek_legislative_election,_2009" title="Greek legislative election, 2009">2009 Greek legislative election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungary">Hungary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hungary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Hungary" title="Green Party of Hungary">Green Party of Hungary</a> was founded on 19 November 1989.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> But when Zoltán Medveczki became Party President in March 1993, the party gradually changed its political position from moderate to radical right-wing. The MZP adopted anti-liberal, anti-communist, anti-Semitic and pro-fascist elements (<a href="/wiki/Ecofascism" title="Ecofascism">Ecofascism</a>) to its program and also criticized <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">privatization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">market economy</a>. Medveczki also founded and registered the party's paramilitary wing, the Alliance of National Green Youth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In was dissolved in 2011.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Greens_Greens" title="Greens Greens">Greens Greens</a> was founded by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maurizio_Lupi_(1961)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maurizio Lupi (1961) (page does not exist)">Maurizio Lupi</a>, a former member of <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democracy_(Italy)" title="Christian Democracy (Italy)">Christian Democracy</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_the_Greens" title="Federation of the Greens">Federation of the Greens</a>. In the 2004 provincial elections of Turin the Greens Greens supported the centre-right candidate Franco Botta. In the <a href="/wiki/Piedmontese_regional_election,_2005" title="Piedmontese regional election, 2005">Piedmontese regional election, 2005</a> the Greens Greens supported the centre-right candidate Enzo Ghigo; the list got 23,761 votes and the 1.16% of the vote. In the <a href="/wiki/Italian_general_election,_2008" title="Italian general election, 2008">Italian general election, 2008</a> Alessandro Lupi ran for the Chamber in <a href="/wiki/The_People_of_Freedom" title="The People of Freedom">The People of Freedom</a>'s list, in the division Piedmont 1, but he wasn't elected. In the <a href="/wiki/Piedmontese_regional_election,_2010" title="Piedmontese regional election, 2010">Piedmontese regional election, 2010</a> the Greens Greens supported the centre-right candidate Roberto Cota, winning the 1.76% of the vote and one seat. In the <a href="/wiki/Piedmontese_regional_election,_2014" title="Piedmontese regional election, 2014">Piedmontese regional election, 2014</a> the party supported the candidate of Forza Italia Gilberto Pichetto Fratin, but it got only the 0.27% of the vote and no seats.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Latvia">Latvia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Latvia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In February 2004, after the breakdown of the four-party government, Indulis Emsis of the <a href="/wiki/Latvian_Green_Party" title="Latvian Green Party">Latvian Green Party</a> was appointed to form a new government and became the first <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a> of a country anywhere in the world from a <a href="/wiki/Green_party" title="Green party">Green party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Latvian_parliamentary_election,_2014" title="Latvian parliamentary election, 2014">2014 Latvian parliamentary election</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Greens_and_Farmers" title="Union of Greens and Farmers">Union of Greens and Farmers</a> became the third largest party in the country with 21 seats, 6 of which are held by the <a href="/wiki/Latvian_Green_Party" title="Latvian Green Party">Latvian Green Party</a> which is the second largest of the 4 parties which form the Union (in terms of the number of seats it holds).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithuania">Lithuania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Lithuania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_parliamentary_election,_2016" title="Lithuanian parliamentary election, 2016">2016 Lithuanian parliamentary election</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_Farmers_and_Greens_Union" title="Lithuanian Farmers and Greens Union">Lithuanian Farmers and Greens Union</a> became the largest party in a surprise victory with 51 seats. The party's standpoint on the economy and many other issues is broadly center-right.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsweek_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newsweek-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the success of the Farmers and Greens Union at the 2016 election, Chairman <a href="/wiki/Ram%C5%ABnas_Karbauskis" title="Ramūnas Karbauskis">Ramūnas Karbauskis</a> said he was open to a coalition with either the right-wing <a href="/wiki/Homeland_Union" title="Homeland Union">Homeland Union</a>, the center-left <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Lithuania" title="Social Democratic Party of Lithuania">Social Democratic Party of Lithuania</a>, or both, however the former declined to form a coalition with the latter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsweek_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newsweek-34">[34]</a></sup> The Farmers and Greens Union eventually formed government with the Social Democrats but maintains its center-right policies.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Portugal">Portugal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Portugal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Earth_Party" title="Earth Party">Earth Party</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partido_da_Terra" title="Partido da Terra">Partido da Terra</a>) is a green conservative<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magone2015_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Magone2015-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Portugal" title="List of political parties in Portugal">political party in Portugal</a>, founded on 12 August 1993.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1970, the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> Government of <a href="/wiki/Edward_Heath" title="Edward Heath">Edward Heath</a> created United Kingdom’s first Department of the Environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the 1988 Conservative Party Conference <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> said, "It’s we Conservatives who are not merely friends of the Earth – we are its guardians and trustees for generations to come. The core of Tory philosophy and for the case for protecting the environment are the same. No generation has a freehold on this earth. All we have is a life tenancy – with a full repairing lease. This Government intends to meet the terms of that lease in full."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early <a href="/wiki/21st_century" title="21st century">21st century</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a> under <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> promised a green agenda which included proposals designed to impose a tax on workplace car parking spaces, a halt to airport growth, a tax on petrol thirsty 4x4s and restrictions on car advertising.  The measures were suggested by The Quality of Life Policy Group, which was set up by Cameron to help fight climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cameron spoke of embracing 'green' issues, and had made <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> a key component of his speeches.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> He called for an independent climate change commission to ensure that emissions reductions targets are met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> However, Cameron's claim of leading the "greenest government ever"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> has been repudiated by <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_England_and_Wales" title="Green Party of England and Wales">Green Party</a> MP Caroline Lucas, who argued that Cameron had "shown little interest in green policy and the sustainability agenda."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Zac_Goldsmith" title="Zac Goldsmith">Zac Goldsmith</a>, who was the Conservative candidate for <a href="/wiki/London_mayoral_election,_2016" title="London mayoral election, 2016">London Mayor in 2016</a>, describes himself as an environmentalist. He had received the <i>Global Green Award for International Environmental Leadership</i> in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awards_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-awards-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bright_Blue_(organisation)" title="Bright Blue (organisation)">Bright Blue</a>, a liberal conservative think tank launched<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (July 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the Green Conservatism project to create conservative support for policies that address climate change.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first significant Green conservative in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Fraser" title="Malcolm Fraser">Malcolm Fraser</a>. During his time in office, he legislated significant environmental protection laws for the <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a> while leader of the centre-right <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Australia" title="Liberal Party of Australia">Liberal Party</a> as early as the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Australian politics became more polemic around the year 2000. Once an uneasy alliance between the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party" title="Australian Labor Party">Australian Labor Party</a> (the traditional opposition to the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party" title="Liberal Party">Liberal Party</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Australian_Greens" title="Australian Greens">The Greens</a> had formed, it caused many conservative voters to become suspicious of Green politics and so interest in Green conservatism waned.
</p><p>In 2004, the conservative <a href="/wiki/Family_First_Party" title="Family First Party">Family First Party</a> aired a television advertisement labelling The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Greens" title="Australian Greens">Australian Greens</a> as the <i>Extreme Greens</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Since then, the continuously increasing anti-conservative dominance within The Greens has led to this label receiving broader use in the media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Consequently, Green conservatives are entering political parties other than The Greens instead.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_conservatism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_of_New_Zealand" title="Conservative Party of New Zealand">Conservative Party of New Zealand</a> has an environmental policy focused around actions within the country that can help the local environment rather than being tied to international agreements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
