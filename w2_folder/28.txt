<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Greenhouse and icehouse Earth</h1> <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p>Throughout the Phanerozoic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_Earth" title="History of the Earth">history of the Earth</a>, the planet's climate has been fluctuating between two dominant <a href="/wiki/Climate_state" title="Climate state">climate states</a>: the <b>greenhouse Earth</b> and the <b>icehouse Earth</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  
These two climate states last for  millions of years and should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">glacial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a> periods, which occur only during an icehouse period and tend to last less than 1 million years. There are <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_glaciation" title="Timeline of glaciation">five known great glaciations</a> in Earth's climate history; the main factors involved in changes of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleoclimate" title="Paleoclimate">paleoclimate</a> are believed to be the concentration of atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, changes in the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_orbit" title="Earth's orbit">Earth's orbit</a>, and oceanic and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orogenic" title="Orogenic">orogenic</a> changes due to <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">tectonic plate dynamics</a>. Greenhouse and icehouse periods have profoundly shaped the <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a> of life on Earth.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_Earth">Greenhouse Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Greenhouse Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect" title="Runaway greenhouse effect">Runaway greenhouse effect</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_greenhouse_Earth">Overview of greenhouse Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview of greenhouse Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A "greenhouse Earth" or "hothouse Earth" is a period in which there are no continental <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> whatsoever on the planet, the levels of carbon dioxide and other <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>) are high, and <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> (SSTs) range from 28 °C (82.4 °F) in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> to 0 °C (32 °F) in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_region" title="Polar region">polar regions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_of_greenhouse_Earth">Causes of greenhouse Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Causes of greenhouse Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are several theories as to how a greenhouse Earth can come about.  The geological record shows CO<sub>2</sub> and other greenhouse gases are abundant during this time. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_movement" title="Tectonic movement">Tectonic movements</a> were extremely active during the more well-known greenhouse ages (such as 368 million years ago in the <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a> Era). Because of <a href="/wiki/Divergent_boundary" title="Divergent boundary">continental rifting</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Continental_plate" title="Continental plate">continental plates</a> moving away from each other) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> activity becomes more prominent, producing more CO<sub>2</sub> and heating up the Earth's atmosphere.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Earth is more commonly placed in a greenhouse state throughout the epochs, and the Earth has been in this state for approximately 80% of the past 500 million years, which makes understanding the direct causes somewhat difficult.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpicerCorfield1992_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-SpicerCorfield1992-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_icehouse_Earth">Overview of icehouse Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Overview of icehouse Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An "icehouse Earth" is the earth as it experiences an <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>. Unlike a greenhouse Earth, an icehouse Earth has <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a> present, and these sheets wax and wane throughout times known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glacial_periods" title="Glacial periods">glacial periods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial periods</a>. During an icehouse Earth, greenhouse gases tend to be less abundant, and temperatures tend to be cooler globally. The Earth is currently in an icehouse stage,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montanez-p24-27_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Montanez-p24-27-5">[5]</a></sup> as ice sheets are present on both <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">poles</a> and glacial periods have occurred at regular intervals over the past million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes_of_icehouse_Earth">Causes of icehouse Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Causes of icehouse Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The causes of an icehouse state are much debated, because not much is really known about the transition periods between greenhouse to icehouse climates and what could make the climate so different. One important aspect is clearly the decline of CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere, possibly due to low volcanic activity.
</p><p>Other important issues are the movement of the tectonic plates and the opening and closing of oceanic gateways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith-p337-340_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-p337-340-7">[7]</a></sup> These seem to play a crucial part in icehouse Earths because they can bring forth cool waters from very deep water circulations that could assist in creating ice sheets or thermal isolation of areas. Examples of this occurring are the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Oligocene#The_opening_of_the_Tasman_Gateway" title="Oligocene">Tasmanian gateway</a> 36.5 million years ago that separated <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> and which is believed to have set off the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a> icehouse,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Exon-p11-17_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Exon-p11-17-8">[8]</a></sup> and the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Drake_Passage" title="Drake Passage">Drake Passage</a> 32.8 million years ago by the separation of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> though it was believed by other scientists that this did not come into effect until around 23 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Exon-p11-17_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Exon-p11-17-8">[8]</a></sup> The closing of the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama" title="Isthmus of Panama">Isthmus of Panama</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Indonesia#Indonesian_Seaway" title="Geology of Indonesia">Indonesian seaway</a> approximately 3 or 4 million years ago may have been a major cause for our current icehouse state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith-p337-340_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-p337-340-7">[7]</a></sup> For the icehouse climate, tectonic activity also creates mountains, which are produced by one continental plate colliding with another one and continuing forward. The revealed fresh <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a> act as <a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">scrubbers</a> of carbon dioxide, which can significantly affect the amount of this greenhouse gas in the atmosphere. An example of this is the collision between the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asian</a> continent, which created the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayan Mountains</a> about 50 million years ago.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glacials_and_interglacials">Glacials and interglacials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Glacials and interglacials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">Glacial period</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">Interglacial</a></div>
<p>Within icehouse states, there are "<a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">glacial</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a>" periods that cause ice sheets to build up or retreat. The causes for these glacial and interglacial periods are mainly variations in the movement of the earth around the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broecker-p49-56_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Broecker-p49-56-10">[10]</a></sup> The astronomical components, discovered by the Serbian geophysicist <a href="/wiki/Milutin_Milankovi%C4%87" title="Milutin Milanković">Milutin Milanković</a> and now known as <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a>, include the <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a> of the Earth, the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">orbital eccentricity</a> (or shape of the <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">precession</a> (or wobble) of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">Earth's spin</a>. The tilt of the axis tends to fluctuate between 21.5° to 24.5° and back every 41,000 years on the vertical axis. This change actually affects the <a href="/wiki/Seasonality" title="Seasonality">seasonality</a> upon the earth, since more or less <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insolation" title="Insolation">solar radiation</a> hits certain areas of the planet more often on a higher tilt, while less of a tilt would create a more even set of seasons worldwide. These changes can be seen in ice cores, which also contains information that shows that during glacial times (at the maximum extension of the ice sheets), the atmosphere had lower levels of carbon dioxide. This may be caused by the increase or redistribution of the <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acid</a>/<a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a> balance with <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> that deals with <a href="/wiki/Alkalinity" title="Alkalinity">alkalinity</a>. During an Icehouse, only 20% of the time is spent in interglacial, or warmer times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Broecker-p49-56_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Broecker-p49-56-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Snowball_earth">Snowball earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Snowball earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Snowball_Earth" title="Snowball Earth">Snowball Earth</a></div>
<p>A "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Snowball_earth" title="Snowball earth">snowball earth</a>" is the complete opposite of greenhouse Earth, in which the earth's surface is completely frozen over; however, a snowball earth technically does not have continental ice sheets like during the icehouse state. "The Great Infra-<a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a> Ice Age" has been claimed to be the host of such a world, and in 1964, the scientist <a href="/wiki/W._Brian_Harland" title="W. Brian Harland">W. Brian Harland</a> brought forth his discovery of indications of glaciers in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_latitudes" title="Low latitudes">low latitudes</a> (Harland and Rudwick). This became a problem for Harland because of the thought of the "Runaway Snowball Paradox" (a kind of <a href="/wiki/Snowball_effect" title="Snowball effect">Snowball effect</a>) that, once the earth enters the route of becoming a snowball earth, it would never be able to leave that state. However, in 1992 <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Joe_Kirschvink&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Joe Kirschvink (page does not exist)">Joe Kirschvink</a> brought up a solution to the paradox. It is believed that since the continents at this time were huddled at the low and mid-latitudes that there was a great cooling event by planetary <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>, or reflection of the earth’s surface. Kirschvink explained that the way to get out of the snowball could be connected to carbon dioxide, since volcanic activity would not halt, and that the buildup and lack of "<a href="/wiki/Scrubber" title="Scrubber">scrubbing</a>" of this carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, that the earth would return to a greenhouse state. Some scientists believe that the end of the snowball Earth caused an event known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambrian_Explosion" title="Cambrian Explosion">Cambrian Explosion</a>, which produced the beginnings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multi-cellular" title="Multi-cellular">multi-cellular</a> life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However some biologists claim that a complete snowball Earth could not have happened since <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photosynthetic" title="Photosynthetic">photosynthetic</a> life would not have survived underneath many meters of ice without <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>. However, it has been observed that, even under meters of thick ice around Antarctica, sunlight shows through. Most scientists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> today believe that a "hard" Snowball Earth, one completely covered by ice, is probably impossible. However, a "slushball earth", with points of openings near the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, is possible.
</p><p>Recent studies may have again complicated the idea of a snowball earth. In October 2011, a team of French researchers announced that the carbon dioxide during the last speculated "snowball earth" may have been lower than originally stated, which provides a challenge in finding out how Earth was able to get out of its state and if it were a snowball or slushball.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transitions">Transitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a>, which occurred between 53 and 49 million years ago, was the Earth's warmest temperature period for 100 million years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> However, this "super-greenhouse" eventually became an icehouse by the late Eocene. It was believed that the decline of CO<sub>2</sub> caused this change, though there are possible <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedbacks</a>, or added influence that contributes to the cooling.
</p><p>The best record we have for a transition from an icehouse to a greenhouse period where plant life exists is during the <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> <a href="/wiki/Age_(geology)" title="Age (geology)">epoch</a> that occurred around 300 million years ago. In 40 million years a major transition took place, causing the Earth to change from a moist, icy planet where <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforests</a> covered the tropics, into a hot, dry, and windy location where little could survive. Professor <a href="/wiki/Isabel_P._Monta%C3%B1ez" title="Isabel P. Montañez">Isabel P. Montañez</a> of <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Davis" title="University of California, Davis">University of California, Davis</a>, who has researched this time period, found the climate to be "highly unstable" and "marked by dips and rises in carbon dioxide".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCDavis_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-UCDavis-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts">Impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Eocene-Oligocene transition, the latest transition occurring approximately 34 million years ago, resulted in rapid global temperature decrease, the glaciation of Antarctica and a series of biotic extinction events. The most dramatic species turnover event associated with this time period is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grande_Coupure" title="Grande Coupure">Grande Coupure</a>, a period which saw the replacement of European tree-dwelling and leaf-eating mammal species by migratory species from Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The science of <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatology</a> attempts to understand the history of greenhouse and icehouse conditions over geological time. Through the study of <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">dendrochronology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediments</a> (<a href="/wiki/Varve" title="Varve">varve</a>), <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">palynology</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossilized</a> <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> analysis (such as <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">Radiometric dating</a>), scientists can create models of past climate. One study has shown that atmospheric carbon dioxide levels during the <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> age rocked back and forth between 250 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">parts per million</a> (which is close to present-day levels) up to 2,000 parts per million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCDavis_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-UCDavis-14">[14]</a></sup> Studies on lake sediments suggest that the "Hothouse" or "super-Greenhouse" <a href="/wiki/Eocene" title="Eocene">Eocene</a> was in a "permanent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Nino" title="El Nino">El Nino</a> state" after the 10 °C warming of the deep ocean and high latitude surface temperatures shut down the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>'s El Nino-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Oscillation" title="Southern Oscillation">Southern Oscillation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> A theory was suggested for the <a href="/wiki/Paleocene%E2%80%93Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum</a> on the sudden decrease of carbon isotopic composition of global inorganic carbon pool by 2.5 parts per million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> A hypothesis noted for this negative drop of isotopes could be the increase of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methane_hydrate" title="Methane hydrate">methane hydrates</a>, the trigger for which remains a mystery. This increase of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">methane in the atmosphere</a>, which happens to be a potent, but short-lived greenhouse gas, increased the global temperatures by 6 °C with the assistance of the less potent carbon dioxide.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_Icehouse_and_Greenhouse_Periods">List of Icehouse and Greenhouse Periods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: List of Icehouse and Greenhouse Periods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>A greenhouse period ran from 4.6 to 2.4 billion years ago.<br/></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huronian" title="Huronian">Huronian Glaciation</a> - an icehouse period that ran from 2.4 billion years ago to 2.1 billion years ago<br/></li>
<li>A greenhouse period ran from 2.1 billion to 720 million years ago.<br/></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cryogenian" title="Cryogenian">Cryogenian</a> - an icehouse period that ran from 720 to 635 million years ago, at times the entire Earth was frozen over<br/></li>
<li>A greenhouse period ran from 635 million years ago to 450 million years ago.<br/></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andean-Saharan_glaciation" title="Andean-Saharan glaciation">Andean-Saharan glaciation</a> - an icehouse period that ran from 450 to 420 million years ago<br/></li>
<li>A greenhouse period ran from 420 million years ago to 360 million years ago.<br/></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karoo_Ice_Age" title="Karoo Ice Age">Late Paleozoic Ice Age</a> - an icehouse period that ran from 360 to 260 million years ago<br/></li>
<li>A greenhouse period ran from 260 million years ago to 33.9 million years ago<br/></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Late_Cenozoic_Ice_Age" title="Late Cenozoic Ice Age">Late Cenozoic Ice Age</a> - The current ice age which began 33.9 million years ago<br/></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_conditions">Modern conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_and_icehouse_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Modern conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currently, the Earth is in an icehouse climate state. About 34 million years ago, ice sheets began to form in <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>; the ice sheets in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> did not start forming until 2 million years ago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montanez-p24-27_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Montanez-p24-27-5">[5]</a></sup> Some processes that may have led to our current icehouse may be connected to the development of the Himalayan Mountains and the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Drake_Passage" title="Drake Passage">Drake Passage</a> between <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and Antarctica.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Scientists have been attempting to compare the past transitions between icehouse and greenhouse, and vice versa to understand where our planet is now heading.
</p><p>Without the human influence on the greenhouse gas concentration, the Earth would be heading toward a <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">glacial</a> period. Predicted changes in <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">orbital forcing</a> suggest that in absence of human-made <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> the next glacial period would begin at least 50,000 years from now<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BergerLoutre_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-BergerLoutre-18">[18]</a></sup> (see <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a>).
</p><p>But due to the ongoing anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions, the Earth is instead heading toward a greenhouse Earth period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montanez-p24-27_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Montanez-p24-27-5">[5]</a></sup> Permanent ice is actually a rare phenomenon in the history of the Earth, occurring only in coincidence with the icehouse effect, which has affected about 20% of Earth's history.
</p>
