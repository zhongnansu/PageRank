<p><b>British racing green</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> or <b>BRG</b>, a colour similar to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brunswick_green" title="Brunswick green">Brunswick green</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunter_green" title="Hunter green">hunter green</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Forest_green" title="Forest green">forest green</a></i> or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moss_green" title="Moss green">moss green</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RAL_6005" title="RAL 6005">RAL 6005</a>), takes its name from the green <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_international_auto_racing_colors" title="List of international auto racing colors">international motor racing colour</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. This originated with the 1903 <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Bennett_Cup_(auto_racing)" title="Gordon Bennett Cup (auto racing)">Gordon Bennett Cup</a>, held in Ireland (then still part of the UK), as motor-racing was illegal in England. As a mark of respect, the British cars were painted shamrock green.
</p><p>Although there is still some debate as to an exact hue for BRG, currently the term is used to denote a spectrum of deep, rich greens. "British racing green" in motorsport terms meant only the colour green in general – its application to a specific shade has developed outside the sport.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_the_association">Origins of the association</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_racing_green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins of the association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:1928_Bentley_4_1_2_litre_Vanden_Plas_Le_Mans_Sports_(3828588777).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/1928_Bentley_4_1_2_litre_Vanden_Plas_Le_Mans_Sports_%283828588777%29.jpg/220px-1928_Bentley_4_1_2_litre_Vanden_Plas_Le_Mans_Sports_%283828588777%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/1928_Bentley_4_1_2_litre_Vanden_Plas_Le_Mans_Sports_%283828588777%29.jpg/330px-1928_Bentley_4_1_2_litre_Vanden_Plas_Le_Mans_Sports_%283828588777%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/1928_Bentley_4_1_2_litre_Vanden_Plas_Le_Mans_Sports_%283828588777%29.jpg/440px-1928_Bentley_4_1_2_litre_Vanden_Plas_Le_Mans_Sports_%283828588777%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:1928_Bentley_4_1_2_litre_Vanden_Plas_Le_Mans_Sports_(3828588777).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1928 <a href="/wiki/Bentley_4%C2%BD_Litre" title="Bentley 4½ Litre">Bentley 4½ Litre</a>. The lighter colour is painted metalwork, the darker a fabric body</div></div></div>
<p>In the days of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gordon_Bennett_Cup_in_auto_racing" title="Gordon Bennett Cup in auto racing">Gordon Bennett Cup</a>, Count <a href="/wiki/Eliot_Zborowski" title="Eliot Zborowski">Eliot Zborowski</a>, father of inter-war racing legend <a href="/wiki/Louis_Zborowski" title="Louis Zborowski">Louis Zborowski</a>, suggested that each national entrant be allotted a different colour. Every component of a car had to be produced in the competing country, as well as the driver being of that nationality. The races were hosted in the country of the previous year's winner. When Britain first competed <a href="/wiki/1902_Gordon_Bennett_Cup" title="1902 Gordon Bennett Cup">in 1902</a>, they had to choose a different colour from the national flag colours of red, white and blue, because those had already been taken for the <a href="/wiki/1900_Gordon_Bennett_Cup" title="1900 Gordon Bennett Cup">1900 race</a> by America, Germany and France respectively. (Italy did not adopt its famous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rosso_Corsa" title="Rosso Corsa">'Racing Red'</a> until a red <a href="/wiki/Itala" title="Itala">Itala</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Peking_to_Paris" title="Peking to Paris">Peking to Paris</a> race in 1907). When <a href="/wiki/Selwyn_Edge" title="Selwyn Edge">Selwyn Edge</a> won the <a href="/wiki/1902_Gordon_Bennett_Cup" title="1902 Gordon Bennett Cup">1902 Gordon Bennett Cup</a> race for England in his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napier_%26_Son" title="Napier &amp; Son">Napier</a> it was decided that the <a href="/wiki/1903_Gordon_Bennett_Cup" title="1903 Gordon Bennett Cup">1903 race</a> would be held in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, at that time a part of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a>, as motor racing at the time was illegal in Great Britain. As a mark of respect for their Irish hosts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> Napier cars were painted shamrock green.
</p><p>In keeping with these Irish/Napier roots, many of the earliest greens used on British racing cars were of a lighter <i><a href="/wiki/Olive_(color)" title="Olive (color)">olive</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moss_green" title="Moss green">moss</a></i> or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emerald_(color)" title="Emerald (color)">emerald</a></i> green. Later, darker shades became more common, though there was a return to lighter greens by <a href="/wiki/Hersham_and_Walton_Motors" title="Hersham and Walton Motors">HWM</a> and other teams in the 1950s. Initially the colour use only applied to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grande_%C3%89preuve" title="Grande Épreuve">grandes épreuves</a>, but was later codified in the <i>Code Sportif International</i> (<i>CSI</i>) of the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_l%27Automobile" title="Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile">Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile</a> (<i>FIA</i>) for use in all international-level motor racing events.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_rise_to_prominence">International rise to prominence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_racing_green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: International rise to prominence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jaguar_XKD403.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1992" data-file-width="3026" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Jaguar_XKD403.JPG/220px-Jaguar_XKD403.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Jaguar_XKD403.JPG/330px-Jaguar_XKD403.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Jaguar_XKD403.JPG/440px-Jaguar_XKD403.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jaguar_XKD403.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1954 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jaguar_D-type" title="Jaguar D-type">Jaguar D-type</a></div></div></div>
<p>The foremost British participant in International Motor Racing at the highest echelons both before and after the Great War was Sunbeam (from 1920 part of the <a href="/wiki/Darracq_and_Company_London#S.T.D._Motors" title="Darracq and Company London">STD Motors</a> Combine). Green liveried <a href="/wiki/Sunbeam_Motor_Car_Company" title="Sunbeam Motor Car Company">Sunbeam</a> Racing Cars won the 1912 Coupe de l’Auto as well as being the first (and last for several decades) British team to win the European Grand Epreuves Grand Prix in both 1923 and 1924. The Green Sunbeams driven by the likes of Henry Segrave and K L Guinness were during the vintage period, the prominent competitors to watch for.
</p><p>In the 1920s <a href="/wiki/Bentley" title="Bentley">Bentley</a> cars were hugely successful at the <a href="/wiki/Le_Mans" title="Le Mans">Le Mans</a> 24h races, all sporting a mid- to dark-green. The first recorded use of the darkest green shades was on the <a href="/wiki/Bugatti" title="Bugatti">Bugatti</a> of Briton <a href="/wiki/William_Grover-Williams" title="William Grover-Williams">William Grover-Williams</a>, driving in the very <a href="/wiki/1929_Monaco_Grand_Prix" title="1929 Monaco Grand Prix">first Monaco Grand Prix</a>, in 1929. This colour has become known as <i>British Racing Green</i>. In the 1950s and 1960s British teams such as <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin" title="Aston Martin">Aston Martin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanwall" title="Vanwall">Vanwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cooper_Car_Company" title="Cooper Car Company">Cooper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Team_Lotus" title="Team Lotus">Lotus</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BRM" title="BRM">BRM</a> were successful in <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sports_car_racing" title="Sports car racing">Sports car racing</a>, all in different shades of green. Scottish teams such as <a href="/wiki/Ecurie_Ecosse" title="Ecurie Ecosse">Ecurie Ecosse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rob_Walker_Racing_Team" title="Rob Walker Racing Team">Rob Walker Racing</a> used a dark blue, which did not strictly conform to the CSI rules but was tolerated by officials. The Australian-owned but British-based and licensed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Brabham" title="Brabham">Brabham</a> team also used a shade of BRG, and this was augmented with a gold (later yellow) stripe, gold and green being the national sporting colours of Australia. Another British-based and licensed team, <a href="/wiki/McLaren" title="McLaren">McLaren</a>, made their debut at the <a href="/wiki/1966_Monaco_Grand_Prix" title="1966 Monaco Grand Prix">1966 Monaco Grand Prix</a> with the <a href="/wiki/McLaren_M2B" title="McLaren M2B">McLaren M2B</a> car painted white with a green stripe, to represent a fictional Yamura team in the <a href="/wiki/John_Frankenheimer" title="John Frankenheimer">John Frankenheimer</a>´s film <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_(1966_film)" title="Grand Prix (1966 film)">Grand Prix </a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Under pressure from a number of teams, most famously the <a href="/wiki/Team_Lotus" title="Team Lotus">Lotus team</a> who wished to use the <a href="/wiki/John_Player_%26_Sons" title="John Player &amp; Sons">Gold Leaf</a> livery on the <a href="/wiki/Lotus_49" title="Lotus 49">Lotus 49</a>, in <a href="/wiki/1968_Formula_One_season" title="1968 Formula One season">1968</a> sponsorship regulations were relaxed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F1" title="F1">F1</a>. Subsequently, Lotus made their debut in this new livery at the <a href="/wiki/1968_Spanish_Grand_Prix" title="1968 Spanish Grand Prix">1968 Spanish Grand Prix</a>. In <a href="/wiki/1970_Formula_One_season" title="1970 Formula One season">1970</a> the FIA formally gave Formula One an exemption from the national colours ruling and the previously common green colour soon disappeared, being replaced by various sponsor liveries. This exemption has since been extended to all race series, unless specific regulations require the adoption of national colours.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_usage">Modern usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=British_racing_green&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:X-type_Jaguar_in_British_Racing_Green,_model_Casini.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2056" data-file-width="3088" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/X-type_Jaguar_in_British_Racing_Green%2C_model_Casini.jpg/260px-X-type_Jaguar_in_British_Racing_Green%2C_model_Casini.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/X-type_Jaguar_in_British_Racing_Green%2C_model_Casini.jpg/390px-X-type_Jaguar_in_British_Racing_Green%2C_model_Casini.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/X-type_Jaguar_in_British_Racing_Green%2C_model_Casini.jpg/520px-X-type_Jaguar_in_British_Racing_Green%2C_model_Casini.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:X-type_Jaguar_in_British_Racing_Green,_model_Casini.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>X-type Jaguar in metallic British Racing Green, model Casini</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:58_Aston_Martin_DBR9.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="3072" height="173" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/58_Aston_Martin_DBR9.jpg/260px-58_Aston_Martin_DBR9.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/58_Aston_Martin_DBR9.jpg/390px-58_Aston_Martin_DBR9.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/58_Aston_Martin_DBR9.jpg/520px-58_Aston_Martin_DBR9.jpg 2x" width="260"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:58_Aston_Martin_DBR9.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DBR9" title="Aston Martin DBR9">Aston Martin DBR9</a>, showing a modern metallic interpretation of a lighter shade of British Racing Green.</div></div></div>
<p>The history of the famous greens was revived in 2000 by <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_Racing" title="Jaguar Racing">Jaguar Racing</a> in Formula One, but after this team was sold to <a href="/wiki/Red_Bull" title="Red Bull">Red Bull</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a> in 2004, the new <a href="/wiki/Red_Bull_Racing" title="Red Bull Racing">Red Bull Racing</a> team used their own colours.
</p><p>Other traditionally British manufacturers have since followed suit. Bentley returned briefly to the Le Mans circuit in 2001, 2002 and 2003, winning with the <a href="/wiki/Bentley_Speed_8" title="Bentley Speed 8">Bentley Speed 8</a>, painted in a very dark shade of BRG. In recent years Aston Martin has also returned to endurance racing, with their <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DBR9" title="Aston Martin DBR9">DBR9s</a> painted in, a typically Aston, light BRG. <a href="/wiki/Rocketsports_Racing" title="Rocketsports Racing">Rocketsports Racing</a> also used green for its <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_XK" title="Jaguar XK">Jaguar XK</a> in the 24 Hours of Le Mans and <a href="/wiki/American_Le_Mans_Series" title="American Le Mans Series">American Le Mans Series</a> and other.
</p><p>In 2010 the Lotus name returned to Formula One after a gap of 16 years with the <a href="/wiki/Team_Lotus_(2010%E2%80%9311)" title="Team Lotus (2010–11)">Lotus Racing</a> team's <a href="/wiki/Lotus_T127" title="Lotus T127">Lotus T127</a> car liveried in dark green with yellow. Although registered in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, the new team is based in Britain and chose BRG with the aim of "striking an emotional chord with young and old alike and evoking memories of some of motor racing most iconic moments".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the many successes of British racing teams through the years, British Racing Green became a popular paint choice for British sports and luxury cars.  Originally a solid colour, British Racing Green is increasingly a <a href="/wiki/Metallic_paint" title="Metallic paint">metallic paint</a> due to the limited range of solids offered by today's manufacturers.
</p><p>Paying tribute to the small British roadsters of the 1960s that inspired the <a href="/wiki/Mazda_MX-5" title="Mazda MX-5">Mazda MX-5</a> (such as the <a href="/wiki/Triumph_Spitfire" title="Triumph Spitfire">Triumph Spitfire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austin-Healey_Sprite" title="Austin-Healey Sprite">Austin-Healey Sprite</a>, <a href="/wiki/MG_MGB" title="MG MGB">MG MGB</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lotus_Elan" title="Lotus Elan">Lotus Elan</a>), Mazda produced a limited edition version of the model in 1991 and 2001 called the "British Racing Edition", which included green paint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Similarly, the modern <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a>-owned <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mini_Hatchback" title="Mini Hatchback">Mini Hatchback</a> marque, which is assembled at their <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swindon" title="Swindon">Swindon</a> factories,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> includes a BRG colour option. Originally a somewhat murky dark olive, this was updated to a fresher shade of metallic green in the 2011 version.
</p>
